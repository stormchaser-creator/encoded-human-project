## SelfDecode DNA Analysis

**Body Codx Operation:** Cross-Operation (all operations modulated)
**What It Measures:** 1,500+ personalized health reports across 48 health areas derived from imputation across ~83 million genetic variants from 700,000 SNPs.
**Sample Type:** Upload raw DNA data from 23andMe, AncestryDNA, or similar GWAS platform
**Fasting Required:** N/A

### What This Number Means

SelfDecode uses a proprietary machine learning algorithm trained on 10+ million publicly available genomes to impute the individual's genetic status across the entire genomic landscape from the 700,000 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) directly genotyped. The algorithm learns linkage disequilibrium patterns — the tendency for nearby variants to be inherited together — and uses these patterns to estimate the probability of ungenotyped variants across the genome. The output is a risk score for each health condition, computed as the weighted sum of effect sizes for all variants contributing to that trait, multiplied by the individual's specific genotype.

What distinguishes this from clinical grade genetic panels is the computational breadth. A targeted gene panel (e.g., for familial hypercholesterolemia) genotypes ~50 genes at high depth. SelfDecode imputes across the entire genome and reports on 1,500+ traits simultaneously. The cost is reduced interpretability — the effects are statistical associations from GWAS meta-analyses, not mechanistically validated variants. The benefit is a truly panoramic view of the genetic architecture underlying the person's health and disease liability across all eight operations simultaneously.

The reports employ several risk stratification schemes. The primary is a percentile rank: whether the individual's polygenic risk score (PRS) for a given trait places them in the top 10%, top 25%, average, bottom 25%, or bottom 10% of the population. The secondary is a "genetic strength" score reflecting the strength of evidence from the underlying GWAS studies. The tertiary is relative risk — how many times higher or lower their genetic risk is compared to the population average. These three schemas together communicate not just whether someone is at elevated genetic risk, but with how much confidence and relative to what population baseline.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

For SelfDecode, reference ranges do not apply in the conventional sense. Instead, the interpretation is comparative and probabilistic:

- **Top 10% genetic risk:** Substantially elevated genetic liability; may warrant preventive intervention even in the absence of phenotypic markers
- **Top 25% genetic risk:** Moderately elevated genetic liability; consider interaction with environmental factors and intermediate biomarkers
- **Average genetic risk (25–75th percentile):** Population-typical genetic background; does not indicate immunity to disease, merely population-level risk
- **Bottom 25% genetic risk:** Lower-than-average genetic liability; lifestyle factors become the primary modifiable lever
- **Bottom 10% genetic risk:** Substantially reduced genetic liability; even poor lifestyle choices may not produce the disease in question

The critical interpretive principle: SelfDecode reports *genetic liability*, not destiny. A person in the top 10% for Alzheimer's disease risk based on polygenic risk score does not have Alzheimer's disease; they have an elevated statistical likelihood of developing it, modulated by all environmental and epigenetic factors not captured by the genotype alone.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**All Eight Operations — Genetic Architecture**

SelfDecode provides a kind of "genetic MRI" of the person's biological landscape. The 1,500 reports should be organized not trait-by-trait but by operation:

- **Digestion:** Genetic risk scores for celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, lactose intolerance, celiac pathophysiology
- **Circulation:** Coronary artery disease, atrial fibrillation, stroke, venous thromboembolism, arterial stiffness predisposition
- **Immunity/Defense:** Rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, psoriasis, multiple sclerosis, asthma, allergic disease, COVID-19 severity
- **Respiration:** Lung function (FEV1), COPD, cystic fibrosis carrier status
- **Elimination:** Chronic kidney disease, gout, kidney stone formation, proteinuria
- **Transduction:** Thyroid disease (Graves', Hashimoto's), type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance, hemoglobin A1c variance
- **Synthesis:** Testosterone levels, bone mineral density, Vitamin D synthesis variants
- **Restoration:** Sleep duration predisposition, insomnia, REM sleep, slow-wave sleep variants

The individual's SelfDecode "profile" emerges as a constellation of elevated and diminished genetic liabilities across operations — a genetic fingerprint distinct from any other human being.

### What Deviation Signals

**If top 10% genetic risk for cardiometabolic disease (CAD, diabetes, stroke, atrial fibrillation):**
- Prioritize early intervention on modifiable factors — even modest changes in LDL-C, blood pressure, or glucose may disproportionately benefit someone with high genetic liability
- Consider imaging studies (carotid intima-media thickness, coronary calcium) earlier than population guidelines suggest
- Monitor cardiac biomarkers (troponin, BNP) at lower thresholds
- Genetic lipid disorder screening warranted if LDL-C and triglycerides are elevated

**If bottom 10% genetic risk for multiple disease processes:**
- Does not indicate perfect health; do not neglect lifestyle factors
- May indicate unusual resilience to specific insults; useful for planning intervention hierarchy
- Environmental toxin exposure, severe trauma, or extreme stress may still produce disease even with protective genetic background

**If top 10% genetic risk for Alzheimer's disease or cognitive decline:**
- Even with normal cognitive testing and biomarkers today, prioritize lifestyle factors with strongest evidence (sleep, aerobic exercise, cognitive engagement, social connection)
- Consider biomarker screening (plasma tau, phosphorylated tau, amyloid-beta) before symptom onset
- Modifiable risk factors become disproportionately important with high genetic liability

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Individual genetic panels (Color, 23andMe Health Predispositions)** — These focus on Mendelian conditions and large-effect variants; SelfDecode captures the distributed genetic architecture of complex diseases
- **Polygenic scores from research platforms** — Broader disease scores or biomarker-specific scores (glucose, cholesterol, etc.) provide complementary grain sizes
- **Intermediate biomarkers** (LDL-C, Lp(a), blood pressure, fasting glucose, HbA1c, hs-CRP) — These show the phenotypic expression of the genotype; absence of biomarker elevation despite high genetic risk may indicate protective gene-environment interactions
- **Lifestyle data and clinical outcomes** — The most powerful reading combines genetic risk scores with actual biomarker and outcome data; a high CAD-risk genotype with excellent lipids and no plaques is a different clinical picture than high-risk genotype with poor lipids and atherosclerotic disease

**Key pattern:** SelfDecode is most useful not as a deterministic predictor but as a *risk prioritization tool*. If the person has top 10% genetic risk for five separate disease processes, the question becomes: which modifiable factors will have the greatest leverage? Where should monitoring begin?

### Intervention Levers

SelfDecode identifies the genetic substrate; it does not identify the intervention points. Use the genetic risk scores to inform the intensity and prioritization of screening and preventive strategies for high-risk conditions. For individuals with high polygenic risk burden, focus on the environmental factors with the strongest effect size for each disease process. For individuals with protective genetic backgrounds, do not assume immunity — use the reassurance to focus energy on lifestyle factors that have evidence across multiple disease domains (sleep, exercise, relational connection).

## Promethease Report

**Body Codx Operation:** Cross-Operation (all operations modulated)
**What It Measures:** 100,000+ genetic variants matched against SNPedia database, each classified by estimated effect size, evidence strength, and frequency.
**Sample Type:** Upload raw DNA data from 23andMe, AncestryDNA, or similar genotyping platform
**Fasting Required:** N/A

### What This Number Means

Promethease ("PROMethean Ancestry")  is a crowdsourced genomics knowledge base that aggregates millions of published association studies and curates them into a single searchable variant database. When you upload raw DNA data, Promethease matches your genotype against the SNPedia database and generates a report categorizing your variants as:

- **Pathogenic (Clinically significant):** Variants associated with Mendelian disorders or very high disease risk with strong validation
- **Likely pathogenic:** Strong evidence but not yet definitively validated in populations
- **Variants of uncertain significance (VUS):** Limited evidence; effect direction or magnitude unclear
- **Likely benign:** Minimal disease association; may have protective or neutral effects
- **Benign:** Strong evidence against pathogenicity; may be beneficial

For each variant, Promethease provides: the effect size (odds ratio or hazard ratio if available), the population frequency of the risk allele, the number of genome-wide association studies (GWAS) supporting the association, and links to the underlying papers. The output is browsable by chromosome, by trait category, or by effect size — allowing the individual to navigate their genome as a landscape in which each variant is located and annotated.

Unlike clinical gene panels that focus on rare, high-penetrance variants, Promethease catalogs common variants (minor allele frequency > 0.01) with small-to-moderate effect sizes — the same variants that aggregate into the polygenic risk scores used by population health screens. It thus occupies a middle ground: more variant-rich than clinical panels, more interpretable than raw imputation, more transparent than proprietary algorithms.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

Promethease does not generate ranges; instead, it provides a variant-level evidence grading system:

- **Clinically pathogenic variants:** Require verification through clinical genetic testing and genetic counseling; may warrant preventive screening or intervention regardless of other factors
- **Variants with "strong" or "very strong" evidence:** Effect sizes above 1.5-fold relative risk; warrant monitoring or intervention if they cluster with other risk factors
- **Variants with "moderate" evidence:** Relative risk 1.2–1.5 fold; should be interpreted in context of other genetic and environmental factors
- **Variants with "limited" or "conflicting" evidence:** Relative risk < 1.2 fold or contradictory associations; use cautiously; await further validation
- **Variants classified as benign:** Risk of false reassurance; a single benign variant among hundreds of pathogenic ones does not alter overall genetic liability

The key interpretive frame: Promethease is a variant browser, not a risk calculator. It shows you what's in your genome and what the literature says about each piece; it does not aggregate them into a unified score. You must synthesize the reading yourself.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Cross-Operation, Variant-Level**

Promethease reports should be organized by body operation and cross-referenced with specific biological mechanisms:

- **Digestion:** Variants in celiac-associated HLA alleles, lactase persistence (MCM6), FODMAPs sensitivity genes, bile acid receptor variants
- **Circulation:** ABO blood group (affects thrombotic risk), apolipoprotein variants (APOE, APOB), lipase variants (LIPC), cholesterol ester transfer protein (CETP), familial hypercholesterolemia variants
- **Immunity/Defense:** HLA variants (associated with autoimmune disease risk), cytokine gene variants, toll-like receptor (TLR) variants, NOD2 (Crohn's disease), vitamin D receptor (VDR) variants
- **Respiration:** Alpha-1 antitrypsin (SERPINA1) variants, pulmonary function variants
- **Elimination:** Urate transporters (URAT1), kidney disease variants, cystatin C variants
- **Transduction/Metabolism:** MTHFR variants (methylation), glycosylation variants, CYP family variants (Phase I detoxification)
- **Synthesis:** Growth hormone receptor variants, sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG) variants, vitamin D synthesis variants
- **Restoration:** Clock gene variants (CLOCK, PER2, PER3), melatonin pathway variants

Each variant is read not in isolation but as part of a functional cascade: if the person has variants in both MTHFR and MTRR, methylation cycling may be impaired; if they have multiple dyslipidemia variants clustering in Circulation, lipid management requires multimodal intervention.

### What Deviation Signals

**If Promethease reports pathogenic variants in clinically validated genes:**
- Pathogenic variants in BRCA1/2 (breast/ovarian cancer), LDLR (familial hypercholesterolemia), PCSK9 (familial hypercholesterolemia), MLH1/MSH2 (Lynch syndrome), HFE (hereditary hemochromatosis), or similar
- Warrants clinical genetic counseling and confirmatory testing through an accredited clinical lab
- May change screening frequency, preventive strategies, or family communication
- Should be communicated to healthcare provider and documented in medical record

**If Promethease shows clustering of common variants in a single pathway:**
- Multiple variants in lipid metabolism genes → elevated cardiovascular risk; stronger indication for statin therapy or intensive lipid management
- Multiple variants in glucose metabolism → increased type 2 diabetes and metabolic syndrome risk; prioritize glucose control and exercise
- Multiple variants in methylation pathway → may indicate need for methylation-supporting supplementation (folate, B12, betaine)

**If Promethease shows discordance between genotype and clinical phenotype:**
- Person carries protective variants but has disease phenotype → look for gene-environment interactions, epigenetic modification, or testing error
- Person carries risk variants but lacks disease phenotype → evidence of genetic resilience; identify protective environmental factors

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **SelfDecode polygenic scores** — Broader disease liabilities; Promethease provides the underlying variant-by-variant detail
- **Genetic Genie or similar functional reports** — These interpret variants in context of specific biological pathways; Promethease provides the raw catalogue
- **Intermediate biomarkers** (lipids, glucose, liver function) — Show which variants are actually expressing a phenotype in this person
- **Clinical outcomes and family history** — Penetrance of genetic variants is highly variable; actual clinical outcomes inform whether a variant warrants intervention

**Key pattern:** Promethease is most useful as a "genetic literacy tool" — it teaches you what's in your genome and where the variant-level evidence comes from. The medical utility comes when high-burden pathways are identified and cross-referenced with biomarkers and clinical outcomes.

### Intervention Levers

Use Promethease to identify high-burden genetic pathways (cluster of variants in a single operation or metabolic cascade) and cross-reference with biomarkers. A person with five variants affecting lipid metabolism is a candidate for earlier statin therapy or more intensive lifestyle intervention than population guidelines suggest. A person with pathogenic variants in a cancer-predisposition gene requires adjusted screening protocols. The leverage point is not the individual variant but the *functional coherence* of the variant burden — do the variants cluster into a coherent biological dysfunction, or is the burden scattered?

## Genetic Genie Methylation/Detox Report

**Body Codx Operation:** Transduction
**What It Measures:** Methylation cycle enzymes, detoxification enzymes, and nutrient-metabolism variants impacting Phase I/II/III enzymatic function and methylation-dependent processes.
**Sample Type:** Upload raw DNA data from 23andMe, AncestryDNA, or similar platform
**Fasting Required:** N/A

### What This Number Means

The "methylation pathway" encompasses the enzymatic machinery that adds and removes methyl groups (-CH3) from DNA, proteins, lipids, and other biomolecules. Proper methylation is essential for epigenetic regulation, neurotransmitter synthesis, phospholipid metabolism, and detoxification. The Genetic Genie report focuses on genes whose variants impair one of five key enzymatic systems:

1. **Methionine cycle enzymes** — MTHFR, MTRR, MTR, CBS — responsible for converting homocysteine to methionine and then to S-adenosyl methionine (SAM), the universal methyl donor
2. **Folate pathway enzymes** — FOLR1, DHFR, TYMS — responsible for folate activation and DNA synthesis
3. **Detoxification Phase I enzymes** — CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, CYP3A5, CYP1A2 — responsible for oxidative metabolism of drugs and xenobiotics
4. **Detoxification Phase II enzymes** — COMT, MAO, GSTP1, TPMT — responsible for conjugating toxins to glutathione, sulfate, or other compounds for water-solubility and excretion
5. **Nutrient absorption and transport** — TCN2, FOLR1, FUT2 — affecting B12, folate, and micronutrient bioavailability

Variants in these genes produce "loss-of-function" (reduced enzyme activity), "gain-of-function" (increased enzyme activity), or "altered-function" (changed substrate specificity or cofactor requirement) effects. The phenotypic result is highly variable: a heterozygous MTHFR C677T variant may have no clinical consequence, or it may contribute to methylation dysfunction if combined with other dietary, genetic, or environmental factors.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

Genetic Genie uses a "variant severity" classification rather than a quantitative range:

- **Homozygous pathogenic variants (e.g., MTHFR C677T/T):** Enzyme activity reduced to ~30% of normal; may require methylation-supporting intervention even without other risk factors
- **Heterozygous pathogenic variants (e.g., MTHFR C677T/C):** Enzyme activity reduced to ~65% of normal; clinically significant primarily when combined with nutritional deficiency or other pathway dysfunction
- **Compound heterozygous variants in same gene (e.g., MTHFR C677T + A1298C):** Possible additive effect; enzyme activity further reduced
- **Multiple variants across methylation cascade (e.g., MTHFR + MTRR + MTR):** High likelihood of methylation dysfunction; strong indication for methylation support
- **No pathogenic variants in core pathway:** Lower risk of inherited methylation dysfunction; deficiency likely environmental (nutrient deficiency) rather than genetic

The critical interpretive principle: Genetic variants are *predispositions*, not diagnoses. A person with homozygous MTHFR C677T has reduced enzyme function; they do not necessarily have a methylation disorder. The phenotype depends on dietary folate and B12 status, homocysteine levels, and whether other pathway enzymes are also impaired.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction — Methylation and Detoxification Capacity**

The methylation pathway and detoxification systems are both core Transduction functions — converting incoming signals and xenobiotic challenges into biochemical outputs. Reading these variants through the Transduction lens:

**Methylation sufficiency:** The capacity to generate sufficient SAM (the universal methyl donor) to support epigenetic regulation, neurotransmitter synthesis, and phospholipid remodeling. Variants that reduce MTHFR, MTRR, or MTR activity lower the system's methylation capacity. Compensatory strategies include increased dietary methylated B vitamins (methylcobalamin, methylfolate) and trimethylglycine (betaine).

**Detoxification efficiency:** The speed and completeness with which Phase I/II/III enzymatic systems can render xenobiotics, drugs, and endogenous metabolites water-soluble for excretion. Variants in CYP2D6, CYP2C19, COMT, MAO, and TPMT determine whether the person is a "fast metabolizer" (rapid drug clearance, lower drug levels, potential for subtherapeutic dosing), "normal metabolizer" (population typical), or "poor metabolizer" (slow drug clearance, higher drug levels, increased toxicity risk). These variants have direct implications for pharmacogenomics: specific drugs are contraindicated or require dose adjustment based on metabolizer status.

**Nutrient transport:** Variants affecting folate receptor (FOLR1), transcobalamin II (TCN2), and taste receptor (FUT2) influence whether the person's gut absorption and cellular uptake of critical methylation cofactors (folate, B12, choline) are efficient or impaired. Poor absorption requires higher supplementation doses.

### What Deviation Signals

**If homozygous MTHFR C677T (TT genotype):**
- Elevated baseline risk of methylation dysfunction, particularly if dietary folate or B12 is low
- Monitor homocysteine levels; if elevated, consider methylation-supporting supplementation
- May be more sensitive to folate antagonists (methotrexate) or anti-seizure drugs that deplete folate
- If planning pregnancy, consider preconception methylation support (methylfolate, methylcobalamin, trimethylglycine)

**If multiple variants across methylation cascade (MTHFR + MTRR + MTR or MTHFR + FOLR1 + TCN2):**
- Significantly elevated risk of methylation dysfunction and B12/folate deficiency
- Warrants biomarker confirmation: homocysteine, methylmalonic acid, holotranscobalamin, red cell folate
- Strong indication for methylation-supporting supplementation protocol
- May benefit from active forms of B vitamins (methylcobalamin, methylfolate) rather than inactive forms (cyanocobalamin, folic acid)

**If CYP2D6 poor metabolizer status (predicted by genotype):**
- List of medications requiring dose reduction or contraindication: codeine, tramadol, most antidepressants (fluoxetine, paroxetine, venlafaxine), antipsychotics (risperidone, aripiprazole), beta-blockers (metoprolol), opioids
- Should inform prescribing decisions; clinician should be notified of poor metabolizer status
- Requires longer washout periods between medication changes

**If CYP2C19 poor metabolizer status (predicted by genotype):**
- Medications requiring dose reduction: clopidogrel (Plavix), omeprazole, escitalopram, sertraline
- Clopidogrel is of particular clinical importance: poor metabolizers do not achieve adequate antiplatelet effect at standard doses; may require higher doses or alternative antiplatelet agents

**If COMT Val/Val genotype (fast COMT activity):**
- Rapidly catabolizes catecholamines (dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine) and estrogen
- May predispose to lower dopamine and norepinephrine levels, higher estrogen clearance
- Clinical implications: may benefit from dopamine-supporting interventions, estrogen stability, reduced caffeine (further increases catecholamine catabolism)

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Homocysteine and methylmalonic acid levels** — Biomarker confirmation of methylation cycle dysfunction
- **Holotranscobalamin and red cell folate** — Assessment of B12 and folate adequacy
- **Salivary estrogen and estrogen metabolites** — COMT variants influence estrogen metabolism; salivary estrogen can assess clearance adequacy
- **Nutritional assessment** — Dietary intake of methylation cofactors (folate, B12, choline, betaine); supplementation response
- **Pharmacogenomics panel** — Comprehensive CYP2D6 genotyping offers higher resolution than imputed genotypes

**Key pattern:** Methylation and detoxification are coupled; variants affecting one often affect the other. A person with MTHFR dysfunction who is also a poor CYP2D6 metabolizer faces a double constraint: reduced methylation capacity (for phospholipid synthesis, neurotransmitter regulation, epigenetic function) plus impaired drug clearance (higher accumulation of xenobiotics and medications). These individuals benefit from heightened attention to xenobiotic avoidance and targeted nutritional support.

### Intervention Levers

Methylation dysfunction is addressable through B-vitamin supplementation (methylfolate, methylcobalamin, active B6), betaine, and cofactor support (magnesium for methionine synthase activity, zinc for methyltransferases). Detoxification can be supported through Phase II nutrient substrates (sulfur-containing amino acids, glutathione precursors) and xenobiotic reduction. The key is matching intervention intensity to the genetic burden: homozygous dysfunction warrants comprehensive support; heterozygous with normal biomarkers may need only dietary attention.

## NutraHacker Reports

**Body Codx Operation:** Cross-Operation (Transduction primary, Digestion secondary)
**What It Measures:** Methylation pathway, detoxification, nutrient metabolism, carrier status for recessive conditions, and drug response variants across 50+ genes.
**Sample Type:** Upload raw DNA data or raw data from 23andMe, AncestryDNA
**Fasting Required:** N/A

### What This Number Means

NutraHacker is a consumer-facing genetic interpretation service that analyzes the same methylation and detoxification genes as Genetic Genie but adds additional reports on nutrient metabolism (response to different B vitamins, vitamin D synthesis efficiency, folate metabolism), carrier status for recessive diseases (cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, Tay-Sachs, spinal muscular atrophy), and drug-gene interactions (pharmacogenomics for common medications). The output is organized by functional category: methylation, detox, nutrient metabolism, carrier status, and medication response — with actionable recommendations for each.

Where Genetic Genie is variant-level and requires interpretation literacy, NutraHacker pre-interprets and provides directional guidance: if you have MTHFR dysfunction, take methylfolate and B12; if you're a poor CYP2D6 metabolizer, avoid codeine and watch for antidepressant side effects. The trade-off is reduced granularity — you see the recommendation but not necessarily all the variant-level detail underlying it.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

NutraHacker uses a categorical scoring system rather than quantitative ranges:

- **Optimized variants:** Variants associated with enhanced function or resistance to deficiency (e.g., folate-synthesizing variants, vitamin D receptor variants associated with higher 25-OH vitamin D at equal intake)
- **Normal/typical variants:** Population-typical genetic background; no special nutrient requirements or medication sensitivities expected
- **Suboptimal variants:** Single heterozygous or compound heterozygous variants associated with reduced function or increased requirement for specific nutrients
- **Significantly impaired variants:** Homozygous loss-of-function variants or compound heterozygotes across multiple genes in same pathway; high likelihood of clinically meaningful dysfunction

Recommendations scale with variant severity: normal variants receive standard population-level guidance; suboptimal variants receive targeted supplementation; significantly impaired variants receive intensive protocol.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction & Digestion — Nutrient Metabolism and Bioavailability**

NutraHacker focuses on the operatic functions of nutrient uptake (Digestion) and metabolism (Transduction). The reading centers on two questions:

**1. Nutrient bioavailability (Digestion):** Are the person's variants conducive to efficient absorption and transport of essential micronutrients? Variants in FUT2 (affects B12 and folate absorption), FOLR1 (folate receptor), TCN2 (transcobalamin, B12 transport), ALPL (alkaline phosphatase, mineral absorption), and VDR (vitamin D absorption) determine baseline absorption capacity.

**2. Nutrient metabolism (Transduction):** Are the person's variants conducive to efficient activation and utilization of micronutrients once absorbed? Variants in MTHFR (folate activation), TCN2 (B12 activation), VDR (calcitriol synthesis), CYP2R1 (vitamin D25-hydroxylation), and CYP27B1 (vitamin D1,25-dihydroxylation) determine whether the person requires standard, moderate, or high-dose nutrient supplementation to achieve optimal circulating levels.

A person with FUT2 non-secretor status (reduced B12 absorption) plus TCN2 variants (reduced B12 transport) plus poor CYP2C9 metabolism (if they take proton pump inhibitors further reducing gastric acid and B12 absorption) faces a profound B12 bioavailability challenge and may require sublingual or intramuscular B12 supplementation rather than oral forms.

### What Deviation Signals

**If FUT2 non-secretor status:**
- Reduced bacterial populations in gut microbiota
- Impaired B12 and folate absorption, particularly from plant sources
- May require cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin supplementation; oral forms less effective
- Associated with higher susceptibility to certain infections

**If multiple nutrient metabolism variants clustering in B-vitamin pathway:**
- May require comprehensive B-complex supplementation with active forms (methylfolate, methylcobalamin, pyridoxal-5-phosphate, riboflavin-5-phosphate)
- Higher-than-population-standard doses may be required; monitor homocysteine and methylmalonic acid to confirm adequacy
- Dietary sources of these vitamins often insufficient

**If VDR variants associated with lower vitamin D synthesis or status:**
- Requires higher vitamin D intake to achieve target circulating 25-OH vitamin D levels (>30 ng/mL, ideally 40–60 ng/mL)
- May benefit from sun exposure, supplementation, or both
- Test circulating 25-OH vitamin D to confirm status rather than assuming supplementation is adequate

**If carrier status for recessive genetic conditions (cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, spinal muscular atrophy, Tay-Sachs):**
- Carrier = one copy of pathogenic variant; person is healthy and unaffected
- If partner is also a carrier or affected, genetic counseling warranted for reproductive planning
- May warrant prenatal carrier screening if planning pregnancy

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Circulating micronutrient levels** (B12, folate, vitamin D, vitamin A, zinc, iron, magnesium) — Confirm whether variants are producing clinical deficiency or whether dietary intake is adequate
- **Homocysteine and methylmalonic acid** — Functional markers of B12 and folate status; assessment of whether supplementation is effective
- **Genetic Genie or Promethease** — Provides variant-level detail underlying NutraHacker recommendations
- **Dietary intake assessment** — Quantify actual nutrient intake before assuming genetic dysfunction requires supplementation

**Key pattern:** NutraHacker is most useful as a *personalized nutrient protocol generator*. The genetic variants identify which nutrients the person should prioritize, which forms are optimal, and what dosing range is likely required. The confirmation comes from biomarker testing and symptomatic response to supplementation.

### Intervention Levers

Use NutraHacker to identify personalized nutrient priorities and supplement form/dose recommendations. If multiple nutrient metabolism variants cluster in the same pathway, combine interventions: methylation pathway dysfunction + vitamin D synthesis variants + carrier status for specific conditions creates a multifactorial protocol. The leverage is in *specificity*: instead of recommending a standard multivitamin, use genetic insight to design a person-specific nutritional prescription.

## StrateGene Report

**Body Codx Operation:** Transduction
**What It Measures:** Genetic variants affecting 8 biochemical pathways: methylation, detoxification, nutrient transport, energy metabolism, hormonal metabolism, structural protein integrity, stress response, and cardiovascular pathways.
**Sample Type:** Upload raw DNA data from 23andMe, AncestryDNA, or similar platform
**Fasting Required:** N/A

### What This Number Means

StrateGene ("Strategic Genomics") analyzes approximately 100 genes organized into 8 functional biochemical pathways, with the goal of identifying the person's "metabolic strategy" — their genetically determined strengths and weaknesses across energy production, detoxification, stress resilience, and hormone regulation. The output is a pathway-level analysis (rather than gene-by-gene or trait-by-trait): the person's detoxification capacity, methylation efficiency, energy-production genotype, stress-response baseline, and hormonal metabolism phenotype.

Each pathway is analyzed for variants affecting rate-limiting enzymes or allosteric regulators. For example, the energy metabolism pathway includes variants in mitochondrial genes (MTCO2, MTATP8), nuclear genes encoding mitochondrial proteins (PPARGC1A, TFAM), and metabolic enzyme variants (pyruvate dehydrogenase, succinate dehydrogenase). The report generates an integrated assessment of the person's mitochondrial and aerobic capacity likelihood based on genotype.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

StrateGene uses a "pathway efficiency" scoring system:

- **Optimal/efficient:** Variants across the pathway favor upregulation and efficiency; person has genetic predisposition to high pathway output
- **Moderate/typical:** Population-average genetic background; pathway output expected to be typical
- **Suboptimal/compromised:** Variants across the pathway reduce efficiency; person has genetic predisposition to reduced pathway output or higher nutrient requirement
- **Significantly impaired:** Multiple variants reducing efficiency across the pathway; high likelihood of clinical dysfunction in that pathway

Recommendations scale from "optimized for this person's genetics" to "requires intensive support" depending on pathway efficiency.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction — Metabolic and Biochemical Pathway Efficiency**

StrateGene organizes the genetic architecture into the 8 core biochemical operations (transduction pathways): methylation, detoxification, energy production, stress response, hormonal metabolism, nutrient transport, structural integrity, and cardiovascular regulation. Reading through the Transduction lens, each pathway is assessed for its capacity to transduce incoming signals and substrates into appropriate metabolic outputs:

- **Methylation pathway efficiency:** SAM production capacity, epigenetic regulation potential
- **Detoxification pathway efficiency:** Phase I/II enzyme capacity, xenobiotic clearance rate
- **Energy metabolism pathway efficiency:** ATP production capacity, mitochondrial efficiency, aerobic endurance potential
- **Stress response pathway efficiency:** Cortisol regulation, HPA axis resilience, antioxidant buffering capacity
- **Hormonal metabolism pathway efficiency:** Sex hormone synthesis and clearance, thyroid hormone metabolism
- **Nutrient transport efficiency:** Micronutrient absorption and cellular uptake capacity
- **Structural integrity pathway efficiency:** Collagen synthesis, joint/connective tissue resilience
- **Cardiovascular pathway efficiency:** Vascular function, inflammation regulation, clotting-fibrinolysis balance

The person's "genomic metabolic strategy" emerges as a constellation of pathway efficiencies — someone might have high energy metabolism potential but low detoxification efficiency, requiring a different intervention strategy than someone with the opposite pattern.

### What Deviation Signals

**If energy metabolism pathway shows compromised variants (low mitochondrial capacity genotype):**
- May have genetic predisposition to lower aerobic capacity and endurance potential
- Interval/high-intensity exercise may be more effective than steady-state aerobic training
- Mitochondrial-supporting nutrients (CoQ10, L-carnitine, magnesium, riboflavin) warrant consideration
- Should avoid chronic overtraining; recovery may be slower

**If detoxification pathway shows multiple suboptimal variants:**
- Reduced capacity to clear xenobiotics, medications, and endogenous metabolites
- Warrants heightened xenobiotic avoidance (pesticides, heavy metals, endocrine-disrupting chemicals)
- May require gentle detoxification support (Phase II substrate provision) rather than aggressive detox protocols
- Drug dosing may require adjustment based on CYP variant status

**If stress response pathway shows variants predisposing to higher cortisol or lower antioxidant capacity:**
- May have genetic predisposition to stress sensitivity and slower recovery from psychological stressors
- Stress management, sleep quality, and antioxidant nutrient status become disproportionately important
- Consider adaptogenic herbs or specialized stress-support supplementation
- Monitor for chronic stress-induced epigenetic changes; early intervention warranted

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Intermediate biomarkers** for each pathway (homocysteine/methylmalonic acid for methylation, cortisol for stress response, VO2max for energy metabolism, liver function tests for detoxification)
- **Phenotypic data** (exercise capacity, stress resilience, dietary tolerance, energy levels, recovery speed) — Confirm that genetic predisposition matches lived experience
- **Genetic Genie or NutraHacker** — Provides gene-by-gene detail underlying StrateGene pathway assessments
- **Functional medicine assessment** — Many StrateGene pathways correlate with functional medicine diagnostic categories (detox capacity, energy capacity, hormonal balance)

**Key pattern:** StrateGene is most useful as a *metabolic strategy map*. If the person has low detoxification capacity but high energy metabolism potential, the strategy differs from someone with high detox capacity but low energy potential. Use the pathway efficiencies to prioritize which interventions will have the greatest leverage.

### Intervention Levers

StrateGene identifies the person's genetic "bottleneck" pathways — the ones that require the most support to achieve optimal function. Focus supplementation and lifestyle interventions on the compromised pathways first. A person with efficient detoxification but compromised energy metabolism benefits more from mitochondrial support than from additional detox support. The leverage is in *targeted pathway optimization* rather than generic wellness protocols.

## 3x4 Genetics Blueprint

**Body Codx Operation:** Cross-Operation (all 8 operations modulated)
**What It Measures:** 134 genes across 36 metabolic pathways affecting cardiovascular health, metabolic health, hormonal health, neurological health, immune health, detoxification, nutrient metabolism, and structural integrity.
**Sample Type:** Upload raw DNA data from 23andMe, AncestryDNA, or similar platform
**Fasting Required:** N/A

### What This Number Means

3x4 Genetics ("3 conditions × 4 levels of analysis") is a comprehensive genomic analysis that maps 134 genes across 36 distinct metabolic and physiological pathways. The "3 conditions" refers to health (how to optimize), disease (how pathways fail), and longevity (how to extend healthspan); the "4 levels" refer to single-gene effects, gene-gene interactions (epistasis), gene-environment interactions, and pathway-level coherence. The output is a layered report that shows not only which variants the person carries but how those variants interact with each other and with environmental factors to produce phenotypic outcomes.

Unlike single-variant assessments or even pathway-focused reports like StrateGene, 3x4 Genetics explicitly models gene-gene interactions: a person with MTHFR dysfunction plus MTRR dysfunction faces a more severe methylation penalty than either variant alone; a person with multiple cardiovascular risk variants faces an exponentially higher risk than the sum of individual risks would suggest.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

3x4 Genetics uses a multi-level scoring system:

**Level 1 — Single-variant impact:** Individual variants classified as optimal, typical, or suboptimal
**Level 2 — Gene-gene interactions:** Multiple variants in the same pathway or related pathways assessed for additive or multiplicative effects
**Level 3 — Gene-environment interactions:** Variants scored in the context of modifiable environmental factors (diet, sleep, exercise, stress)
**Level 4 — Pathway coherence:** Overall assessment of whether the person's genetic architecture supports robust function in that metabolic domain or is prone to decompensation

Recommendations scale from "optimize within your genetic strengths" to "comprehensive intervention protocol required" based on the integrated assessment.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Cross-Operation Genetic Architecture**

3x4 Genetics maps across all 8 body operations + their interactions:

- **Digestion:** Nutrient absorption variants, food sensitivity variants (lactase, gluten), microbial interaction genes
- **Circulation:** All cardiovascular variants (lipid metabolism, coagulation, endothelial function, inflammation regulation)
- **Defense:** Immune variants, inflammation variants, infection susceptibility
- **Respiration:** Lung function variants, oxygen transport variants
- **Elimination:** Kidney function variants, uric acid metabolism, metabolic acidosis predisposition
- **Transduction:** All metabolic enzyme variants (detox, methylation, thyroid, glucose metabolism, hormone synthesis)
- **Synthesis:** Anabolic hormones, growth factors, structural proteins
- **Restoration:** Sleep, recovery, mitochondrial function variants

The reading organizes around *coherence*: do the person's genetic variants across operations support robust simultaneous function in all domains, or do they create a bottleneck in a specific operation that cascades into secondary effects? A person with high detoxification capacity but low energy metabolism capacity faces a mismatch — they can clear toxins efficiently but may lack the energy to support aggressive detoxification protocols.

### What Deviation Signals

**If 3x4 Assessment shows "green" (optimal/typical) across most pathways:**
- Genetic background supports robust health across multiple domains
- Primary intervention is optimized lifestyle and environmental enrichment
- Genetic variants are unlikely to be major limiting factors
- Focus on modifiable factors (sleep, exercise, stress, nutrition) rather than complex supplementation

**If 3x4 Assessment shows "yellow" (suboptimal/compromised) in multiple pathways:**
- Genetic burden suggests need for targeted intervention across multiple domains
- Prioritize by: (1) which pathways are most clinically symptomatic, (2) which pathways have strongest evidence for intervention leverage, (3) which interventions address multiple pathways simultaneously
- Integration of reports (e.g., if methylation and detox are both compromised, methylation support and detox nutrient provision work synergistically)

**If 3x4 Assessment shows "red" (significantly impaired) in a critical pathway:**
- Requires intensive, multimodal intervention specific to that pathway
- May warrant clinical referral (e.g., if cardiovascular genetic burden is very high, consider preventive cardiology evaluation)
- Close monitoring of intermediate biomarkers to confirm intervention adequacy

**If gene-gene interactions reveal multiplicative risk:**
- Two variants in different genes affecting the same pathway (e.g., APOE4 + LPA high risk allele + CETP variant all affecting lipid metabolism) create exponentially higher cardiovascular risk than any single variant predicts
- Lifestyle intensity and pharmacological intervention thresholds should be adjusted downward (intervene earlier and more aggressively)

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Complete intermediate biomarker panel** — Test the predicted phenotype; confirm genetic predictions match actual biochemistry
- **Clinical outcome data** (family history, prior tests, current symptom patterns) — Genetic risk scores are population predictions; individual outcomes vary widely based on gene-environment interactions
- **All other genetic reports** (Promethease, Genetic Genie, StrateGene, NutraHacker) — 3x4 Genetics provides the most comprehensive overview; other reports provide gene-level detail
- **Lifestyle and environmental data** — Where 3x4 Genetics explicitly models gene-environment interactions, the recommendations can be calibrated to the individual's actual environment

**Key pattern:** 3x4 Genetics is most useful as a *whole-system genetic assessment*. It identifies not just which pathways are impaired but how they interact with each other. A person with both methylation dysfunction and poor detoxification capacity requires an integrated protocol; addressing only one leaves the person vulnerable to the other's consequences.

### Intervention Levers

Use 3x4 Genetics to identify the person's most consequential genetic bottlenecks and design an intervention strategy that addresses multiple pathways simultaneously when possible. Methylation support (B vitamins) improves many downstream functions; detoxification support (Phase II nutrients, glutathione) protects against both genetic detox impairment and methylation-dependent detoxification steps. The leverage is in *integrated multi-pathway optimization* rather than single-pathway focus.

## Panorama NIPT (Noninvasive Prenatal Testing)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Fetal DNA in maternal circulation screened for trisomy 21, trisomy 18, trisomy 13, monosomy X, and additional chromosomal abnormalities; also tests for select gene deletions (DiGeorge syndrome, Prader-Willi/Angelman, Smith-Magenis).
**Sample Type:** Maternal blood (9 weeks gestation or later)
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

During pregnancy, a small percentage of fetal DNA enters the maternal circulation. A non-invasive prenatal test (NIPT) isolates this cell-free fetal DNA (cfDNA) from maternal blood and sequences it, determining the proportion of reads mapping to each chromosome. If >4% of reads map to chromosome 21, the fetus is likely trisomic; if >3% to chromosome 18, likely trisomy 18; if >2% to chromosome 13, likely trisomy 13. Monosomy X (Turner syndrome) shows missing chromosome X reads. The test also screens for common microdeletion syndromes (DiGeorge, Prader-Willi/Angelman, Smith-Magenis).

NIPT is a screening test, not a diagnostic test. A positive result indicates elevated risk and warrants confirmation through diagnostic testing (amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling). A negative result indicates low risk but does not exclude chromosomal abnormalities entirely; the test has 99% sensitivity for trisomy 21 and trisomy 18 but lower sensitivity for other conditions.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

NIPT results are binary or risk-stratified:

- **Negative/Low risk:** Fetal fraction adequate, no chromosomal abnormalities detected, risk below threshold
- **Positive/High risk:** Chromosomal abnormality detected; warrants immediate confirmatory testing and genetic counseling
- **Inconclusive/Insufficient fetal fraction:** Test cannot be interpreted; repeat testing or diagnostic testing required
- **No call:** Technical failure; repeat sample required

Risk is expressed as a likelihood ratio: "Risk of Down syndrome is approximately 1 in 10,000" indicates very low risk; "Risk of Down syndrome is approximately 1 in 50" indicates high risk requiring confirmation.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Regulation — Chromosomal Integrity and Developmental Programming**

NIPT addresses the Regulation operation at a foundational level: chromosomal integrity determines whether the basic developmental program can execute coherently. Trisomy 21, 18, and 13 each create distinct developmental cascades that produce multi-system consequences:

- **Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome):** Extra copy of chromosome 21; produces intellectual disability, cardiac malformations (40–50% require surgery), GI anomalies, thyroid disease, hearing loss. Life expectancy now ~60 years with appropriate care.
- **Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome):** Extra copy of chromosome 18; produces severe intellectual disability, distinctive facial features, cardiac malformations, GI anomalies, skeletal abnormalities. ~50% fetal demise; those born typically survive days to months.
- **Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome):** Extra copy of chromosome 13; produces severe intellectual disability, facial/brain malformations (holoprosencephaly), cardiac malformations, GI/urinary anomalies. Median lifespan ~7 days; <5% survive first year.
- **Monosomy X (Turner syndrome):** Missing one X chromosome; produces short stature, cardiac malformations (bicuspid aortic valve, aortic stenosis), renal anomalies, infertility, learning challenges (but normal intelligence in most cases).

NIPT is fundamentally a question about developmental feasibility: is the basic chromosomal architecture intact, or will the developmental program encounter a critical bottleneck?

### What Deviation Signals

**If positive for trisomy 21:**
- Warrants immediate confirmatory testing (amniocentesis or CVS)
- Genetic counseling essential to discuss life expectancy, health management, support resources
- Opportunity for prenatal interventions (fetal echocardiography, neonatal preparation for possible surgery)
- Kaplan for delivery at tertiary care center with neonatology and pediatric cardiology capability

**If positive for trisomy 18 or 13:**
- Warrants immediate genetic counseling
- Very high likelihood of fetal demise or severe postnatal complications
- Parental choice regarding continuation vs. termination requires full informed consent
- If continuation chosen, perinatal palliative care and family support essential

**If positive for monosomy X (Turner syndrome):**
- More variable phenotype than trisomy; can include normal intelligence, normal development
- Fetal echocardiography warranted to assess for cardiac anomalies
- Genetic counseling to discuss typical features and life expectancy (usually normal lifespan)
- Prenatal planning can prepare for growth hormone therapy, cardiac monitoring

**If test inconclusive or shows insufficient fetal fraction:**
- Repeat testing at later gestational age (fetal fraction increases with gestational age)
- Or diagnostic testing (amniocentesis) if results are clinically urgent

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Ultrasound findings** — Structural anomalies visible on ultrasound (cardiac defects, GI anomalies, skeletal abnormalities) strongly predict trisomy; concordant findings confirm risk
- **Maternal serum biomarkers** — First trimester combined screening (PAPP-A, hCG) or quad screen (AFP, hCG, uE3, inhibin A) provide independent risk estimates; if discordant with NIPT, may prompt discussion
- **Family history** — Increased risk if prior children with trisomy or if parent is balanced translocation carrier
- **Advanced maternal age** — Risk increases with age; NIPT more important in older mothers

**Key pattern:** NIPT is best understood as a downstream developmental checkpoint. A positive result means the downstream developmental program will encounter a critical constraint — whether that constraint produces disability, early loss, or manageable differences depends on the specific trisomy and the support available.

### Intervention Levers

NIPT is a screening tool that identifies risk; it does not provide treatment. The clinical leverage comes from the time gained — weeks for confirmatory testing, counseling, and preparation if the result is positive. A positive result warrants genetic counseling, confirmatory testing, and perinatal planning (delivery location, neonatal preparation, family support). A negative result provides reassurance but does not exclude all chromosomal and genetic conditions.

## MTHFR Thermolabile Variant (C677T and A1298C)

**Body Codx Operation:** Transduction
**What It Measures:** Two specific missense mutations in the methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) gene: C677T (Ala222Val, thermolabile) and A1298C (Glu429Ala), affecting enzyme stability and cofactor binding.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

MTHFR (methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) catalyzes the final step in folate activation: conversion of 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate (the form used in DNA synthesis) to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (the form used in methylation reactions). The C677T mutation creates a thermolabile enzyme — more heat-sensitive, with ~65% activity in heterozygotes and ~30% activity in homozygotes compared to wild-type. The A1298C mutation is less well-characterized; it may have modest effects on enzyme stability and cofactor binding.

The clinical significance of MTHFR variants is debated. Large epidemiological studies show weak associations between MTHFR genotype and disease outcomes; targeted studies in specific populations (e.g., those with preeclampsia, neural tube defects, or thrombosis) show stronger associations. The current consensus is that MTHFR variants are *predisposing* factors — they reduce methylation capacity and increase disease risk if other factors (nutritional deficiency, genetic burden, environmental stress) are also present — but they are not sufficient to cause disease on their own in most people.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

MTHFR genotype-phenotype interpretation:

- **C677T/C677T (wild-type):** Normal MTHFR enzyme activity; no expected methylation dysfunction
- **C677T heterozygote (C677T/T677T):** Reduced enzyme activity (~65% of normal); may have modest impact on methylation if dietary folate is low
- **C677T homozygote (T677T/T677T):** Significantly reduced enzyme activity (~30% of normal); elevated risk of methylation dysfunction, particularly if dietary folate is deficient
- **A1298C heterozygote or homozygote:** Limited data; likely modest effect if any
- **Compound heterozygote (C677T + A1298C):** Possible additive effect on enzyme dysfunction; clinical significance unclear

The critical interpretive principle: MTHFR genotype is a risk factor, not a diagnosis. Phenotype (actual methylation capacity) depends on genotype, dietary folate and B12 status, other polymorphisms in the methylation cascade, and environmental stressors.

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction — Methylation Capacity**

MTHFR directly affects the Transduction operation: it determines how efficiently the cell can convert dietary folate into the active methylation cofactor (5-methyltetrahydrofolate, needed for DNA methylation, neurotransmitter synthesis, and phospholipid remodeling).

**C677T homozygotes** have measurably reduced methylation capacity. This manifests in:
- Reduced DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) activity — epigenetic regulation becomes less plastic
- Reduced neurotransmitter synthesis (serotonin via methylation of tryptophan, dopamine via methylation pathways) — mood and cognitive function may be affected
- Reduced phospholipid membrane synthesis — cellular and mitochondrial membrane fluidity may be compromised
- Elevated homocysteine (if the methylation bottleneck backs up) — cardiovascular and neurological risk increases

Heterozygotes and those with adequate dietary folate and B12 often show no clinical consequence.

### What Deviation Signals

**If C677T homozygote (TT genotype):**
- Elevated risk of methylation dysfunction, particularly if dietary folate is low (<400 mcg/day)
- Monitor homocysteine; if >10 μmol/L, consider methylation-supporting supplementation (methylfolate 1,000–5,000 mcg/day, methylcobalamin 1,000 mcg/day)
- May benefit from active forms of B vitamins (methylfolate rather than folic acid, methylcobalamin rather than cyanocobalamin)
- Higher risk of neural tube defects if planning pregnancy; preconception methylation support warranted

**If C677T heterozygote:**
- Modest reduction in MTHFR activity; clinical significance unclear without other risk factors
- If homocysteine is normal and folate/B12 status is adequate, supplementation may not be necessary
- If high homocysteine or other methylation-dependent dysfunction, consider methylation-supporting supplementation

**If compound heterozygote (C677T + A1298C):**
- Possible additive enzyme dysfunction; more conservative approach warranted
- Biomarker screening (homocysteine, methylmalonic acid) recommended
- If markers show dysfunction, methylation-supporting supplementation indicated

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Homocysteine level** — The most important marker of whether MTHFR dysfunction is producing a methylation bottleneck; elevated homocysteine (>10 μmol/L) indicates dysfunction
- **Methylmalonic acid** — Marker of B12 adequacy; elevated MMA can coexist with elevated homocysteine, indicating dual B12 + methylation dysfunction
- **Folate and B12 levels** — Adequate nutritional status may compensate for MTHFR variants
- **Red cell folate** — Better marker of long-term folate status than serum folate
- **Other genetic variants** in methylation cascade (MTRR, MTR, MTHFR A1298C, COMT, PEMT) — Clustering of variants increases risk of phenotypic dysfunction

**Key pattern:** MTHFR variants are significant primarily when they cluster with other methylation-pathway variants or when nutritional status is poor. A C677T homozygote with adequate folate and B12, normal homocysteine, and no other genetic methylation dysfunction may have no clinical consequence.

### Intervention Levers

If MTHFR dysfunction is confirmed (homozygous C677T + elevated homocysteine), methylation-supporting supplementation has strong evidence: methylfolate (1,000–5,000 mcg/day, depending on response), methylcobalamin or cyanocobalamin (1,000 mcg/day intramuscularly or sublingually if absorption is impaired), pyridoxal-5-phosphate (25–100 mg/day for homocysteine metabolism), and trimethylglycine/betaine (2,000–6,000 mg/day as a backup methyl donor). The response should be monitored via homocysteine reduction.

## CYP2D6 Genotyping

**Body Codx Operation:** Transduction
**What It Measures:** Copy number and sequence variants in the CYP2D6 gene, determining predicted metabolizer status (poor, intermediate, normal, rapid/ultra-rapid) for ~30 medications.
**Sample Type:** Blood or saliva
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

CYP2D6 is a Phase I hepatic enzyme responsible for metabolizing ~25% of all prescription medications, including antidepressants, antipsychotics, antiarrhythmics, beta-blockers, and opioid codeine (which requires CYP2D6 conversion to morphine for analgesic activity). Variants in CYP2D6 produce functional consequences:

- **Loss-of-function variants:** Produce poor or intermediate metabolizer status; reduced enzyme activity means drugs accumulate to higher levels
- **Increased-copy-number variants:** Produce ultra-rapid metabolizer status; increased enzyme activity means drugs are cleared quickly, potentially to subtherapeutic levels
- **Gene deletions:** Homozygous CYP2D6 deletion produces complete loss of enzyme function (poor metabolizer)

CYP2D6 has complex genetics: the gene can be deleted entirely, can exist in multiple copies (2–13 copies reported), and can harbor dozens of sequence variants. Clinical interpretation requires copy number analysis AND sequence variant analysis — neither alone is sufficient.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

CYP2D6 metabolizer classification:

- **Ultra-rapid metabolizer (UM):** Predicted function >100%; typically 3+ gene copies or specific activating variants; drugs metabolized extremely rapidly, often to subtherapeutic levels at standard doses
- **Rapid metabolizer (RM):** Predicted function 100%; typically 2+ gene copies; drugs metabolized faster than typical; may require higher doses
- **Normal metabolizer (NM):** Predicted function 100%; typical gene structure (2 copies, wild-type sequence); standard drug dosing expected to be effective
- **Intermediate metabolizer (IM):** Predicted function 25–75%; heterozygous loss-of-function or heterozygous copy-number variation; higher plasma drug concentrations expected at standard doses
- **Poor metabolizer (PM):** Predicted function 0–25%; homozygous loss-of-function or gene deletion; significantly higher plasma drug concentrations expected; dose reduction or drug avoidance warranted

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction — Drug and Xenobiotic Metabolism**

CYP2D6 status directly determines the Transduction operation's capacity to clear medications and xenobiotics. Poor metabolizers accumulate drugs to toxic levels; ultra-rapid metabolizers clear drugs before therapeutic levels are achieved. The clinical consequence is treatment failure or adverse drug reactions.

**Poor metabolizers** cannot effectively metabolize:
- SSRIs: fluoxetine, paroxetine (fluoxetine has an active metabolite so effects are less pronounced; paroxetine is more sensitive)
- SNRIs: venlafaxine, duloxetine
- Tricyclic antidepressants: amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine
- Antipsychotics: risperidone, paliperidone, aripiprazole, haloperidol
- Beta-blockers: metoprolol (atenolol, less dependent on CYP2D6, preferred)
- Codeine (cannot convert to morphine; poor analgesia)
- Tramadol (cannot produce active metabolite; poor analgesia)
- Type IC antiarrhythmics: flecainide, propafenone
- Antitussives: dextromethorphan (OTC cough suppressant)
- Antiemetics: ondansetron

**Ultra-rapid metabolizers** may have subtherapeutic levels even with higher doses. Clinical monitoring is required to confirm therapeutic effect.

### What Deviation Signals

**If CYP2D6 poor metabolizer (PM):**
- Any medication metabolized by CYP2D6 requires dose reduction (typically 50% of standard dose) or alternative
- Codeine is completely ineffective; alternative opioid required
- Antidepressant selection becomes critical; if SSRI/SNRI is preferred, paroxetine and fluoxetine should be avoided in favor of sertraline or citalopram (less CYP2D6-dependent), or switch to non-CYP2D6-metabolized class (bupropion, mirtazapine, tricyclics at reduced dose)
- Generic medications may not indicate CYP2D6 metabolism; check individual drug interaction databases
- Should be documented in medical record; should be communicated to all prescribers

**If CYP2D6 intermediate metabolizer (IM):**
- Modest dose reduction may be beneficial for CYP2D6-metabolized drugs
- Monitoring for adverse effects more important than for normal metabolizers
- If drug is not effective at standard dose, consider inadequate dosing (may have low metabolizer function) rather than treatment failure
- Alternative: switch to drug less dependent on CYP2D6

**If CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolizer (UM):**
- May require higher-than-standard doses to achieve therapeutic drug levels
- Codeine may be highly effective (rapid conversion to morphine)
- Tricyclic antidepressants may require higher doses than typical
- Clinical response should guide dosing; standard doses may be inadequate

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Plasma drug levels** — If available, direct measurement confirms predicted vs. actual drug metabolism
- **Prior medication trials** — History of poor response to standard doses despite compliance, or adverse effects at standard doses, provides clues to metabolizer status
- **Pharmacogenomics panel** — CYP2D6 is one of several CYP variants affecting drug metabolism; comprehensive panel identifies all relevant variants
- **Medication list** — Audit current medications for CYP2D6 dependence; calculate anticipated drug interactions

**Key pattern:** CYP2D6 status is most important for psychotropic medications (antidepressants, antipsychotics) and opioids (particularly codeine). If the person is on multiple CYP2D6-metabolized drugs, the interactions become critical: drug A inhibits metabolism of drug B, leading to drug B accumulation.

### Intervention Levers

If CYP2D6 poor metabolizer status is identified and the person is on a CYP2D6-metabolized medication: (1) discuss with prescriber; (2) dose adjust based on predicted metabolism; (3) if adverse effects, consider alternative drug less dependent on CYP2D6; (4) monitor for therapeutic effect and adverse effects more closely than population standard. Documentation in the medical record prevents future prescribers from making the same dosing mistake.

## GeneSight Psychotropic Panel

**Body Codx Operation:** Transduction
**What It Measures:** 15 genes affecting psychotropic medication metabolism: CYP1A2, CYP2B6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, CYP3A4, CYP3A5, GRIN2B, MTHFR, SLC6A2, SLC6A3, SLC6A4, OPRM1, HTR1A, HTR1B, COMT
**Sample Type:** Buccal swab or saliva
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

GeneSight is a commercial pharmacogenomics test specifically designed for psychiatric medication selection and dosing. It analyzes 15 genes with known effects on antidepressant, antipsychotic, anti-anxiety, and mood-stabilizer metabolism and efficacy. The output is a medication-by-medication report showing: (1) whether the person's genotype predicts efficacy, (2) predicted drug plasma concentrations relative to standard dosing, and (3) recommended dosing adjustments.

Unlike broader pharmacogenomics panels, GeneSight is targeted to psychotropic drugs, making it particularly useful for psychiatry. It includes not only metabolizer status (CYP variants) but also variants in neurotransmitter transporters and receptors that may affect medication response independent of plasma levels.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

GeneSight classifies each medication in three categories:

- **Use as directed:** Genotype predicts standard dosing and good efficacy; no adjustment needed
- **Moderate gene-drug interaction:** Genotype predicts reduced efficacy or need for dose adjustment; monitor response closely; consider dose increase or alternative if inadequate response
- **Significant gene-drug interaction:** Genotype predicts substantially reduced efficacy or elevated adverse effect risk; dose adjustment or alternative strongly recommended

The output is medication-specific, not gene-by-gene. A person sees a list of antidepressants color-coded green (recommended), yellow (monitor/adjust), or red (reconsider).

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction — Psychiatric Medication Metabolism and Efficacy**

GeneSight assesses how efficiently the Transduction operation processes psychiatric drugs — both in terms of metabolism (CYP variants) and in terms of neurotransmitter system engagement (transporter and receptor variants).

**Metabolism component (CYP genes):** Same as CYP2D6 description above, but includes additional CYPs affecting different drug classes (CYP1A2 for certain antidepressants, CYP2B6 for bupropion, CYP3A4/3A5 for many antipsychotics).

**Efficacy component (neurotransmitter genes):** Variants in serotonin transporter (SLC6A4/SERT), norepinephrine transporter (SLC6A2/NET), dopamine transporter (SLC6A3/DAT), opioid receptor (OPRM1), and serotonin/dopamine receptors (HTR1A, HTR1B, GRIN2B) influence whether a medication will bind effectively to its target neurotransmitter system. For example, the SERT promoter variant (5-HTTLPR) influences SSRI efficacy in some studies.

### What Deviation Signals

**If GeneSight shows "significant gene-drug interaction" for multiple antidepressant classes:**
- Standard antidepressant selection may not be optimal
- Consider: (1) dose adjustment with closer monitoring, (2) switch to medication with fewer interactions, (3) addition of augmentation agent, or (4) consideration of non-pharmacological treatment (psychotherapy, TMS)
- Genetic testing is one input; clinical presentation and prior response history are equally important

**If GeneSight shows poor CYP2D6 metabolizer status + SERT promoter variant favoring poor SSRI response:**
- Double constraint: poor medication metabolism + biological reduced SSRI efficacy
- SSRI monotherapy at standard dose unlikely to be effective
- Consider: (1) SSRI at higher dose with close monitoring, (2) switch to non-SSRI antidepressant (bupropion, tricyclic, SNRI, mirtazapine), or (3) psychotherapy + medication combination
- Augmentation strategies may be necessary

**If GeneSight shows rapid CYP2C19 metabolizer + recommendation against certain antipsychotics:**
- Rapid metabolism means drug levels fall quickly; may need higher doses or more frequent dosing
- Consider antipsychotics less dependent on rapid-metabolizing CYPs (e.g., aripiprazole metabolized by both CYP3A4 and CYP2D6, less dependent on 2C19)

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Prior psychiatric medication trials** — History of SSRI efficacy/failure provides ground truth; genetic predictions should align with actual response
- **Current psychiatric symptoms** — Medication choice should match both genotype and phenomenology (OCD, depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder may respond to different drugs)
- **Substance use history** — Some psychotropic drugs have abuse potential; metabolizer status may influence this risk
- **Side effect history** — If past medications caused intolerable side effects, GeneSight can help identify drugs predicted to have better tolerability

**Key pattern:** GeneSight is most useful when the person has failed multiple medications or has complex psychiatric symptoms. For first-episode psychiatric illness, clinical presentation is the primary guide; genetic testing is a secondary decision tool.

### Intervention Levers

Use GeneSight to guide antidepressant and antipsychotic selection, particularly if the person has already failed one or more medications. Green-coded medications are more likely to be effective; yellow-coded require monitoring; red-coded should be avoided or dose-adjusted significantly. The genetic information depersonalizes medication selection — it's not that the person is "treatment-resistant," but that their genotype predicts poor response to certain drug classes. This reframing can reduce shame and improve adherence.

## Genomind PGx Panel (Comprehensive Psychotropic Pharmacogenomics)

**Body Codx Operation:** Transduction
**What It Measures:** 26–35 genes affecting psychotropic medication metabolism: all major CYPs (1A2, 2B6, 2C9, 2C19, 2D6, 3A4, 3A5), transporter and receptor variants, and additional psychiatric phenotype markers.
**Sample Type:** Buccal swab or saliva
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Genomind's Comprehensive Psychiatric Panel is an expanded version of GeneSight, analyzing 26–35 genes (variants vary by test version). In addition to the 15 genes in GeneSight, it includes: CYP1A2, additional GABA-receptor variants, additional dopamine-pathway genes, and sometimes novel psychiatric phenotype markers. The output is similar to GeneSight — medication-by-medication recommendations — but with additional depth in the neurobiological mechanisms.

The rationale for the expanded panel is that psychiatric drug response involves not only metabolism (CYPs) but also complex neurobiology (receptor density, transporter efficiency, stress-response architecture). A more comprehensive genetic assessment can refine predictions.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

Same categorical system as GeneSight: green (use as directed), yellow (monitor/adjust), red (reconsider/avoid).

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Transduction — Comprehensive Psychiatric Metabolism and Neurobiology**

Genomind's expanded panel maps more extensively onto the neurotransmitter systems affected by psychiatric medications:

- **Serotonergic system:** SLC6A4 (SERT), HTR1A, HTR1B, variants in serotonin synthesis and reuptake
- **Noradrenergic system:** SLC6A2 (NET), alpha-2 adrenergic receptor variants
- **Dopaminergic system:** SLC6A3 (DAT), dopamine receptor variants, COMT (catecholamine catabolism)
- **GABAergic system:** GABRA1, GABRB1, GABRA4 variants (if included)
- **Glutamatergic system:** GRIN2B and other NMDA receptor variants
- **Opioid system:** OPRM1 (opioid receptor)
- **CYP metabolism:** All major CYPs affecting psychiatric drugs

The reading integrates these systems: a person with SERT transporter dysfunction + low 5-HT1A receptor expression + COMT fast-metabolizer status faces a multimodal serotonergic challenge — reduced serotonin reuptake, reduced serotonin receptor efficacy, and rapid catecholamine metabolism — requiring either higher-dose SSRI combined with dopaminergic augmentation, or switch to medication class less dependent on intact serotonergic function.

### What Deviation Signals

**If multiple red-coded medications across antidepressant and antipsychotic classes:**
- Suggests fundamental neurotransmitter system dysfunction rather than simple metabolizer mismatch
- Consider augmentation strategies (e.g., SSRI + bupropion + lithium) targeting multiple neurotransmitter systems
- Consider non-pharmacological treatment as primary (psychotherapy, TMS, ketamine if treatment-resistant)
- Genetic testing confirms that the challenge is biological, not willful non-compliance or inadequate effort

**If Genomind shows dominant pattern (e.g., predominantly dopamine-system variants, predominantly serotonin-system variants):**
- May predict which medication class is most likely to be effective (dopamine-focused disorders favor bupropion or stimulants; serotonin-focused favor SSRIs; norepinephrine-focused favor SNRIs or tricyclics)
- Phenomenology should guide choice: OCD and anxiety disorders often respond well to high-dose SSRIs even with genetic variants; depression may respond better to dopamine-focused agents if genetic pattern suggests dopaminergic liability

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Prior psychiatric medication trials** (drugs tried, doses, duration, efficacy, side effects) — The strongest predictor of future response is past response; genetic testing explains why
- **Family psychiatric history** — Genetic variants cluster in families; presence of psychiatric disorder in first-degree relatives suggests heritable component
- **Stress-response phenotype** — Some people are genetically predisposed to stress sensitivity; others to stress resilience; this modulates treatment response
- **Current substance use** — Some medications have abuse potential; metabolizer status may influence this
- **Symptom severity and complexity** — Mild depression with good prior response to SSRIs may not require comprehensive genetic testing; treatment-resistant complex psychiatric illness warrants it

**Key pattern:** Genomind's expanded panel is most useful for complex psychiatric cases — either those with multiple failed medication trials or those with multiplex psychiatric diagnosis (depression + anxiety + OCD, for example). For straightforward depression or anxiety, clinical presentation remains the primary guide.

### Intervention Levers

Use Genomind to identify the person's dominant neurotransmitter systems (serotonergic liability, dopaminergic insufficiency, noradrenergic dysfunction) and select medications targeting the person's specific neurobiological phenotype. A person with predominantly dopaminergic variants and serotonergic sufficiency may respond better to dopamine-focused agents (bupropion, stimulants) than to SSRIs. The leverage is in *phenotype-matched drug selection* rather than trial-and-error medication cycling.
