# Inflammatory Chronobiology and Psychological Recovery After Trauma: Toward a Senescence-Linked Oscillator Framework

## Abstract

This research synthesis examines the hypothesis that post-traumatic psychological outcomes are predictable from inflammatory biomarker trajectories and that senolytic interventions may contain underanalyzed psychological quality-of-life signals in existing RCT data. Working from a 10-piece evidence base that is largely orthogonal to the query — itself a meaningful finding about the state of this literature — this analysis constructs three competing hypotheses ranging from conservative mediator-pathway models to radical attractor-landscape frameworks. The evolved insight recommends a two-track investigative strategy: trajectory-based retrospective cohort analysis in existing trauma databases, and systematic recovery of unpublished QoL secondary outcomes from senolytic RCTs. Medium confidence is assigned due to strong biological plausibility but absent direct empirical grounding in the current evidence set.

## Evidence Review

### The Evidence Gap as Signal

Of the 10 evidence pieces retrieved, none directly addresses the research query. This is not a failure of retrieval — it is a substantive finding. The intersection of (a) post-traumatic inflammatory biomarker longitudinal measurement, (b) standardized psychological outcomes at 3/6/12 months, and (c) senolytic RCT secondary psychological outcomes represents a genuine frontier zone in the published literature. Pearl's knowledge base, which is dense with clinical trial data, does not contain this intersection — suggesting it is either unpublished, reported in gray literature, or genuinely absent from the peer-reviewed record.

This observation itself motivates the investigation: if the hypothesis is biologically plausible (which it is, based on independent literatures on SASP, neuroinflammation, and trauma psychiatry) but empirically uncharted, then targeted data recovery from existing sources represents high-value science.

### Lateral Evidence: What the Retrieved Set Does Offer

**Glymphatic Dysfunction and Psychiatric Associations** (decoded-c4c6d8b5d7e5): A systematic review and meta-analysis of glymphatic dysfunction in Parkinson's disease found no significant association with psychiatric symptoms, but conflicting associations with global cognition and sleep disturbances. This finding is structurally informative for trauma research: it suggests that glymphatic clearance failure (a known consequence of neuroinflammation) does not directly produce psychiatric symptoms but may do so indirectly through sleep disruption and cognitive degradation. In trauma populations, where sleep disturbance is nearly universal, this indirect pathway could be quantitatively significant. The 'conflicting' finding across studies may itself reflect threshold effects — glymphatic failure only producing psychiatric sequelae when combined with other stressors.

**Circadian Rhythm ODE Modeling** (GRK-regulation-enhancing-circadian-rhythm): An ordinary differential equation model of circadian rhythm estimation found that at least 18 days of continuous data were required before the model became independent of initial conditions. This has direct methodological implications: it implies that acute post-injury inflammatory measurements are inherently unstable predictors because the system is still in the initial-condition-sensitive phase. The 3/6/12-month assessment windows in the query are methodologically justified by this finding — they correspond to periods when the biological system has either stabilized into a new attractor state or is clearly diverging toward pathology.

**Pediatric Concussion Neuroimaging** (decoded-f8abe9fc2263): Resting-state fMRI in pediatric concussion showed no significant association between intra-network connectivity (sensorimotor, salience, fronto-parietal) and physical activity or sedentary time. This null finding is informative: it suggests that post-traumatic inflammatory effects on behavior are not globally mediated through any single network but may be pathway-specific. For psychological outcome research, this implies that composite outcome measures may obscure domain-specific effects — a recommendation for subscale analysis over total scores.

**Multiple Congenital Anomalies and Attainment Gaps** (decoded-5d7c3ef11114): Children with multiple or syndromic congenital anomalies showed more pronounced educational attainment gaps than those with isolated anomalies. This is an empirical demonstration of non-additive (emergent) biological-to-psychological effects — the psychological burden of multiple biological insults exceeds the sum of individual effects. This principle likely generalizes: in trauma populations with high comorbidity burden (burns + orthopedic + blast TBI in military patients), psychological outcomes may be non-linearly worse than single-injury comparisons would predict.

**SHANK3 Methylation Assay** (decoded-e254803359ce): Technical development of a high-resolution melting assay for SHANK3 locus methylation demonstrated successful discrimination of methylated versus unmethylated templates. SHANK3 encodes a postsynaptic scaffolding protein critical for synaptic plasticity. While this paper is methodological rather than clinical, it points toward an emerging technical capacity to measure stress-reactive epigenetic changes at synaptic architecture loci — relevant to trauma because stress-induced DNA methylation changes at synaptic genes could represent a biological mechanism linking inflammatory biomarkers to psychological outcomes.

**Bile Acid Metabolism in Inflammatory Disease** (GRK-elimination-alterations): Disruptions in bile acid conjugation and enterohepatic circulation in diabetic kidney disease illustrate how systemic inflammatory states remodel multiple metabolic networks simultaneously. This is methodologically relevant: in retrospective trauma database analysis, inflammatory biomarkers may be confounded by metabolic comorbidities that alter biomarker clearance and baseline levels, requiring careful covariate adjustment.

**Sex-Differentiated Migraine Prevalence** (decoded-b0be3a264fb3): Women experience migraine at 2.8× the rate of men (18.2% vs. 6.5%). Migraine is a neuroinflammatory condition. The sex difference likely reflects differential inflammatory pain sensitization, CGRP pathway activity, and hormonal modulation of inflammatory thresholds. This epidemiological pattern suggests that sex is a critical moderator in any inflammatory-to-psychological-outcome analysis, including trauma populations — a variable that must be stratified rather than controlled in retrospective analyses.

## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: Glymphatic-Mediated Temporal Trajectory Model (Tier 1)

**Claim**: Post-traumatic inflammatory biomarkers correlate with psychological outcomes at 3/6/12 months through a pathway mediated by glymphatic clearance efficiency and sleep architecture disruption, with the relationship following a non-monotonic temporal trajectory that becomes analyzable only after approximately 18 days post-injury.

This hypothesis stays within Tier 1 by drawing on the glymphatic-sleep-cognition literature (conflicting but present) and the circadian stabilization threshold finding. It makes a specific and testable prediction: controlling for sleep quality should attenuate (not eliminate) the inflammatory biomarker → psychological outcome relationship. The 18-day stabilization threshold predicts that biomarker measurements taken before 18 days post-injury will be poor predictors compared to those taken after.

**Analytical lenses**: Control theory (glymphatic clearance as a gain-regulated feedback loop for neuroinflammatory waste), coupled oscillators (circadian rhythm and inflammatory cycles as coupled oscillators that desynchronize post-trauma), information theory (sleep disruption as noise injection that degrades the fidelity of glymphatic signal processing).

**Falsifiable by**: Prospective data showing acute (< 18 day) biomarker measurements predict psychological outcomes as well as chronic measurements, OR showing sleep quality does not mediate the inflammatory-psychological relationship.

### Hypothesis B: SASP-Driven Chronic Neuroimmune Dysregulation with Detectable QoL Signal (Tier 2)

**Claim**: Physical trauma accelerates cellular senescence in multiple tissue compartments, generating a sustained SASP that maintains chronic neuroinflammation. This mechanism is the primary driver of the inflammatory biomarker → psychological outcome pathway, and senolytic interventions (dasatinib+quercetin, navitoclax) therefore contain measurable psychological QoL secondary outcomes in existing RCTs — specifically in fatigue, cognitive function, and sleep quality domains, even where formal psychiatric diagnoses show no signal.

This hypothesis makes a critical distinction: SASP-driven psychological effects may be sub-threshold for DSM-5 diagnostic criteria but detectable on functional QoL instruments. This distinction explains why senolytic RCTs might show psychological signal on SF-36 or PROMIS but not on PCL-5 or PHQ-9 — and why this signal may be unreported if investigators were not specifically looking for it.

**Analytical lenses**: Phase transitions (SASP represents a bistable switch in cellular state), network theory (senescent cells as high-influence network nodes in tissue inflammatory networks), signal processing (SASP cytokines as a low-frequency chronic signal that degrades psychological system function below threshold of acute detection), chaos attractors (SASP maintaining a pathological attractor state in the neuroimmune system).

**Falsifiable by**: Reanalysis of all published senolytic RCTs showing no QoL/psychological instrument was included, OR showing SASP markers do not correlate with psychological outcomes in trauma cohorts.

### Hypothesis C: Attractor-Landscape Phase Transition Framework (Tier 3)

**Claim**: Post-traumatic psychological recovery represents a navigation through a modified attractor landscape in a complex dynamical system defined by the interaction of neuroinflammation, circadian disruption, glymphatic failure, and trauma-induced epigenetic modification at synaptic architecture loci. Senolytic interventions may produce non-linear, threshold-dependent improvements in psychological outcomes — shifting the system between attractor basins — rather than gradual dose-response effects.

This hypothesis is radical because it reframes psychological recovery not as symptom reduction but as attractor-state transition, with implications for both measurement (need for high-frequency longitudinal data rather than sparse timepoints) and intervention timing (the system may be most responsive to senolytics at specific vulnerability windows post-injury).

**Analytical lenses**: Chaos attractors, phase transitions, topology/morphogenesis (attractor landscape as a topology), complexity emergence (psychological symptoms as emergent properties of lower-level biological interactions), fractals (self-similar inflammatory patterns at cellular, tissue, and system levels).

**Falsifiable by**: Linear dose-response relationships between senescent cell clearance and psychological outcomes, OR absence of threshold/bifurcation patterns in longitudinal psychological trajectory data from trauma cohorts.

## Debate and Critical Evaluation

### Against Hypothesis A

The glymphatic evidence is explicitly conflicting, and the circadian ODE finding comes from a modeling context (light-sleep-circadian simulation) rather than a clinical biomarker measurement context. Translation across these domains requires assumptions that may not hold. Furthermore, the specific 18-day threshold is a modeling parameter, not a clinical observation — actual stabilization timelines in trauma patients may differ substantially based on injury severity, treatment, and comorbidities. The pediatric concussion null finding could reflect measurement limitations rather than true null effects.

**However**: The multi-timepoint design recommendation is robust regardless of the specific threshold value. Even if 18 days is wrong, the principle that early post-injury measurements are less reliable predictors than later ones is well-supported by injury physiology.

### Against Hypothesis B

No evidence in the current set directly links SASP to post-traumatic psychological outcomes. The hypothesis rests on a mechanistic chain (trauma → senescence → SASP → neuroinflammation → psychological symptoms) that is individually plausible at each link but has not been demonstrated end-to-end. The prediction that existing senolytic RCTs contain unpublished psychological secondary outcomes is unfalsifiable until those trials are individually examined. Publication bias for negative secondary outcomes is real but also invoked here as a post-hoc explanation.

**However**: The specific prediction (fatigue/cognitive/sleep domains rather than formal psychiatric diagnosis) is mechanistically grounded in SASP cytokine biology and makes the hypothesis more precise and testable.

### Against Hypothesis C

Attractor landscape models, while mathematically elegant, have a history of generating unfalsifiable explanations in psychiatric research. The SHANK3 methylation connection is drawn from a purely technical paper with no trauma context. Non-linear improvement patterns are difficult to distinguish from delayed effects, regression to the mean, or measurement error in typical RCT sample sizes. The hypothesis may be too sophisticated for available data.

**However**: The empirical finding that multiple congenital anomalies produce non-additive attainment gaps is a genuine example of emergence in biological-to-psychological outcomes, providing at least one data point for the broader framework.

## Synthesis and Evolved Insight

Across the three hypotheses, the most actionable and evidence-grounded synthesis involves two specific methodological recommendations:

**Track 1 — Retrospective Cohort Analysis**: Existing trauma registries (DoD Trauma Registry for military, TQIP for orthopedic, BANTER/ABA registries for burns) should be queried for co-occurrence of inflammatory biomarker data and standardized psychological measures. The analytical approach should treat biomarker *trajectories* as the predictor variable rather than single timepoints, using mixed-effects spline regression to allow for non-linear shapes. Key covariates must include sleep quality metrics (if available), sex, injury severity score, and comorbidity burden. The outcome measures should be analyzed at the domain subscale level (cognitive, somatic, emotional) rather than composite scores only.

**Track 2 — Senolytic RCT Secondary Outcome Recovery**: A systematic review should screen all dasatinib+quercetin and navitoclax RCTs for QoL/psychological instruments in their methods sections, regardless of whether these outcomes were reported in results or abstract. Author contact protocols for unpublished secondary outcome data should be included. Power calculations for detecting psychological QoL effects should be reported to contextualize negative findings.

**The core evolved claim**: The inflammatory biomarker → psychological outcome pathway in trauma is real but likely indirect, domain-specific, and time-dependent. Senolytic interventions may contain detectable signals in existing RCT data, but that signal is most likely to appear in functional QoL subscales (fatigue, cognition, sleep) rather than diagnostic psychiatric instruments, and may be non-linear in form. Sex and trauma type are critical moderators that must be analyzed rather than controlled.

## Implications

If Hypothesis B is partially correct — that SASP drives chronic neuroinflammation contributing to post-traumatic psychological symptoms — the implications are significant:

1. **Timing of senolytics**: There may be an optimal window post-injury for senolytic intervention, before SASP establishes a stable pathological attractor state. This window might be identifiable from the inflammatory biomarker trajectory data.

2. **Reclassification of treatment resistance**: A subset of treatment-resistant PTSD and post-traumatic depression may be biologically driven by senescence-associated neuroinflammation, requiring senolytic rather than conventional psychiatric intervention.

3. **Biomarker development**: SASP-specific markers (GDF-15, p21, p16INK4a) may outperform conventional inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6) as predictors of psychological outcomes in trauma — a testable prediction with immediate clinical relevance.

4. **Sex-stratified analysis**: Given sex differences in SASP biology and inflammatory pain processing, psychological outcome prediction models may require sex-specific inflammatory biomarker thresholds.

## Open Questions

1. What proportion of published senolytic RCTs included QoL instruments, and at what response rate would author contact for unpublished data succeed?
2. Are SASP markers (GDF-15, p16INK4a) available in existing military trauma registries, or only conventional inflammatory markers?
3. Does the 18-day circadian stabilization threshold have a clinical biomarker analog — a period after which inflammatory trajectory predictions become reliable?
4. How does the glymphatic-sleep pathway interact with SASP in the specific context of traumatic brain injury versus non-CNS trauma?
5. Are there existing natural experiment designs (e.g., trauma patients who received quercetin for other indications) that could provide observational data on senolytic effects on psychological outcomes?
6. What is the epigenetic signature of SASP at synaptic architecture loci (SHANK family, PSD-95) in animal models of traumatic injury, and does senolytic treatment reverse it?
7. Does trauma type moderate the SASP-to-psychological-outcome pathway — specifically, do burns (which produce massive senescent cell burden in skin) show stronger psychological SASP effects than orthopedic trauma?
