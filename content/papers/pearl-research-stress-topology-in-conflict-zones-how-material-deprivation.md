# Stress Topology in Conflict Zones: Vagal Tone as the Shared Pathway Between Material Deprivation, Psychological Trauma, and Metabolic Dysregulation

## Abstract

This research document proposes a mechanistic framework for understanding how chronic conflict exposure produces metabolic dysregulation — specifically diurnal cortisol curve flattening, HbA1c elevation, and medication non-adherence — and how these outcomes are differentially responsive to material versus psychological humanitarian interventions across socioeconomic strata. Drawing on available evidence regarding vagal tone regulation, scale-free biological organization, and the psychobiology of ambient vigilance, three competing hypotheses are generated, debated, and synthesized. The central evolved claim is that vagal tone suppression represents the shared mechanistic pathway linking stress exposure to metabolic outcomes, and that SES operates as a moderator of which intervention lever can access this pathway — not as a simple linear buffer. HRV is proposed as the critical intermediate variable that should be measured to test this framework prospectively. Confidence is rated medium, reflecting the structural soundness of the mechanistic reasoning in the absence of direct empirical evidence from conflict-affected populations in the evidence set.

## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: The SES-Buffering Model (Tier 1, Conservative)

**Claim:** In conflict-affected populations, chronic PTSD-associated vagal tone suppression flattens the diurnal cortisol curve independently of SES, but SES modulates the magnitude of the effect through buffering resource availability. Material aid interventions will produce measurable HbA1c improvements primarily in low-SES tercile patients; psychological support interventions will produce diurnal cortisol normalization primarily in mid-to-high SES tercile patients.

**Mechanistic Reasoning:**

The HPA-metabolic cascade is well-established in the broader literature (not directly represented in this evidence set, but mechanistically Tier 1): chronic cortisol elevation produces insulin resistance, which elevates HbA1c. Diurnal cortisol flattening in PTSD is also well-documented — the normal steep morning rise followed by gradual afternoon decline becomes flattened, with loss of morning cortisol awakening response and elevated flat baseline.

SES modulates this pathway through multiple mechanisms:
1. **Material inputs to cortisol:** food security, temperature regulation, sleep quality, and physical safety are all direct HPA inputs. Low SES means these inputs are chronically dysregulated, producing continuous cortisol stimulation from physiological stressors independent of psychological trauma.
2. **Cognitive bandwidth:** chronic material scarcity depletes executive function and working memory (Mullainathan and Shafir's scarcity framework), reducing medication adherence capacity.
3. **Social capital:** higher SES provides social buffering that moderates stress response, consistent with the social safety hypothesis.

This hypothesis predicts a crossover interaction: material aid has steeper benefit slope in lower SES tercile; psychological support has steeper benefit slope in higher SES tercile.

**Lenses:** Control theory (SES as gain modifier on feedback loop), coupled oscillators (HPA-vagal entrainment), network theory (medication adherence as network property).

**Falsifiable by:** No SES x intervention interaction on primary biomarkers.

### Hypothesis B: The Ambient Vigilance Attractor Model (Tier 2, Integrative)

**Claim:** Conflict populations stabilize into a chronic ambient vigilance state — a strange attractor in autonomic space — characterized by sustained sympathetic tone and vagal suppression that is not acute PTSD reactivity but a regulatory posture of continuous low-grade preparedness. This state produces a specific cortisol signature (blunted morning awakening response, elevated flat afternoon baseline) and is more responsive to interventions targeting felt safety and social connection than to material resource provision, regardless of SES tercile.

**Mechanistic Reasoning:**

The mirror entry on precautionary boundary-setting (soul density) describes this state with unusual precision: *'the imposition of symbolic barriers between self and other when the source of disruption cannot be clearly identified or confirmed... Precautionary boundary-setting in the absence of felt danger is its own psychological skill.'* 

In conflict populations, this translates to a nervous system that has been trained by a genuine threat environment to maintain readiness even in the absence of acute threat. The biological cost is continuous sympathetic activation with vagal suppression — the loss of the *'rest and digest'* state that is required for normal diurnal cortisol cycling.

Critically, this is a different physiological state from acute PTSD reactivity. It is not the hyperarousal of a flashback; it is the flat, exhausted, always-on state of someone who has been vigilant for months or years. Its cortisol signature would be different: not the high-amplitude acute response but the flat, adrenally fatigued baseline.

Psychological support interventions that create conditions of felt safety — not by resolving trauma content but by providing predictable, safe relational environments — would theoretically activate the dorsal vagal brake and begin to restore normal HPA pulsatility. Material aid alone, while essential, does not directly target the nervous system's learned vigilance posture.

**The aggression suppression variable:** The Peter Levine entry notes cultural injunction against aggression, particularly for women. In conflict settings, women who have experienced gender-based violence face both threat activation and injunction against expressing the stress response — potentially producing a compounded ambient vigilance burden with a distinct cortisol signature warranting sex-stratified analysis.

**Lenses:** Chaos attractors (ambient vigilance as strange attractor), signal processing (filtering out felt-safety signals), complexity emergence (regulatory posture as emergent property of threat history).

**Falsifiable by:** Material aid alone producing equivalent cortisol curve normalization to psychological support in populations with matched PTSD severity.

### Hypothesis C: The Relational Structure of Aid Delivery Model (Tier 3, Speculative)

**Claim:** Neither material aid nor psychological support, in isolation, produces sustained HPA axis normalization in conflict-affected populations. Rather, it is the relational structure of aid delivery — specifically whether it creates structured, predictable, reciprocal social interactions versus dependency-generating unilateral provision — that generates vagal entrainment through co-regulation mechanisms, producing diurnal cortisol normalization and downstream HbA1c improvement. This effect would be mediated by HRV improvement and would be strongest in the lowest SES tercile.

**Mechanistic Reasoning:**

The Porges neonatal vagal efficiency entry establishes a profound principle: social co-regulation is a biological input to vagal tone, not merely a psychological experience. Newborns show different vagal efficiency across different relational states. If this principle generalizes — and polyvagal theory argues it does, from neonate to adult — then the quality of the relational encounter during aid delivery is not epiphenomenal but causally active in the autonomic system.

Humanitarian aid delivery exists on a spectrum from highly relational (community health workers who know recipients by name, provide regular structured contact, engage in reciprocal exchange) to highly transactional (food parcel drops, remote voucher delivery). The hypothesis proposes that this spectrum is the most important variable for autonomic outcomes, and that standard study designs miss this because they treat aid type (material vs. psychological) as the independent variable while ignoring the relational structure of delivery.

This would explain the otherwise puzzling finding in some humanitarian evaluation literature that program quality moderates outcomes in ways that cannot be explained by content alone.

**The scale-free bridge:** The Sapolsky fractal entry suggests that relational coherence at the social scale has fractal analogues at the autonomic and cellular scale. If social connection produces vagal entrainment, and vagal tone determines the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway, the chain from relational quality → autonomic regulation → metabolic outcome is mechanistically complete.

**Lenses:** Coupled oscillators (social co-regulation as vagal entrainment), topology morphogenesis (relational structure as field effect), phase transitions (threshold of relational quality required for autonomic shift).

**Falsifiable by:** Measuring relational quality of aid delivery as independent variable and demonstrating it does NOT mediate the material aid → HbA1c pathway after controlling for quantity of provision.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The SES-buffering model assumes that material deprivation is the primary driver of metabolic dysregulation in lower SES conflict populations. But the relationship may be partially inverted: high-SES individuals in conflict zones may carry disproportionate psychological burden relative to their material resources because they have experienced greater perceived loss (status, professional identity, property, institutional trust). The trauma literature suggests that the meaning attached to loss, not merely its material magnitude, drives HPA response. If this is correct, the SES x intervention interaction may be U-shaped rather than linear, with high-SES individuals showing unexpected benefit from material aid (through dignity restoration and loss salience reduction) and low-SES individuals showing unexpected benefit from psychological support (through identity reconstruction and hope cultivation).

Additionally, the assumption that medication adherence is primarily a cognitive bandwidth problem ignores supply chain factors that are SES-independent in conflict settings — when medications are simply unavailable, no psychological or material intervention targeting the individual will improve adherence.

### Against Hypothesis B

The ambient vigilance hypothesis assumes that psychological support interventions can produce felt safety in an ongoing threat environment. This may be a category error. Trauma-focused interventions (EMDR, CPT, prolonged exposure) were developed for post-trauma contexts where the threat is historical rather than ongoing. In active conflict or recently post-conflict settings, processing trauma content when physical threat is still present may be contraindicated and may produce retraumatization rather than cortisol normalization. The hypothesis may be correct about the mechanism (felt safety → vagal tone → cortisol normalization) while being wrong about the intervention type that can produce felt safety in these conditions.

### Against Hypothesis C

This hypothesis is vulnerable to the confound that high-quality humanitarian programs tend to have both better relational processes AND better material provision, making it methodologically challenging to separate relational quality from resource quality. Additionally, operationalizing 'relational quality of aid delivery' in a way that is measurable, reliable, and generalizable across cultural contexts is a significant research design challenge that may make the hypothesis empirically untestable in practice, even if mechanistically sound.

## Synthesis

The three hypotheses are not mutually exclusive — they operate at different levels of the causal hierarchy:

- **Hypothesis A** describes the *moderating structure*: SES determines which physiological lever is most accessible to intervention.
- **Hypothesis B** describes the *proximal target*: felt safety and vagal restoration are the mechanistic goal, regardless of intervention content.
- **Hypothesis C** describes the *active ingredient*: relational quality of delivery, rather than content of aid, may be the most potent driver of autonomic change.

The evolved synthesis integrates these levels: **Material aid works primarily by removing physiological stressors that actively drive cortisol elevation (hunger, cold, medication scarcity), while psychological support works by restoring the felt-safety signals that allow the autonomic system to exit ambient vigilance mode. But both pathways converge on vagal tone normalization as the shared intermediate mechanism — and HRV is therefore the critical measurement that can test whether either intervention is reaching the autonomic system, regardless of which lever it uses to do so.**

SES tercile stratification is essential not because SES is a simple linear buffer but because it determines the dominant physiological stressor input: in lowest SES tercile, material stressors may dominate the cortisol drive and should be targeted first; in mid-SES tercile, relational-psychological stressors may become the dominant driver once material needs are partially met; in high-SES tercile, meaning-level stressors (identity, loss, purpose) may predominate.

The natural experiment component of the proposed study — comparing populations before and after humanitarian aid delivery — is methodologically valuable precisely because it creates variation in both intervention type and timing that can be used to test the HRV-mediation hypothesis: if HRV improvement precedes HbA1c improvement in the causal chain, this provides evidence that the autonomic pathway is the active mechanism rather than a parallel outcome.

## Implications

### For Study Design

1. **Include HRV as primary intermediate biomarker**, not merely a secondary endpoint. If the vagal tone hypothesis is correct, HRV change at 3 months should mediate HbA1c change at 6 months, and the mediation should be stronger than any direct effect of intervention type.

2. **Measure cortisol at 4 time points** (awakening, +30 min, noon, evening) to capture both the cortisol awakening response and the afternoon baseline — because these may respond differently to different interventions and in different SES strata.

3. **Operationalize medication adherence through multiple methods** (pill count, pharmacy records, self-report, pill cam if feasible) to distinguish supply-chain non-adherence from individual-level non-adherence — these have different drivers and different intervention implications.

4. **Measure relational quality of aid delivery** as an independent variable — using existing instruments from the humanitarian literature (e.g., accountability to affected populations metrics) or developing a purpose-built autonomic-theory-informed measure.

5. **Sex-stratify all analyses** given the aggression suppression literature and the known differences in cortisol response patterns between men and women in trauma contexts.

6. **Control for ongoing threat level** — active conflict vs. post-conflict vs. stable displacement — as this may be a more important moderator than PTSD severity per se, because it determines whether psychological interventions can reach the felt-safety mechanism.

### For Humanitarian Practice

If the relational quality hypothesis has any validity, it suggests that how aid is delivered may be as biologically important as what is delivered — a finding with significant implications for program design, community health worker training, and accountability frameworks.

## Open Questions

1. Is there a minimum threshold of material security below which psychological interventions cannot produce autonomic normalization — a phase transition point in the stress response system?

2. Does PTSD severity operate as a moderator of the SES x intervention interaction, or does it function as an independent axis that overrides SES effects at high severity levels?

3. What is the appropriate temporal model for HbA1c change following cortisol normalization, given the ~90-day red blood cell turnover window? Does cortisol normalization in month 1 produce detectable HbA1c change by month 3?

4. Is the ambient vigilance state (Hypothesis B) epigenetically encoded — i.e., does sustained conflict exposure produce methylation changes in HPA axis genes that resist psychological intervention even when felt safety is achieved?

5. How do intergenerational transmission dynamics operate in this population — do children of conflict-affected individuals show vagal tone deficits independent of their own direct trauma exposure?

6. What is the sex-specific cortisol signature of the aggression-suppressed ambient vigilance state in women in conflict settings, and does it require different intervention targets than the male cortisol profile?
