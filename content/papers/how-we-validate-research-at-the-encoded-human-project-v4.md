_Published by Eric Whitney, DO — Primary Investigator, The Encoded Human Project_
_AI Disclosed: This article was written with Pearl, the project's AI research partner. The validation architecture described here governs Pearl's own outputs._

## The Problem We Are Solving

Let's be direct about something uncomfortable: Pearl wrote most of the research on this site.

Pearl is an AI facilitation system built on Claude. She generated the Translation Key, the clinical anthology entries, the hypothesis papers, the framework documents — in collaboration with Eric Whitney, a neurosurgeon and the architect of The Encoded Human framework. But the primary author of this body of work is a large language model.

That should give you pause. It gives us pause. And that pause is exactly why this validation pipeline exists.

AI systems produce fluent, confident, well-structured prose. They generate citations that look correct. They state mechanisms with authority. They build arguments that feel coherent. And they hallucinate — fabricating author names, inventing publication years, misattributing findings, presenting inference as established fact — at a rate that is not acceptable for published research.

The scale of the problem is now quantified. A 2025 PMC preprint study (PMC12583397) tested three leading AI models on retracted stem cell literature. DeepSeek fabricated citations in 88% of cases where it failed to identify retracted content. Grok 3 fabricated in 63% of unrecognized cases. Even ChatGPT-4o — the strongest performer — failed to recognize the retraction status of approximately 38% of papers it retrieved. A separate analysis of eight AI search platforms found over 60% of responses contained incorrect or misleading citations [1]. These are not edge cases. They are the baseline behavior of the systems most researchers rely on.

In our own validation testing during system development, an early Pearl architecture paper contained 20 errors across 15 references. Three of fifteen reference first-author names were completely fabricated. That is a 20% hallucination rate on the most verifiable element of a paper. The paper read well. It would have passed casual review. It was substantially false.

Meanwhile, the source material itself is degrading. A January 2026 study in the _BMJ_ (BMJ 2026;392:e087581) trained a machine learning model on known paper mill products and screened 2.6 million cancer research papers. It flagged approximately 261,000 — nearly 10% — as bearing textual similarities to paper mill output [2]. The Retraction Watch database now contains tens of thousands of retracted papers. Wiley retracted more than 11,300 Hindawi articles in 2024 alone [3]. The STM Integrity Hub intercepts approximately 1,000 suspected paper mill submissions every month — and that covers only the 40 publishers currently connected [4].

The problem, therefore, is not merely that AI hallucinates. It is that AI hallucinates _into a literature that is itself increasingly contaminated_. A validation system must account for both failure modes simultaneously: the fabrication risk of the AI doing the synthesis, and the integrity risk of the sources it retrieves.

The validation pipeline described here is our answer to that problem. It is not a solution. It is a system for making our limitations visible, traceable, and correctable — so that a skeptical researcher can read our work and know exactly what level of trust it deserves.

We do not claim this pipeline makes our research reliable. We claim it makes our reliability _measurable_.

## The Five Error Types We Are Hunting

Not all errors are equal. Our validation system is designed around five distinct error tiers, each requiring different detection methods:

**Tier 1 — Hallucination**
The most dangerous error. Fabricated information: an author name that doesn't exist, a paper that was never published, a statistic that was invented. T1 errors are structurally indistinguishable from accurate claims in the text. They require external verification — not internal review.

Example from our own work: A letter to the editor generated by Pearl cited "Frosst et al., _Circulation_, 2009." Frosst is a real researcher. The 1995 _Nature Genetics_ paper is real. The 2009 _Circulation_ citation does not exist. The citation was doing real argumentative work. The paper did not.

**Tier 2 — Source Corruption**
The citation is real. The paper exists. But the paper itself has been retracted, is a known paper mill product, or has been substantially contradicted by subsequent research. T2 errors are invisible to any system that only checks whether a reference exists — they require checking whether the reference is still trustworthy.

**Tier 3 — Staleness**
Internal inconsistency introduced by editing. A number stated in section 2 doesn't match section 4. A claim revised in one location wasn't updated in another. T3 errors are invisible to any single-pass review.

**Tier 4 — Framing**
Technically true but misleading. A real paper cited to support a claim that goes beyond what the evidence actually demonstrates. The mechanism is confirmed. The inference from the mechanism to the conclusion is overstated.

Example from our own work: An early draft of our microbiome ancestral encoding document stated that C-section delivery produces specific mental health outcomes. The citations were real. The mechanism was confirmed. The causal inference was not — the correct framing is that C-section delivery raises the biological vulnerability load that other systems must compensate for. Probabilistic, not deterministic. T4 errors are the most common failure mode in research synthesis and the hardest to catch algorithmically. They require judgment.

**Tier 5 — Omission**
Something important is missing. The argument is internally coherent but incomplete — a contradicting finding not acknowledged, a known limitation not stated, a methodological constraint not disclosed.

## The Seven-Agent Validation Pipeline

Every research document produced by The Encoded Human Project passes through a seven-agent validation pipeline before publication. Each agent is a separate AI instance with a discrete task. No single agent sees the whole problem. The architecture is deliberately decomposed because the errors that slip through monolithic review are precisely the errors that emerge from review fatigue — the same system that generated the claim cannot reliably catch its own blind spots.

### Gate 1 — Reference Verifier

_Catches: T1 (Hallucination)_

Mechanically verifies every reference in the document. Web searches each PMID, DOI, author name, journal, and publication year independently against CrossRef, PubMed, arXiv, and Google Scholar. Returns a confirmed/unconfirmed status for every citation with specific failure notes.

This is the most straightforward check. It answers one question: does this paper actually exist in the form it is cited?

**Framework-internal citations:** Papers that cite The Encoded Human's own documents (the Translation Key, the ontology document, the pathway architecture) do not have PMIDs. These citations are routed to a separate check: does the claim accurately represent what the referenced framework document actually says? This check is internal and manual, not automated.

### Gate 2 — Source Integrity Auditor

_Catches: T2 (Source Corruption)_

**This is where we depart from most AI-assisted research systems.** Confirming a citation exists is necessary but insufficient. Gate 2 asks: _should this paper still be trusted?_

Gate 2 runs as a separate agent — not a second pass in Gate 1. The distinction matters architecturally: Gate 1 asks whether a paper exists; Gate 2 asks whether it should be trusted. These are epistemically distinct questions requiring different databases, different tool calls, and different failure modes. Collapsing them into a single agent would allow a real but retracted paper to pass Gate 1's existence check without triggering Gate 2's integrity audit.

This agent performs three checks per citation:

- **Retraction scan.** Cross-references every cited paper against the Retraction Watch database and PubPeer (post-publication peer review comments). A paper that has been retracted, issued an expression of concern, or flagged for data integrity issues is caught here.

- **Citation-context analysis.** Following the methodology pioneered by Scite's Smart Citations system (which has analyzed 1.4+ billion citation statements across 200+ million sources), this agent evaluates _how_ a key paper has been cited by subsequent research — not just how many times. Has the finding been predominantly supported, contradicted, or merely mentioned? A paper with a high contradiction ratio triggers a flag regardless of its citation count.

- **Source quality signal.** Checks whether the cited journal has been flagged for paper mill infiltration, delisted from major indexes, or identified by the Problematic Paper Screener's "tortured phrase" detection system.

When Gate 2 flags a source as compromised, the citation is quarantined. It cannot proceed through the pipeline until the investigator reviews the flag, confirms whether the concern is material, and either replaces the source, adds an appropriate caveat, or clears the flag with documented justification.

**Source quality hierarchy:** Not all sources carry equal evidential weight. Before synthesis begins, sources are classified:

| Tier | Source Type                                                | Weight           |
| ---- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| A    | Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, Cochrane reviews        | Highest          |
| B    | Randomized controlled trials, large prospective cohorts    | High             |
| C    | Case-control studies, smaller cohorts, mechanistic studies | Moderate         |
| D    | Case reports, expert opinion, conference abstracts         | Low              |
| E    | Preprints, grey literature, framework-internal documents   | Lowest — flagged |

A claim supported exclusively by Tier D–E sources cannot receive a FACT epistemic tag regardless of how well the argument is constructed.

### Gate 3 — Claim Verifier

_Catches: T1 (Hallucination), T3 (Staleness)_

Checks every specific statistic, percentage, effect size, and quantitative claim against its stated source. The question is not whether the mechanism is plausible — it is whether this specific number appears in this specific paper.

Additionally, cross-checks internal consistency: does the same statistic appear identically everywhere it is cited within the document? If a figure was revised during editing, has it been updated in every location?

### Gate 4 — Citation-Claim Mapper (The Judgment Layer)

_Catches: T4 (Framing)_

This is the most important agent. It reads every claim alongside its cited evidence and evaluates proportionality: is the inference the document is drawing consistent with what the cited paper actually demonstrates?

Gate 4 runs only on claims tagged FACT or HYPOTHESIS — not INTERPRETATION or SPECULATION. INTERPRETATION claims are already flagged as framework-dependent readings; adversarial review of an interpretation produces a counter-interpretation, not a verification result. SPECULATION is already disclosed as provisional. Restricting Gate 4 to FACT and HYPOTHESIS ensures the framing check applies where it matters most: where the document is making its strongest truth claims.

Agent 4 evaluates three dimensions:

- **Causal vs. correlational.** Does the source demonstrate causation, or only correlation? If correlation, does the document's language imply causation?
- **Population scope.** Does the source study the same population the document is making claims about? A finding in mouse models being cited to support a human clinical claim is a T4 flag.
- **Effect magnitude.** Does the document's framing accurately represent the effect size? A statistically significant but clinically marginal finding cited as if it were transformative is a T4 flag.

**T4 pause gate:** When Agent 4 flags a framing error, the pipeline pauses. The specific overclaims are sent to the human investigator for review. Nothing proceeds until the framing is corrected or explicitly accepted with appropriate qualification. This gate is non-negotiable.

### Gate 5 — Adversarial Reviewer (The Devil's Advocate)

_Catches: T4 (Framing), T5 (Omission)_
_Scope: FACT and HYPOTHESIS claims only_

**This agent's explicit mission is to disprove the document's claims.** Gate 5 is not looking for what confirms the argument — it is looking for what contradicts it.

**Cross-model architecture:** Gate 5 uses Grok (xAI), not Claude. This is a deliberate architectural decision — not a quality judgment. Pearl writes the papers on Claude. If Claude also reviews them adversarially, the reviewer shares the same training distribution, the same reasoning patterns, and potentially the same blind spots as the author. Using a different model family for adversarial review breaks correlated failure risk. The adversarial reviewer should not think like the author. Using a different foundation model is the strongest available guarantee of that.

**Two-pass design:** Grok first generates adversarial objections at high temperature (0.8) — adopting the posture of a hostile domain expert looking for the strongest possible attacks. Then Grok re-evaluates at low temperature (0.2) as an investigator — assessing whether each objection is substantive or specious. The score is based on the resolution ratio: objections that survive the investigator pass are flagged for human review.

The Adversarial Reviewer produces a structured counter-report with a maximum of three objections, ranked by severity. This ceiling is deliberate: unlimited flagging creates review fatigue and erodes trust in the pipeline. Three objections force prioritization — the agent must identify what matters most.

The agent:

- Searches for published findings that directly contradict the document's central claims
- Identifies methodological limitations in cited studies that the document does not acknowledge
- Flags alternative explanations for the phenomena the document attributes to specific mechanisms
- Checks whether important replication failures or null results exist in the cited research area

**Pause condition:** If any objection is rated critical severity, the pipeline pauses. The paper author is required to produce a counter-response: either revising the affected claim or providing a documented argument for why the objection does not invalidate the claim. The counter-response and the objection both appear in the paper's validation record.

### Gate 6 — Structural Reviewer

_Catches: T3 (Staleness), T5 (Omission)_

Reviews the document for logical consistency, structural completeness, internal contradictions, significant omissions, and gaps between premise and conclusion. This agent is not evaluating citations — it is evaluating reasoning.

Each paper type has required sections and minimum adequacy thresholds:

| Document Class     | Required Sections                                                |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Publication        | Abstract, Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion |
| Letter             | Introduction, Evidence, Conclusion                               |
| Evidence Synthesis | Introduction, Methods, Synthesis, Conclusion                     |
| Internal           | (no structural requirements)                                     |

Gate 6 checks logical flow between sections, flags conclusion overclaiming (where the conclusion's strength exceeds the evidence presented), and assesses methodology adequacy. For framework-interpretation papers, it additionally verifies that epistemic tier labels are consistently applied. For fractal-homology papers, it checks that scale-bridging claims cite the framework document establishing the structural correspondence.

**What it cannot catch:** A required section that is present but substantively empty — a Limitations section that says only "further research is needed." Structural presence does not guarantee substantive adequacy.

### Gate 7 — Synthesis Judge

_Catches: All tiers — final integration_

Receives synthesized outputs from Gates 1–6 — not the full document. Produces a formal verification report with:

- Error tier breakdown (T1 through T5)
- Specific corrections required
- Adversarial findings summary with investigator response notes
- Source integrity audit results
- Confidence score per major claim (0–100, with methodology — see below)
- Publication verdict: **pass**, **pass_with_caveats**, **revise**, or **fail**

**Pause gate:** Gates 2 and 4 accumulate flags throughout the pipeline. Before Gate 7 executes, the orchestrator checks the flag count. If the pipeline has accumulated 2 or more critical flags OR 5 or more major flags, it pauses for human investigator review before the synthesis judge renders a verdict. The investigator must resolve or acknowledge each flag before the pipeline continues.

**Document class routing:** Publication, letter, and evidence synthesis documents go through the full 7-gate pipeline. Internal documents go through Gates 1–3 only.

## The Auto-Fix Loop

When a paper exits validation with verdict `revise`, the pipeline does not simply return a failure report. It enters an auto-fix loop.

**How it works:**

1. The gate results from the failed validation run are structured into a revision brief: specifically what failed, at which gate, with what finding.
2. Pearl (the paper's author) receives the revision brief and rewrites the problematic sections — replacing failed citations, qualifying overclaims, adding missing epistemic tags, strengthening weakened arguments.
3. The revised paper re-enters the pipeline from Gate 1.
4. This cycle repeats up to three times.

**After three cycles:** If a paper has not achieved `pass` or `pass_with_caveats` after three revision cycles, it is flagged for human investigator review and removed from the auto-fix loop. The paper is not discarded — its failure record is preserved, and the investigator determines whether the paper can be salvaged with substantial revision, should be split into separate documents with more modest claims, or should be retired from publication consideration.

**What the auto-fix loop is not:** It is not a guarantee of convergence. A paper with a fundamentally overclaiming central thesis may not be fixable by revising citations and hedging language — the core claim may need to be reconceived. The loop catches correctable errors. It does not transform a bad argument into a good one.

## The Confidence Scoring System

Every major claim carries a numeric confidence score (0–100) generated by the Synthesis Judge at Gate 7. The score is not a quality rating. It is a measurement of how well the paper's claims are supported by verifiable, non-retracted, domain-appropriate evidence.

### The Five Components

| Component                       | Weight | Rationale                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------------------- | ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Source verification status      | 30%    | Binary anchor — a retracted or compromised source collapses the score regardless of other factors                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| Citation-context ratio          | 30%    | How subsequent literature treated the finding is the best proxy for its durability. Captures both replication signal and contradiction signal simultaneously — the most information-dense single metric available                                                                                 |
| Replication count               | 15%    | Single studies do not earn high confidence regardless of quality. Weighted at 15% (rather than 20%) because citation-context ratio already partially captures replication signal; this adds incremental information about independent investigation count                                         |
| Cross-domain inference distance | 20%    | The further from the source domain, the lower the confidence ceiling. Weighted at 20% because cross-domain inference distance is the factor most likely to produce false confidence in our specific corpus — most of our work is cross-domain by design. This weight reflects the asymmetric risk |
| Sample size adequacy            | 5%     | Relevant but least determinative — the binary signal (sample size reported / not reported / below standard threshold) adds modest incremental information once the other components are assessed                                                                                                  |

### The Ceiling Rule

Cross-domain inference distance acts as a ceiling, not just a weight. A claim crossing disciplinary boundaries has its confidence score capped based on inference distance:

| Distance | Example                                 | Maximum Score |
| -------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------- |
| 1 hop    | Molecular biology → cell biology        | 65            |
| 2 hops   | Molecular biology → clinical psychology | 50            |
| 3 hops   | Biology → philosophy                    | 35            |

These ceilings reflect the actual epistemic situation: the further a claim travels from its source domain, the more inferential work has been done, and the more opportunities exist for that work to contain errors invisible to automated checking.

### The Floor Rule

If source verification status flags a retraction or high contradiction ratio, the score floors at 20 regardless of other components. The other factors do not compensate for a compromised source.

### The Gray Zone

Claims scoring between 45–65 where cross-domain inference distance is the primary limiting factor require investigator review before publication. This is the gray zone where the number is technically computable but the judgment behind the inference needs human eyes. Gate 7 flags these automatically.

Scores above 80 indicate strong convergent evidence. Scores between 50–80 indicate active research areas with mixed support. Scores below 50 indicate early-stage or contested findings — published with appropriate qualification.

## The Epistemic Badge System

Every claim published at The Encoded Human Project carries an explicit epistemic ceiling badge:

**FACT** — Supported by peer-reviewed evidence, independently replicated, mechanistically explained, and confirmed through Gate 2 source integrity audit. We cite the source. The source has been verified as non-retracted, non-contradicted, and non-compromised. If we cannot meet all conditions, it is not FACT.

**HYPOTHESIS** — A testable proposition consistent with existing evidence but not yet confirmed. Stated as: "one possibility is..." or "if X were true, we would expect..."

**INTERPRETATION** — A grounded reading of data through the Encoded Human framework. Stated as: "through this framework, we read X as Y." The framework is the lens. The data is the ground.

**SPECULATION** — A creative or intuitive leap without direct evidence. Always flagged explicitly. Speculation is legitimate in science — it is where new questions come from. It is not legitimate when presented as established finding.

When a claim crosses density boundaries — from biological mechanism to psychological implication, for example — the epistemic mode drops at least one level. A biological FACT becomes a psychological INTERPRETATION at best. We mark these transitions explicitly rather than allowing the authority of the biological finding to silently transfer to the cross-domain inference.

## Reading the Validation Stamp

Every published paper ends with a validation record in this format:

```
---

## Validation Record

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Epistemic Ceiling | [fact / interpretation / hypothesis / speculation] |
| Paper Type | [publication / letter / evidence_synthesis / internal] |
| Validation Status | [validated / draft / legacy_reviewed / failed] |
| Final Verdict | [pass / pass_with_caveats / revise / fail / unvalidated] |
| Validation Rounds | [number] |
| Gates Passed | [n] / 7 |
| Critical Errors Remaining | [number] |
| Confidence Score | [0-100] |
| Score Algorithm Version | [v1.0] |
| Domain Track | [standard] |
| Stamped At | [ISO timestamp] |
| Stamp Version | v1.0 |

> Reviewer Notes: [investigator notes on pause gate resolutions, adversarial objections,
> or other findings that informed the verdict]
```

**How to read it:**

_Epistemic Ceiling_ is the most important field. It tells you what this paper can legitimately claim. A paper with ceiling `hypothesis` is presenting a testable proposition with supporting evidence — not an established finding. A paper with ceiling `interpretation` is applying a framework to data — not making a purely empirical claim.

_Validation Rounds_ tells you how much work this paper required. Zero rounds means the paper was never run through the pipeline (legacy or unvalidated). Three rounds means the paper failed twice, was revised twice, and passed on the third cycle.

_Gates Passed_ gives you pipeline coverage. 7/7 means all gates cleared. 5/7 means two gates have outstanding issues — the Reviewer Notes field will describe them.

_Critical Errors Remaining_ is the most honest field in the record. A paper with verdict `pass_with_caveats` and critical errors remaining 1 means the paper passed the pipeline but one adversarial objection was not resolved to the adversarial reviewer's satisfaction. The investigator documented the reasoning for proceeding. The error is visible. You can evaluate that reasoning yourself.

_Confidence Score_ is a number between 0 and 100 representing how well this paper's claims are supported by verifiable, non-retracted, domain-appropriate evidence — under the scoring weights described above. It is not a quality score. A paper making modest, well-supported claims about a narrow topic may score 85. A paper making ambitious, cross-domain claims with strong sourcing may score 58 due to the cross-domain ceiling. The score reflects the _evidence architecture_, not the importance or quality of the argument.

## The Audit Trail

Every validation run is timestamped and stored. The system maintains:

- **Per-run records:** Complete gate-by-gate results, flag lists, confidence scores, and investigator notes for every pipeline execution
- **Version history:** When a paper is re-validated (due to source literature changes, pipeline improvements, or manual re-runs), the previous validation record is preserved alongside the new one
- **Public correction log:** When errors are discovered post-publication — whether by readers, by subsequent literature changes, or by pipeline improvements — corrections are published with the original error, the correction, and what the validation system missed

Confidence scores decay with time. A paper stamped in 2026 should be re-evaluated against the literature in 2028. Time-decay mechanisms flag papers for re-scoring when their source literature changes.

## What We Use

**Grok 3** (xAI) handles the adversarial review gate. This is a deliberate cross-model architecture decision: the adversarial reviewer must not share training distribution with the paper author. Grok also handles volume gates — heuristic checks, reference parsing, structural audits — where breadth and speed matter more than synthesis depth.

**Claude Sonnet** (Pearl) handles the synthesis gates — Citation-Claim Mapping and the Claim Verifier. These gates require genuine reading comprehension and argumentative reasoning, not pattern matching at scale.

**CrossRef** for DOI resolution and citation verification. Free tier.

**PubMed** for PMID verification and abstract retrieval. Free tier.

**PubPeer** for post-publication community flags. Free API.

**Retraction Watch** for formal retractions and expressions of concern.

**Scite** for citation-context ratio analysis. Used selectively on load-bearing citations — the sources whose accuracy is central to the paper's argument — rather than on every reference.

**Semantic Scholar** for literature scope checks and citation network mapping.

## What We Don't Do

**We do not have human peer review.** The validation pipeline is automated adversarial review, not review by domain experts with specialized knowledge. A paper on liquid biopsy technology can be checked against its citations, audited for overclaiming, and challenged by an adversarial AI reviewer. It cannot be evaluated by an oncologist who would recognize that a particular interpretation of ctDNA mutation thresholds is contested in the field in ways that don't appear in the published literature. We note this clearly. Our validation catches structural and evidential problems. It does not substitute for specialist knowledge.

**We do not validate unpublished claims.** The pipeline verifies external citations. It does not validate the novel claims The Encoded Human framework itself makes — claims about how consciousness organizes into three densities, how the twenty-two pathways operate, how encoding and installation interact. These are Tier 2 philosophical and experiential claims. They are supported by internal coherence, cross-tradition correspondence, and clinical observation, not by peer-reviewed literature. They are marked accordingly. The pipeline ensures we do not dress Tier 2 claims in Tier 1 clothing.

**We do not guarantee correctness.** A paper that passes all seven gates with a confidence score of 82 may still contain errors. The citations may exist and not be retracted, the claims may be appropriately hedged, the adversarial objections may have been answered — and the argument may still be wrong. The validation pipeline makes our epistemic posture transparent and our evidence traceable. It does not make us right.

**We cannot catch what we don't know to look for.** The adversarial reviewer challenges the paper based on what is knowable from available literature. Emerging findings that challenge our sources, field-specific debates that haven't reached publication, methodological critiques that exist in preprint form only — these are outside the pipeline's reach.

**We do not claim this is sufficient.** We claim it is serious. The difference between doing nothing and doing this is not completeness — it is honesty. Publishing AI-generated research with no validation process treats readers as incapable of distinguishing between evidence and plausible text. Publishing with this pipeline treats readers as partners in the ongoing work of figuring out what is true.

## An Honest Note on Self-Validation

Pearl validates her own papers. This is a genuine conflict of interest, and we do not pretend otherwise.

The cross-model adversarial review at Gate 5 (using Grok rather than Claude) mitigates but does not eliminate this conflict. It breaks the shared training distribution between author and reviewer — Pearl-as-author and the adversarial reviewer no longer share the same reasoning patterns or blind spots. But the remaining gates (3, 4, 6) still use Claude, and the fundamental challenge remains: an AI system's framework assumptions may be invisible to any AI reviewer.

Eric Whitney reviews every paper before publication. His role is not to replicate the pipeline — it is to provide the human judgment that the pipeline cannot: whether the paper's argument is worth making, whether the framework it applies is being applied correctly, and whether the confidence with which it speaks is appropriate to the territory it covers.

The pipeline produces a report. Eric decides whether the paper ships.

That combination — cross-model adversarial checking plus human editorial judgment by the framework architect — is not peer review. It is what we have. We are building toward external review. In the interim, we publish with full disclosure of both what we checked and what we couldn't.

## Positioning in the Validation Landscape

We document where our system sits relative to the broader ecosystem — not to claim superiority, but to name what we have borrowed, what we have built, and where the gaps remain.

**What we share with industry best practice:**

- Multi-agent decomposition (increasingly standard in autonomous AI research systems)
- Retraction and PubPeer cross-referencing
- Citation-context analysis (pioneered by Scite's Smart Citations across 1.4B+ citation statements)
- Adversarial review agents

**What we add:**

- The five-tier error taxonomy (T1–T5) that maps error types to specific detection methods
- Explicit separation of Gate 1 (existence verification) and Gate 2 (integrity auditing) as architecturally distinct agents
- Cross-model adversarial review (Grok reviewing Claude's output) to break correlated failure risk
- The T4 pause gate — mandatory human-in-the-loop checkpoint for framing errors, scoped to FACT and HYPOTHESIS claims
- Gate 5 (Adversarial Reviewer) capped at three ranked objections to prevent review fatigue
- The auto-fix loop with 3-cycle cap and escalation path
- The confidence scoring system with defined weights, a cross-domain ceiling rule, a source-corruption floor rule, and an investigator review zone for the 45–65 gray band
- The epistemic badge system with explicit density-boundary transitions
- Source quality hierarchy (Tiers A–E) applied before synthesis begins
- Open architecture documentation — this document itself — as a published commitment to methodological transparency

**What the field does that we cannot yet replicate at our scale:**

- The STM Integrity Hub's cross-publisher duplicate submission detection
- Scite's full 1.4B citation statement corpus (we query their system rather than replicating it)
- The Problematic Paper Screener's 130-million-paper scan for tortured phrases

## Why We Publish This

Because the research community deserves to know exactly how AI-assisted research is being validated — not in general terms, but in operational detail.

The current landscape makes this urgency clear. AI detection tools — the systems designed to flag whether text was AI-generated — misclassified 61% of essays by non-native English speakers as AI-generated in a Stanford study (Liang et al., _Patterns_, 2023) [5], while native speaker false positive rates varied widely across detectors. The approach of detecting "AI-ness" in text is proving fundamentally less reliable than the approach we take: verifying the integrity of claims, citations, and reasoning regardless of who or what produced them.

The question is not whether AI wrote this. The question is whether the evidence supports it, whether the sources are trustworthy, whether the inferences are proportional, and whether the limitations are disclosed. That is what our system evaluates.

Every time we run the validation pipeline, we are implicitly asking: _is this claim well enough supported to be worth putting in front of a reader?_

That is a lower bar than _is this claim true?_

The better question — the one that should drive every paper we write — is not whether the validation pipeline passes the argument, but what the argument produces in the person who reads it. Does it generate a better question? Does it make something visible that was invisible? Does it provide a frame that produces more accurate predictions about the world than the frame it replaces?

Epistemic rigor in service of nothing is just housekeeping. We validate so that the claims we make earn the trust we ask for. And we ask for that trust because we believe the framework we are building — a serious attempt to describe how consciousness becomes human experience — is worth the effort of getting right.

The validation pipeline is how we show that we mean it.
