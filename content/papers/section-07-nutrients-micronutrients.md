## Vitamin D (25-Hydroxy)

**Operation:** Reception | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Circulating 25-hydroxycholecalciferol, the stable hepatic conversion product of cutaneous and dietary vitamin D, reflecting total-body vitamin D status across a 2–3 week half-life window.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient | < 20 ng/mL | — |
| Insufficient | 20–29 ng/mL | — |
| Sufficient | 30–100 ng/mL | 50–80 ng/mL |
| Toxic threshold | > 100 ng/mL | Avoid |

### Body Reading
FACT: Vitamin D receptor (VDR) is expressed on T-cells, B-cells, dendritic cells, and macrophages; VDR activation suppresses Th1/Th17 inflammatory tone and promotes regulatory T-cell differentiation. INTERPRETATION: Values below 30 ng/mL correlate with elevated thyroid antibody titers, increased autoimmune disease prevalence, and impaired innate antimicrobial response — the immune system is running without its primary tolerance signal. HYPOTHESIS: The conventional sufficiency threshold of 30 ng/mL, derived from calcium absorption data, underestimates the concentration required for robust immune tolerance; 50–80 ng/mL better reflects the functional threshold for autoimmunity prevention. Hypervitaminosis D (> 100 ng/mL) drives hypercalcemia and cardiovascular risk; excess is supplementation-driven, not sun-exposure-driven.

## Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)

**Operation:** Reception | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Circulating cobalamin concentration, reflecting dietary intake, absorption integrity, and intrinsic-factor-dependent ileal uptake — a composite marker of the entire B12 acquisition pathway.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient | < 200 pg/mL | — |
| Low-normal | 200–400 pg/mL | — |
| Normal | 200–900 pg/mL | 500–900 pg/mL |
| Elevated (consider dysregulation) | > 1,000 pg/mL | Investigate |

### Body Reading
FACT: B12 is the obligate cofactor for methionine synthase (homocysteine → methionine) and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase; deficiency in either pathway produces measurable downstream metabolites — elevated homocysteine and methylmalonic acid — before serum B12 falls below conventional cutoffs. INTERPRETATION: A serum B12 of 300 pg/mL may appear "normal" while functional methylation is already compromised; elevated MMA (> 0.4 µmol/L) is the definitive confirmation of cellular B12 insufficiency. HYPOTHESIS: Neurological B12 deficiency — subacute combined degeneration — may develop at serum levels considered adequate by population reference ranges in patients with elevated MMA, representing a diagnostic gap with serious irreversibility risk. Elevated serum B12 in the absence of supplementation warrants investigation for liver disease or myeloproliferative disorder.

## Folate (Serum or RBC)

**Operation:** Reception | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Circulating folate (serum) or erythrocyte folate (RBC), reflecting recent dietary intake and tissue folate stores respectively; RBC folate is the preferred marker for functional status.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient | < 2.7 ng/mL | — |
| Low-normal | 2.7–5.9 ng/mL | — |
| Normal | > 5.9 ng/mL (serum) | > 10 ng/mL (serum) |
| RBC folate optimal | > 280 ng/mL | 400–800 ng/mL |

### Body Reading
FACT: Folate, as 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF), donates the methyl group that converts homocysteine to methionine via methionine synthase; without it, the methylation cycle stalls and homocysteine accumulates. INTERPRETATION: MTHFR C677T or A1298C polymorphisms impair conversion of dietary folate to 5-MTHF, meaning serum folate can appear adequate while functional methylfolate availability is substantially reduced; homocysteine elevation is the functional readout. HYPOTHESIS: Supplementation with synthetic folic acid in MTHFR-variant patients may paradoxically worsen folate metabolism by competitively inhibiting 5-MTHF transport; methylfolate (5-MTHF) is both safer and functionally superior in this population. Megaloblastic anemia from folate deficiency is morphologically identical to B12-deficiency anemia — MCV elevation without distinguishing B12 status risks masking neurological damage.

## Iron Panel (Serum Iron + Ferritin + TIBC)

**Operation:** Reception | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
A three-marker composite assessing iron in circulation (serum iron), iron in storage (ferritin), and transferrin iron-binding saturation (TIBC); together these distinguish deficiency, functional deficiency, inflammation-masked deficiency, and overload.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Serum Iron | 30–170 mcg/dL | 60–100 mcg/dL |
| Ferritin | 12–150 ng/mL | 30–100 ng/mL (women); 50–150 (men) |
| TIBC | 250–450 mcg/dL | 250–360 mcg/dL |
| Transferrin saturation | 15–50% | 25–40% |

### Body Reading
FACT: Ferritin is both the primary iron storage protein and an acute-phase reactant; elevated ferritin (> 150 ng/mL) may reflect iron overload or may reflect inflammatory masking of functional depletion — CRP must be co-interpreted to distinguish these states. INTERPRETATION: The classic iron deficiency pattern — low ferritin (< 30), elevated TIBC (> 400), low transferrin saturation (< 20%), low serum iron — represents depleted reserves and impaired hemoglobin synthesis; ferritin below 30 ng/mL correlates with fatigue and cognitive symptoms even in the absence of frank anemia. HYPOTHESIS: A significant proportion of women reporting chronic fatigue with hemoglobin in the low-normal range have iron-deficiency-without-anemia identifiable only through ferritin; the anemia cutoff is too late a threshold to catch clinically meaningful depletion. Hemochromatosis pattern (high ferritin, high serum iron, low TIBC, elevated transferrin saturation > 45%) requires HFE gene testing and hepatic assessment.

Iron deficiency encodes the biology of a system giving from reserves it no longer has — the oxygen-carrier is depleted, mirroring a relational pattern of chronic over-giving without adequate replenishment. The question is not only what is missing but what is bleeding.

## Magnesium (RBC Intracellular)

**Operation:** Transduction | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Magnesium concentration within erythrocytes, reflecting intracellular magnesium stores across the 90–120 day RBC lifespan — a stable, clinically accurate measure of total-body magnesium status that serum magnesium cannot provide.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient | < 4.2 mg/dL | — |
| Low-normal | 4.2–5.4 mg/dL | — |
| Optimal | 4.2–6.8 mg/dL | 5.5–6.8 mg/dL |
| Excessive | > 6.8 mg/dL | Reduce supplementation |

### Body Reading
FACT: Magnesium is the obligate cofactor for ATP synthase; without adequate intracellular magnesium, ATP cannot be produced or utilized, and over 300 enzymatic reactions requiring Mg²⁺-ATP are impaired simultaneously. INTERPRETATION: Low RBC magnesium in the presence of normal serum magnesium is the defining pattern of magnesium depletion — the body sacrifices intracellular stores to maintain the tightly regulated serum level, making serum magnesium a lagging and unreliable marker until stores are critically depleted. HYPOTHESIS: The extraordinary prevalence of magnesium depletion (estimated 60–80% of populations on processed food diets) means that low RBC magnesium is likely a contributing substrate to chronic conditions attributed to other etiologies — anxiety, insomnia, hypertension, constipation, and migraine — all of which have mechanistic magnesium explanations. Excess RBC magnesium (> 6.8 mg/dL) signals over-supplementation or renal impairment.

## Zinc (Serum or RBC)

**Operation:** Transduction | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Circulating or intracellular zinc, a trace mineral serving as catalytic cofactor for over 300 enzymes including those governing testosterone synthesis, immune activation, wound healing, and gut barrier integrity.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient (serum) | < 60 mcg/dL | — |
| Low-normal | 60–74 mcg/dL | — |
| Normal | 60–120 mcg/dL | 80–120 mcg/dL |
| Elevated | > 120 mcg/dL | Assess copper ratio |

### Body Reading
FACT: Zinc is the cofactor for 5-alpha-reductase, aromatase regulation, thymulin (T-cell maturation hormone), and delta-6-desaturase (essential fatty acid conversion); deficiency simultaneously impairs reproductive hormone synthesis, immune competence, and fatty acid metabolism. INTERPRETATION: Serum zinc is a poor marker of total-body zinc status — as with magnesium, the body maintains serum levels at the expense of intracellular stores; RBC zinc is more accurate, and alkaline phosphatase (a zinc-dependent enzyme) falling below 70 IU/L is a functional readout of zinc insufficiency independent of serum levels. HYPOTHESIS: The copper:zinc ratio is more diagnostically informative than absolute zinc — a ratio above 1:8 (copper exceeding roughly 1/8th of zinc) shifts the redox environment toward pro-oxidant copper dominance, generating anxiety, OCD-spectrum symptoms, and autoimmune amplification even when neither mineral falls outside conventional reference range. Excess zinc supplementation (> 50 mg/day sustained) competes with copper absorption and can induce iatrogenic copper deficiency.

## Selenium (Serum or Whole Blood)

**Operation:** Transduction | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Circulating selenium, the trace mineral substrate for glutathione peroxidase (antioxidant defense), thioredoxin reductase (redox regulation), and iodothyronine deiodinase (T4→T3 thyroid hormone activation).

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient | < 70 mcg/L | — |
| Low-normal | 70–90 mcg/L | — |
| Normal | 70–150 mcg/L | 110–150 mcg/L |
| Toxic | > 400 mcg/L | Avoid |

### Body Reading
FACT: Selenium is the obligate cofactor for the type-I and type-II iodothyronine deiodinases that convert thyroxine (T4) to the metabolically active triiodothyronine (T3); without adequate selenium, T3 synthesis is impaired even when TSH, T4, and iodine status appear adequate. INTERPRETATION: The combination of low-normal selenium + elevated TPO antibodies + low free T3 despite normal TSH is a clinically common pattern indicating selenium-dependent failure at two nodes simultaneously — antioxidant protection of thyroid tissue and T4 activation; selenomethionine supplementation addresses both. HYPOTHESIS: Geographic selenium depletion (soil selenium varies by orders of magnitude across regions, and many populations eat food grown on low-selenium soil) is a significant and under-measured contributor to subclinical hypothyroidism and autoimmune thyroiditis at the population level; this hypothesis is supported by ecological studies correlating regional selenium soil levels with Hashimoto's disease prevalence. Selenium toxicity (selenosis) from supplementation is possible above 400 mcg/day; Brazil nuts contain highly variable selenium (10–90 mcg per nut) and are an unreliable supplementation strategy.

## Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxal-5-Phosphate)

**Operation:** Transduction | **Body Codx**

### What It Measures
Pyridoxal-5-phosphate (PLP), the bioactive coenzyme form of vitamin B6, required for transamination, neurotransmitter synthesis, heme production, and glycogen metabolism — measured directly as the active cofactor rather than total B6.

### Reference Ranges

| Metric | Conventional | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Deficient | < 5 ng/mL | — |
| Low-normal | 5–10 ng/mL | — |
| Normal | 5–50 ng/mL | 20–50 ng/mL |
| Elevated (toxicity risk) | > 100 ng/mL | Reduce dose |

### Body Reading
FACT: PLP is the obligate cofactor for DOPA decarboxylase (L-DOPA → dopamine), aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (5-HTP → serotonin), and GABA-T (GABA metabolism); a single B6 deficiency simultaneously impairs synthesis of dopamine, serotonin, and GABA — the three primary neuroregulatory transmitters. INTERPRETATION: The clinical pattern of B6 insufficiency — depression, anxiety, poor stress tolerance, insomnia, peripheral neuropathy, and PMS — is mechanistically coherent; all map onto impaired monoamine and GABA synthesis, and this pattern is frequently misread as a primary psychiatric or hormonal presentation without B6 assessment. HYPOTHESIS: Pyridoxine (the non-phosphorylated supplemental form) at high doses (> 200 mg/day) can paradoxically cause peripheral neuropathy by competing with PLP at active sites; supplementation with P5P (pyridoxal-5-phosphate) is both more bioavailable and avoids this paradoxical toxicity risk, particularly in patients with impaired phosphorylation capacity. Co-administration with magnesium enhances PLP-dependent enzymatic activity; B6 and magnesium are co-therapeutic targets for anxiety and PMS.
