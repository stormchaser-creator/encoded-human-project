### Mechanical Scale: Elastic Energy Storage

The entry on spring-like muscle elasticity for running economy (WS2-PA) describes how musculoskeletal systems — particularly tendons and fascia — store elastic energy during loading and return it during the recoil phase of locomotion. This is textbook mechanical hysteresis: the force-displacement curve traced during loading differs from the curve traced during unloading. The *area enclosed by the hysteresis loop* represents energy dissipated as heat; the *returned energy* is what confers the efficiency advantage in trained runners. Critically, this property is HISTORY-DEPENDENT: a tendon's response to a given force depends on whether it is being loaded or unloaded, i.e., on the immediately prior state of the system.

More profoundly, the *shape* of the hysteresis loop itself is modified by training history. A trained runner's tendons exhibit different viscoelastic properties than an untrained person's — the accumulated history of loading has physically altered the tissue's mechanical memory. This is hysteresis at the structural level: the organism encodes its training history as altered tissue properties.

Sleep appears to be the mechanism by which the system manages its hysteresis landscape — selectively consolidating some loops (memory), dissolving others (forgetting), and restoring the dynamic range needed to form new ones. Without sleep, the system's hysteresis loops saturate and the system loses encoding capacity.

### Cellular Scale: Protein Aggregation and Aging

The entry on protein aggregation (WS3-RP) describes how accumulation of misfolded proteins is associated with aging. This is perhaps the most extreme form of biological hysteresis: *irreversible* path-dependency. Unlike elastic hysteresis (which returns energy) or synaptic hysteresis (which can be reset by sleep), protein aggregation represents a one-way ratchet. The cell encodes the history of its oxidative damage, proteotoxic stress, and metabolic errors in the form of insoluble protein aggregates that resist clearance. The system cannot traverse its history in reverse — the return path through the hysteresis loop is closed.

This framing reconceives aging as the progressive narrowing and eventual closure of the organism's hysteresis loops — the reduction of its ability to return to prior, more functional states. The gradual loss of cellular resilience is not merely the accumulation of damage but the loss of the system's capacity to *exit the damaged attractor state*. Interventions like autophagy induction (fasting, exercise) represent attempts to reopen these loops by clearing aggregates — literally attempting to restore hysteresis capacity at the cellular level.
