## 3.1 The Psychedelic Paradox

Classical psychedelics — psilocybin, LSD, DMT — produce a measurable increase in the repertoire of brain connectivity states while simultaneously generating experiences reported as maximally meaningful, coherent, and unified. This inversion of the conventional relationship between neural order and experiential coherence is not a curiosity; it is a structural datum that any adequate theory of consciousness must confront.

The empirical foundation is real but contested. A preprint replication critique (medRxiv 2023.07.03; not yet peer-reviewed at time of writing) finds that seven of twelve previously reported brain entropy metrics fail to replicate directionally [695816, 695817]. This is a serious methodological challenge and should be stated plainly. However, the critique does not dissolve the paradox — it sharpens it. Two measures show consistent directional effects across studies: Shannon entropy of path-length distributions and dynamic conditional correlation (DCC) entropy [695824, 695825]. FACT. The replication failure is metric-specific, not phenomenon-specific.

Crucially, "entropy" here does not refer to any single fMRI index. It refers to the expanded geometric repertoire of neural connectivity — the increased dimensionality of brain-state trajectories through phase space, sometimes called neural geometrodynamics. INTERPRETATION: this structural expansion, not its particular measurement, is what the paradox names. The brain enters configurations of higher-dimensional disorder while the subject reports lower phenomenological disorder. These observations pull in opposite directions, and that tension is the data point.

HYPOTHESIS: conscious coherence is not produced by restricting the neural state space but by navigating an expanded one. If correct, the psychedelic case is not an anomaly — it is a window.

## 3.2 The Coherence Surge at Near-Silence

The paradox holds across independent datasets: the moment the brain approaches electrocerebral silence is also, in multiple preparations, the moment its remaining activity becomes most organized.

The foundational datum is Borjigin et al. (2013), who recorded — continuously and in real time — a transient surge of synchronous gamma oscillations within the first 30 seconds following induced cardiac arrest in rats, occurring immediately prior to isoelectric EEG [PMID 23940340]. These oscillations were global, highly coherent, and marked by a striking increase in anterior-posterior-directed connectivity and tight phase-coupling to both theta and alpha waves — features that collectively exceed the coherence signatures of normal waking activity. This is **Tier 1 FACT**: a continuously recorded waveform, not retrospective report. The signal is independent of subject recall. It is not an NDE. It is a transient pattern of electromagnetic organization at the threshold of isoelectric silence. A subsequent asphyxial cardiac arrest model in rats (PMID 28398813) extended this picture: ECoG progressed through four distinct stages before electrocerebral silence (AS4), with a burst of frontal coherence and a posterior shift in ECoG power at the stage of near-electrocerebral silence (AS3), described by the authors as a potential neural correlate of conscious processing.

Human replication exists but carries real methodological constraints. Chawla et al. documented a terminal gamma spike at near-flatline in human cardiac arrest survivors. The 2024 high-density NDE-EEG study (PMID 39067553) — 22 volunteers monitored during syncope-induced unresponsiveness — identified elevated delta, theta, and beta2 band activity concentrated in temporal and frontal regions, with higher signal complexity, greater local network efficiency (segregation), and greater global network efficiency (integration) in participants who met threshold criteria for NDE-like experience (Greyson NDE Scale ≥7/32) compared to those who did not [PMID 39067553]. These findings directionally replicate the peri-arrest coherence pattern in a controlled human model. The constraints are real and must be stated: rat cortical architecture differs substantially from human; peri-arrest EEG in clinical settings is routinely contaminated by CPR-associated muscular artifact; cortical electrical activity during CPR may be absent or severely disturbed regardless [PMID 40627517]. The human replication is directionally consistent, not mechanistically confirmed.

The phenomenological NDE literature constitutes a categorically separate evidence stream. Approximately 10–20% of cardiac arrest survivors report cognitive experiences from the arrest period [PMID 17416449], with prospective cardiac arrest studies consistently converging on the lower bound of this range (~10%) [PMID 11801343; PMID 39096700]. This literature is subject to selection bias, survivorship bias, and recall distortion — all operative and none trivial. Critically, as the 2024 narrative review notes, no reports of subjective experience have been directly associated with the cortical electrical activations recorded in dying patients; the electrophysiological signal data and the phenomenological report data have not been co-registered in a single subject [PMID 40627517]. **The gamma surge does not validate NDE content.** The two datasets require independent epistemic handling.

The 2024 critique (PMID 39238834) disputes a direct mechanistic link between gamma-band activity and NDE phenomenology, raising concerns about myogenic artifact contamination of perimortem GBA and the absence of evidence that GBA underlies the phenomenological substrate of NDEs. The critique is well-grounded. The electrophysiological surge is **Tier 1 FACT**. Its interpretation as a correlate of conscious experience is a **Tier 2 hypothesis** — plausible, consistent with the data, but not established. Its invocation as evidence for non-local or post-mortem consciousness is **Tier 3 speculation**. The waveform is real. What it indexes — in terms of neural computation, phenomenal experience, or anything beyond — remains an open and properly contested empirical question.

## 3.3 The Cardiac-Neural Sequence

The conventional model positions the heart as a downstream effector — a pump regulated by autonomic commands issued from brainstem and cortex. The empirical record challenges this hierarchy in specific, measurable ways.

Each cardiac contraction generates a cortical response detectable via simultaneous EEG/ECG recording: the heartbeat-evoked potential (HEP). These potentials are not passive noise. Late HEPs — those occurring approximately 300–500 ms post-R-wave — predict neurological survival after cardiac arrest [PMID 29447999]. **FACT (Tier 1).** If HEPs were epiphenomenal artifacts of cardiovascular-neural coupling, they would carry no predictive signal for outcome. Their survival-prediction utility implies functional relevance — the cardiac-neural sequence is doing something consequential, not merely correlating with it. **INTERPRETATION (Tier 1).**

The causal architecture becomes sharper at the perceptual level. Cardiac phase at stimulus presentation — specifically, systole versus diastole — determines whether a near-threshold visual stimulus reaches conscious awareness [PMID 39356552]. This is cardiac afferent facilitation of perceptual dominance: the heart is not receiving the percept; it is partially gating it. **FACT (Tier 1).** HRV further modulates interoceptive accuracy through the prefrontal-limbic axis [PMID 33536870], suggesting that cardiac signal variability shapes the precision-weighting of incoming interoceptive predictions. **INTERPRETATION (Tier 1).**

The correlation-versus-causation objection must be acknowledged directly: HEP studies are correlational. Controlled perturbation designs — pacing cardiac rhythm at specified frequencies and measuring downstream perceptual changes — remain sparse and methodologically constrained. **This is a genuine limitation.** Partial causal support comes from pacing evidence and from HEP predictive validity, but it remains partial.

The precise claim here is deliberately bounded: cardiac afference *participates in constituting* the conscious moment within specific attention windows. This is not the claim that the heart generates consciousness unilaterally. **HYPOTHESIS (Tier 3).** The signal is bidirectional; the priority, in circumscribed temporal windows, appears cardiac-first. That asymmetry warrants the paradox name it deserves: upstream heart.

## 3.4 The Paradoxical Lucidity

The paradox is structural: a patient with advanced Alzheimer's disease — documented cortical atrophy, dense amyloid burden, years of near-total communicative absence — abruptly reconstitutes coherent, relational selfhood for minutes to hours. She recognizes family members by name, expresses gratitude, completes unfinished relational business, and then recedes. The substrate has not changed. The expression has.

This phenomenon is formally termed *paradoxical lucidity* (Mashour et al., 2019, PMID 31229433). A critical terminological distinction must hold from the outset: paradoxical lucidity is not synonymous with *terminal* lucidity. The 2024 scoping review of 30 sources (PMC10977389) establishes that these episodes occur across all stages of Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD), not exclusively in the pre-death window. This is not a semantic refinement — it is mechanistically determinative. If lucidity can emerge months before death, then dying-brain disinhibition cannot function as the sole explanatory framework. The pre-death subset is real; the phenomenon is larger than that subset.

The reductionist account deserves direct engagement rather than dismissal: paradoxical lucidity may reflect transient network disinhibition, inflammatory fluctuation, or metabolic shift sufficient to temporarily restore synaptic signaling in partially preserved circuits. **[HYPOTHESIS — Tier 2]** This paper does not dismiss that mechanism. It presses the mechanism one step further: why does transient network disinhibition in a severely damaged cortex produce *coherent relational selfhood* rather than confusion, confabulation, or noise? Damaged networks, transiently unblocked, have no known substrate-level reason to generate directed recognition — the naming of a daughter, the expression of a specific regret. That they do generates a prediction gap. Current substrate-centric models of consciousness offer no prospective account of when, in whom, or with what phenomenological content these episodes will occur (PMC10977389, 2024). The gap itself constitutes a scientific problem, independent of how one resolves it philosophically.

Epistemic tier discipline is non-negotiable here. The phenomenological record — caregiver reports, retrospective chart review, clinical case series — is **Tier 2: INTERPRETATION**. It is clinically credible and systematically reviewed, but it has not yet been instrumented. Prospective, concurrent EEG studies capturing cortical activity *during* paradoxical lucidity episodes do not exist at scale; the existing neurophysiology literature addresses the dying brain (PMID 28398813) and near-death-like states in syncope (PMID 39067553) but not paradoxical lucidity in ADRD directly. This is a research lacuna, not a theoretical objection. The substrate-dissociability hypothesis — that the signal and its structural substrate are partially separable — is **Tier 3: SPECULATION**, stated as such, and represents this paper's most provisional claim. What is not speculative is the explanatory gap. The gap is an empirical fact. How it is closed is a scientific and, ultimately, philosophical question that this paper declines to foreclose.
