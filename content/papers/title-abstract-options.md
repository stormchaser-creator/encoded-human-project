# Title and Abstract Options for v7

Grok's review flagged that "Ancient Phenomenology as Neuroscience Prediction Engine" oversells what the paper demonstrates. The body is careful about retrospective vs. prospective, but the title leads with the stronger claim. Here are three options ranked by modesty:

---

## Option A — Most Modest (Safest for peer review)

**Title:** A Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument for Evaluating Cross-Domain Correspondence Between Classical Phenomenology and Contemporary Neurobiology: Development and Preliminary Validation

**Abstract:** We develop and validate a four-criterion isomorphism instrument — functional parallel (C1), independent derivation (C2), non-trivial predictive constraint (C3), and asymmetric disconfirmation (C4) — for evaluating whether structured phenomenological systems generate claims that correspond to independently derived neurobiological constructs with sufficient specificity to constitute genuine isomorphism rather than post-hoc analogy. The instrument enforces directionality by requiring neutral cognitive descriptions to be extracted from phenomenological texts prior to any consultation of the neurobiological reference corpus. In a preliminary three-corpus pilot study, 33 claim units extracted from Patanjali's Yoga Sutras (I.17–I.51) were scored alongside two negative control corpora: Charaka Samhita (n=33) and Hero of Alexandria's Pneumatica (n=30). Tier A classification (all four criteria met) was achieved by 30.3% of Patanjali units versus 0% for both controls (Fisher's exact, p<0.001). The primary selectivity mechanism operates at Criterion 1 — domain boundary with consciousness-specific neurobiology — rather than at Criterion 2 (independent derivation), demonstrating that the instrument discriminates between consciousness-directed phenomenological systems and biologically coherent but domain-mismatched alternatives. A secondary analysis relaxing the domain constraint at C1 produced dramatically different results (Charaka survival rising from 0% to near-complete), confirming that reference corpus specificity is load-bearing for discriminant validity. Ten surviving Tier A units are presented with explicit retrospective/prospective designations. The pratyahara domain is identified as the clearest candidate for prospective experimental paradigm development. Limitations include automated scoring without human inter-rater validation, absence of a close phenomenological control (Abhidhamma texts), and restriction to a single textual section.

## Option B — Middle Ground (Recommended)

**Title:** Cross-Domain Isomorphism Between Patanjali's Yoga Sutras and Contemporary Neurobiology: A Four-Criterion Instrument and Preliminary Pilot Validation

**Abstract:** Classical phenomenological systems developed through sustained introspective practice may encode regularities about mental state-space that retain correspondence with independently derived neurobiological constructs. We develop a four-criterion isomorphism instrument — functional parallel (C1), independent derivation (C2), non-trivial predictive constraint (C3), and asymmetric disconfirmation (C4) — and apply it to 33 claim units extracted from Patanjali's Yoga Sutras (I.17–I.51), with two negative control corpora: Charaka Samhita (n=33) and Hero of Alexandria's Pneumatica (n=30). The instrument enforces directionality by requiring neutral cognitive descriptions prior to neurobiological consultation. Tier A classification (all four criteria met) was achieved by 30.3% of Patanjali units versus 0% for both controls (Fisher's exact, p<0.001). The selectivity mechanism operates at Criterion 1 — domain boundary with consciousness-specific neurobiology — rather than at independent derivation, demonstrating principled discrimination between consciousness-directed phenomenological systems and domain-mismatched alternatives. Qualitative mappings across five domains (attentional hierarchy, default mode dynamics, interoceptive processing, state-dependent memory, cognitive effort) are presented with explicit retrospective/prospective designations. The pratyahara domain is identified as the clearest opportunity for prospective paradigm development where phenomenological description outpaces available neuroscientific measurement tools. Ten Tier A pairs generate specific, falsifiable experimental predictions. The methodological contribution is the directional procedure itself and the demonstration that reference corpus specificity is load-bearing for discriminant validity.

## Recommendation

**Option B** is the sweet spot. It names Patanjali (which is what makes the paper interesting), names the instrument (which is the methodological contribution), and says "preliminary pilot validation" (which is honest). Option A is safest but buries the lede. Option C is engaging but question-form titles can read as clickbait in methods journals.

The current title — "Ancient Phenomenology as Neuroscience Prediction Engine" — treats as established what the paper is actually testing. Any of these three fixes that mismatch.
