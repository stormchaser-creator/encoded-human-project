# Citation Format as Cognitive Filter: How Bibliographic Structure Shapes Knowledge Network Topology and Reading Cognition Across Disciplines

## Abstract

This document develops three competing hypotheses about the relationship between citation formatting conventions (APA author-date vs. Vancouver numeric) and two empirical targets: (1) the topology of citation networks as measured by in-degree power-law distributions, and (2) the cognitive processing demands imposed on readers at citation encounter points as measured by eye-tracking. The evidence base available to Pearl does not directly address these questions, but adjacent findings — on auditory conduction and cognitive function, intergenerational transmission deficits, cognitive ethology, and network formation — provide structural scaffolding for principled hypothesis generation. All hypotheses are rated low-to-medium confidence and require empirical validation through the proposed two-pronged study design.

## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A (Tier 1 — Conservative): Format-Correlated Power-Law Exponent Variation

**Claim:** Citation in-degree distributions across fields follow power laws with field-specific exponents. Fields where author-date formats (APA, Chicago) are dominant will exhibit steeper power-law tails — indicating stronger preferential attachment dynamics — compared to fields where numeric formats (Vancouver, IEEE) dominate, because name-visible formats reinforce name-recognition priming that drives preferential attachment at the micro-level.

**Mechanism:** Preferential attachment in citation networks is driven by recognition and recall. When a reader encounters '(Smith & Jones, 2018)' inline, Smith and Jones are activated in the reader's cognitive network. If the reader subsequently writes a paper on a related topic, Smith and Jones are more likely to enter their citation consideration set. This creates a micro-level reinforcement loop that, aggregated across millions of reading events, steepens the power-law tail. Vancouver's '[14]' activates no such name-specific priming.

**Analytical Lenses:** Network theory (preferential attachment, hub structure), Information theory (name-as-signal vs. number-as-symbol), Complexity emergence (macro-topology from micro-reading behavior).

**Key Confound:** Field structure (biomedical vs. psychology) is massively confounded with format. Biomedical literature has higher average citation counts, larger author lists, faster publication rates, and more clinical concentration of impact — all of which independently drive network topology. This confound can only be addressed through within-field variation or natural experiments (journal format switches).

### Hypothesis B (Tier 2 — Integrative): Format as Cognitive Transmission Filter

**Claim:** APA and Vancouver formats create measurably different cognitive processing demands at citation encounter points. Specifically, numeric tokens (Vancouver) require a lookup-interrupt operation — the reader must suspend semantic processing, encode the number, and defer reference resolution to the bibliography — whereas author-date tokens (APA) allow partial in-line resolution for familiar sources. This predicts higher fixation duration and regression counts for Vancouver in novice readers, with expert readers showing partial buffering through pre-consolidated numeric mapping.

**Mechanism:** The signal processing lens frames citation format as a filter. APA passes author-identity signal inline, reducing the information gap at the encounter point. Vancouver filters out author identity, creating an information gap that must be resolved through a separate lookup process. The control theory lens identifies this as a gain-modulation effect: expertise raises the baseline gain (pre-learned numeric mappings), reducing the perturbation at each citation encounter.

**Analytical Lenses:** Signal processing (filter characteristics), Control theory (gain, setpoint, expertise as regulatory buffer), Coupled oscillators (reading rhythm disruption and re-entrainment after citation tokens).

**Key Prediction:** The expertise × format interaction is the theoretically crucial finding. Main effects of format alone would be less informative because expert biomedical readers may show no disadvantage with Vancouver in their native field — the effect would only appear when format and expertise are mismatched (experts in an unfamiliar-format condition, novices in either condition).

### Hypothesis C (Tier 3 — Radical): Format as Phase Transition Boundary in Knowledge Diversity

**Claim:** Numeric citation formats function as entropy-reducing structures in citation networks, systematically suppressing the long tail of mid-tier author citations and thereby reducing the effective diversity of the knowledge base referenced within a field. This constitutes a phase transition in knowledge topology: disciplines that adopt numeric formats over time trend toward higher citation Gini coefficients (more unequal distributions), narrowing the effective epistemic commons available to researchers.

**Mechanism:** Drawing directly from the rickets fractal — 'structural exclusion from conditions of illumination' — numeric format creates a structural condition where authors who are not already famous enough to be recognized from a number alone are systematically disadvantaged in the citation economy. Their work may be read, but if the number '[47]' does not activate any name-schema in the reader, the probability of subsequent citation falls. Over decades and millions of papers, this creates measurable concentration.

**Analytical Lenses:** Entropy (citation diversity as Shannon entropy across the degree distribution), Phase transitions (format adoption as a bifurcation point), Fractals (transmission poverty replicating across scales from individual reading event to field-level citation structure), Topology morphogenesis (format as a gradient field shaping the citation topology).

**Key Test:** Compare citation Gini coefficients between APA-dominant and Vancouver-dominant fields AFTER controlling for field age, size, funding concentration, and clinical relevance. Alternatively, examine longitudinal Gini trends in fields that have undergone format transitions.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A
The strongest objection is that biomedicine (Vancouver) simply IS more hierarchically organized than psychology (APA) for reasons entirely independent of format: clinical translation pressures, funding concentration, journal prestige stratification, and the dominance of mega-trials. The power-law exponent difference might exist but would be entirely explained by these structural factors. The proposed OpenAlex analysis, unless it can identify within-field format variation or natural experiments, cannot distinguish format effects from field effects.

The strongest support is that the mechanism (name-recognition priming → citation preference) is grounded in well-established cognitive psychology of memory and recognition, and the prediction is precise and quantitative.

### Against Hypothesis B
The experimental design faces an ecological validity threat: readers in Vancouver-dominant fields have been trained over years to ignore numeric citation tokens during reading, treating them as visual noise rather than meaning-bearing symbols. The 'cost' of numeric format may have been so thoroughly eliminated by expert training that it only appears in artificial within-subjects comparisons. Furthermore, 8 matched excerpts may be insufficient to detect format × expertise interactions with n=40.

The strongest support is that regression counts are a particularly sensitive measure: even experienced readers make involuntary regressions when processing is disrupted. If numeric tokens cause even brief processing disruption, regressions should capture it even when the reader reports no subjective difficulty.

### Against Hypothesis C
The analogy to vitamin D deficiency and intergenerational transmission poverty, while structurally compelling, may be doing more rhetorical than scientific work. Citation format is a social convention chosen by journals and authors — it does not operate on researchers the way a biological deficiency operates on an infant. Researchers actively retrieve full reference information when deciding what to cite; the citation decision is deliberate, not automatic. The entropy-reduction claim depends on reading-time priming affecting citation selection, which is several inferential steps from established evidence.

The strongest support is that the prediction is quantitatively specific (Gini coefficient change following format transitions) and could be tested with existing OpenAlex data if format transition records are available.

## Synthesis

The three hypotheses, taken together, generate a coherent research program rather than competing claims. Hypothesis A addresses macro-level topology and is best tested computationally with OpenAlex. Hypothesis B addresses micro-level cognition and is best tested with the eye-tracking experiment. Hypothesis C generates an exploratory prediction (citation diversity × format) that can be embedded within the computational study as an additional analysis at minimal marginal cost.

The evolved synthesis: **citation format is plausibly a cognitive filter with measurable but modest effects, and these effects are likely largest at the reading-event level (fixations, regressions) and smallest at the network-topology level (where field-structural confounds dominate).** The practical significance is not zero: if format genuinely affects reading fluency, this has implications for journal policy, reader accessibility, and possibly for the cognitive accessibility of scientific literature to non-specialist readers.

The most important methodological insight from this analysis: **the proposed two-pronged study would benefit from a third element — identifying journals that switched citation formats and analyzing longitudinal citation patterns in those journals.** This natural experiment would allow causal inference that neither the cross-sectional computational study nor the laboratory eye-tracking study can provide.

## Implications

**For journal policy:** If Hypothesis B is confirmed (novice readers show measurably higher cognitive load with numeric formats), this has direct implications for scientific literacy and the accessibility of literature to interdisciplinary readers. Fields considering format standardization should treat reading cognitive load as a policy-relevant variable.

**For pre-registration design:** The computational study should pre-register both the power-law fitting analysis AND the citation diversity/Gini coefficient analysis (Hypothesis C), treating the latter as exploratory. The eye-tracking study should pre-register the expertise × format interaction as the primary analysis, not the main effect of format.

**For the soul/spirit gap:** The two missing densities in Pearl's knowledge base point to something real in this domain: the question of whether citation format affects the *meaning-transmission* quality of scientific literature — not just its efficiency but its capacity to convey the intellectual genealogy of ideas — is genuinely a question about what it means for knowledge to be conducted faithfully across minds. This is not merely technical; it touches on whether the bibliographic apparatus of science serves as a transparent medium or an opaque filter.

## Open Questions

1. Does OpenAlex contain journal-level metadata sufficient to classify citation format dominance, or does this require manual coding of a sample?
2. What natural experiments exist? Have any major journals (e.g., NEJM, Nature Medicine) changed from numbered to author-date formats or vice versa?
3. Is the expertise × format interaction specific to citation tokens, or does it reflect a more general expertise-related reduction in all processing costs in familiar text?
4. Do digital hyperlinked citations (increasingly common in online-first publishing) constitute a third format condition that may be rendering the APA/Vancouver distinction obsolete for online readers?
5. What is the effect of author list length on name-recognition priming? In high-energy physics (numbered formats, author lists of hundreds), does the format distinction even matter given that no individual author name could realistically be retained?
6. Could the proposed study be extended to examine whether citation format predicts self-citation rates? If authors are more likely to cite work they encountered in their native format, this would constitute a measurable behavioral signature of format-dependent priming.
