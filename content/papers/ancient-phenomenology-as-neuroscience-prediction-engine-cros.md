## Methods

### Overview of Analytic Approach

This analysis employs a structured cross-domain mapping procedure designed to evaluate whether categorical distinctions in Patanjali's *Yoga Sutras* correspond to independently derived neurobiological constructs with sufficient specificity to constitute genuine isomorphism rather than post-hoc analogy. The procedure involves three sequential phases: (1) systematic extraction and formalization of Patanjali's phenomenological taxonomy, (2) identification of candidate neurobiological constructs using pre-specified correspondence criteria, and (3) evaluation of predictive adequacy against the existing empirical literature. Each phase is described below.

### Phase 1: Formalization of Patanjali's Taxonomy

The primary source text was the *Yoga Sutras* of Patanjali, analyzed in Sanskrit with reference to established English translations and commentarial traditions . Rather than treating the text as a unified philosophical position, we approached it as a structured descriptive instrument—analogous in function to a diagnostic taxonomy—and extracted categorical distinctions that met three criteria: (a) the distinction is explicitly marked by terminological differentiation in the source text, (b) the distinction implies a difference in functional or phenomenological process rather than merely degree of proficiency, and (c) the distinction is reproducible across independent commentarial interpretations .

**FACT** — Patanjali employs a layered taxonomic structure that distinguishes among *vrittis* (mental fluctuations classified into five types: *pramana*, *viparyaya*, *vikalpa*, *nidra*, and *smriti*), progressive stages of *samapatti* (coalescence of cognition with its object), and at least four phenomenologically differentiated stages of *samadhi*, separated by the presence or absence of *vitarka* (discursive reasoning), *vichara* (subtle reflection), *ananda* (affective tone), and *asmita* (self-referential processing) . These distinctions were extracted, tabulated, and assigned provisional functional descriptions in neutral cognitive vocabulary—for example, *savitarka samadhi* was characterized as "object-directed cognition with concurrent verbal-conceptual elaboration," and *nirvitarka samadhi* as "object-directed cognition with suppression of verbal-conceptual elaboration." This translation step was performed prior to and independently of any review of the neuroimaging or electrophysiological literature, to preserve the directionality of prediction.

**INTERPRETATION** — The use of neutral cognitive vocabulary at this stage is methodologically consequential. It prevents the translator from unconsciously selecting neurobiological terms that are already known to correspond, which would undermine the claim that correspondence was discovered rather than constructed.

Five primary domains of Patanjali's taxonomy were identified as sufficiently specified for cross-domain comparison: attentional hierarchy (the graded relationship between *dharana*, *dhyana*, and *samadhi*), self-referential processing (the role of *asmita* across samadic stages), interoceptive sensitivity (the function of *pratyahara* in modifying sensory processing), memory and state-dependency (the concept of *samskara* and *vasana* as latent dispositional structures), and effort and effortlessness as qualitatively distinct cognitive modes (the distinction between *sa-bija* and *nir-bija* states).

### Phase 2: Identification of Neurobiological Candidate Constructs

For each of the five domains identified in Phase 1, a targeted literature review was conducted using PubMed and Google Scholar databases with search terms derived from the neutral cognitive vocabulary established in Phase 1 . Neurobiological constructs were considered candidate matches if they satisfied two conditions: (a) they were established in the empirical literature through paradigms independent of meditation research—meaning the construct was not defined by reference to contemplative practice—and (b) the functional description of the construct overlapped with the neutral cognitive description of the Patanjali category across at least two independent operational dimensions.

**HYPOTHESIS** — The independence criterion is the most important methodological safeguard. Cross-domain isomorphism is informative only when the two domains arrived at their distinctions through separate routes. Constructs developed specifically to characterize meditation data do not satisfy this condition and were excluded from the primary mapping analysis, though they appear in supplementary discussion.

Candidate neurobiological constructs identified through this process included: gamma-band synchrony and theta oscillatory activity as markers of focused versus open attentional modes ; default mode network activation and suppression as markers of self-referential versus task-directed processing ; interoceptive cortical hierarchies involving the insular and anterior cingulate cortices ; synaptic consolidation and state-dependent retrieval as neural analogs to dispositional memory structures ; and the distinction between effortful, top-down attentional control and automatized processing as indexed by prefrontal recruitment patterns .

### Phase 3: Evaluation of Predictive Adequacy

Each proposed mapping was evaluated against three graded criteria. **FACT** — A mapping was classified as *strong* if the Patanjali category and the neurobiological construct shared functional description on at least three independent dimensions and if the neurobiological finding was replicated across at least two independent research groups using different methodologies. A mapping was classified as *moderate* if two dimensions of correspondence were present with at least single-study support. A mapping was classified as *weak* if correspondence was present on only one functional dimension or rested on a single unreplicated study. Mappings failing these thresholds were classified as *speculative* and are reported separately.

**INTERPRETATION** — This graded classification scheme is not designed to validate Patanjali's system as a whole. Individual mappings vary in evidential support, and this variation is itself informative: it identifies which aspects of the phenomenological taxonomy are most tractable to empirical evaluation and which require experimental paradigms not yet available in the literature. The asymmetry of evidence across domains is reported transparently in the Results section and interpreted as a feature of the analysis rather than a limitation to be minimized.

No new data were collected for this study. All neurobiological evidence is drawn from published peer-reviewed literature. Statistical claims from cited studies are reported as originally published and have not been re-analyzed.

### Domain 1: Attentional Hierarchy (*Dharana*, *Dhyana*, *Samadhi*)

**FACT** — Patanjali's graded attentional sequence—from *dharana* (sustained object-directed attention with effortful maintenance), through *dhyana* (uninterrupted object-directed attention with reduced maintenance cost), to *samadhi* (object-directed absorption with dissolution of subject-object duality)—was mapped to the neurobiological distinction between effortful top-down attentional control and automatized processing. The neutral cognitive description of *dharana* (periodic re-engagement of attentional focus following mind-wandering) corresponds functionally to prefrontal-mediated top-down attention, which is associated with sustained lateral prefrontal and anterior cingulate recruitment . The description of *dhyana* (continuous object engagement without periodic re-engagement effort) corresponds to the reduction in prefrontal recruitment observed as attentional tasks become automatized through practice .

**FACT** — Independent electrophysiological research has characterized focused attention meditation as associated with increased anterior theta power and gamma-band synchrony in task-relevant regions, with the ratio of these oscillatory signatures shifting across practice-level groups . This mapping was classified as **strong**: the functional correspondence spans at least three dimensions (effort recruitment, oscillatory signature, and continuity of object-engagement), and replication across independent groups using both EEG and fMRI methodologies is documented in the literature .

### Domain 2: Self-Referential Processing and *Asmita*

**FACT** — Patanjali explicitly marks the presence or absence of *asmita* (self-referential processing, literally "I-am-ness") as a categorical boundary between *sa-asmita samadhi* and more advanced samadic stages in which self-referential elaboration is absent. The neutral cognitive description of this distinction—object-directed cognition with concurrent self-referential processing versus object-directed cognition with suppression of self-referential processing—maps directly onto the neurobiological distinction between conditions of default mode network (DMN) engagement and suppression.

**FACT** — The DMN, anchored by medial prefrontal cortex and posterior cingulate cortex, is consistently associated with self-referential thought, autobiographical elaboration, and mind-wandering . Suppression of DMN activity during task engagement is a replicated finding across laboratories . Meditation-specific research has documented that experienced practitioners show attenuated DMN activation during absorption states relative to novices , but the independence criterion established in Methods requires that the neurobiological construct be established outside meditation research. The DMN as a self-referential processing network satisfies this condition, having been characterized extensively in non-meditating populations through resting-state paradigms, self-referential judgment tasks, and mind-wandering induction protocols .

**INTERPRETATION** — Patanjali's taxonomic marking of *asmita* as a stage-defining variable rather than a continuous covariate constitutes a categorical prediction: the presence or absence of self-referential processing is treated as a qualitative boundary, not merely a quantitative gradient. This is consistent with evidence that DMN and task-positive networks exhibit anticorrelated dynamics that may function as a bistable rather than graded system , though the bistability hypothesis itself requires further empirical support. This mapping was classified as **strong** on the basis of three-dimensional functional correspondence and extensive replication of DMN self-referential function.

### Domain 3: Interoceptive Sensitivity and *Pratyahara*

**FACT** — *Pratyahara*, translated in Phase 1 as voluntary withdrawal or decoupling of sensory processing from external stimulation, implies a mechanism by which attention to interoceptive signals is preserved or enhanced while responsivity to exteroceptive stimuli is reduced. The neurobiological candidate construct is the interoceptive processing hierarchy organized through the insular cortex and anterior cingulate cortex, which receives ascending visceral signals and integrates them with top-down predictive signals .

**FACT** — Research on interoceptive cortical organization documents that the anterior insula encodes high-level interoceptive representations including affective tone and body-ownership signals, while posterior insula encodes lower-level somatovisceral input . Meditation practice has been associated with increased gray matter density in insular regions and heightened interoceptive accuracy on heartbeat detection tasks , though these findings derive from meditation-specific paradigms and therefore serve as convergent rather than primary evidence under the independence criterion.

**INTERPRETATION** — The *pratyahara* mapping is the most structurally novel of the five domains, because the mechanism implied by Patanjali—a top-down gating of exteroceptive input concurrent with preserved interoceptive access—is not a standard paradigm in systems neuroscience and remains undercharacterized. The mapping was classified as **moderate**: two-dimensional functional correspondence is present (decoupling from exteroceptive stimulation; preserved access to interoceptive content), but the specific gating mechanism implied by *pratyahara* lacks direct empirical test in non-meditation paradigms. This domain represents the clearest candidate for novel experimental paradigm design.

### Domain 4: Dispositional Memory Structures (*Samskara* and *Vasana*)

**FACT** — Patanjali's constructs of *samskara* (latent impressions formed by repeated mental activity) and *vasana* (dispositional tendencies arising from accumulated samskaras) were characterized in neutral cognitive vocabulary as state-dependent dispositional memory structures that bias future cognition without requiring explicit retrieval. The neurobiological candidate constructs are synaptic consolidation processes, including long-term potentiation and the role of hippocampal-neocortical transfer in the formation of schematic memory structures , as well as state-dependent memory retrieval, in which internal physiological state at encoding influences retrieval probability .

**HYPOTHESIS** — Patanjali's account implies that dispositional structures formed during altered states of consciousness (samadic states) would be preferentially retrieved under similar state conditions. This constitutes a testable prediction derivable from the *samskara* construct prior to any review of the state-dependency literature. The state-dependent retrieval literature in non-meditation contexts provides partial support for the structural principle , but direct experimental tests using samadic state induction and subsequent memory probes do not exist in the current literature. This mapping was classified as **moderate-to-speculative**, with the state-dependency correspondence classified as moderate and the samadic transfer implication classified as speculative pending experimental investigation.

## Discussion

The central methodological claim of this paper is not that Patanjali's *Yoga Sutras* constitute a proto-neuroscientific text, but that a structured phenomenological system developed through centuries of refined introspective practice may encode regularities about mental state-space that retain predictive value when translated into contemporary cognitive vocabulary. The results offer partial support for this claim, with the degree of support varying systematically and informatively across domains.

**INTERPRETATION** — The asymmetric distribution of evidential quality across the five domains is the most analytically significant finding. Two domains (attentional hierarchy and self-referential processing) met strong classification criteria; one met moderate criteria; two were classified as weak-to-speculative. This distribution is not random. It tracks, with reasonable fidelity, the degree to which contemporary neuroscience has developed rich, independently validated constructs for the phenomena in question. Default mode network function and the automatization of attentional control are among the most replicated constructs in cognitive neuroscience ; it is precisely here that Patanjali's categorical distinctions find their strongest neurobiological analogues. Where neuroscience has not yet developed established non-meditation paradigms—as in the case of *sa-bija* / *nir-bija* distinctions or the specific exteroceptive gating mechanism implied by *pratyahara*—the mappings remain speculative not because the phenomenological descriptions are imprecise, but because the relevant experimental infrastructure does not yet exist. **INTERPRETATION** — This pattern suggests the phenomenological framework may be ahead of available measurement tools in at least two domains, rather than simply failing to correspond to neural reality.

The strongest correspondences warrant individual interpretation. The mapping of Patanjali's graded attentional sequence onto prefrontal recruitment dynamics and oscillatory signatures (Domain 1) is notable because the functional description Patanjali provides—periodic re-engagement following attentional lapse, followed by continuous engagement at reduced maintenance cost—is structurally identical to the phenomenological account implicit in focused attention paradigms used in modern experimental protocols, yet Patanjali's taxonomy predates these paradigms by approximately eighteen centuries . **FACT** — The directionality of the mapping procedure, which extracted neutral cognitive descriptions from the Sanskrit text prior to literature review, guards against the most obvious confound: that the similarity reflects post-hoc selective reading of both traditions. The preregistered extraction phase establishes that the categorical structure was identified before the neurobiological candidate constructs were consulted.

The *asmita* / DMN correspondence (Domain 2) carries an additional implication that deserves scrutiny. **INTERPRETATION** — Patanjali treats the presence or absence of self-referential processing as a qualitative categorical boundary between stages of *samadhi*, rather than as a continuous covariate that happens to vary across states. This is a structural prediction: that the transition between self-referential and non-self-referential absorption is stage-like rather than graded. The anticorrelated dynamics between the default mode network and task-positive networks have been interpreted by some investigators as evidence of bistable system organization , which would be consistent with Patanjali's categorical formulation. However, the bistability interpretation is not yet consensus neuroscience , and the correspondence between Patanjali's categorical logic and network dynamics cannot be taken as evidence for bistability. The isomorphism is between formal structures, and the underlying neural dynamics require independent empirical investigation.

The *pratyahara* mapping (Domain 3) represents the most generatively novel result of the analysis. **HYPOTHESIS** — The mechanism implied by *pratyahara*—preserved or enhanced interoceptive access concurrent with reduced responsivity to exteroceptive stimulation—is not a standard paradigm in systems neuroscience and has not, to the authors' knowledge, been directly tested in non-meditation populations. The insular interoceptive hierarchy provides a plausible neuroanatomical substrate , and the functional description maps coherently onto what is known about top-down modulation of sensory gain . This domain is therefore identified as the clearest candidate for experimental paradigm development motivated by the phenomenological taxonomy. A paradigm that tests whether voluntary shifts of attentional orientation toward interoceptive signals produce measurable reductions in exteroceptive sensitivity—measured independently of any meditation instruction—would constitute a direct test of the mechanism Patanjali's construct implies.

Several limitations of this analysis require explicit statement. First, the mapping procedure depends on translation and interpretive judgments at the level of neutral cognitive description. Different translators render *vritti*, *samskara*, and *asmita* using different English terminology, and the cognitive vocabulary applied in Phase 1 reflects interpretive choices that may introduce bias despite the preregistration design . Second, the independence criterion—which excludes neurobiological constructs developed specifically within meditation research—is methodologically principled but conservative. It may exclude some of the most directly relevant neuroscientific evidence and should be relaxed in follow-up analyses that shift from prediction testing to comprehensive correspondence mapping. Third, the graded classification scheme (strong, moderate, weak, speculative) applies discrete labels to what is likely a continuous evidential landscape; the moderate-to-speculative classification assigned to Domain 4 reflects genuine uncertainty at the boundary.

**FACT** — This analysis does not demonstrate that Patanjali's framework is neurobiologically accurate. It demonstrates that categorical distinctions Patanjali drew on phenomenological grounds correspond to independently derived neurobiological constructs at a level of specificity that exceeds what chance overlap would predict, in at least two of five domains tested. **INTERPRETATION** — The appropriate inference is that structured phenomenological systems developed through sustained and systematic introspective practice may constitute a legitimate source of hypothesis generation for cognitive and clinical neuroscience—not that ancient texts provide ground truth about neural mechanisms.

The analogy invoked in the Introduction—to thermodynamic formalism and Mendelian ratios as productive formal structures that preceded mechanistic understanding—applies here with qualification. Thermodynamics and Mendelian genetics achieved their predictive value from within scientific practice, not from phenomenological report. **SPECULATION** — Whether the regularities encoded in Patanjali's taxonomy reflect genuine access to the functional organization of the mind, or reflect the convergent influence of shared human neural architecture on phenomenological description across cultures and centuries, cannot be determined from structural isomorphism alone. Resolving this question requires experimental paradigms, not comparative analysis. The present analysis is offered as motivation for those paradigms, not as their substitute.
