## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The conservative hypothesis relies appropriately on Tier 1 evidence but cannot access the MAIA literature it would need to be fully grounded. The sleep debt confound (Walker) is underspecified — the prediction that clinical populations show inflated alexithymia scores due to sleep debt is plausible but requires direct testing. The gradient-from-adversity claim is so broadly consistent with developmental neuroscience that it risks being unfalsifiable in practice.

**Strongest support:** Developmental timing effects are among the most robustly replicated phenomena in neuroscience. If any hypothesis in this set is likely to be confirmed by the missing literature, it is this one.

### Against Hypothesis B

The molecular-to-therapeutic analogy (OSK → phase-based sequencing) is the intellectual core of this hypothesis and also its greatest vulnerability. Epigenetic reprogramming is a deterministic molecular process with identified biochemical prerequisites. Therapeutic phase sequencing is a clinical heuristic whose evidence base is contested — some clinicians argue that trauma processing can occur safely without extended stabilization in stable populations. The analogy may be structurally elegant but mechanistically misleading.

**Strongest support:** The soul-density mirror was specifically generated to track this pattern across densities, and it explicitly states the analog. The OSK sequential constraint (cellular preparation required) maps cleanly onto the clinical observation that dysregulated clients cannot access interoceptive work safely — the parallel is specific enough to generate novel predictions.

### Against Hypothesis C

The generation/interpretation distinction, while clinically intuitive (the body 'has feelings' the person 'cannot access'), is difficult to operationalize with current instruments. Most interoceptive measures conflate signal detection (do you notice your heartbeat?) with signal interpretation (do you trust what that sensation means?). Without cleaner operationalization, Hypothesis C cannot be adequately tested. The regulatory capture model is also derived from a food policy context — the inferential jump to interoceptive processing requires assumptions not validated in the available evidence.

**Strongest support:** The inhibitory neurotransmission cascade in NF1 provides a biological mechanism for trained suppression. The ACT literature (not in the database but well-established) explicitly targets experiential avoidance — which maps onto the interpretive suppression model — and shows MAIA improvement, consistent with C over A.

## Open Questions

1. **Measurement architecture:** Do existing MAIA validation studies separate signal detection from signal interpretation, or do current instruments conflate them? This is the most pressing methodological question.

2. **Phase dependency:** In phase-based PTSD RCTs (Cloitre STAIR, EMDR protocols), is there evidence that interoceptive measurement as a secondary outcome shows phase-dependent trajectories?

3. **Sleep confound:** Do clinical trials control for sleep quality when administering interoceptive measures? Walker's finding about sleep debt cognitive impairment implies systematic inflation of alexithymia scores in sleep-deprived clinical populations.

4. **Inhibitory vs. generative deficit:** Can existing neuroimaging paradigms (Farb et al. heartbeat detection paradigm, if accessible) distinguish anterior insula (interpretive) from primary somatosensory cortex (generative) contributions to interoceptive accuracy?

5. **Developmental window analogs:** If therapeutic reprogramming follows OSK logic, are there windows of enhanced plasticity during treatment where the system is most responsive to interpretive re-training?
