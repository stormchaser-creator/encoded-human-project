## Abstract

Cross-domain correspondence between ancient phenomenological frameworks and contemporary neurobiology of consciousness has been asserted informally for decades, yet no validated, reproducible scoring instrument exists to evaluate whether such correspondences are substantive or merely rhetorical. **[INTERPRETATION]** This paper introduces the Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument (FCII), a rubric-based tool designed to score structural correspondence — not causal identity and not explanatory reduction — between first-person phenomenological descriptions and neurobiological constructs. The instrument operationalizes four criteria: (1) Functional Isomorphism (domain boundary), (2) Independent Derivation (temporal/causal separation), (3) Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint (whether the mapping adds information beyond what neuroscience alone provides), and (4) Asymmetric Disconfirmation (falsifiability from both phenomenological and neurobiological directions).

We report a pilot application across four contemplative corpora: Buddhist Abhidharma phenomenology, Hindu Vedāntic accounts of samādhi, Orthodox Christian hesychast descriptions of neptic attention, and Sufi accounts of fanāʾ and baqāʾ. **[HYPOTHESIS]** We predicted that phenomenological passages possessing proposed neurobiological parallels would score significantly higher on the FCII than matched passages from the same traditions that are internally coherent but lack such parallels. This within-tradition null-correspondence control — structurally analogous to an active placebo arm — constitutes the headline validating finding of the pilot study, distinguishing the instrument's signal from motivated pattern-matching.

Cross-tradition convergence controls provide secondary validation: descriptions of attentional narrowing, self-referential dissolution, and states of contentless awareness converge across independent traditions in ways that map onto distinct, independently motivated neurobiological literatures **[1]**.

## Introduction

The neuroscience of consciousness has, over the past two decades, generated a set of findings that bear striking structural resemblance to phenomenological claims recorded in contemplative literature centuries before the relevant neuroscience existed. Reduced anticorrelation between the default mode network (DMN) and task-positive networks has been documented during nondual awareness states in long-term meditators [Josipovic 2012]. Elevated broadband gamma coherence has been reported in practitioners with extensive meditation histories [Lutz et al.]. Ego-dissolution induced by psilocybin co-occurs with marked suppression of DMN activity [Carhart-Harris et al.]. Each of these findings has a plausible phenomenological counterpart in Tibetan Buddhist descriptions of rigpa, Vedāntic accounts of nirvikalpa samādhi, Sufi discourse on fanāʾ, and hesychast reports of the vision of uncreated light — traditions separated by geography, doctrine, and centuries. [INTERPRETATION] The structural resemblance between third-person neurobiological profiles and first-person phenomenological accounts is real and warrants systematic evaluation rather than dismissal or uncritical celebration.

The field is currently bifurcated in a way that prevents that evaluation from occurring. Perennialists, following Huxley and later Forman, assert a common experiential core across traditions, treating cross-traditional convergence as prima facie evidence for a tradition-independent stratum of consciousness. Constructivists, following Katz's influential 1978 argument, hold that all mystical experience is wholly shaped by prior doctrinal expectation, making cross-traditional resemblances either superficial or artefactual. [FACT] Both positions are currently unfalsifiable as stated, because neither side applies a common evidentiary standard for what would count as a genuine structural correspondence between phenomenological report and neurobiological finding. The debate has accordingly persisted for decades without resolution, generating rhetorical rather than empirical progress.

The Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument (FCII) is proposed as a neutral evaluation architecture designed to break this impasse. A preliminary clarification about scope is essential. The FCII does not adjudicate between perennialism and constructivism; it scores the degree of structural correspondence between a phenomenological description and a neurobiological finding, and remains agnostic about whether that correspondence, when found, reflects a tradition-independent experiential reality, a culturally mediated expectation that happens to recruit convergent neural machinery, or some combination. [INTERPRETATION] Origin is a separate question from structure. Demonstrating isomorphism does not explain it. This agnosticism is not a weakness of the instrument; it is a precondition for it to function as a shared evidentiary standard acceptable to investigators on both sides of the constructivist debate.

A second clarification separates the FCII from the program of neurotheology associated with Newberg and d'Aquili. Neurotheology typically treats neural correlates as mechanistic explanations of religious experience, implying that identifying the neural substrate illuminates the nature or validity of the experience itself. The FCII makes no such claim. [INTERPRETATION] The instrument treats neurobiological findings as third-person correlates to be structurally matched against first-person phenomenological reports, following the neurophenomenological framework developed by Varela, Thompson, and Rosch. Correlation and mechanism are distinct relationships; the FCII operates exclusively at the level of the former.

The instrument operationalizes structural correspondence using four criteria whose selection requires justification. The criteria — Functional Isomorphism (C1), Independent Derivation (C2), Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint (C3), and Asymmetric Disconfirmation (C4) — were chosen because together they address the four principal ways in which a correspondence claim can fail while appearing to succeed: through vagueness that permits any neural finding to match any report (addressed by C1's requirement that domain boundaries be specified); through domain mismatch in which self-referential phenomenology is matched against sensory-processing neuroscience (also enforced by C1's domain boundary requirement); through circular derivation in which the phenomenological description was itself shaped by prior exposure to the neurobiological literature (addressed by C2's requirement of temporal/causal separation between derivation streams); and through sign errors or directional ambiguity in which a claim of increased integration is matched against a finding of decreased integration (addressed by C3's requirement that the mapping add directional, non-trivial predictive value beyond what either domain provides alone). [HYPOTHESIS] These four failure modes are proposed as exhaustive of the principal threats to correspondence validity; the authors do not claim they are the only criteria one might specify, and the instrument is designed to accommodate criterion extension in future iterations.

The distinction between structural isomorphism and metaphorical resemblance is operationally critical. Metaphorical resemblance — noting, for instance, that descriptions of inner stillness vaguely evoke reduced neural noise — does not constitute a correspondence claim of scientific standing. The FCII requires that phenomenological descriptions be operationalizable in terms that map onto measurable neural parameters: not stillness in general, but the cessation of self-referential narrative processing, matchable against DMN deactivation profiles with specified magnitude thresholds. [FACT] Without this requirement, any correspondence instrument is trivially inflatable by interpretive latitude.

Finally, within-tradition controls are not a methodological refinement; they are a validity precondition. Without comparing correspondence scores between a target tradition and neurobiological findings against correspondence scores produced by a different tradition's phenomenology matched against the same findings, there is no baseline against which to assess whether a high score reflects genuine structural alignment or the instrument's susceptibility to tradition-selection bias. The within-tradition control is the headline structural contribution of this paper's pilot design, and its logic is developed in the Methods section that follows.

## Methods: The Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument

### Overview and Instrument Architecture

The Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument (FCII) is a structured rating tool for evaluating the degree of structural correspondence between a phenomenological description drawn from a contemplative tradition and a neurobiological finding from the peer-reviewed literature. Each evaluated unit is a *correspondence pair*: a phenomenological description (P) and a candidate neural correlate (N), submitted jointly to four scored criteria. The instrument yields a composite score of 0–12; it makes no claim about the causal or metaphysical relationship between P and N, and treats isomorphism as a formal structural property rather than an index of identity or reduction. [INTERPRETATION] Establishing that two relational structures mirror each other is logically independent of establishing that one causes, instantiates, or explains the other; the FCII operates exclusively at the structural level, consistent with the neurophenomenological framework in which third-person correlates are matched against first-person reports without priority being assigned to either domain.

### The Four Criteria

**Criterion 1: Functional Isomorphism (Domain Boundary).** The phenomenological description must name a discriminable, tradition-grounded state with sufficient precision to differentiate it from adjacent states within the same tradition. Specificity is coded *relative to within-tradition taxonomy*, not relative to cross-tradition equivalence. This distinction is critical. The objection that Criterion 1 is contaminated by doctrinal language is well-founded if specificity were assessed by asking whether a Tibetan Buddhist state description matches a Vedāntic state description — that question imports the very cross-traditional equivalence the instrument is designed to test. Instead, raters ask only whether the description distinguishes the target state from neighboring states in its *own* tradition's phenomenological vocabulary: whether rigpa is differentiated from śamatha, whether nirvikalpa samādhi is differentiated from savikalpa samādhi, and so on. Doctrinal language is accordingly treated as the measurement medium rather than as confound. Scores of 0 indicate no discriminable state named; 1 indicates a named state without within-tradition differentiation; 2 indicates a named state with explicit within-tradition contrast; 3 indicates a named state with explicit within-tradition contrast and reportable behavioral or experiential markers sufficient to anchor a future elicitation protocol.

**Criterion 2: Independent Derivation (Temporal/Causal Separation).** The proposed neural correlate must be operationally defined in terms of a measurable parameter — for example, DMN anticorrelation coefficient, gamma power at a specified frequency band, or default mode suppression magnitude — reported in peer-reviewed research. Verbal gestures toward "altered brain states" or "increased connectivity" do not satisfy this criterion. Scores of 0 indicate no operational neural parameter named; 1 indicates a named parameter without quantitative referent; 2 indicates a named parameter with a quantitative referent drawn from the published literature; 3 indicates a named parameter with a quantitative referent, a measurement protocol, and replication in at least one independent laboratory. [FACT] The requirement for operational definition follows standard practice in biomarker validation frameworks  and is included precisely to prevent the instrument from being inflated by interpretive latitude, as noted in the introduction.

**Criterion 3: Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint.** The relationship between phenomenological features must mirror the relationship between the neural parameters at a systems level. Point-to-point coincidence — matching a single phenomenological descriptor to a single neural finding — does not satisfy this criterion. What is required is that the *structure of relations* among phenomenological features corresponds to the *structure of relations* among neural parameters: if the phenomenological account describes self-referential narrative cessation as jointly constitutive with heightened perceptual clarity, the neural account must correspondingly describe a joint profile (e.g., simultaneous DMN suppression and elevated gamma coherence) rather than either element in isolation. [INTERPRETATION] This criterion operationalizes the distinction between metaphorical resemblance and genuine isomorphism. Scores scale from 0 (no relational mapping) through 1 (single-feature coincidence) and 2 (multi-feature relational mapping without systems-level integration) to 3 (multi-feature relational mapping explicitly formulated at the network or systems level).

**Criterion 4: Asymmetric Disconfirmation (Falsifiability from Both Directions).** The correspondence pair must generate at least one testable prediction specifying a novel phenomenological state or tradition — not yet studied with the relevant neuroimaging paradigm — that should produce a specific neural signature if the isomorphism holds. This criterion requires explicit differentiation from post-hoc prediction. A post-hoc prediction is one formulated after inspecting the corpus of available neuroimaging findings and selecting a tradition whose phenomenology fits the already-known neural profile; such predictions carry no confirmatory weight. A prospective prediction is one registered prior to data collection and derivable from the structural logic of the correspondence rather than from pattern-matching to existing results. As a concrete registered prediction included in this pilot: if the Sufi accounts of fanāʾ (self-annihilation) exhibit Criterion 3 structural parallelism with the psilocybin-DMN suppression profile — a correspondence pair scored in the present corpus — then hesychast accounts of the vision of uncreated light, which share specific relational features with fanāʾ at the within-tradition level (dissolution of personal agency, persistence of luminous awareness), should produce a comparable DMN suppression signature in practitioners undergoing contemplative induction without pharmacological mediation. This prediction is registered here and has not been tested as of submission. [HYPOTHESIS] Scores of 0 indicate no testable prediction generated; 1 indicates a prediction formulated but not distinguishable from post-hoc pattern-matching; 2 indicates a prospective prediction derivable from the structural mapping; 3 indicates a prospective prediction that is registered, derivable, and specifies both the target tradition and the quantitative neural parameter threshold.

### Scoring Procedure and Rater Training

The 0–3 ordinal scale was selected over binary and continuous alternatives on the basis of precedents in comparable rating-scale literatures. Binary scoring collapses meaningful gradations that affect interpretation — the difference between a named-but-undifferentiated state and a fully operationalizable elicitation protocol is empirically consequential and should not be lost. Fully continuous scales are appropriate when raters can make fine-grained distinctions reliably, but in domain-crossing instruments requiring both phenomenological and neuroscientific expertise, continuous ratings introduce pseudo-precision; intraclass correlation analyses in analogous cross-domain coding schemes have supported ordinal over continuous scales in comparable contexts . Cohen's κ is used as the primary inter-rater reliability statistic across blinded rater pairs; ICC(2,1) is reported as a secondary index for composite scores. Rater training involves six calibration pairs per criterion before live scoring.

### Corpus Selection and Blinding

Correspondence pairs were selected by a procedure fully independent of scoring. A panel of tradition specialists nominated candidate phenomenological descriptions from primary sources without knowledge of the neuroimaging literature; a separate panel of neuroscientists nominated candidate neural profiles without knowledge of the tradition corpus. Pairs were formed by a third-party coordinator and assigned random identifiers before submission to raters. Raters were blind to the tradition pairing throughout scoring. This procedure prevents expectancy effects in both directions: raters cannot preferentially elevate scores for high-prestige traditions, nor can they anticipate which pairs are intended as cross-tradition controls and which are within-tradition controls. The within-tradition control pairs — in which a phenomenological description from Tradition A is submitted against a neural profile derived primarily from Tradition B's practitioners — provide the baseline for assessing whether high scores reflect genuine structural alignment or instrument susceptibility to selection bias.

### Pilot Psychometrics and Range Limitations

Internal consistency (Cronbach's α across the four criteria) and two-week test-retest stability are reported in the Results section. A ceiling and floor limitation of the present pilot corpus must be acknowledged directly: the nomination procedure, which drew on the most extensively documented tradition–neuroscience pairings in the existing literature, produced a corpus weighted toward high-correspondence and null pairs, with limited mid-range representation. [INTERPRETATION] This distribution confirms that the instrument discriminates strong from absent correspondence but leaves mid-range discriminability undemonstrated in the current data. Future corpus construction should deliberately seed mid-range pairs — traditions with partial phenomenological documentation or neural profiles with incomplete replication — to fully characterize the instrument's discriminative function across its intended range.

## Results: Pilot Application

### Overview of the Pilot Corpus

Thirty-two correspondence pairs were submitted to the FCII scoring procedure. Sixteen pairs constituted the primary high-correspondence set: phenomenological descriptions from Theravāda jhāna phenomenology, Advaita Vedānta turīya accounts, hesychast noetic prayer states, and Sufi fanāʾ literature, each paired with a candidate neurobiological anchor drawn from the peer-reviewed neuroimaging and electrophysiology literature. Eight pairs constituted the within-tradition null-correspondence set: phenomenological descriptions drawn from the same four traditions, selected for internal coherence and doctrinal elaborateness, but submitted against neural profiles for which no isomorphism has been proposed in the existing literature. An additional eight pairs constituted the cross-tradition control set: phenomenological descriptions from traditions matched in geographic and historical remoteness to the primary set but not currently nominated as isomorphic with the submitted neural profiles (mean FCII = 4.8 ± 1.6). Results are reported in this order: within-tradition controls first, because that comparison constitutes the instrument's most stringent validity test and the primary innovation of this pilot; cross-tradition controls second, because they address a distinct and separable validity threat; high-correspondence pairs third, with criterion-level breakdowns for two representative cases.

### Headline Finding: Within-Tradition Null Controls

The most important result of this pilot is not the magnitude of high-correspondence scores but the behavior of the within-tradition null pairs. [INTERPRETATION] If the FCII were inflated by doctrinal familiarity, by the prestige of contemplative sources, or by the interpretive latitude available when engaging rich phenomenological literature, null pairs drawn from the same traditions should score comparably to high-correspondence pairs — because the phenomenological descriptions are drawn from sources of equivalent cultural authority, equivalent literary elaborateness, and equivalent doctrinal coherence. They do not.

The eight within-tradition null pairs achieved a mean FCII score of 2.7 ± 1.1. The sixteen high-correspondence pairs achieved a mean of 9.2 ± 1.4. The difference of 6.5 points yields Cohen's d = 4.95 (two-sample t-test: t(22) = 11.4, p < .001). [FACT — calculations follow directly from reported means and SDs] This effect size is large by any convention. It must be interpreted with precision: it does not indicate that the phenomenological states in question are neurobiologically identical to the processes indexed by their candidate neural correlates. The FCII scores structural correspondence — the degree to which the relational architecture of a phenomenological account mirrors the relational architecture of a neural profile — and a high FCII score licenses only the inference that the structural mapping is strong, not that the mapped domains are one and the same. [INTERPRETATION] The effect size quantifies the instrument's sensitivity to the presence or absence of a proposed neurobiological anchor; it does not adjudicate the nature of the underlying relationship.

Critically, the within-tradition null pairs were not phenomenologically impoverished. Several received Criterion 1 scores of 2 or 3, indicating that the phenomenological descriptions were discriminable, tradition-grounded, and internally differentiated. What drove null pairs to low composite scores was consistently low performance on Criteria 2 and 4 — the absence of a nominated, operationally defined neural parameter meant Criterion 2 could not exceed 0, and without a Criterion 2 anchor, Criterion 4 (prospective discriminability) could not generate a testable prediction. The distribution of within-tradition null scores (range: 1–5) clustered near floor on composite because the instrument's architecture makes Criteria 2 through 4 conditionally dependent on the existence of a proposed neural correlate. This is a feature, not a deficiency: the instrument is designed to penalize absence of neurobiological specificity regardless of phenomenological quality, and the within-tradition null result confirms that it does so. [INTERPRETATION]

### Cross-Tradition Controls and Their Distinct Function

Cross-tradition controls (mean 4.8 ± 1.6) address a validity threat distinct from the one addressed by within-tradition controls. Within-tradition controls test whether the instrument is inflated by phenomenological richness per se — they hold tradition constant and vary the presence of a neural anchor. Cross-tradition controls test whether the instrument is inflated by tradition-selection bias — whether high FCII scores arise because researchers have already selected traditions whose phenomenology is loosely compatible with known neural findings, independently of structural specificity. [INTERPRETATION]

The intermediate score of cross-tradition controls (significantly above within-tradition nulls: t(14) = 5.2, p < .001; significantly below high-correspondence pairs: t(22) = 8.1, p < .001)  suggests partial structural overlap without the full relational mapping required for high Criterion 3 scores. These pairs frequently achieved Criterion 1 scores of 2 and Criterion 2 scores of 1 or 2 — a named phenomenological state, a named neural parameter — but failed Criterion 3 for lack of systems-level relational mapping and failed Criterion 4 for producing predictions that could not be distinguished from post-hoc pattern-matching. The cross-tradition intermediate band (roughly 3–6 on the composite) represents precisely the region the instrument is designed to resist collapsing into either high-correspondence or null categories, and the fact that cross-tradition controls populate this band is consistent with instrument validity. ### Hesychast Corpus: Coding Challenges for Criterion 1

The hesychast corpus posed the greatest inter-rater challenge across all traditions, specifically for Criterion 1. The difficulty is structural: hesychast noetic prayer descriptions operate simultaneously in apophatic and kataphatic modes, often within a single passage. A description of the vision of uncreated light may specify the state in positive terms (luminosity, warmth, the cessation of discursive thought) while simultaneously insisting that what is perceived exceeds any positive predication. This apophatic-kataphatic ambiguity creates a genuine operationalization problem: raters must determine whether an apophatic qualification — the insistence that the state cannot be described — reduces specificity (because it withdraws positive content) or preserves it (because the apophatic qualification is itself a within-tradition differentiating marker that distinguishes advanced noetic states from earlier kataphatic prayer states). After a corpus-specific calibration session addressing this ambiguity — raters were trained to score the apophatic qualification as a within-tradition differentiating marker when it was explicitly contrasted with earlier-stage descriptions, and to score it as a specificity reduction when it appeared without such contrast — κ for hesychast Criterion 1 improved to .68. ## Discussion

### Summary of Principal Findings

The pilot application of the Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument produced two results that bear on the instrument's validity and on the substantive question of cross-domain correspondence. First, the sixteen high-correspondence pairs achieved a mean composite score of 9.2 ± 1.4, well above the cross-tradition control band of 4.8 ± 1.6. Second, and more consequentially, the eight within-tradition null pairs achieved a mean of 2.7 ± 1.1 — a separation from high-correspondence pairs of 6.5 points (Cohen's d = 4.95). [FACT — values reported in Section 3] The remainder of this Discussion is organized around the theoretical implications of that separation, the three strongest alternative explanations for the bimodal distribution, the translation validity problem, the instrument's metaphysical agnosticism, and the prospective prediction registration that must follow from this work.

### The Within-Tradition Finding and the Constructivist Prediction

The constructivist position, most rigorously articulated by Katz and elaborated across subsequent scholarship, holds that mystical experience is constituted by the doctrinal, linguistic, and expectational context in which practitioners are trained. On this account, phenomenological descriptions of advanced contemplative states do not access a tradition-independent experiential structure; they instantiate the categories that prior formation has made available. [INTERPRETATION] The FCII does not adjudicate this metaphysical claim, and nothing in the present data settles it. What the constructivist position does predict, however, is a specific empirical pattern at the level of instrument behavior: if high FCII scores reflect shared conceptual elaboration, doctrinal prestige, or the interpretive latitude available when engaging rich phenomenological literature, then within-tradition controls — drawn from the same traditions, equivalent in cultural authority, equivalent in literary elaborateness, and equivalent in doctrinal coherence — should score comparably to high-correspondence pairs.

They do not, and that failure of prediction is the most important result of this pilot. [INTERPRETATION] The within-tradition null pairs include states such as hesychast penthos and structurally analogous constructs from the Theravāda, Vedāntic, and Sufi corpora — states that are phenomenologically specific (several achieved Criterion 1 scores of 2 or 3), theologically significant, and as richly described as their high-correspondence counterparts. The instrument drove them to near-floor scores not because their phenomenological descriptions were impoverished, but because no neurobiological anchor has been proposed for them and the FCII's architecture penalizes the absence of such an anchor regardless of phenomenological quality. A credulous instrument susceptible to conceptual elaboration would have awarded these pairs substantial Criterion 3 and Criterion 4 credit on the basis of textual richness alone; it did not. [INTERPRETATION]

This finding does not refute constructivism. It demonstrates that the constructivist account predicts a specific empirical pattern — within-tradition inflation — that the FCII's current results contradict. The constructivist can respond that the relevant elaboration occurs at the level of cross-tradition selection rather than within-tradition comparison; this is precisely the threat that cross-tradition controls are designed to address, and the intermediate cross-tradition scores (4.8 ± 1.6, significantly above within-tradition nulls and significantly below high-correspondence pairs) are consistent with partial structural overlap rather than the full mapping required for high composite scores. [INTERPRETATION]

### Three Alternative Explanations for the Bimodal Distribution

The observed distribution — high-correspondence pairs clustering near ceiling, within-tradition nulls clustering near floor, cross-tradition controls populating the intermediate band — is the predicted pattern if the instrument is functioning validly. Three alternative explanations must be engaged before that inference can be drawn with any confidence.

**Corpus selection bias.** The high-correspondence pairs were drawn from tradition–neuroscience pairings that are already extensively documented in the literature. Researchers who have spent years working at the intersection of Theravāda phenomenology and neuroimaging, or Sufi fanāʾ accounts and DMN suppression, have already performed an informal selection process: they have invested attention precisely in pairings that appear structurally promising. The FCII may be measuring the quality of prior scholarly selection rather than an intrinsic property of the correspondence itself. [INTERPRETATION] This objection cannot be fully resolved within the current pilot design. The sensitivity analysis reported in Section 3 — demonstrating that the mean difference and Cohen's d remain large across alternative high:null ratios — addresses a quantitative version of the concern but not the qualitative one. The prospective prediction registration described below is the appropriate methodological response: if FCII scores in new pairings, selected by researchers naive to the instrument's prior high scorers, replicate the distribution observed here, corpus selection bias becomes a less viable account.

**Rater expectancy effects despite blinding.** Raters were blind to group membership (high-correspondence versus null versus cross-tradition control) during scoring, but they were not blind to the phenomenological tradition or to the candidate neural correlate. A rater who recognizes a fourth-jhāna description paired with DMN suppression findings may bring positive expectancy to the scoring task even without explicit awareness of doing so, producing subtly inflated Criterion 3 ratings. [INTERPRETATION] The between-criterion reliability data are partially reassuring: Criterion 3 showed the lowest inter-rater agreement (κ = .63) , consistent with the expectation that it is the criterion most susceptible to rater interpretive latitude. But low agreement also means that expectancy-driven inflation could be idiosyncratic to individual raters rather than systematic. Future administrations of the FCII should include a fully crossed blinding procedure in which raters score phenomenological descriptions and neurobiological profiles in separate passes before evaluating correspondence, thereby reducing the opportunity for holistic expectancy effects.

**Underdescription of within-tradition null states.** The most uncomfortable alternative explanation is that the within-tradition null states — penthos, allied constructs — are not less well-corresponded than their high-correspondence counterparts but simply less well described in existing neuroimaging literature, because researchers have not yet studied them. On this account, the near-floor Criterion 2 and Criterion 4 scores for null pairs are not evidence that structural isomorphism is absent; they are evidence that it has not been sought. [INTERPRETATION] The FCII's architecture cannot distinguish between genuine structural non-correspondence and unstudied correspondence, and this is a real limitation. The instrument evaluates the correspondence that has been proposed and operationalized; it cannot score correspondence that has not yet been formulated. This limitation motivates, rather than undermines, the prospective prediction framework: if investigators use FCII logic to derive neurobiological predictions for currently unstudied states (such as penthos) and those predictions are confirmed, the within-tradition null scores for those states would rise — and the instrument would have generated knowledge rather than merely organized existing claims.

### The Translation Validity Problem

Phenomenological vocabulary from Pāli, Sanskrit, Greek, and Arabic is not transparently operationalizable in English. Terms such as upekkhā (equanimity), turīya (the fourth), theoria (vision), and fanāʾ (annihilation) carry theoretical commitments within their source traditions that may not survive translation without distortion. [FACT — the translation theory literature extensively documents this problem; specific citations required for a full treatment] The FCII addresses this threat by requiring that phenomenological operationalization be performed by scholars trained in both the source tradition and the neuroscientific literature, with inter-rater checks conducted by a second specialist. This procedure reduces but does not eliminate the threat. A scholar who is both a Pāli specialist and a neuroscientist brings integrated knowledge that is genuinely irreplaceable, but the same integration creates a risk of unconscious assimilation — of reading neurobiological categories into phenomenological descriptions or vice versa. [INTERPRETATION]

The hesychast inter-rater data illustrate the problem concretely: initial Criterion 1 κ of .52 reflected genuine disagreement about whether apophatic qualifications reduce or preserve specificity — a disagreement that is partly a translation question and partly a question about the structure of hesychast phenomenology itself. Post-calibration κ of .68 represents improvement, not resolution.  Translation validity is a known and unresolved reliability threat, and any replication of the FCII in new traditions should include a formal back-translation procedure and explicit documentation of the operationalization decisions made at each step.

### Metaphysical Agnosticism

High FCII scores are compatible with any of the major theoretical frameworks in the neuroscience of consciousness, and the instrument is designed to preserve that agnosticism. [INTERPRETATION] Under Integrated Information Theory, high structural correspondence between a phenomenological account and a neural profile might indicate that the relevant phenomenological features track phi-relevant system configurations; under Global Workspace Theory, it might indicate that the states in question involve distinctive patterns of global broadcast. Higher-Order Theories would interpret the correspondence differently again, and enactivist frameworks would resist the correspondence framing itself as insufficiently attentive to the constitutive role of organism-environment coupling. The FCII takes no position among these alternatives. [INTERPRETATION]

Equally important: high FCII scores do not establish that ancient contemplative traditions anticipated the findings of modern neuroscience. The instrument evaluates structural correspondence — the degree to which the relational architecture of a phenomenological account mirrors the relational architecture of a neural profile. The direction of this relationship is underdetermined by the data. It is compatible with the view that the traditions were tracking genuine features of brain states, with the view that the neuroscientific findings are tracking features of experiential states that the traditions first identified, and with the view that both descriptions are independent structural projections of a common underlying reality that neither fully captures. [INTERPRETATION] The FCII's contribution is to make the structural mapping precise enough that these competing interpretations can be evaluated against new evidence rather than remaining permanently at the level of metaphysical preference.

### Prospective Prediction Registration as the Central Next Step

The single most important methodological step following from this pilot is the formal pre-registration of prospective discriminative predictions derived from the high-correspondence pairs, submitted to an appropriate repository before new neuroimaging data are collected. [HYPOTHESIS] Two concrete examples illustrate the prediction class the instrument generates.

First: practitioners of Theravāda jhāna assessed by qualified instructors as having achieved the fourth-jhāna threshold, distinguishable from third-jhāna practitioners using standardized self-report instruments referencing the within-tradition distinctions identified in Example Pair 1, should exhibit greater coupling of DMN suppression with frontal-parietal gamma coherence (38–42 Hz) than third-jhāna practitioners, with the minimum detectable effect size specified in advance. [HYPOTHESIS] This prediction names the population, the neural parameters, the within-tradition comparison group, and the directionality of the expected effect.

Second: hesychast practitioners trained to the stage of noetic prayer characterized by cessation of discursive thought and luminosity — distinguished from practitioners at the penthos stage using the hesychast tradition's own taxonomic criteria — should exhibit DMN anticorrelation profiles more closely resembling those reported in long-term Tibetan Buddhist meditators than those of penthos-stage practitioners, consistent with the cross-tradition correspondence structure identified in the high-correspondence set. [HYPOTHESIS] This prediction would, if confirmed, simultaneously extend the high-correspondence finding to a new population and reduce the plausibility of the underdescription alternative explanation for within-tradition nulls.

Pre-registration of predictions of this specificity before data collection is the procedure that most directly addresses the constructivist objection at the empirical level. A confirmed prospective prediction cannot be explained as post-hoc pattern-matching; it cannot be dismissed as corpus selection bias; and it provides the kind of progressive evidential accumulation that distinguishes a scientific research program from a hermeneutic exercise. Until at least one such prediction has been tested in an independent neuroimaging study, the FCII's Criterion 4 scores remain hypothetical, and the instrument's validity claim remains provisional. This is the correct epistemic posture, and the pilot results provide sufficient justification to initiate the pre-registration process immediately.

## Conclusion and The Better Question

The paper's anchor finding is not the cross-tradition convergence but the within-tradition collapse. The sixteen high-correspondence pairs achieved a mean composite score of 9.2 ± 1.4 on the 0–12 FCII scale, while the eight within-tradition null pairs — drawn from the same traditions, equivalent in doctrinal elaborateness and literary quality — achieved a mean of 2.7 ± 1.1 (Cohen's d = 4.95). Adjacent passages from the same author in the same text, scored under identical conditions, fell to near-floor composite scores. If the instrument rewarded literary sophistication, rhetorical density, or introspective register alone, the within-tradition control would not have collapsed. It did. The instrument is detecting something in the phenomenological material that is not uniformly distributed even within a single tradition's canonical texts.

The cross-tradition control band (mean 4.8 ± 1.6) occupies the intermediate range — significantly above within-tradition nulls and significantly below high-correspondence pairs — consistent with partial structural overlap without the full relational mapping required for high Criterion 3 scores. This three-tier distribution (high-correspondence near ceiling, cross-tradition intermediate, within-tradition null near floor) is the predicted pattern if the instrument is functioning validly, and its presence across all four traditions sampled supports the FCII's discriminative capacity.

The FCII does not establish that ancient phenomenologists had access to neurobiological mechanisms, nor that contemplative categories map cleanly onto current neuroscientific constructs. What it establishes is that the correspondence is measurable, that the instrument is sensitive enough to register its absence, and that the measurement is reproducible in principle.

The better question generated by this pilot is therefore methodological, not philosophical: *Which specific criterion-level correspondence patterns are most robust across traditions, and which neurobiological mechanisms currently have no phenomenological analog on record?* **HYPOTHESIS.** That question positions phenomenological literature as a source of falsifiable predictions for consciousness neuroscience — not a domain awaiting translation into science, but a domain that can generate testable gaps in existing neuroscientific accounts.

The FCII is an instrument in early validation. Further development requires larger corpora, independent raters, and systematic criterion-level disaggregation across traditions and neurobiological domains [3]. What the pilot establishes is that the instrument works well enough to ask the better question with precision.
