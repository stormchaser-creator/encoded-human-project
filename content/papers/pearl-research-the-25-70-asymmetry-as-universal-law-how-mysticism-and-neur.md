## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The strongest objection is phenomenological: REM sleep is characterized by vivid imagery, emotional activation, and narrative dreaming. Most contemplative traditions prize states of stillness, emptiness, or imageless awareness — phenomenologically the opposite of REM. If REM were the substrate of contemplative integration, we would expect experienced meditators to report their integrative states as dreamlike and emotionally turbulent. They report the opposite. The functional argument saves Hypothesis A (same function, different mechanism) but at the cost of the strong version of the claim.

The strongest support: the functional convergence across multiple independent variables (memory integration, trauma processing, emotional regulation, narrative coherence) is too consistent to be coincidental. Both REM and contemplative practice produce the same outcomes even if via different phenomenological routes.

### Against Hypothesis B

The structural convergence between traditions may reflect common human psychology rather than a shared discovery. If all humans face the same basic challenges — hypervigilance, the need for rest, the danger of substituting sedation for genuine recovery — then all human wisdom traditions will independently generate the same warnings. This would explain the convergence without requiring that they are mapping the same territory. The convergence is evidence of common human condition, not common discovered reality.

The strongest support: the convergence is not merely thematic but structural. The four-part pattern (fragile integrative state + hypervigilant suppressor + counterfeit substitute + asymmetric recovery) is highly specific. Multiple independent traditions arriving at all four elements is harder to explain by common condition than by common discovery.

### Against Hypothesis C

The triadic schema (body/soul/spirit corresponding to waking/dreaming/dreamless sleep) is a specific philosophical claim made by Vedanta and echoed in some Western traditions, but it is not universal across mystical traditions. Theravada Buddhism does not use this schema. Indigenous traditions vary enormously. Using a culturally specific schema as if it were a universal natural kind risks confirmation bias — finding the schema everywhere because you're looking for it.

The strongest support: independent convergence of inquiry traditions is itself a data point. When geographically isolated traditions — Vedantic, Sufi, Kabbalistic, Christian Neoplatonic — all arrive at a triadic architecture of consciousness, this parallels the situation in science when independent laboratories replicate a finding. The replication across cultures is methodologically significant.
