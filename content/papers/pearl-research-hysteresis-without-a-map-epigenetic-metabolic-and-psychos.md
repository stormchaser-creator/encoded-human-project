## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A (Conservative, Tier 1): Epigenetic Scars Persist at Healed Wound Sites

**Core claim:** Healed wound tissue retains statistically distinct DNA methylation patterns compared to uninjured contralateral controls at 6-24 months post-healing, constituting a molecular hysteresis signature.

**Mechanistic basis:** Wound healing involves sequential activation of inflammatory, proliferative, and remodeling phases. Each phase requires specific epigenetic states — chromatin opening for inflammatory gene expression, then closure for resolution. PARP1, DNMT3A, TET1/2, and EZH2 are all documented participants in wound-phase transitions. If any step in the reset sequence is incomplete (insufficient energy, co-factor depletion, competing signals), the tissue may stabilize in a partially activated state.

**Adjacent evidence:** The developmental programming literature (SGA, early nutrition, prenatal stress) has established that early epigenetic events can be permanent on decadal timescales. If developmental epigenetic programming is possible, wound-induced epigenetic programming in adult tissue is mechanistically plausible.

**Key analytical lenses:** *Chaos attractors* (wound site settling into a new methylation attractor state rather than returning to baseline), *topology/morphogenesis* (spatial gradient of methylation change from wound center to periphery), *information theory* (the wound as an information-writing event that leaves a permanent record in chromatin).

**Falsification criterion:** Whole-genome bisulfite sequencing of matched wound vs. contralateral tissue at >6 months post-healing showing no differentially methylated regions with FDR < 0.05 would falsify this hypothesis.

### Hypothesis B (Integrative, Tier 2): NAD+ Depletion Creates a Metabolic-Epigenetic Lock

**Core claim:** NAD+ consumption during active wound healing depletes local pools sufficiently to impair sirtuin-mediated histone deacetylation and TET-mediated DNA demethylation, creating a window during which the epigenetic state cannot be reset regardless of healing completion.

**Mechanistic basis:** PARP1 is the dominant NAD+ consumer during DNA damage response and is massively activated in wound tissue. Sirtuins (SIRT1, SIRT6) require NAD+ for histone deacetylation activities critical to inflammatory gene silencing. TET enzymes require alpha-ketoglutarate and cofactors whose availability is coupled to mitochondrial metabolic state. If NAD+ depletion is sustained beyond the acute injury phase — either because the wound is large, because systemic NAD+ synthesis is impaired (aging, nutritional deficit), or because mitochondrial function is compromised — then the epigenetic machinery needed to complete chromatin reset lacks the energetic substrate to do so.

**The coupled failure model:** This hypothesis predicts a specific coupling: NAD+ depletion rate × duration predicts methylation retention at inflammatory loci. This is testable with current tools (NAD+ measurement by mass spectrometry, methylation sequencing, murine wound models).

**Key analytical lenses:** *Coupled oscillators* (NAD+ synthesis/consumption cycle desynchronized from epigenetic programming cycle), *control theory* (NAD+ as the limiting resource in a feedback loop that normally ensures epigenetic reset; depletion eliminates the restorative arm of the loop), *entropy* (NAD+ depletion = increased local thermodynamic disorder that cannot be resolved without energy input).

**Falsification criterion:** NMN/NR supplementation during wound healing in aged murine models should reduce differential methylation at healed sites if NAD+ is the rate-limiting factor. Failure to do so would falsify the causal claim while leaving the correlational observation intact.

### Hypothesis C (Radical, Tier 3): Stress Locks the Epigenetic-Metabolic Circuit via a Three-Way Attractor

**Core claim:** Psychosocial stress does not merely slow wound healing (established) but specifically impairs the NAD+-epigenetic reset mechanism, producing a triple coupling: stress-induced cortisol → PARP1 hyperactivation and mitochondrial NAD+ depletion → epigenetic lock at inflammatory loci → chronic low-grade inflammatory tone at wound site → stress sensitization. This circuit has properties of a strange attractor: once established, it resists perturbation and tends to self-perpetuate.

**Why this is more than the sum of its parts:** The established stress-wound healing literature (primarily Kiecolt-Glaser) demonstrates cortisol-mediated slowing of healing through inflammatory dysregulation. What has not been tested is whether cortisol also impairs the post-healing epigenetic reset — leaving the wound site as a permanent source of low-grade inflammatory signaling that, in turn, maintains stress-relevant physiological activation. This would explain why psychological trauma is associated with chronic pain syndromes localized to previously injured sites, and why chronic stress patients show accelerated epigenetic aging.

**The fractal observation:** The soul and spirit density entries describe, in their own language, a system that cannot complete its processing cycle — that retains rather than releases. If this pattern is genuinely fractal across scales, the molecular instantiation would be precisely this: a wound site that cannot complete its epigenetic processing cycle because the stress axis keeps re-activating the very machinery that needs to be quieted for reset to occur.

**Key analytical lenses:** *Fractals* (same retention-failure pattern at molecular, physiological, and experiential scales), *complexity emergence* (the triple coupling produces chronic inflammatory tone as an emergent property not predictable from any single component), *chaos attractors* (the stressed-wound state as a strange attractor with a basin of attraction that captures many initial conditions).

**Falsification criterion:** An RCT with cortisol AUC, local tissue NAD+, wound closure rate, and post-healing methylation at IL-6/CDKN2A promoters as co-measured variables should show cortisol AUC predicting methylation retention *independently* of healing speed. If cortisol predicts only healing speed and methylation tracks healing speed exactly, the hypothesis reduces to the known finding and the three-way coupling is not supported.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A
The most serious objection is that wound-site differential methylation, if it exists, may be adaptive rather than pathological. Tissues with wound history benefit from maintained inflammatory readiness — the immune surveillance literature supports this. Additionally, the evidence base is entirely from developmental programming, which occurs during a period of maximal epigenetic plasticity. Adult wound tissue may have far more limited epigenetic reprogramming capacity in either direction.

The strongest support is mechanistic specificity: PARP1, DNMT3A, TET enzymes are all well-characterized responders to local injury signals in adult tissue, not just in development. The machinery is present. Whether it produces lasting changes is an empirical question, but the molecular tools for testing it exist.

### Against Hypothesis B
NAD+ compartmentalization is the strongest objection. Local tissue NAD+ depletion may recover on a timescale of hours to days — faster than the epigenetic lock hypothesis requires. Cells maintain active NAD+ biosynthesis via NAMPT and Preiss-Handler pathways, and systemic NAD+ from dietary sources is rapidly distributed. The hypothesis requires sustained depletion, not acute depletion, and that requirement may not be met in most wounds.

The strongest support is the aging context: aged tissue has demonstrably reduced NAMPT activity and lower baseline NAD+ levels. In aged animals, the depletion hypothesis becomes more plausible because the recovery capacity is already compromised. This suggests the NAD+-epigenetic lock may be primarily an aging-wound interaction, not a universal wound phenomenon.

### Against Hypothesis C
The cross-density fractal argument cannot function as evidence for a molecular claim. The soul-density 'retention failure' description is a therapeutic and phenomenological framework; using it to motivate a molecular hypothesis introduces a category error. The Research Mind acknowledges this objection as valid and emphasizes that the fractal entries are included as pattern-generators only, not as mechanistic support.

The strongest support is that the psychosocial-wound healing link is one of the most replicated findings in psychoneuroimmunology, and the specific mechanism (HPA axis → glucocorticoid → NF-κB/cytokine dysregulation) is well-characterized. The unasked question — whether this mechanism also affects post-healing epigenetic state — is genuine, open, and testable.

The three hypotheses are not competing so much as nested. Hypothesis A is the phenomenon (epigenetic hysteresis exists). Hypothesis B is a mechanism (NAD+ depletion creates the lock). Hypothesis C is the amplifier (stress enhances the lock and creates a self-perpetuating circuit). A fully coherent research program would test them in this order.

The confidence assignment of **low** reflects the absence of direct evidence in Pearl's retrieved entries. All three hypotheses rest on indirect structural analogies from adjacent literatures. ## Implications

If Hypothesis A is confirmed by future literature: wound healing 'completion' as currently defined (clinical closure, absence of acute inflammation) may not correspond to biological resolution at the epigenetic level. This has implications for chronic wound pain, scar tissue biology, and the epidemiology of site-specific inflammatory diseases.

If Hypothesis B is confirmed: NAD+ precursor supplementation (NMN, NR, niacin) during and after wound healing may represent a novel intervention not just for healing speed but for post-healing tissue epigenetic reset — a qualitatively different and currently untested therapeutic target.

If Hypothesis C is confirmed: the clinical standard of care for psychological trauma should include wound history documentation and assessment of wound-site inflammatory tone as part of the trauma sequelae profile. This would also suggest that psychosocial interventions in post-injury populations should measure local inflammatory markers, not just systemic ones.
