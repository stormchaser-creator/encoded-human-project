## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: Standard Vancouver Sequential Ordering (Tier 1 — High Prior Probability)

**Claim:** Cureus uses Vancouver-style sequential citation numbering. References are numbered [1], [2], [3]... in the order they first appear in the manuscript body. The reference list at the end mirrors this order. When a reference is cited again, it retains its original number.

**Reasoning:** The Vancouver system (also called the ICMJE system) dominates medical publishing globally. Cureus positions itself as a peer-reviewed medical journal and has sought MEDLINE indexing, which incentivizes adherence to standard conventions. The prior probability that any medical journal uses Vancouver-style citations is high — estimated above 80% for English-language biomedical journals.

**Information Theory Lens:** Sequential-by-appearance ordering maximizes positional information — a reader can use citation number as a proxy for 'when in the argument this evidence was introduced.' This is a compression scheme that trades network richness for narrative navigability.

**Control Theory Lens:** Sequential numbering creates a feedback loop between writing order and citation order — the author is implicitly discouraged from introducing a reference out of argumentative sequence, because doing so disrupts the number progression. This is a soft constraint that reinforces linear argumentation.

**Falsifiable by:** Any Cureus article showing non-sequential in-text numbering, or Cureus author guidelines specifying AMA, APA, or custom styles.

**Reasoning:** Cureus is unusual among medical journals in using a fully integrated online authoring environment rather than requiring authors to submit formatted documents. This architecture makes dynamic reference management not just possible but likely — the system handles numbering so authors don't have to. This is structurally analogous to how Mendeley or Zotero work within Word, but native to the platform.

**Complexity Emergence Lens:** The final citation list emerges from an authoring process that is inherently non-sequential — authors write non-linearly, insert citations opportunistically, revise order. ## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The strongest objection is that Cureus is genuinely unconventional. It publishes articles that many traditional journals would not accept (broad case report policy, rapid review timelines, crowd-sourced peer review). Unconventional publication philosophy may extend to citation formatting. However, citation formatting is a reader-facing feature that affects indexing, database interoperability, and cross-journal navigability — areas where Cureus has strong incentives to remain conventional even when other aspects of its operation are not.

### Against Hypothesis B

The dynamic citation management described in Hypothesis B is already provided by external reference managers (Mendeley, Zotero, EndNote) that most researchers use regardless of journal. What the CPS adds over these tools for citation management specifically is unclear. The hypothesis may be attributing to Cureus's platform what is actually just standard reference manager behavior. The truly distinctive question would be whether CPS makes any policy-level decisions about citation ordering that differ from what a reference manager would produce.

### Against Hypothesis C

Hypothesis C is philosophically interesting but lacks empirical differentiation. The claim that sequential-by-appearance ordering reflects author-epistemic-journey values is true of ALL Vancouver-system journals, not just Cureus. NEJM, BMJ, JAMA, and Lancet all use sequential ordering. This makes Hypothesis C's Cureus-specific framing unjustified — the hypothesis identifies a general feature of medical publishing and attributes it specifically to Cureus's editorial philosophy without evidence that Cureus has consciously framed it this way.

3. **When reviewing Cureus articles:** Expect reference numbers to follow narrative order, not importance order. Reference [1] is the first cited source, not the most important one.

### For Understanding Citation Systems More Broadly

The NULISA analogy is generative: sequential capture-and-release reduces noise by isolating signal at each step. Sequential citation systems do the same — by anchoring each piece of evidence to a specific narrative moment, they make it harder to cite vaguely or redundantly. The 'cost' is that the reader cannot easily reconstruct which citations are foundational vs. illustrative from the number alone.

The MRI artifact analogy is cautionary: sequential acquisition introduces motion artifacts when the subject is dynamic. Sequential citation ordering introduces 'artifacts' when the argument is revised post-citation — a reference added late in revision gets a high number regardless of its logical position in the argument, creating a mismatch between citation number (late) and argumentative role (foundational).
