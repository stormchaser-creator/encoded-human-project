## The Physics of Wound Bioelectricity (Synthesized from External Literature)

Because this topic is absent from Pearl's knowledge base but well-established in published biology, this section synthesizes the scientific consensus for grounding purposes:

**The Epithelial Battery**: Intact skin and mucosa maintain a transepithelial potential difference (TEP) of approximately 50–70 mV, lumen/wound-side negative. This is maintained by active ion transport — primarily Na+/K+ ATPase and ENaC channels creating net Na+ transport from apical to basal surface. The TEP constitutes a stored electrical potential energy, analogous to a battery.

**Wound Current Generation**: When the epithelium is breached, the high-resistance epithelial layer is disrupted. Current (predominantly Na+ and K+ ions) flows from the intact skin through the wound, creating a lateral DC electric field at the wound edge. Field strengths of 40–200 mV/mm have been measured in human wounds, with the wound center as cathode (most negative) relative to intact tissue.

**Electrotaxis**: Keratinocytes, fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and macrophages — all key wound healing cell types — migrate directionally in applied DC electric fields matching wound magnitudes. Keratinocytes migrate toward the cathode (wound center). This electrotaxis occurs at lower field strengths than those required to activate voltage-gated ion channels, suggesting a separate sensing mechanism, likely involving asymmetric activation of PI3-kinase and PTEN across the cell.

**Temporal Priority**: The DC wound field is established within milliseconds of injury, while chemical gradients (VEGF, EGF, TGF-β) require minutes to hours to establish meaningful gradients. This makes the bioelectric field the organism's fastest spatial signal — the first instruction issued to neighboring cells.

**DC vs AC**: Wound bioelectricity operates in the DC (direct current) or extremely low frequency domain — it is a tonic, sustained field, not an oscillation. This is categorically distinct from the neural oscillations (EEG, sleep spindles, thalamocortical rhythms) that Pearl's evidence base currently covers. Both are bioelectricity; they operate through different mechanisms and at different scales.

The strongest objection is that DC and AC bioelectricity use different cellular machinery (electrotaxis via PI3K asymmetry vs. voltage-gated channel activation in neurons), and demonstrating responsiveness to one does not demonstrate the other. **However**: Hypothesis A doesn't need Pearl's knowledge base to support it — it's established Tier 1 biology outside the base. ### Against Hypothesis B
The causal chain (mitochondrial dysfunction → TEP reduction → wound field reduction → scarring) is plausible but each step is independently uncertain in clinical contexts. The strongest objection: ATP depletion from mitochondrial dysfunction probably affects cytoskeletal repair proteins (actin, myosin) before it significantly reduces Na+/K+ ATPase activity enough to alter TEP — the chemical/mechanical repair failure would precede bioelectric template disruption. **However**: These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive, and mitochondrial membrane potential directly contributes to intracellular voltage, not just ATP production.
