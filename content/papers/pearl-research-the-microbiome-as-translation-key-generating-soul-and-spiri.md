### What the Gut-Brain Axis Evidence Supports

The biological mechanisms of antibiotic-induced dysbiosis are well-established, though Pearl currently lacks a dedicated entry synthesizing them:

**Neurotransmitter synthesis disruption**: Approximately 90% of the body's serotonin is synthesized in enterochromaffin cells of the gut, with production dependent on microbial metabolism of tryptophan. Broad-spectrum antibiotics eliminate the bacterial species (particularly Clostridia and Bacteroides) responsible for tryptophan processing, directly collapsing serotonin precursor availability. GABA is similarly modulated by Lactobacillus species, whose elimination increases anxiety-like behavior in animal models.

**Short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) depletion**: Commensal bacteria ferment dietary fiber into butyrate, propionate, and acetate. These cross the blood-brain barrier, regulate microglial activation states, support BDNF expression, and modulate HPA axis setpoint. Their elimination after broad-spectrum antibiotic use creates a state of neuroinflammatory susceptibility and reduced neuroplasticity.

**Vagal tone dysregulation**: The vagus nerve carries approximately 80% afferent (gut-to-brain) fibers. Microbial metabolites directly activate vagal afferents, contributing to HRV, felt safety, and parasympathetic tone. Dysbiosis silences this afferent signal stream, measurably reducing HRV — which polyvagal theory identifies as the biological substrate of social engagement and emotional co-regulation capacity.

**Immune-neural interface disruption**: Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from gram-negative bacterial overgrowth (which can follow antibiotic-induced elimination of protective species) activates TLR4 receptors, producing neuroinflammation via IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α. This is associated with the neuroinflammatory signature of major depression.

**Mucosal barrier compromise**: Loss of Akkermansia muciniphila and other barrier-maintaining species increases intestinal permeability, enabling translocation of bacterial fragments that trigger systemic immune activation.
