# Signal Corruption at Scale: Phase Transitions, Information Filtering, and the Retracted-Knowledge Problem in AI Training Corpora

## Abstract

This analysis investigates the structural hypothesis that retracted scientific papers persist in AI training corpora at rates proportional to their original citation velocity, and that confidence-tier annotation at ingestion may produce nonlinear (phase-transition) improvements in citation accuracy. No direct empirical data on retraction database coverage or annotation experiments exists in the available evidence base. However, structural analogs drawn from perceptual neuroscience, immune system biology, epigenetic signal detection, and fractal pattern recognition converge on a multi-mechanism model of corpus contamination that distinguishes between first-order (direct document contamination) and second-order (citation-network-mediated inferential contamination) effects. Three competing hypotheses are evaluated: a conservative linear annotation-benefit model, an integrative phase-transition model, and a radical architectural-discrimination model. The evolved synthesis assigns low confidence to all three pending empirical investigation, but identifies the second-order contamination mechanism as the most underappreciated and potentially most consequential component of the problem.

## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: Linear Annotation Benefit (Conservative, Tier 1)

**Claim:** Retracted papers persist in AI training corpora at rates proportional to their original citation velocity. Confidence-tier annotation at ingestion produces proportional, linear improvements in citation accuracy by enabling retrieval and generation systems to weight or filter flagged documents.

**Mechanism:** Highly cited papers before retraction have high 'copy number' in training corpora — they appear in primary sources, in citing papers, in review articles, in news coverage, in blog posts. Annotation that flags these documents allows downstream systems to discount them in proportion to annotation completeness. This is a data quality problem with a data quality solution.

**Analytical Lenses:** Information theory (signal-to-noise ratio, copy number), control theory (setpoint correction, gain adjustment), signal processing (filter application).

**Falsifiable by:** A systematic mapping study showing retraction persistence is NOT correlated with pre-retraction citation velocity, or an annotation experiment showing no significant improvement in citation accuracy across annotation densities.

### Hypothesis B: Phase Transition at Critical Annotation Threshold (Integrative, Tier 2)

**Claim:** The relationship between confidence-tier annotation density and citation accuracy is nonlinear. Below a critical annotation threshold, the system treats annotations as weak signals absorbed into corpus noise. Above the threshold, the annotation layer reorganizes the retrieval attractor landscape, producing qualitative shifts in citation behavior — the phase-transition the query hypothesizes.

**Mechanism:** LLMs trained on large corpora develop citation behaviors with basin-of-attraction stability. A small number of annotated documents constitutes insufficient perturbation to shift the system from a 'confident-but-wrong' attractor to a 'calibrated-and-accurate' attractor. Above a critical annotation density, the signal constitutes a counter-attractor sufficient to produce basin-switching. This predicts a sigmoidal rather than linear annotation-benefit curve, with a steep inflection point.

**Analytical Lenses:** Phase transitions, chaos attractors (bifurcation points), complexity emergence (qualitative reorganization from quantitative threshold), network theory (hub structure of retracted papers in citation networks).

**Falsifiable by:** An annotation experiment showing smooth, monotonic improvement in citation accuracy across annotation densities with no inflection point and no qualitative reorganization of error patterns.

### Hypothesis C: Architectural Discrimination Requirement (Radical, Tier 3)

**Claim:** The retracted-knowledge problem is not primarily a data-hygiene problem but an architectural one. AI systems trained on large corpora develop something structurally analogous to 'perceptual set' — retracted claims encoded not just as facts but as organizing principles for interpreting subsequent evidence. Confidence-tier labeling at ingestion is necessary but insufficient; genuine epistemic discrimination requires architectural intervention analogous to the immune system's self/non-self distinction.

**Mechanism:** When a retracted paper was highly influential before retraction, it shaped the inferential patterns of thousands of citing papers that remain in the corpus. The model learns not just the retracted claim but the argumentative structures, experimental framings, and inferential moves that the retracted claim enabled. Annotating the source document does not undo this second-order encoding. What is required is an architecture capable of tracking epistemic provenance at the inference level, not just the document level.

**Analytical Lenses:** Fractals (error patterns recur at every scale), chaos attractors (retracted claims as deep attractors), topology and morphogenesis (gradients of influence across citation network), complexity emergence (second-order contamination as emergent from first-order).

**Falsifiable by:** Demonstration that confidence-tier annotation at ingestion, when applied at sufficient density, fully eliminates retraction-derived citation errors — proving the problem is purely about data hygiene.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A
The linear model assumes retracted papers function as isolated bad data points. But the perceptual set formation evidence suggests that highly cited retracted papers may have already shaped the inferential structure of the model through their influence on citing papers that ARE in the corpus. If the Wakefield MMR-autism paper (to use a historically significant example) shaped thousands of subsequent papers, news articles, and policy documents that remain in Common Crawl, annotating the original Lancet paper at ingestion leaves the second-order contamination intact. The linear model underestimates the network depth of high-impact retractions.

### Against Hypothesis B
Phase-transition hypotheses are seductive but require mechanistic grounding. The T-cell and fractal fluency analogies operate in different systems with different mechanisms. The attractor landscape metaphor, while useful, needs specification: what constitutes the 'order parameter' for this phase transition? Citation accuracy alone? Confidence calibration? Both? Without a mechanistic model of why a threshold would exist in AI citation behavior specifically, and at what annotation density it would occur, this hypothesis risks being unfalsifiable in its current form.

### Against Hypothesis C
The architectural hypothesis risks being unfalsifiable in practice: 'insufficient architecture' can accommodate any negative result. The HERV-K analogy is evocative but the mechanism does not transfer cleanly — retroviral sequences function as regulatory elements through specific molecular mechanisms (transcription factor binding sites, PBS features) that have no clear analog in transformer attention weights. 'Architectural discrimination' needs operational specification: retrieval-augmented generation with live retraction database queries? Constitutional AI constraints? Adversarial probing of retracted claims? Without specification, the hypothesis cannot be tested.

## Synthesis

The strongest synthesis integrates elements of all three hypotheses while maintaining epistemic humility about the absence of direct empirical data.

The retracted-knowledge contamination problem is best understood as a multi-scale, multi-mechanism problem:

**Level 1 (Document):** Retracted papers persist in corpora. This is a data hygiene problem addressable through annotation. The benefit is real but may be smaller than anticipated because Level 2 effects dominate for high-impact retractions.

**Level 2 (Citation Network):** High-impact retracted papers have shaped the inferential patterns of citing papers that remain in the corpus. This second-order contamination is not addressed by document-level annotation. It may require either: (a) propagating retraction flags through the citation network to flag downstream citing documents, or (b) architectural interventions that enable epistemic provenance tracking at inference time.

**Level 3 (Attractor Structure):** If LLM citation behavior has attractor-basin structure, annotation density may exhibit threshold effects. This is testable and worth testing directly. The prediction is a sigmoidal benefit curve, not a linear one.

The phase-transition hypothesis is plausible but not established. The architectural hypothesis points to a real gap but is currently too vague to test directly.

## Implications

If Hypothesis B is correct and a phase-transition threshold exists, the practical implication is significant: partial retraction database coverage (which is the current state of most annotation efforts) may produce near-zero improvement in citation accuracy because the system operates below threshold. This would explain why existing retraction flagging efforts have produced inconsistent results in downstream AI output quality. The intervention must be comprehensive to be effective — partial annotation may be worse than useless if it creates false confidence in corpus hygiene.

If Hypothesis C is correct, the field needs to shift from thinking about training data curation as the primary intervention toward thinking about inference-time epistemic auditing — systems that can query live retraction databases, track citation provenance, and flag claims with uncertain epistemic status at generation time rather than training time.

If Hypothesis A is correct, the path forward is relatively clear: systematic mapping of retraction database coverage against major corpora, followed by comprehensive annotation, followed by evaluation. The investment is large but the mechanism is well-understood.

## Open Questions

1. **Coverage:** What percentage of retracted papers in Retraction Watch actually appear in Common Crawl, PubMed subsets, and Semantic Scholar? Is there differential coverage by domain (nutrition, psychology, COVID preprints)?

2. **Citation velocity correlation:** Does retraction persistence in downstream corpora correlate with pre-retraction citation velocity as predicted by Hypothesis A?

3. **Benefit curve shape:** Is the annotation-benefit curve for citation accuracy linear, sigmoidal, or something else? This is the core empirical question for the phase-transition hypothesis.

4. **Second-order contamination magnitude:** Can the citing-paper contamination effect be isolated and measured? A natural experiment would be: remove retracted papers from corpus but retain citing papers, then measure citation accuracy compared to removing both.

5. **Domain specificity:** Are there domains where retraction rates are high enough, and where the retracted claims are influential enough, to constitute a natural experiment for all three hypotheses simultaneously?

6. **Architectural specification:** What would 'architectural discrimination' look like operationally? Is retrieval-augmented generation with live retraction database access sufficient, or is something more fundamental required?

7. **Temporal dynamics:** Retractions happen after training. What is the half-life of a retracted claim's influence in an LLM trained before the retraction, and does fine-tuning with annotation effectively update this?
