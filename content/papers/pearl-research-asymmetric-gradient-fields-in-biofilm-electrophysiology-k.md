## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A (Conservative — Tier 2)
**Claim:** K⁺/NH₄⁺ electrochemical waves in bacterial biofilms propagate with spatially non-uniform amplitude across the biofilm cross-section as a secondary consequence of the well-established metabolic stratification gradient. This amplitude gradient correlates with antibiotic tolerance through ion wave-mediated regulation of metabolic activity and membrane potential.

This hypothesis is conservative because it does not require any new mechanisms — it simply predicts that an existing, well-established biofilm property (metabolic stratification) would produce a secondary consequence in ion wave dynamics. Metabolic stratification across biofilm depth is one of the most robustly documented phenomena in biofilm biology: oxygen gradients, pH gradients, growth rate gradients, and gene expression gradients across depth are all established. If K⁺ and NH₄⁺ waves are coupled to metabolic state — as established in Prindle et al. 2015 for B. subtilis — then metabolic gradient implies wave amplitude gradient.

The antibiotic tolerance correlation follows from existing understanding of metabolic state as a tolerance determinant: cells in low-metabolic-activity states are intrinsically more tolerant of antibiotics that target active processes (cell wall synthesis, protein synthesis, DNA replication). The ion wave amplitude gradient would map onto the metabolic gradient and the tolerance gradient as co-varying indicators of the same underlying state.

**Falsifiable by:** Microelectrode array measurements at defined biofilm depths showing uniform K⁺/NH₄⁺ wave amplitude independent of depth position. Or: antibiotic tolerance profiling across biofilm depth showing no correlation with wave amplitude distribution.

### Hypothesis B (Integrative — Tier 2)
**Claim:** The K⁺/NH₄⁺ wave amplitude gradient creates a mechanistically coupled electrophysiology-lipidome axis: cells in wave-dampened inner zones alter membrane lipid composition (enriching in sphingolipid-like species or longer-chain/branched fatty acids) as a consequence of altered membrane potential dynamics, and this lipid remodeling reduces antibiotic permeability through physical membrane stiffening — creating a 'protective field asymmetry' that is jointly electrophysiological and lipidomic.

This hypothesis introduces the novel claim that ion wave amplitude is upstream of membrane lipid composition changes. The mechanistic logic: reduced membrane potential oscillation → altered membrane tension and fluidity requirements → altered fatty acid synthesis gene expression → enrichment of membrane-stiffening lipid species → reduced antibiotic intercalation. This is consistent with known connections between membrane potential and fatty acid synthesis regulation in bacteria (FabF, FabB regulation by membrane state), but the specific link to ion wave amplitude has not been empirically tested.

The sphingolipid angle is organism-specific: canonical sphingolipid biosynthesis is largely eukaryotic, but several bacterial species (Bacteroides fragilis, Sphingomonas paucimobilis, Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus) do produce bacterial sphingolipids. The hypothesis is most testable in these organisms. In other species, longer-chain branched fatty acids or cyclopropane fatty acids may serve the analogous membrane-stiffening function.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A
The structural analogy from circadian oscillation to biofilm ion wave is imprecise — circadian rhythms are genetically encoded with clear molecular feedback machinery (CLOCK/BMAL1, PER/CRY), while biofilm ion waves are emergent from population-level electrochemical dynamics. The prediction of a spatial gradient in wave amplitude does not follow as tightly from metabolic stratification as claimed, because wave propagation dynamics depend on cell density, matrix conductivity, and ion channel distribution — variables that may partially compensate for metabolic gradients.

*However:* metabolic stratification in biofilms is so robustly established that some secondary consequence in ion wave dynamics is almost inevitable. The question is the direction and magnitude of the effect, not its existence.

### Against Hypothesis B
The sphingolipid coupling hypothesis faces a taxonomic specificity problem: if sphingolipids are only present in a small subset of bacteria, the hypothesis cannot generalize to the most experimentally tractable biofilm organisms (B. subtilis, P. aeruginosa). Even if branched-chain fatty acids substitute functionally, the claimed electrical-lipid coupling pathway requires an unexplored signal transduction axis from ion wave amplitude to fatty acid synthesis gene regulation that has not been characterized.

*However:* cyclopropane fatty acid (CFA) synthase activity in bacteria is known to be regulated by stationary phase and stress signals — if ion wave dampening is a proxy for these states, CFA enrichment in inner biofilm zones is plausible and already partially supported by existing stress-response literature.

### Against Hypothesis C
The persister cell model — the best-characterized form of inner biofilm antibiotic tolerance — is specifically characterized by low metabolic activity and low replication rate. Low replication rate means fewer replication-associated mutation opportunities, not more. The hypothesis may have the mutation rate directionality inverted: inner cells may actually have LOWER basal mutation rates due to reduced replication, with their antibiotic tolerance being replication-independent (metabolic dormancy). SOS-induced hypermutation requires induction by DNA damage, which requires antibiotic exposure, which requires antibiotic penetration — and inner biofilm cells may be shielded enough that SOS is not robustly induced.

*However:* the sublethal exposure angle is important. Cells at the boundary between protective and permeable zones — which might correspond to intermediate wave amplitude positions — could be the actual hypermutation zone, not the deepest core. This refinement makes the hypothesis more nuanced and potentially more testable.

## Implications

If Hypothesis A is confirmed, it provides a mechanistic basis for understanding why biofilm antibiotic tolerance is spatially heterogeneous and suggests that disrupting ion wave propagation (e.g., with ionophores or ion channel modulators) might homogenize metabolic state across the biofilm and reduce tolerance zone formation.

If Hypothesis B is confirmed, it opens a new therapeutic angle: targeting the electrical-lipid coupling axis to prevent inner-zone membrane stiffening, making deep biofilm cells as permeable to antibiotics as outer cells.

If Hypothesis C is confirmed, it has profound implications for understanding how biofilms serve as evolution accelerators — not just as physical barriers to antibiotics but as spatially organized mutation machines that generate resistance diversity while shielding it from selection until dispersal. This would fundamentally reframe biofilm treatment strategy: eradicating the biofilm would need to precede antibiotic treatment, not follow it, because dispersal of the mutation-enriched inner population under antibiotic selection is the worst possible outcome.
