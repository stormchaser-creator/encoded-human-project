### Peripheral Evidence of Relevance

Despite the gap, several entries provide structurally relevant peripheral evidence:

**Stochastic epigenetic divergence (Sapolsky, Tier 1):** Genetically identical cells — and by extension identical twins — develop different gene regulatory profiles due to stochastic events. This establishes a crucial point: phenotypic similarity between unrelated romantic partners cannot be genetic coincidence. It must reflect active selection operating on expressed phenotype, which means assortative mating on physical traits is selecting for something meaningful in the organism's current developmental and regulatory state, not merely its genotype. This strengthens the hypothesis that phenotypic similarity is a signal of shared regulatory architecture.

**Cortisol receptor sensitivity in the hippocampus (Dan Siegel, Tier 1):** Individual differences in HPA axis receptor sensitivity produce measurably different responses to stress hormones. This is relevant because: (a) HRV is a direct window into ANS regulation, which is shaped by HPA axis tone; (b) individual differences in cortisol reactivity are heritable and developmentally programmed; (c) partners selected for phenotypic similarity may share developmental hormonal environments that produce correlated HPA/ANS regulatory profiles.

**Testosterone secular decline (Rhonda Patrick, Tier 2):** Population-level decline in testosterone levels over historical time — while its causes are debated (endocrine disruptors, adiposity, sedentary behavior) — establishes that testosterone is a population-variable hormonal signal closely linked to body morphology. Facial masculinity, muscle mass, waist-to-hip ratio, and height are all testosterone-modulated traits. Assortative mating research on these traits is therefore implicitly studying assortment on hormonal regulatory environments.

**Left hemisphere 'facile vision' (McGilchrist, Tier 2):** The claim that left hemisphere analytical processing produces 'frankly deluded' models of reality by decomposing wholes into discrete measurable parts is structurally relevant to the methodology of assortative mating research. Most phenotypic assortment studies measure traits individually (height separately from BMI separately from facial symmetry) and report trait-specific correlations. McGilchrist's framework predicts that this approach misses the holistic phenotypic gestalt that right-hemisphere-dominant mate selection actually operates through. The unit of assortative selection may be a configurational pattern, not a list of traits.

**Incandescent vs. LED light spectra (Jack Kruse, Tier 2/3):** While speculative in its extension, this entry establishes that biological systems are sensitive to the full spectrum of light — UV, IR, visible — and that modern environments systematically distort these inputs. Biophotonic emission from living cells (a separate but related literature) involves the same spectral ranges. HRV coupling in couples is explained by behavioral synchrony: shared breathing patterns during conversation, synchronized sleep, coordinated activity rhythms, and emotional co-regulation. ## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The most serious objection to Hypothesis A's independence assumption comes from developmental neurobiology and attachment research. Ruth Feldman's work (not directly retrieved but referenced in the research focus) establishes that physiological synchrony between caregiver and infant is a substrate for behavioral coordination — the causal arrow runs from physiological to behavioral, not vice versa. If this bidirectional but physiologically-grounded model extends to adult romantic couples (and Levenson & Gottman's work suggests it does), then behavioral co-regulation in couples may be scaffolded by underlying physiological compatibility rather than producing it. This reopens the question of what determines that compatibility.

Moreover, mere parsimony is insufficient justification for Hypothesis A. The claim that behavioral synchrony 'fully explains' HRV coupling in couples is an empirical assertion that requires demonstration, not assumption.

### Against Hypothesis B

The developmental hormonal programming pathway is mechanistically plausible but empirically underdetermined. Effect sizes in testosterone-morphology correlations are modest. HRV is notoriously sensitive to acute state variables (sleep quality, caffeine, emotional arousal) that could swamp any phenotypic baseline signal. The hypothesis requires a specific causal chain — prenatal testosterone → facial structure AND HPA/ANS setpoint → adult phenotypic similarity AND ANS compatibility — each link of which needs separate empirical support.

However, Hypothesis B generates the most specific and tractable predictions and is the only one with a plausible mechanism that connects known biological facts without requiring new physics. It should be the primary hypothesis for investigation.

### Against Hypothesis C

Hypothesis C imports Jack Kruse's biophotonic framework (Tier 3, speculative, source rated Tier 2 in Pearl's system) into a domain — mate selection and HRV coupling — where it has no direct empirical support. Biophotonic emission from biological tissues is documented, but its magnitude is extremely small (on the order of a few hundred photons per second per square centimeter), and its role in inter-organism signaling in humans is not established. This hypothesis is intellectually provocative and falsifiable, but its prior probability is low given current evidence.

Its value is that it generates an experiment no other framework predicts: measure biophotonic emission profiles between matched and mismatched couples, correlate with HRV coupling. If the null result obtains, it strengthens Hypotheses A and B. If an unexpected correlation appears, it would be genuinely surprising and worth following.

If Hypothesis B is correct, several implications follow:

**For developmental health:** The secular decline in testosterone (Rhonda Patrick entry) implies a systematic shift in the phenotypic landscape on which assortative selection operates. If testosterone-morphology traits are the strongest assortative signal, and these traits are flattening across the population, assortative mating effects may be weakening — with unknown consequences for dyadic physiological coupling at the population level.
