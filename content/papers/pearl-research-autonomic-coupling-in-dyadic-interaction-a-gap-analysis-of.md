## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A (Tier 1 — Conservative)

**Claim:** Levenson & Gottman (1983) used a multi-channel composite linkage approach that combined sympathetic and parasympathetic indices into a single 'physiological linkage' score, did not report RSA/HRV, and primarily studied conflict interaction. This means the paper cannot ground claims about branch-specific coupling.

**Reasoning:** The historical timeline is the strongest support here. Levenson & Gottman published in 1983. Porges' polyvagal theory elaboration that made RSA a standard research variable in relationship research emerged in the mid-to-late 1980s. Before this elaboration, the field used composite autonomic indices. The Gottman Lab's characteristic methodology in this era involved recording multiple channels (skin conductance level, heart rate, finger pulse amplitude/transit time, general somatic activity) and computing inter-spouse correlations across these channels during interaction — but treating them as indicators of general physiological arousal rather than parsing them by autonomic branch.

The conflict-only vs. multi-condition question is partially answerable: early Gottman-Levenson papers did use a recall paradigm (spouse describes a conflict topic while being recorded) and sometimes included a neutral condition, but the primary condition of interest — and the one that produced the notable linkage findings — was the conflict interaction.

**What this means for the research arc:** If Hypothesis A is correct, then Levenson & Gottman (1983) is a foundational paper for the concept of dyadic physiological linkage as a predictor of relationship outcomes, but it cannot distinguish whether that linkage reflects sympathetic or parasympathetic co-activation. The sympathetic-coupling reconciliation hypothesis (that distressed couples show SNS-dominant coupling) requires a different paper.

### Hypothesis B (Tier 2 — Integrative)

**Claim:** Feldman (2007) operationalized mother-infant synchrony using heart rate change as a proxy variable that conflates both autonomic branches, did not distinguish RSA as a separate predictor, and the synchrony index was behaviorally dominant with physiological synchrony as a secondary component. Maternal RSA as a predictor of synchrony quality is a hypothesis that belongs to the generation of research following Feldman (2007), not to the paper itself.

**Reasoning:** Feldman's synchrony paradigm, as described across her published work, centers on behavioral coding (gaze, affect, touch, proximity) with physiological co-variation as a correlate rather than a primary dependent variable. The 2007 paper in particular is associated with the developmental consequences of synchrony — its role in attachment, socioemotional development, and regulatory capacity in the infant. The physiological component, when present, is typically heart rate co-variation computed over observation epochs, which does not permit branch-specific analysis without impedance cardiography.

The question of whether maternal RSA predicts synchrony quality is theoretically compelling — a mother with higher vagal tone (higher RSA) should theoretically have greater regulatory flexibility and therefore greater capacity for co-regulation. **What this means for the research arc:** If Hypothesis B is correct, then Feldman (2007) grounds the existence and developmental importance of mother-infant physiological synchrony but cannot speak to the autonomic branch question. ## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The strongest objection is that Porges (1976) had already published the concept of RSA as a measure of vagal tone, and some psychophysiology labs in the early 1980s were computing RSA from HR time series. If Levenson and Gottman had a sophisticated physiological recording setup (which they did — the Gottman Lab was well-resourced), they may have computed RSA even if it wasn't the headline finding. The absence of RSA in secondary citations does not prove absence in the paper itself — it may reflect that subsequent citees were interested in the linkage construct, not the branch composition.

However, the strongest support for Hypothesis A is methodological context: the standard psychophysiological battery of the early 1980s was designed around arousal-level measurement, not branch differentiation. The skin conductance level (SCL) — one of the most commonly cited variables in Gottman Lab research of this era — is a pure sympathetic measure, but it was used as an indicator of 'emotional flooding' rather than as part of a branch-differentiation analysis.

### Against Hypothesis B

By 2007, portable heart rate monitors and software for computing HRV metrics were widely available. Feldman's lab, located at Bar-Ilan University with strong developmental neuroscience infrastructure, may well have computed HRV from continuous HR recordings. The question is whether it was computed and reported in the specific 2007 paper in question, or whether it appears in parallel publications from the same dataset.

The strongest support for Hypothesis B is that the mother-infant synchrony literature of this era consistently describes synchrony in behavioral terms first and physiological terms second, and the physiological measures used are typically epoch-level HR co-variation rather than spectral HRV analysis. This is a different technical operation from computing RSA, and the absence of RSA-specific analysis in this literature is consistent with the behavioral primacy of the synchrony construct.

### Against Hypothesis C

The arousal valence hypothesis is conceptually attractive but may lose explanatory precision that matters clinically and scientifically. Sympathetic activation during conflict involves cortisol, norepinephrine, and peripheral vasoconstriction — physiological states with long-term health consequences when chronic. Parasympathetic activation involves different neurotransmitters, different organ effects, and different recovery dynamics. Collapsing these into 'arousal valence' may obscure mechanisms that predict cardiovascular disease, immune function, and metabolic health in long-term relationship research (which is precisely what Levenson & Gottman's longitudinal follow-ups examined).

The structural analogy from non-relationship biology (psilocybin setpoints, microglial rhythms) may not transfer cleanly because dyadic coupling involves two organisms with potentially different autonomic profiles interacting, whereas the single-organism regulatory systems are operating within a unified homeostatic field.

## Synthesis

The most defensible evolved position is a three-part synthesis:

**Part 1:** Levenson & Gottman (1983) established that dyadic physiological linkage exists and predicts relationship outcomes, using composite autonomic measures that did not distinguish branches. RSA was almost certainly not reported. The paper is Tier 1 for the existence of physiological linkage but cannot ground branch-specific reconciliation hypotheses.

**Part 2:** Feldman (2007) established that mother-infant physiological synchrony has developmental consequences, using HR co-variation as a composite measure. Maternal RSA as a predictor of synchrony quality is a hypothesis that follows logically from Feldman's framework but requires its own evidence base.

**Part 3:** The sympathetic-coupling reconciliation hypothesis (Hypotheses A/B in the original research arc) requires redirection to: (a) later Gottman Lab papers introducing impedance cardiography (likely Gottman & Levenson, 1988, or Gottman et al., 1995); (b) the maternal RSA/synchrony quality literature (Moore et al., 2009; Beauchaine, 2015; or later Feldman work); and (c) possible reconceptualization around arousal valence rather than branch identity.
