# The Light Machine — SECTION 4
## Hormones: Sex & Anabolic Operations

The body's sex hormones and anabolic pathways are the signature of reproductive capacity, muscle building, bone integrity, and neurological resilience. These hormones don't just govern sexuality—they are the master builders of tissue, the organizers of energy distribution, and the architects of stress resilience. Dysfunction here manifests as muscle loss, bone fragility, sexual dysfunction, depression, and accelerated aging. The body codex calls this the **Synthesis** operation: the creation of new tissue, repair of damaged protein, and the sustained building of biological infrastructure.

---

## Testosterone, Total

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Total testosterone concentration in serum; includes testosterone bound to SHBG, albumin, and free testosterone.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No, but morning draw preferred (testosterone peaks at dawn)

### What This Number Means

Testosterone is the primary male sex hormone and the primary anabolic hormone in the body (present in both men and women, but at much higher levels in men). It's synthesized in the testes (men), ovaries (women), and adrenal glands (both sexes). Total testosterone is the sum of all three forms: testosterone bound to sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), testosterone bound to albumin, and free testosterone.

The distinction matters because only free and weakly-bound testosterone can actually activate receptors on cells. Testosterone bound tightly to SHBG is functionally unavailable. So a person can have "normal" total testosterone but terrible free testosterone if SHBG is elevated. This is why total testosterone alone is incomplete—you need free testosterone to understand bioavailability.

Total testosterone has a clear diurnal rhythm, peaking in the early morning and declining through the day. A single midday measurement can underestimate true testosterone by 20-30% compared to an 8 AM draw. This is why the timing of the test matters.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 300 ng/dL | > 500 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 300-1000 ng/dL | 600-800 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 1000-1200 ng/dL | > 1200 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 1200 ng/dL | > 1500 ng/dL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 15 ng/dL | > 30 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 15-70 ng/dL | 40-70 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 70-100 ng/dL | > 100 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 100 ng/dL | > 150 ng/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Primary anabolic hormone and tissue-building signal**

Total testosterone is the signature of your synthesis operation's capacity. At optimal levels, it signals your body to build muscle, maintain bone density, sustain neurological resilience (mood, cognition, confidence), and support healthy sexual function. Below optimal, every tissue-building process slows: muscle atrophies, bones weaken, mood flattens, sexual function declines, and recovery from training becomes sluggish.

In men, testosterone decline is one of the primary drivers of accelerated aging after age 30. Free testosterone typically drops 1% per year, and by age 60, most men are functionally testosterone-deficient. In women, testosterone is less abundant but equally important: it drives clitoral sensitivity, maintains muscle and bone, and supports confidence and assertiveness.

Total testosterone is the starting point, but it's incomplete without knowing SHBG and free testosterone. A person with "high" total testosterone due to high SHBG but low free testosterone will experience all the symptoms of deficiency.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 1000 ng/dL men, > 80 ng/dL women):**
- Possible exogenous testosterone use or anabolic steroid administration
- May indicate androgen-secreting tumor (adrenal or gonadal; rare)
- In women, may indicate PCOS or adrenal dysfunction
- Can drive aggressive behavior, acne, and male-pattern hair growth
- May accelerate hair loss in predisposed individuals
- Can increase cardiovascular risk if severely elevated and unmanaged

**If deficient (< 300 ng/dL men, < 30 ng/dL women):**
- Hypogonadism (primary testicular/ovarian dysfunction) or secondary (pituitary/hypothalamic)
- Chronic illness, malnutrition, or severe caloric restriction
- Excess body fat (adipose tissue produces aromatase, converting testosterone to estrogen)
- Chronic stress or sleep deprivation (elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone production)
- Medications (opioids, antipsychotics, some antidepressants) that suppress production
- Age-related decline (normal but not optimal past age 40)
- Tissue-building capacity is significantly impaired; recovery from training is slow

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Free Testosterone** — Bioavailable testosterone; total without free is incomplete.
- **SHBG** — High SHBG can trap testosterone; look at the ratio.
- **Estradiol** — High estradiol can suppress testosterone production via negative feedback.
- **LH and FSH** — Reveal whether low testosterone is central (low LH) or peripheral (high LH with low testosterone).
**Key pattern:** Total testosterone + free testosterone + SHBG tell the full story. A man with "normal" total testosterone but high SHBG and low free testosterone is functionally deficient.

### Intervention Levers
Total testosterone responds powerfully to: resistance training (especially heavy compound movements), adequate sleep (> 7 hours, consistent bedtime), zinc and vitamin D sufficiency, reduced body fat (if obese), and intermittent fasting (acutely elevates testosterone). Omega-3 fatty acids and a diet high in cholesterol (the raw material for testosterone synthesis) also help. Avoid chronic caloric restriction, excessive aerobic training (which suppresses testosterone), and chronic stress. Results typically appear within 6-12 weeks.

## Testosterone, Free, Direct

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Free testosterone; the unbound fraction that can activate androgen receptors on cells.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No, but morning draw preferred

### What This Number Means

Free testosterone is the bioavailable fraction—the only form that actually matters clinically. It's typically 0.5-3% of total testosterone in men and 1-4% in women. The rest is bound to SHBG or albumin and functionally trapped.

When you test free testosterone, you're measuring the hormone that's actually doing work: activating muscle protein synthesis, signaling bone-building, supporting sexual function, and promoting neurological resilience. A person can have "normal" total testosterone and be profoundly testosterone-deficient if SHBG is trapping most of it.

Free testosterone is measured by equilibrium dialysis (the gold standard) or calculated from total testosterone and SHBG using the Vermeulen equation. Equilibrium dialysis is more accurate, especially at the low end of the range where calculation errors are largest. If you're getting free testosterone tested, insist on direct measurement, not calculation.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 5 pg/mL | > 8 pg/mL |
| Optimal | 5-21 pg/mL | 12-18 pg/mL |
| Elevated concern | 21-30 pg/mL | > 25 pg/mL |
| Critical | > 30 pg/mL | > 40 pg/mL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 0.5 pg/mL | > 1.0 pg/mL |
| Optimal | 0.5-4.3 pg/mL | 1.5-3.0 pg/mL |
| Elevated concern | 4.3-7 pg/mL | > 4 pg/mL |
| Critical | > 7 pg/mL | > 10 pg/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Bioavailable anabolic signal and cellular testosterone receptor activation**

Free testosterone is the torch that lights the synthesis operation. Every cell with androgen receptors listens to free testosterone: muscle, bone, brain, skin, sexual organs. When free testosterone is optimal, those cells receive a strong signal to build and repair. When free testosterone is low, they receive almost nothing, and tissue-building slows dramatically.

The critical insight is that free testosterone can be low even when total testosterone is normal if SHBG is high. This is common in metabolic dysfunction, high estrogen states, and aging. A man with a "normal" testosterone panel but low free testosterone and high SHBG will experience all the symptoms of deficiency: weak recovery, low mood, sexual dysfunction, accelerated muscle loss. The conventional lab would say "your testosterone is normal"—but he's not. His SHBG is trapping it.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 25 pg/mL men, > 4 pg/mL women):**
- Exogenous testosterone use or anabolic steroid administration
- Androgen-secreting tumor
- In women, likely PCOS or significant adrenal dysfunction
- Can accelerate hair loss, increase acne, drive aggressive mood changes
- May increase cardiovascular risk if chronically elevated
- Sexual function is heightened; libido is very high

**If deficient (< 5 pg/mL men, < 1 pg/mL women):**
- Hypogonadism; gonads are not producing adequate testosterone
- Likely secondary hypogonadism (pituitary/hypothalamic) if LH is also low
- Possible primary hypogonadism (testicular failure) if LH is high with low testosterone
- Muscle recovery is severely impaired; training ROI is poor
- Bone density is at risk; osteoporosis risk increases
- Mood is likely depressed; sexual function is impaired
- Neurological resilience is compromised; stress tolerance is low

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Total Testosterone and SHBG** — Calculate the free testosterone percentage; if < 1%, SHBG is high.
- **LH (Luteinizing Hormone)** — If free testosterone is low but LH is high, primary hypogonadism (gonadal failure). If low with low LH, secondary hypogonadism (central).
- **Estradiol** — High estradiol can suppress testosterone production and elevate SHBG.
**Key pattern:** Total testosterone + SHBG + free testosterone together reveal the true status. A man with total testosterone 500, SHBG 100 (high), and free testosterone 3 pg/mL is significantly testosterone-deficient despite "normal" total levels.

### Intervention Levers
Free testosterone responds to: resistance training (heavy compound movements drive FSH/LH axis), adequate sleep (sleep deprivation acutely lowers free testosterone), zinc and vitamin D sufficiency, elevated body fat reduction, intermittent fasting, and omega-3 supplementation. SHBG elevation can be driven down by reducing excess estrogen (avoiding endocrine disruptors, optimizing liver function) and improving insulin sensitivity. Results typically appear within 8-12 weeks with consistent adherence.

## Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin (SHBG)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** SHBG concentration in serum; a binding protein that carries sex hormones and regulates their bioavailability.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

SHBG is a transport protein produced by the liver. It binds tightly to testosterone, DHT, and estradiol, carrying them through the bloodstream and making them unavailable to cells. SHBG is not a hormone itself—it's a regulator of hormone availability.

When SHBG is high, it traps sex hormones, and tissue doesn't receive the signal. When SHBG is low, sex hormones are more bioavailable, and tissues receive stronger signals. SHBG rises and falls based on: estrogen levels (estrogen increases SHBG), thyroid function (hypothyroidism increases SHBG), liver health (cirrhosis decreases SHBG), insulin sensitivity (insulin resistance decreases SHBG), and age (SHBG increases with age, especially in men).

This is why SHBG is critical to understand. Someone can have "normal" testosterone but be functionally deficient because SHBG is trapping it. Or someone can have "low" total testosterone but actually be fine because SHBG is low and free testosterone is normal. SHBG is the translator: it tells you what fraction of your hormones are actually working.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 10 nmol/L | 25-40 nmol/L |
| Optimal | 10-57 nmol/L | 25-45 nmol/L |
| Elevated concern | 57-80 nmol/L | > 50 nmol/L |
| Critical | > 80 nmol/L | > 70 nmol/L |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 20 nmol/L | 40-70 nmol/L |
| Optimal | 20-122 nmol/L | 50-90 nmol/L |
| Elevated concern | 122-180 nmol/L | > 100 nmol/L |
| Critical | > 180 nmol/L | > 150 nmol/L |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Sex hormone bioavailability and hormonal signal transduction**

SHBG is the regulatory valve on the sex hormone system. When SHBG is optimal, sex hormones circulate freely enough to be biologically active but stable enough to maintain steady signaling. When SHBG is elevated, hormones are bound and signals are muted. When SHBG is low, hormones are hyperavailable and signals are exaggerated.

The clinical consequence is that SHBG tells you whether the sex hormone panel you're looking at is true. A woman with elevated estradiol and elevated SHBG might actually have normal free estradiol. A man with "normal" testosterone and elevated SHBG is actually deficient in free testosterone. SHBG is the lens through which all other hormone results are read.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 50 nmol/L men, > 100 nmol/L women):**
- Estrogen is elevated; estrogen is the primary driver of SHBG increase
- Possible hypothyroidism; elevated TSH drives SHBG production
- Liver dysfunction (hepatitis, cirrhosis) can increase SHBG in some contexts
- Sex hormone bioavailability is reduced; effective hormone signaling is muted
- Functional testosterone deficiency may be present despite normal total testosterone
- Sexual function is likely impaired; libido is reduced
- In women, may correlate with PCOS if SHBG is paradoxically high (unusual)

**If deficient (< 25 nmol/L men, < 40 nmol/L women):**
- Insulin resistance is likely present; high insulin is the primary driver of SHBG suppression
- Possible hyperthyroidism or excess thyroid hormone
- Metabolic dysfunction is present; SHBG suppression is a marker of insulin resistance
- Sex hormone bioavailability is high; signals are strong
- Free testosterone is elevated relative to total; tissue receives strong anabolic signals
- In men, risk of excessive androgen signaling (acne, hair loss, aggression)
- In women, PCOS is more likely; SHBG suppression is characteristic of PCOS

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Total and Free Sex Hormones** — SHBG determines the ratio; high SHBG suppresses free hormone percentage.
- **Fasting Insulin** — SHBG is inversely related to insulin; insulin resistance suppresses SHBG.
- **Thyroid Panel (TSH, Free T4, Free T3)** — Thyroid dysfunction drives SHBG changes.
- **Estradiol** — High estradiol is the primary driver of elevated SHBG.
**Key pattern:** The free hormone percentage (free/total × 100) tells you how much hormone is actually available. If it's < 1% in men, SHBG is elevated and binding most of it.

### Intervention Levers
SHBG responds powerfully to: insulin sensitivity improvements (fasting, resistance training, omega-3s lower insulin and SHBG), thyroid optimization (treat hypothyroidism if present), estrogen reduction (optimize liver detoxification, reduce xenoestrogens), and weight loss if overweight (especially visceral fat loss). SHBG typically takes 6-12 weeks to respond, but the shift can be dramatic: insulin drops, SHBG rises or falls appropriately, and free hormone bioavailability normalizes.

## Estradiol (E2)

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Estradiol concentration in serum; the primary circulating estrogen in both men and women.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Estradiol is one of three forms of estrogen (the others being estrone and estriol). It's synthesized in the ovaries (women), testes (men, in small amounts), and adipose tissue (both sexes). Estradiol is the most potent and most studied form of estrogen.

In women, estradiol peaks during the follicular and luteal phases and is essential for bone density, cardiovascular health, cognitive function, and sexual function. In men, estradiol is produced in much smaller amounts but still plays important roles in bone health, mood, and sexual function. The balance between testosterone and estradiol in men matters: too much estradiol (relative to testosterone) can cause gynecomastia, reduced libido, and sexual dysfunction.

Estradiol is often misunderstood. It's not inherently "bad" in men—it's necessary. The problem is excess estradiol relative to testosterone, or absolute estradiol that's too high.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 10-40 pg/mL | 15-25 pg/mL |
| Optimal | 10-40 pg/mL | 18-24 pg/mL |
| Elevated concern | 40-60 pg/mL | > 30 pg/mL |
| Critical | > 60 pg/mL | > 50 pg/mL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women, Follicular Phase)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 30-100 pg/mL | 50-100 pg/mL |
| Optimal | 100-300 pg/mL | 150-250 pg/mL |
| Elevated concern | 300-400 pg/mL | > 300 pg/mL |
| Critical | > 400 pg/mL | > 400 pg/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Bone, vascular, neurological, and sexual integrity in both sexes**

Estradiol is the architect of bone density, vascular elasticity, and cognitive resilience in both men and women. It's not a "female hormone"—it's a human hormone that happens to be more abundant in women. In men, it's essential for bone integrity, mood regulation, and sexual function. The problem in men arises when estradiol is excessive (usually because of high body fat, poor liver function, or overtreatment with testosterone replacement).

In women, estradiol fluctuates across the cycle, and this rhythm is essential for reproductive health. Estradiol that's too low (as in perimenopause) triggers hot flashes, vaginal dryness, and bone loss. Estradiol that's too high (as in estrogen dominance or PCOS) can drive breast tenderness, water retention, and mood disturbance.

The key insight is that estradiol is dose-dependent and context-dependent. It's not good or bad—it's optimal when it's in the right range for your sex, age, and metabolic state.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 30 pg/mL men, > 300 pg/mL women):**
- Excessive body fat; adipose tissue produces aromatase, converting testosterone to estradiol
- Poor liver function; liver is responsible for estrogen metabolism and clearance
- High carbohydrate diet with poor insulin control; insulin drives aromatase activity
- In men, risk of gynecomastia, reduced sexual function, and mood disturbance
- In women, risk of breast tenderness, water retention, estrogen-dependent tumors
- Elevated SHBG (estrogen drives SHBG production; feedback loop)
- Possible hormone supplementation if on HRT or exogenous testosterone

**If deficient (< 15 pg/mL men, < 50 pg/mL women):**
- In women, possible anovulation (not ovulating), ovarian failure, or perimenopause
- In men, possible primary hypogonadism or secondary hypogonadism (if testosterone is low)
- Bone density is at risk; both sexes need some estradiol for bone integrity
- In women, risk of hot flashes, vaginal dryness, accelerated bone loss, and mood disturbance
- In men, may correlate with depression and reduced sexual function
- Possible excessive aromatase inhibitor use (if on treatment)

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Total and Free Testosterone** — The testosterone:estradiol ratio matters; high ratio can mask estradiol excess.
- **SHBG** — Estradiol drives SHBG; if estradiol is high and SHBG is elevated, both are driving hormone unavailability.
- **Progesterone** — In women, the progesterone:estradiol ratio is important; progesterone rises to balance estradiol.
- **Body Composition** — Excess adipose tissue drives aromatase activity and estradiol production.
**Key pattern:** Men with estradiol > 30 pg/mL and high body fat should focus on body fat reduction and liver support before considering any pharmaceutical intervention.

### Intervention Levers
Estradiol responds to: body fat reduction (especially visceral fat), liver support (cruciferous vegetables, milk thistle, NAC), improved insulin sensitivity (fasting, resistance training), omega-3 supplementation, and DIM (diindolylmethane) or calcium d-glucarate (which support estrogen conjugation). Reducing seed oils and refined carbohydrates (which drive aromatase activity) also helps. Results typically appear within 8-12 weeks.

## Progesterone

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Serum progesterone concentration; the primary progestin in women, produced by corpus luteum after ovulation.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No, but luteal-phase draw preferred (typically day 21 of 28-day cycle)

### What This Number Means

Progesterone is synthesized by the corpus luteum (the remnant of the ovarian follicle after the egg is released). It rises after ovulation and remains elevated for ~14 days until the corpus luteum degenerates and menstruation begins. In men, progesterone is present in very small amounts and is primarily a precursor to other hormones.

Progesterone's role is to prepare and maintain the uterine lining for pregnancy, suppress uterine contractions, and balance estradiol. It's calming (increases GABA, the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter), promotes sleep, and supports mood. Low progesterone correlates with anxiety, insomnia, and poor mood stability.

Progesterone rises dramatically in pregnancy (from 25 ng/mL at ovulation to 100+ ng/mL in the third trimester). In women taking hormonal contraceptives, progesterone is nearly absent because the synthetic progestin (not the same as progesterone) suppresses ovulation.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women, Luteal Phase)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 3 ng/mL | > 10 ng/mL |
| Optimal | 3-25 ng/mL | 15-25 ng/mL |
| Elevated concern | 25-35 ng/mL | > 30 ng/mL |
| Critical | > 35 ng/mL | > 50 ng/mL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women, Follicular Phase/Non-Ovulating)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | < 1 ng/mL | < 0.5 ng/mL |
| Optimal | < 1 ng/mL | < 0.5 ng/mL |
| Elevated concern | 1-3 ng/mL | > 0.5 ng/mL |
| Critical | > 3 ng/mL | > 2 ng/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Ovulation, uterine receptivity, and neuroendocrine balance in women**

Progesterone is the signature of ovulation. A woman with a robust rise in progesterone in the luteal phase has ovulated and has a functionally healthy reproductive cycle. A woman with progesterone that fails to rise has anovulated—no egg was released—and her cycle is nonfunctional.

In the luteal phase, progesterone's role is to create uterine receptivity (a hospitality state for a potential embryo) and to balance estradiol's proliferative drive. Progesterone is also profoundly neuroactive: it increases GABA (calming), promotes sleep quality, and stabilizes mood. Women often report their best mood, sleep, and sense of groundedness in the high-progesterone luteal phase.

Progesterone that's too low in the luteal phase (a state called luteal phase defect or corpus luteum insufficiency) results in short luteal phases, inadequate uterine preparation, and poor mood/sleep in the second half of the cycle.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 25 ng/mL luteal, > 0.5 ng/mL follicular):**
- In luteal phase, may indicate prolonged corpus luteum function (rare)
- Possible pregnancy (progesterone rises to 150+ ng/mL in pregnancy)
- In follicular phase, suggests anovulation with unopposed estradiol (persistent follicle)
- Can cause breast tenderness, bloating, and mood heaviness if excessive in luteal phase

**If deficient (< 10 ng/mL luteal, normal if < 0.5 ng/mL follicular):**
- Anovulation; no ovulation occurred; corpus luteum was never formed
- Possible luteal phase defect; corpus luteum is failing to produce adequate progesterone
- Hormonal contraceptive use; synthetic progestins suppress ovulation and natural progesterone
- Poor luteal phase; mood, sleep, and metabolic stability are compromised
- Possible ovarian insufficiency; corpus luteum is structurally or functionally deficient
- Infertility likely if attempting pregnancy; inadequate progesterone prevents implantation
- Bone density may be at risk; progesterone (like estradiol) supports bone integrity

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Estradiol, measured in same cycle phase** — Progesterone:estradiol ratio matters; progesterone should rise relative to estradiol in luteal phase.
- **Cycle history** — When are periods? Are they regular? Does the woman ovulate (subjective ovulation signs: basal body temperature rise, cervical mucus changes, ovulation pain)?
- **LH and FSH** — If progesterone is low, check LH at ovulation (should surge); if LH surge didn't happen, anovulation is confirmed.
- **Menstrual cycle length** — Luteal phase is typically 14 days; if periods are shorter (< 10 days), luteal defect is likely.
**Key pattern:** Progesterone must be drawn in the luteal phase (typically day 21 of 28-day cycle) to be meaningful. A follicular-phase progesterone is nearly always low and tells you nothing about ovulation.

### Intervention Levers
Progesterone production is driven by: confirmed ovulation (must have ovulated to make progesterone), luteal-phase support (vitamin E, selenium, vitex in some cases), adequate energy availability (caloric restriction suppresses ovulation and progesterone), consistent sleep, and stress management (chronic stress suppresses corpus luteum function). If anovulation is the problem, interventions like seed cycling, inositol supplementation (especially for PCOS), and addressing metabolic dysfunction (insulin resistance, low adiponectin) can restore ovulation.

## Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** DHT concentration in serum; a potent metabolite of testosterone produced by 5-alpha reductase enzyme.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

DHT is formed when testosterone is metabolized by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase. It's 2-3 times more potent than testosterone and is the primary androgen in prostate, skin, and hair follicles. DHT is essential for male sexual development and secondary sexual characteristics (facial hair, body hair, vocal deepening).

The problem arises when DHT is excessive relative to testosterone, or when someone has genetic sensitivity to DHT (5-alpha reductase hyperactivity or androgen receptor hypersensitivity). High DHT in men correlates with male-pattern baldness and benign prostate hyperplasia. Paradoxically, DHT is also essential for prostate function and sexual function—the problem is excess, not presence.

In women, elevated DHT (relative to normal female levels) can occur in PCOS or other androgen-excess states and can drive male-pattern baldness, acne, and hirsutism (excess facial/body hair).

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 30 ng/dL | 30-60 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 30-85 ng/dL | 40-70 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 85-120 ng/dL | > 80 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 120 ng/dL | > 150 ng/dL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 2.4 ng/dL | > 4 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 2.4-10 ng/dL | 4-8 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 10-15 ng/dL | > 12 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 15 ng/dL | > 20 ng/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Androgen potency and tissue-specific androgenic signaling**

DHT is the concentrated form of androgenic signaling, particularly in tissues with high 5-alpha reductase activity: prostate, skin, scalp hair follicles. A man with optimal DHT has strong sexual function, robust body hair, and minimal hair loss (if not genetically predisposed). Above optimal, DHT excess can accelerate hair loss and contribute to benign prostate hyperplasia.

The key is that DHT and testosterone tell different stories. A man can have normal testosterone but excessive DHT (high 5-alpha reductase activity), or normal DHT but excessive testosterone. The former is more likely to experience hair loss; the latter is more likely to experience muscle gain and strong libido.

In women, elevated DHT (even within the "normal female range") combined with skin symptoms (acne, hirsutism, male-pattern baldness) suggests androgen excess and warrants further evaluation for PCOS or other androgen-secreting conditions.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 80 ng/dL men, > 12 ng/dL women):**
- In men, if genetically predisposed to androgenic alopecia, hair loss is likely accelerating
- Possible benign prostate hyperplasia (BPH) risk; DHT drives prostate growth
- In women, likely PCOS or androgen-secreting tumor; manifests as acne, hirsutism, male-pattern baldness
- In both sexes, may indicate 5-alpha reductase hyperactivity or increased enzyme expression
- Can indicate exogenous testosterone use (often converts to excess DHT if 5-alpha reductase activity is high)

**If deficient (< 30 ng/dL men, < 4 ng/dL women):**
- In men, possible 5-alpha reductase deficiency or inhibitor use (finasteride/dutasteride)
- Sexual function may be impaired; DHT is essential for libido and erectile function
- Prostate health is generally better; DHT is a driver of prostate enlargement
- Hair loss may slow if it was DHT-driven
- In women, may indicate adrenal insufficiency or PCOS with low androgens (less common)

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Total and Free Testosterone** — The testosterone:DHT ratio reveals 5-alpha reductase activity level.
- **Estradiol** — High estradiol can suppress DHT production by feedback inhibition of the androgenic axis.
- **Prostate-specific antigen (PSA)** — DHT drives prostate growth; DHT + elevated PSA warrants prostate evaluation.
- **Hair loss history** — Is hair loss androgenic (male-pattern pattern) or diffuse? DHT-driven loss is androgenic pattern.
**Key pattern:** A man with testosterone 700, DHT 120, and a family history of male-pattern baldness is likely experiencing DHT-driven hair loss, even though androgens are normal overall. 5-alpha reductase inhibitors may slow loss.

### Intervention Levers
DHT responds to: 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride; pharmaceutical approach), zinc supplementation (inhibits 5-alpha reductase mildly), saw palmetto (weak 5-alpha reductase inhibition), and reducing excess body fat (adipose tissue can increase aromatase, shifting the testosterone:DHT ratio toward testosterone). For hair loss, combination approaches (topical minoxidil + finasteride + dermarolling) are most effective. Acceptance of androgenic alopecia is also valid—not everyone chooses to treat it.

## Pregnenolone

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Pregnenolone concentration in serum; the first steroid hormone synthesized from cholesterol in the steroidogenic pathway.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Pregnenolone is the mother hormone: it's the first steroid synthesized from cholesterol in the mitochondria of steroid-producing cells (adrenals, gonads). All other steroid hormones—testosterone, estradiol, progesterone, cortisol, DHEA—are made downstream from pregnenolone.

Pregnenolone itself has biological activity: it enhances nerve myelination (insulation of nerve fibers), supports memory and cognitive function, and has mild immune-modulating effects. But its primary role is as a hub: when pregnenolone is abundant, the body can synthesize adequate amounts of both cortisol (stress response) and sex hormones (reproduction). When pregnenolone is depleted, the body must choose: prioritize stress response (cortisol) or reproduction (sex hormones). Chronic stress depletes pregnenolone, forcing the body to sacrifice reproductive hormones.

Pregnenolone is thus a marker of steroidogenic capacity. Low pregnenolone signals that the raw material for hormone synthesis is limited—either adrenal output is low, or all the pregnenolone is being shunted toward cortisol production.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 50 ng/dL | > 100 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 50-200 ng/dL | 120-200 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 200-300 ng/dL | > 250 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 300 ng/dL | > 400 ng/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Steroidogenic capacity and hormonal pathway abundance**

Pregnenolone is the gauge of your adrenal glands' ability to manufacture hormones. When pregnenolone is high, the adrenals are producing ample raw material, and the body can meet both stress (cortisol) and reproductive (sex hormone) demands. When pregnenolone is low, the adrenals are struggling, and the body is forced to prioritize cortisol over sex hormones.

This is why chronic stress causes reproductive dysfunction: if cortisol demand is high, pregnenolone gets shunted toward cortisol synthesis, and sex hormone synthesis declines. A woman under chronic stress may develop amenorrhea or anovulation. A man may develop erectile dysfunction and low libido. The mechanism is pregnenolone depletion and shunted steroidogenesis.

Pregnenolone restoration requires both adrenal support (rest, stress management, nutrient sufficiency) and time—it can take 3-6 months for pregnenolone to recover after chronic stress.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 250 ng/dL):**
- Possible adrenal tumor (rare)
- May indicate exogenous pregnenolone supplementation
- Unusual; true elevated pregnenolone is uncommon

**If deficient (< 50 ng/dL):**
- Adrenal fatigue or insufficiency; glands are not producing adequate pregnenolone
- Chronic stress has depleted pregnenolone; cortisol is being prioritized
- Sex hormone synthesis is compromised; reproductive function may be impaired
- Reproductive dysfunction likely (amenorrhea, anovulation, erectile dysfunction, low libido)
- Cognitive function may be impaired; pregnenolone supports memory and attention
- Immune function is likely compromised; pregnenolone has immune-modulating effects
- Recovery fatigue is present; progesterone and other hormones are inadequate for restoration

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Cortisol (AM and diurnal)** — If pregnenolone is low and cortisol is high, steroidogenic priority is skewed toward stress.
- **Sex hormones (testosterone, estradiol, progesterone)** — If pregnenolone is low and sex hormones are also low, adrenal insufficiency is confirmed.
- **DHEA-S** — DHEA is also made from pregnenolone; if pregnenolone is low, DHEA-S is usually low too.
- **Stress history** — Acute or chronic stress explains pregnenolone depletion.
**Key pattern:** The pregnenolone:cortisol ratio (if cortisol is measured) reveals priority allocation. High cortisol + low pregnenolone = chronic stress.

### Intervention Levers
Pregnenolone restoration requires: stress reduction (meditation, breathwork, community), adequate sleep (> 7 hours, consistent), nutrient sufficiency (B vitamins, vitamin C, magnesium, zinc), reduced exercise intensity if overtraining (replace intense cardio with strength and walking), and time. Pregnenolone supplementation (100-200 mg daily) can jumpstart recovery if adrenal insufficiency is severe, but it's not a substitute for stress reduction. Results typically take 8-12 weeks.

## DHEA-Sulfate (DHEA-S)

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** DHEA-sulfate concentration in serum; the sulfated metabolite of DHEA, the most abundant steroid hormone in the body.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone) and its sulfated form, DHEA-S, are the most abundant steroid hormones in the body. They're produced primarily by the adrenal cortex and have roles in immune function, metabolic resilience, bone health, mood regulation, and sexual function. DHEA itself is relatively unstable, so it's rapidly sulfated to DHEA-S, which is more stable and persists longer in the bloodstream.

DHEA-S declines predictably with age: from peak levels in the early 20s, it drops ~10% per decade of life. By age 70, most people have DHEA-S levels that are 70% lower than peak. This decline correlates with accelerated aging, increased infection risk, declining muscle mass, and mood changes.

DHEA-S is thus a marker of adrenal reserve and resilience. Unlike cortisol (which spikes acutely with stress), DHEA-S reflects the adrenal gland's capacity for anabolic, restorative hormones. Low DHEA-S in a young person signals adrenal dysfunction or chronic stress.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 250 mcg/dL | > 350 mcg/dL |
| Optimal | 250-1000 mcg/dL | 400-800 mcg/dL |
| Elevated concern | 1000-1200 mcg/dL | > 1000 mcg/dL |
| Critical | > 1200 mcg/dL | > 1500 mcg/dL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 100 mcg/dL | > 150 mcg/dL |
| Optimal | 100-350 mcg/dL | 180-300 mcg/dL |
| Elevated concern | 350-450 mcg/dL | > 400 mcg/dL |
| Critical | > 450 mcg/dL | > 600 mcg/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Anabolic hormone reserve, immune resilience, and adaptation capacity**

DHEA-S is the signature of your adrenal glands' capacity for anabolic, restorative function. When DHEA-S is optimal, your adrenals are producing robust amounts of this foundational hormone, supporting muscle integrity, bone density, immune function, and mood stability. When DHEA-S is low, your adrenals are depleted or dysregulated, and resilience is compromised.

The DHEA-S/cortisol ratio (if cortisol is measured) is particularly revealing: it shows whether the adrenals are prioritizing anabolic (DHEA-S) or catabolic (cortisol) function. A person with high cortisol and low DHEA-S is in a catabolic state: stress hormones are elevated, recovery hormones are depleted, and tissue breakdown is accelerated. A person with high DHEA-S and low cortisol is in an optimal anabolic state.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 1000 mcg/dL men, > 400 mcg/dL women):**
- Possible PCOS in women; elevated DHEA-S is common
- Possible adrenal tumor (rare but serious; requires imaging)
- In women, may drive androgenic symptoms (acne, hirsutism, male-pattern baldness)
- Exogenous DHEA supplementation use

**If deficient (< 250 mcg/dL men, < 100 mcg/dL women):**
- Adrenal insufficiency or adrenal fatigue; glands are not producing adequate DHEA
- Chronic stress has depleted adrenal reserve
- Immune resilience is compromised; infection risk is elevated
- Muscle and bone integrity are at risk; DHEA supports anabolic function
- Mood is likely low; DHEA supports mood stability and resilience
- Recovery from exercise is slow; insufficient anabolic hormone
- Cardiovascular disease risk is elevated; low DHEA-S is an independent risk factor
- Cognitive function may be impaired; DHEA supports memory and executive function

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Cortisol (AM and diurnal)** — The DHEA-S:cortisol ratio reveals whether adrenals are anabolic or catabolic.
- **Pregnenolone** — Both are products of adrenal steroidogenesis; should move together.
- **Sex hormones** — DHEA is a precursor to sex hormones; low DHEA often means low sex hormones.
- **Immune markers (WBC, lymphocytes)** — DHEA supports immune function; low DHEA correlates with immune suppression.
**Key pattern:** A person with low DHEA-S, high cortisol, and low sex hormones is in a state of chronic stress with compromised adrenal function. Recovery requires comprehensive stress management and time.

### Intervention Levers
DHEA-S responds to: stress reduction (meditation, breathwork, community), adequate sleep (critical for adrenal recovery), nutrient sufficiency (especially vitamin C, B vitamins, zinc), resistance training (supports adrenal resilience), and reduced overtraining. DHEA supplementation (25-75 mg daily, micronized) can support recovery in severe deficiency, but it's not a substitute for lifestyle change. Results typically take 8-12 weeks with consistent intervention.

## IGF-1 (Insulin-Like Growth Factor)

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Insulin-like growth factor 1 concentration in serum; the primary mediator of growth hormone effects.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

IGF-1 is produced primarily by the liver in response to growth hormone (GH) signaling. It's the primary mediator of GH's anabolic effects: muscle growth, bone building, protein synthesis, and metabolic resilience. Growth hormone itself is short-lived (half-life of minutes), so IGF-1 is the downstream hormone that carries the anabolic signal throughout the body.

IGF-1 declines with age and with caloric restriction, overtraining, or chronically elevated cortisol. It also declines with liver dysfunction (since the liver produces IGF-1), malnutrition, and poor sleep. IGF-1 is thus a marker of anabolic capacity: when it's high, your body is in a growth-building state; when it's low, your body is in a catabolic state or stress state.

Paradoxically, extremely high IGF-1 (> 350 ng/mL) is associated with increased cancer risk in some populations, which has led to discussions about "optimal" IGF-1. However, the balance with IGF-binding proteins (which moderate IGF-1 bioavailability) is critical—total IGF-1 alone is incomplete.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Adult)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 45 ng/mL | > 100 ng/mL |
| Optimal | 45-175 ng/mL | 140-200 ng/mL |
| Elevated concern | 175-250 ng/mL | > 250 ng/mL |
| Critical | > 250 ng/mL | > 350 ng/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Growth hormone-mediated anabolic signaling, protein synthesis, and metabolic resilience**

IGF-1 is the embodied signal of growth hormone's anabolic power. When IGF-1 is optimal, your body is in a state of active anabolism: muscle protein is being synthesized, bone is being remodeled, cellular repair is happening, and recovery from training is robust. When IGF-1 is low, your body has shifted into a catabolic or conservation state: protein is being preserved, new tissue is not being built, and recovery is slow.

The age-adjusted IGF-1 level is important: a 60-year-old with an IGF-1 of 140 is in optimal anabolic state (age-adjusted optimal is lower), while a 30-year-old with the same IGF-1 is in a suppressed state (age-adjusted optimal is higher).

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 250 ng/mL):**
- Anabolic state is very high; muscle growth, bone building, and recovery are optimized
- Growth hormone signaling is strong; GH production or sensitivity is robust
- Some studies suggest increased cancer risk with very high IGF-1; controversial but worth considering
- May indicate GH supplementation or excessive overfeeding (rare in population)

**If deficient (< 100 ng/mL):**
- Anabolic capacity is suppressed; muscle growth is limited, recovery is slow
- Growth hormone production or signaling is compromised
- Possible growth hormone deficiency (rare)
- Liver dysfunction likely; IGF-1 is produced in the liver
- Chronic caloric restriction, overtraining, or chronic stress
- Aging is a normal cause, but in a younger person, dysfunction is likely
- Muscle loss, bone density decline, and impaired recovery are evident

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Growth Hormone (fasting)** — GH is the upstream signal; if GH is low and IGF-1 is low, GH deficiency is likely.
- **Liver function tests (AST, ALT, albumin)** — The liver produces IGF-1; liver disease suppresses IGF-1.
- **Cortisol** — High cortisol suppresses IGF-1 and GH; chronic stress impairs anabolism.
- **Strength and body composition** — IGF-1 should correlate with muscle mass and recovery capacity.
**Key pattern:** Low IGF-1 + low strength + slow recovery + high cortisol = catabolic state from stress/overtraining.

### Intervention Levers
IGF-1 responds powerfully to: resistance training (mechanical stimulation drives GH and IGF-1), adequate sleep (> 7 hours; GH is secreted during deep sleep), adequate protein intake (> 1.6 g/kg), caloric sufficiency (not restricting), reduced overtraining (balance intensity with recovery), and stress management. Colostrum supplementation (which contains IGF-1) can support recovery in deficient states. Results typically appear within 4-8 weeks of consistent training and sleep prioritization.

## Amino Acid Analysis (Plasma)

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** 40+ individual amino acid concentrations in plasma; provides detailed profile of amino acid metabolism and protein status.
**Sample Type:** Blood (plasma)
**Fasting Required:** Preferred (8-12 hours, reflects true amino acid status)

### What This Number Means

Amino acid analysis is a deep dive into protein metabolism. Rather than measuring just total protein, it measures individual amino acids: the branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs: leucine, isoleucine, valine), essential amino acids (EAAs), non-essential amino acids, and various ratios that reveal metabolic state.

The ratios are particularly revealing. The Fischer ratio (BCAA:AAA, where AAA is aromatic amino acids—phenylalanine, tyrosine, tryptophan) is a marker of liver function: a low Fischer ratio correlates with liver disease or liver stress. The amino acid balance (ratio of essential to total amino acids) reflects protein synthesis capacity. Individual amino acids reveal specific deficiencies or excesses.

Plasma amino acid analysis is more comprehensive than a typical amino acid screen and is useful in cases of suspected protein malnutrition, liver disease, genetic amino acid disorders, or optimization of amino acid supplementation.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Selected Key Amino Acids)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Leucine | 75-155 mcmol/L | 120-180 mcmol/L |
| Isoleucine | 30-70 mcmol/L | 50-80 mcmol/L |
| Valine | 50-160 mcmol/L | 100-180 mcmol/L |
| Total BCAAs | 155-385 mcmol/L | 250-400 mcmol/L |
| Glutamine | 376-709 mcmol/L | 500-700 mcmol/L |
| Taurine | 37-122 mcmol/L | 70-150 mcmol/L |
| Carnitine (L-) | 20-70 mcmol/L | 40-60 mcmol/L |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Synthesis — Amino acid metabolism, protein synthesis capacity, and tissue-building readiness**

Amino acid analysis is the molecular profile of your protein metabolism. When amino acids are optimally distributed, protein synthesis is robust, muscle recovery is efficient, and tissue-building capacity is high. When amino acids are imbalanced—low BCAAs, high aromatic amino acids, low glutamine—protein synthesis is impaired, recovery is slow, and metabolic stress is evident.

The amino acid profile also reveals metabolic state in other ways: elevated aromatic amino acids suggest liver stress (they can't be metabolized normally), low glutamine suggests immune suppression or gut dysfunction (glutamine is the fuel for immune cells and enterocytes), and low taurine suggests mitochondrial stress or oxidative stress (taurine is a mitochondrial protectant).

### What Deviation Signals
**If BCAAs elevated:**
- Protein synthesis capacity is very high; recovery from training is robust
- Anabolic state is optimal
- May indicate high dietary protein intake or protein supplementation

**If BCAAs deficient:**
- Protein synthesis is suppressed; recovery is slow
- Malnutrition or inadequate protein intake
- Overtraining without adequate recovery
- Possible muscle wasting (cachexia) if severe

**If aromatic amino acids elevated (high Fischer ratio):**
- Liver dysfunction; aromatic amino acids are normally metabolized by the liver
- Possible hepatitis, cirrhosis, or fatty liver disease
- Liver is not able to clear aromatic amino acids
- Requires hepatic evaluation

**If glutamine low:**
- Immune suppression or immune dysregulation
- Possible gut barrier dysfunction (leaky gut)
- Immune cells are consuming glutamine faster than it's being produced
- Possible sepsis or severe illness

**If taurine low:**
- Mitochondrial stress; taurine is a mitochondrial protectant
- Possible oxidative stress or energy production impairment
- Vegan/vegetarian diet (taurine is found primarily in animal products)
- May indicate need for taurine supplementation

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Protein intake history** — Dietary protein intake should correlate with amino acid distribution.
- **Strength and recovery** — Amino acid status should correlate with training recovery and muscle gain.
- **Liver function tests (AST, ALT, albumin, bilirubin)** — If aromatic amino acids are elevated, liver tests should be evaluated.
- **Immune markers (WBC, lymphocyte count)** — Low glutamine correlates with immune suppression.
- **Mitochondrial markers (CoQ10, carnitine levels)** — Low taurine may correlate with mitochondrial dysfunction.
**Key pattern:** The amino acid "fingerprint" (which amino acids are high, which low) reveals metabolic status: is the body anabolic or catabolic? Is liver function intact? Is protein intake adequate?

### Intervention Levers
Amino acid balance responds to: adequate protein intake (> 1.6-2.0 g/kg for active individuals), distribution across meals (each meal should contain 20-40 g protein to trigger mTORC1), BCAA-rich foods (eggs, meat, dairy, legumes), glutamine-rich foods (bone broth, parmesan), and taurine supplementation if low (2-3 g daily). For specific deficiencies: low glutamine warrants gut healing (collagen, gelatin, L-glutamine supplementation); low taurine warrants taurine supplementation (2-3 g daily); high aromatic amino acids warrant liver support (see SECTION-5).
