## GI-MAP (Gastrointestinal Microbial Assay Plus)

**Body Codx Operation:** Reception
**What It Measures:** Complete microbial ecology: beneficial bacteria, pathogenic organisms, parasites, fungi, viruses, and Helicobacter pylori by quantitative PCR.
**Sample Type:** Stool
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The GI-MAP is the highest-resolution microbial census available. It sequences over 40 organisms across six kingdoms using qPCR (quantitative polymerase chain reaction), which counts actual DNA copies rather than culturing. Your gut is a garden. This test is the soil analysis — showing what's growing, what's missing, what's invading, and what balance looks like.

Reception is the gut's first operation: the act of receiving food and transforming it into signals the body can use. But the gut cannot receive alone — it receives *through* the microbiota. Dysbiosis (imbalance) in the microbial ecosystem is dysbiosis in Reception itself. The test shows you exactly which organisms are abundant, which are deficient, and which are pathogenic.

The 40+ organisms include: beneficial Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, pathogenic Clostridium species, Staphylococcus aureus, Eikenella corrodens, Parasites (Blastocystis, Dientamoeba fragilis, hookworm), Candida and other fungi, viruses (including norovirus, rotavirus), and H. pylori. Each tells a different story about what the gut received and failed to clear.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Organism Category | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Bacteroidetes (beneficial) | Detected | 10–50% of total microbiota |
| Firmicutes (beneficial) | Detected | 40–60% of total microbiota |
| Clostridium clusters (pathogenic) | Not detected | Not detected |
| Staphylococcus aureus (pathogenic) | Not detected | Not detected |
| H. pylori | Not detected | Not detected |
| Parasites | Not detected | Not detected |
| Candida albicans | Trace/low | Not detected |
| Total microbial diversity | >100 species | >150 species |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Reception — Microbial-Mediated Nutrient & Signal Transduction**

The gut epithelium receives approximately 30–40 trillion bacterial cells. They are not invaders; they are co-residents. They ferment indigestible carbohydrates into short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate), which fuel the colonocytes and produce the GABA and serotonin precursors that signal safety to the nervous system. They train the immune system to distinguish friend from foe. They synthesize B vitamins and vitamin K. They maintain the mucus layer that prevents bacterial translocation.

When Reception is dysbiotic, two things happen: (1) nutrient signals fail to reach the bloodstream in proper form, and (2) the gut-brain axis receives distorted safety signals. The person feels chronically unsafe, malnourished despite eating, and inflammatory despite resting. The GI-MAP directly shows whether the microbial foundation of Reception is intact.

### What Deviation Signals

**If pathogenic organisms are elevated (H. pylori, Clostridium difficile, Staphylococcus aureus, parasites):**
- Reception is compromised by active infection; immune tolerance has been breached
- Nutrient absorption is blocked; inflammation is present in the epithelium
- The gut-brain axis is receiving alarm signals rather than safety signals
- Secondary dysbiosis may develop as the microbiota shifts away from beneficial species
- Food sensitivities may develop reactively, even to foods previously tolerated

**If beneficial organisms are deficient (low Bacteroidetes, low Firmicutes, low diversity):**
- Reception is fragile; the microbial commons that supports nutrient extraction has collapsed
- Short-chain fatty acid production is impaired; colonocytes are undernourished
- The immune system is undertrained; autoimmune drift becomes possible
- Barrier function (tight junctions, mucus layer) is weakened; intestinal permeability rises
- Mood and sleep regulation are impaired; the gut-brain axis has lost its primary language

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **GI-MAP + Zonulin** — Shows whether dysbiosis has triggered intestinal permeability (leaky gut)
- **GI Effects Comprehensive Profile or Gut Zoomer** — Adds digestive enzyme status, inflammation markers, and neurotransmitter profiles
- **Organic Acids Test** — Shows whether dysbiotic organisms are producing toxic fermentation byproducts (D-lactic acid, high-output formate)
- **Metabolomix+** — Captures the metabolic consequences of dysbiosis (amino acid imbalances, altered bile acid metabolism)

**Key pattern:** Dysbiosis is often not primary — it is secondary to either (1) barrier breakdown (from food sensitivity, medication, stress), (2) pathogenic infection (H. pylori, parasites), or (3) dietary shift (low fiber, high refined carbohydrate). The GI-MAP shows the ecology; cross-reference with Zonulin, stool inflammation markers, and dietary history to find the cause.

### Intervention Levers

The microbiota responds to: antimicrobial herbs or pharmaceuticals (to clear pathogens), dietary fiber (to feed beneficial organisms), soil-based organisms (spore-forming Bacillus species, to restore diversity), and removal of foods that trigger dysbiosis-promoting inflammation. Reception repair is always multi-step: clear pathogens, then rebuild the beneficial ecosystem, then stabilize with dietary consistency. It takes 6–12 weeks minimum to see sustained shift.

## GI-MAP + Zonulin

**Body Codx Operation:** Reception
**What It Measures:** Complete microbial assay (40+ organisms) plus zonulin, the primary marker of intestinal permeability.
**Sample Type:** Stool
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Zonulin is a protein that regulates tight junctions in the intestinal epithelium. High zonulin means the junctions are loose — intestinal permeability is elevated. The combination of the GI-MAP (microbial status) + Zonulin (barrier status) reveals the full Reception story: not just what the gut is receiving and from whom, but whether the epithelial barrier that controls what gets absorbed is intact.

The intestinal epithelium is a one-cell-thick barrier between the environment (the gut lumen, containing 30 trillion bacteria, food particles, toxins) and the bloodstream. Tight junctions seal the spaces between cells. Zonulin opens these junctions. In response to pathogenic bacteria, gluten (in susceptible individuals), or chronic inflammation, the epithelium releases zonulin to increase permeability — sometimes adaptively (to accelerate immune sampling), sometimes maladaptively (triggering systemic inflammation).

When zonulin is high, Reception is broken. Undigested food particles, lipopolysaccharides (bacterial endotoxins), and microbial metabolites enter the bloodstream. The immune system mounts an attack. The person develops food sensitivities that weren't there before. Systemic inflammation rises. The epithelium itself becomes inflamed, worsening permeability further — a vicious cycle.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Marker | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Zonulin (Stool) | <0.5 ng/mL | <0.2 ng/mL |
| Zonulin interpretation | <0.5 = normal | <0.2 = tight junctions intact |
| Zonulin elevated | >0.5 | indicates permeability breach |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Reception with Barrier Integrity — Epithelial Gating**

The intestinal epithelium receives approximately 2–3 liters of fluid per day, plus food, plus bacterial metabolites. It must absorb nutrients while rejecting pathogens and toxins. This requires two simultaneous events: (1) tight regulation of what crosses (transcellular absorption), and (2) tight sealing of the spaces between cells (paracellular closure). Zonulin controls the paracellular pathway.

When zonulin is elevated, the epithelium has made a decision: permeability serves some purpose — immune surveillance, rapid antigen sampling, bacterial endotoxin clearance — but the cost is breach. Nutrients leak through uncontrolled. Large food particles cross intact. Bacterial lipopolysaccharides (LPS) trigger endotoxemia. The person feels it as brain fog, systemic inflammation, and food sensitivities appearing suddenly.

Reception is no longer receiving. It is hemorrhaging.

### What Deviation Signals

**If zonulin is elevated (>0.5 ng/mL, especially >0.8):**
- The intestinal epithelium has activated an emergency permeability mode
- Pathogenic organisms or food triggers are signaling threat to the epithelium
- Systemic endotoxemia (LPS in the bloodstream) is likely; chronic low-grade inflammation is active
- Food sensitivities will cascade; newly reactive foods are multiplying
- Autoimmune drift is accelerating; the immune system is attacking self-antigens cross-reactive with bacterial epitopes
- Brain inflammation (neuroinflammation) is probable; cognitive fog, mood changes, sleep disruption are common
- Barrier repair will take 3–6 months minimum; the epithelium regenerates completely every 3–5 days, but zonulin-driven inflammation must be cleared first

**If zonulin is normal but GI-MAP shows pathogens or dysbiosis:**
- The barrier is still intact, but pressure is mounting
- Intervention window is wider; prevention of permeability breach is still possible
- Pathogen clearance and dysbiosis repair may prevent progression to "leaky gut"

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **GI-MAP alone** — Shows whether pathogenic organisms are driving zonulin release
- **Array 2 - Intestinal Permeability** — Complements zonulin; shows immune reactivity to barrier-crossing antigens (LPS, occludin, zonulin itself)
- **Metabolomix+** — Shows the metabolic consequence of elevated LPS (elevated LPS-binding proteins, altered lipid metabolism)
- **Food Sensitivity Tests (IgG arrays)** — Shows newly reactive foods; permeability is the mechanism

**Key pattern:** High zonulin + elevated food IgG reactivity is pathognomonic for intestinal permeability. High zonulin + normal food IgG suggests the breach is recent or the immune response hasn't yet mounted. High zonulin + H. pylori or Clostridium on GI-MAP points to pathogenic organism-driven permeability; high zonulin + dysbiosis (low diversity, high Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio) points to dysbiosis-driven inflammation. Each pattern has different intervention priorities.

### Intervention Levers

Zonulin elevation requires simultaneous action on three fronts: (1) remove the trigger (pathogen, gluten, food sensitivity), (2) repair the epithelium (L-glutamine, zinc carnosine, bone broth collagen, short-chain fatty acids from butyrate supplementation), and (3) restore the microbial ecosystem (fiber, fermented foods, specific probiotics that upregulate tight junction proteins like Akkermansia muciniphila). Zonulin typically falls within 6–12 weeks if the trigger is removed and the three-front approach is maintained. Monitor with repeat testing at 8–12 weeks.

## GI Effects Comprehensive Profile (Genova Diagnostics)

**Body Codx Operation:** Reception
**What It Measures:** Microbial balance (beneficial and pathogenic), digestion capacity, inflammatory markers, dysbiosis index, and pathogenic detection across bacteria, parasites, and fungi.
**Sample Type:** Stool
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The GI Effects is a second-generation comprehensive stool analysis that combines microbial ecology, digestive function, and inflammation in one test. Where the GI-MAP is a census (40+ organisms quantified), the GI Effects is a functional assessment: it asks not just "what's there?" but "can the gut digest, absorb, and defend itself?"

Reception depends on three simultaneous functions: (1) the microbiota producing digestive enzymes and metabolites that signal nutrient availability, (2) the epithelium absorbing those nutrients through an intact barrier, and (3) the immune system tolerating food and beneficial bacteria while attacking pathogens. The GI Effects measures all three. It shows microbial balance as a dysbiosis index (a ratio of beneficial to pathogenic bacteria), digestive enzyme activity (how much protease, lipase, and amylase is present in the stool), inflammatory markers (lactoferrin and white blood cells in the stool — is the immune system active?), and pathogenic organism detection (bacteria, parasites, fungi, yeasts).

This is Reception as a full system: input (food) → microbial processing → digestive enzyme production → nutrient extraction → immune tolerance → absorption → output.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Marker | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Dysbiosis Index | <0.0 (dysbiotic) to >3.0 (balanced) | >2.5 (strong beneficial majority) |
| Beneficial Bacteria | Adequate/Detected | High (Bacteroidetes 20–50%, Firmicutes 40–60%) |
| Pathogenic Bacteria | Absent/Rare | Absent |
| Parasites | Absent | Absent |
| Candida | Rare/Trace | Absent |
| Digestive Enzymes (Chymotrypsin, Pancreatic Elastase) | 0.0–5.0 (low detection) | >5.0 (adequate) |
| Fecal Lactoferrin | <7.25 µg/g | <2 µg/g (minimal inflammation) |
| White Blood Cells (in stool) | <2/hpf | 0/hpf (no active immune response) |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Reception — Digestion, Tolerance, and Microbial Symbiosis**

The gut accomplishes Reception through delegation. The epithelium cannot digest food alone — it lacks the enzymatic capacity. The microbiota ferments carbohydrates into butyrate. The pancreatic enzymes break down protein and fat. Together, they convert food into absorbable molecules and signaling compounds. The immune system watches, learns which organisms are food-processing partners and which are threats, and allows beneficial bacteria to pass unmolested while culling pathogens.

When this system is intact, Reception feels like ease: you eat, you are nourished, you have energy, your mood is stable. When it is broken, Reception becomes interrogation — every meal triggers inflammation, digestive distress, or reactivity. The GI Effects reveals whether the system is intact or broken.

The dysbiosis index is the report card. Scores above 2.5 mean the beneficial bacteria have won the ecological competition. Below 0, pathogenic bacteria dominate. A high dysbiosis index with low digestive enzymes means the microbiota cannot feed itself or the epithelium; a high dysbiosis index with high lactoferrin means dysbiosis is triggering immune activation despite good microbial balance — suggesting food reactivity or barrier breakdown rather than microbial imbalance.

### What Deviation Signals

**If dysbiosis index is low (<0.5) and beneficial bacteria are reduced:**
- Pathogenic organisms (Clostridium, Staphylococcus, Klebsiella) have won the ecological war
- Digestive enzyme output is likely reduced; fermentation is producing inflammatory metabolites instead of butyrate
- The epithelium is likely inflamed (high lactoferrin, elevated white blood cells)
- Food is not being adequately digested; undigested particles are reaching the epithelium
- Secondary food sensitivities are probable; the immune system is reactively attacking incompletely digested proteins
- Nutrient absorption is impaired despite adequate dietary intake; the person is malnourished despite eating

**If digestive enzymes are low (<5.0 chymotrypsin):**
- Pancreatic insufficiency or chronic pancreatic inflammation is present
- Protein and fat digestion is compromised; fat-soluble vitamin absorption is impaired
- Undigested protein reaching the colon triggers bacterial fermentation and toxic byproducts (elevated D-lactic acid, phenols, p-cresol)
- Secondary dysbiosis is likely; pathogenic organisms thrive on undigested food
- The person has indigestion, bloating, floating stools, and fat malabsorption

**If fecal lactoferrin is elevated (>7.25 µg/g):**
- Active immune inflammation is present in the gut lining
- Either pathogenic organisms are triggering immune attack, or food/bacterial lipopolysaccharides are
- The epithelium is stressed; barrier function may be compromised
- Intestinal permeability (leaky gut) is likely; cross-reference with Zonulin or Array 2
- Continued antigenic stimulation will accelerate autoimmune drift

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **GI-MAP** — More detailed organism-by-organism census; clarifies which specific pathogens or beneficial species are present
- **GI-MAP + Zonulin** — Adds barrier integrity marker; clarifies whether inflammation is barrier-driven or dysbiosis-driven
- **Organic Acids Test** — Shows the metabolic consequences of dysbiosis (elevated fermentation byproducts, altered amino acid metabolism)
- **Metabolomix+** — Adds amino acids and fatty acids; reveals systemic nutritional consequence of malabsorption

**Key pattern:** Low dysbiosis index + low digestive enzymes + elevated lactoferrin = pancreatic insufficiency driving secondary dysbiosis. Low dysbiosis index + high digestive enzymes + elevated lactoferrin = pathogenic organism-driven inflammation. High dysbiosis index (balanced microbiota) + high lactoferrin = food sensitivity or barrier breakdown, not dysbiosis.

### Intervention Levers

Dysbiosis repair requires three steps: (1) if digestive enzymes are low, restore pancreatic function (digestive enzymes, bitter herbs, choline), (2) if pathogenic organisms are present, clear them (antimicrobial herbs or targeted antibiotics), (3) rebuild beneficial bacteria through fiber, fermented foods, and probiotics. Lactoferrin typically normalizes within 4–8 weeks if the antigen trigger is removed. Dysbiosis index normalization takes 8–12 weeks of consistent dietary and supplement support.

## Gut Zoomer 3.0 (Vibrant Wellness)

**Body Codx Operation:** Reception
**What It Measures:** Microbiota balance, bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS), intestinal barrier integrity, short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, neurotransmitter metabolites, and stress hormone metabolites in stool.
**Sample Type:** Stool
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The Gut Zoomer 3.0 is the most integrated Reception assessment available. It combines six measurement dimensions in one test: (1) microbial diversity and balance, (2) bacterial lipopolysaccharide (endotoxin), (3) barrier integrity markers (zonulin, occludin, lipopolysaccharide-binding protein), (4) short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate — the "currency" of microbial symbiosis), (5) neurotransmitter metabolites (GABA, serotonin production by the microbiota), and (6) stress hormone metabolites (cortisol, cortisone metabolites, which rise in response to endotoxemia and barrier breach).

Reception is not just about absorbing food. It is about receiving signals. The microbiota produce three primary signals: (1) short-chain fatty acids (butyrate signals "epithelium is nourished, you are safe"), (2) neurotransmitters (GABA and serotonin signal "rest-and-digest, you are safe"), and (3) metabolite patterns that signal "nutrients are available"). When these signals are robust, Reception feels like trust. When they collapse, Reception becomes alarm.

The Gut Zoomer captures the full signal landscape. It is the best single test for understanding whether Reception is a place of nourishment or a place of threat.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Marker | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Microbial Diversity Score | 0–100 | 70–100 |
| Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes Ratio | 1.0–10.0 | 1.5–3.0 |
| Bacterial LPS (pg/mL) | <200 | <50 |
| Zonulin | <0.5 ng/mL | <0.2 ng/mL |
| Occludin (tight junction protein) | Normal | >2.5 ng/mL |
| Butyrate (short-chain fatty acid) | Detected | 100–200 µmol/L equivalent |
| Propionate | Detected | 50–100 µmol/L equivalent |
| GABA (marker of microbial production) | Detected | High (>median) |
| Serotonin precursors (5-HTP markers) | Detected | High |
| Cortisol Metabolite Ratio | 0.5–2.0 | 1.0–1.5 |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Reception — Microbial Signal Ecology and Parasympathetic Priming**

Reception is a listening operation. The gut epithelium is lined with nerve endings — the enteric nervous system, which contains 500 million neurons and 100 trillion synapses. These neurons listen to the microbiota. What they hear shapes whether the person feels safe (parasympathetic — rest, digest, repair) or threatened (sympathetic — fight, flight, freeze, inflammatory).

The microbiota produce short-chain fatty acids through fermentation of dietary fiber and resistant starch. Butyrate, the primary SCFA, binds to G-protein-coupled receptors on enteric neurons and the epithelium, signaling "nutrition is abundant, threat is low, you are safe." It upregulates tight junction proteins, increases mucus production, and shifts the immune system toward tolerance. The microbiota also produce GABA and precursors to serotonin — neurotransmitters that tell the brain "you are nourished, you can rest."

When SCFA production collapses (dysbiosis, low fiber intake), the signal goes dark. When bacterial LPS rises (dysbiosis, barrier breakdown), the signal becomes an alarm. When cortisol metabolites are elevated despite normal serum cortisol, it signals that the gut is chronically perceiving threat, driving endotoxemia-induced hypervigilance.

The Gut Zoomer reads all these signals at once. It is the most complete picture of whether Reception is functioning as nourishment or as an alarm system.

### What Deviation Signals

**If microbial diversity is low (<50) and SCFA production is reduced:**
- The microbiota has collapsed; the ecosystem cannot produce the metabolites that signal safety
- The epithelium is not receiving the SCFA signals that upregulate tight junctions and mucus production
- Barrier function is compromised; intestinal permeability is rising
- The enteric nervous system is not receiving GABA or serotonin signals; the person feels anxious, constipated, or dysphoric despite adequate sleep
- Nutrient extraction is impaired; the person is malnourished despite eating

**If bacterial LPS is elevated (>100 pg/mL):**
- Dysbiosis is driving Gram-negative pathogenic bacteria (Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Pseudomonas)
- Endotoxemia is present; bacterial lipopolysaccharides are crossing the epithelial barrier into the bloodstream
- Systemic inflammation is active; cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) are elevated
- The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis is hyperactive; cortisol is dysregulated
- Neuroinflammation is probable; the person has brain fog, mood instability, or neurological symptoms

**If zonulin is elevated (>0.5) and occludin is low (<2.5):**
- The intestinal barrier is actively broken; tight junctions are loose
- Bacterial endotoxins and undigested food particles are crossing into the bloodstream
- Autoimmune activation is likely; cross-reactive immune attack on self-antigens is probable
- Food sensitivities are cascading; new reactivities appear weekly
- The person will not recover until barrier function is restored; this is a prerequisite for any other intervention

**If cortisol metabolite ratio is abnormal (>2.0 or <0.5):**
- The gut-brain axis is dysregulated; even if serum cortisol is normal, the body is perceiving chronic threat
- This often indicates endotoxemia-driven HPA axis hyperactivation despite normal cortisol production
- Sleep quality is compromised; melatonin and GABA production are impaired
- Anxiety, hypervigilance, and mood instability are primary symptoms despite psychological work

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **GI-MAP or GI Effects** — Clarifies which specific organisms are absent (causing low SCFA production) or elevated (causing high LPS)
- **Array 2 - Intestinal Permeability** — Shows immune reactivity to barrier-crossing antigens; confirms endotoxemia
- **Organic Acids Test** — Shows whether dysbiosis is producing toxic fermentation byproducts (elevated D-lactic acid, phenols, p-cresol) that would explain neurotoxicity
- **Serum Cortisol + ACTH + Diurnal Pattern** — Clarifies whether cortisol is truly dysregulated or whether the gut is perceiving threat despite normal cortisol; distinguishes HPA axis hyperactivity from true adrenal insufficiency

**Key pattern:** Low diversity + low SCFA + normal LPS + elevated zonulin = dysbiosis-driven barrier breakdown; the microbiota have collapsed but pathogens haven't bloomed. Low diversity + low SCFA + elevated LPS + elevated zonulin = dysbiosis + pathogenic overgrowth + endotoxemia; three-front repair needed. High diversity + high SCFA + normal LPS + normal zonulin but elevated cortisol metabolites = the microbiota are intact but the person is perceiving threat despite good gut function; the primary issue is in the nervous system (trauma, chronic stress, sleep deprivation), not the gut.

### Intervention Levers

If diversity and SCFA are low, the primary intervention is dietary fiber and resistant starch (to feed beneficial bacteria), plus targeted probiotics (Akkermansia muciniphila, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia spp.). If LPS is elevated, antimicrobial intervention comes first (to clear Gram-negative pathogens), followed by barrier repair (L-glutamine, zinc carnosine, butyrate). If cortisol metabolites are dysregulated despite normal serum cortisol, the nervous system needs down-regulation (vagal toning, sleep extension, stress reduction) before gut-directed interventions will succeed. Diversity and SCFA typically normalize within 8–12 weeks; LPS normalization takes 4–8 weeks; cortisol pattern normalization takes 8–16 weeks of parallel nervous system work.

## Comprehensive Stool Analysis (CDSA — Doctors Data or Genova)

**Body Codx Operation:** Reception
**What It Measures:** Microbiota balance, parasites, pathogenic bacteria and yeast, digestive enzyme adequacy, bile acid metabolism, fecal pH, short-chain fatty acids, and inflammation markers.
**Sample Type:** Stool
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The Comprehensive Stool Analysis is a classical functional medicine assessment that has been the industry standard for 20+ years. It is simpler and broader than the GI-MAP (fewer organisms identified by name, but identified by functional category), and it includes measurements that newer tests omit: fecal fat (whether fat malabsorption is present), starch (undigested starch, indicating enzyme insufficiency), chymotrypsin (pancreatic enzyme function), bile salts (whether the liver/gallbladder is adequately supporting fat digestion), and pH (whether the intestinal environment favors beneficial or pathogenic organisms).

The CDSA is Reception through the lens of classical digestion: food → stomach acid → pancreatic enzymes → bile → absorption. It shows whether each step is working. It is less sophisticated than the Gut Zoomer (no barrier integrity markers, no stress hormone metabolites, no neurotransmitter production data), but it is more practical for identifying specific deficiencies.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Marker | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Beneficial Bacteria (Bacteroides, Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, Enterococcus) | Adequate/Positive | High counts, all present |
| Pathogenic Bacteria (E. coli, Staphylococcus, Clostridium, Klebsiella) | Absent/Trace | Absent |
| Yeast (Candida, Geotrichum) | Rare/Absent | Absent |
| Parasites | Absent | Absent |
| Fecal Chymotrypsin (pancreatic enzyme) | 0.0–5.0 (low) | >5.0 (adequate) |
| Fecal Fat | 0–7 g/day | <2 g/day (minimal malabsorption) |
| Fecal Starch | Rare | Absent (indicates complete carbohydrate digestion) |
| Bile Salts | Adequate | Present (>2) |
| Fecal pH | 6.0–7.0 | 6.0–6.5 (favorable for beneficial bacteria) |
| Short-Chain Fatty Acids (Butyrate, Propionate, Acetate) | 0–150 mmol/g | >50 mmol/g (robust SCFA production) |
| Fecal Lactoferrin | <7.25 µg/g | <2 µg/g |
| Fecal Occult Blood | Negative | Negative |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Reception — Enzymatic Digestion, Bile Flow, and Microbial Metabolite Production**

The CDSA frames Reception as a three-part collaboration: (1) the stomach and pancreas (producing acid and enzymes), (2) the liver and gallbladder (producing and storing bile), and (3) the microbiota (fermenting indigestible carbohydrates and producing protective metabolites). When all three are intact, Reception is smooth: food is broken down, fats are emulsified and absorbed, carbohydrates are fermented into SCFA, and the person is nourished.

The presence of undigested starch means the pancreatic enzymes are insufficient. The presence of elevated fecal fat means either fat is not being emulsified by bile (liver/gallbladder dysfunction) or it is not being absorbed (epithelial damage). Low chymotrypsin means the pancreas is not producing adequate protease. High pH (>7.0) means the intestinal environment is alkaline — favorable for pathogenic bacteria like Clostridium and unfavorable for beneficial Bacteroides and Bifidobacterium.

Each finding points to a specific part of the Reception system that is breaking down.

### What Deviation Signals

**If chymotrypsin is low (<5.0) and starch is present:**
- Pancreatic insufficiency is present; protease, amylase, and lipase output are reduced
- Protein digestion is incomplete; large peptides reach the colon
- Carbohydrate digestion is incomplete; undigested starch feeds pathogenic bacteria
- Fermentation byproducts are elevated; the person has bloating, gas, and constipation or loose stools
- Secondary dysbiosis is probable; pathogenic bacteria thrive on undigested food

**If fecal fat is elevated (>7 g/day):**
- Fat malabsorption is present; either pancreatic lipase is insufficient, or bile salts are inadequate, or epithelial absorption is damaged
- If fecal fat is elevated AND fecal starch is present, pancreatic insufficiency is the primary problem
- If fecal fat is elevated but starch is absent, liver/gallbladder dysfunction or epithelial damage is the primary problem
- Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are not being absorbed; deficiency is developing
- The person has floating, pale stools; steatorrhea

**If pH is elevated (>7.0):**
- The colonic environment is alkaline; pathogenic bacteria favor alkalinity
- Beneficial bacteria (Bacteroides, Bifidobacterium) produce short-chain fatty acids that lower pH; their absence is allowing pH to rise
- This is often secondary to low SCFA production (dysbiosis, low fiber) or rapid transit (diarrhea)
- Continued alkalinity will select further for pathogenic organisms; dysbiosis will worsen

**If beneficial bacteria counts are low and pathogenic bacteria are elevated:**
- Dysbiosis is established; the ecological balance has shifted
- This may be primary (pathogenic infection) or secondary (to enzyme insufficiency, pH imbalance, or bile flow disruption)
- The CDSA alone cannot distinguish cause from consequence; cross-reference with GI-MAP (to identify specific organisms) and dietary history

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **GI-MAP** — Provides organism-by-organism census; clarifies which specific pathogens are present and which beneficial bacteria are absent
- **Organic Acids Test** — Shows the metabolic consequences of enzyme insufficiency (elevated D-lactic acid from bacterial overgrowth, elevated phenols and p-cresol from pathogenic fermentation)
- **Pancreatic Enzymes, Lipase, Amylase** — Serum-based markers; clarify whether low stool chymotrypsin reflects pancreatic disease or insufficient enzyme production
- **Serum Bile Acids** — Clarify whether bile flow is truly reduced or whether fat malabsorption reflects epithelial damage rather than digestive insufficiency

**Key pattern:** Elevated starch + normal fat + low chymotrypsin = amylase insufficiency (carbohydrate digestion problem). Elevated fat + elevated starch + low chymotrypsin = pancreatic insufficiency (all enzymes low). Elevated fat + normal starch + low chymotrypsin = bile salt insufficiency or epithelial damage (fat digestion problem, not enzyme problem). Low beneficial bacteria + high pH + elevated pathogenic bacteria = dysbiosis secondary to enzyme insufficiency; repair pancreatic function first, dysbiosis will often self-correct.

### Intervention Levers

If chymotrypsin is low, pancreatic enzyme replacement (proteases, amylases, lipases) typically resolves starch and fat malabsorption within 2–4 weeks. If pH is elevated, SCFA production (through fiber and dysbiosis repair) will lower it. If fecal fat is elevated despite adequate enzymes, bile support (beet leaf, milk thistle, lecithin) or epithelial repair (L-glutamine, zinc carnosine) may be needed. The CDSA typically normalizes within 4–8 weeks if the primary dysfunction (enzyme insufficiency, bile flow, dysbiosis) is addressed.

## Array 2 - Intestinal Permeability (Cyrex Laboratories)

**Body Codx Operation:** Reception
**What It Measures:** Immune reactivity (IgA, IgM, IgG) to lipopolysaccharide (LPS), occludin, zonulin, and other barrier-related antigens that indicate intestinal permeability and breach-triggered immune activation.
**Sample Type:** Blood serum
**Fasting Required:** Preferred but not required

### What This Number Means

The Array 2 is the immune-centric view of intestinal permeability. Where zonulin (in stool) tells you the barrier has opened, Array 2 tells you whether immune reactivity to barrier-crossing antigens is present. When the intestinal barrier opens (zonulin rises), bacterial endotoxins (lipopolysaccharides), bacterial metabolites, and undigested food particles cross into the bloodstream. The immune system detects them and mounts an antibody response.

Array 2 measures three specific immune responses: (1) IgA antibodies to LPS (the barrier-crossing bacterial endotoxin), (2) IgG antibodies to LPS (chronic immune activation to bacterial endotoxins), and (3) IgA/IgG to occludin and zonulin (autoimmune attack on the person's own tight junction proteins). Together, these antibodies are markers of — and potentially drivers of — a vicious cycle: barrier opens → immune attacks barrier → barrier stays open.

Reception is broken when the immune system attacks the tools the epithelium uses to seal itself.

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Reception — Immune Tolerance Failure and Autoimmune Pathway Activation**

Reception depends on the immune system learning three distinctions: (1) beneficial bacteria are food-processing partners, not threats, and should be tolerated, (2) food molecules are nutrients, not invaders, and should be absorbed without mounting an inflammatory response, and (3) self-antigens (occludin, zonulin, the epithelium itself) are sacred and must never be attacked.

When intestinal permeability develops, one of two things happens: (1) the immune system correctly identifies the barrier breach and attacks the pathogens/toxins that crossed (healthy inflammatory response), or (2) the immune system makes a mistake and attacks the barrier itself (autoimmune activation). Array 2 detects scenario two.

Elevated IgG to LPS means the immune system has been chronically exposed to bacterial endotoxins — the barrier has been open for weeks to months. Elevated IgA to occludin or zonulin means the immune system is actively attacking the proteins that seal the barrier — a self-perpetuating cycle. The person's immune system has lost tolerance and gained autoimmunity.

### What Deviation Signals

**If IgA, IgM, or IgG to LPS are elevated:**
- Chronic endotoxemia is present; bacterial lipopolysaccharides have been crossing the barrier for weeks to months
- The immune system is chronically activated; systemic inflammation is persistent
- The person likely has elevated lipopolysaccharide-binding protein (LBP) in serum (a marker of endotoxin clearance attempts)
- Autoimmune drift is accelerating; the immune system is primed for hypervigilance
- Brain inflammation is probable; neuroinflammation and mood changes are common
- This typically requires 3–4 months of sustained intervention to clear

**If IgA to occludin or zonulin is elevated:**
- The immune system is actively attacking the tight junction proteins that seal the barrier
- This is an autoimmune condition; it is self-perpetuating — attacking the barrier keeps it open
- Complete barrier healing is difficult until autoimmune attack is suppressed
- The person may have concurrent autoimmune conditions elsewhere (celiac disease, thyroiditis, rheumatoid arthritis)
- Barrier repair will be slow (6–12 months) and will require immune suppression (elimination diet, potentially low-dose naltrexone or immunosuppressive botanicals) plus epithelial repair

**If multiple antibodies are elevated:**
- Systemic autoimmune activation is present
- This is not simple dysbiosis; the barrier has been open for months, and the immune system has shifted into autoimmune mode
- The person likely has elevated tissue-specific antibodies (thyroid peroxidase, tissue transglutaminase, nuclear antigens, depending on their genetic predisposition)
- Recovery will require removing the barrier breach trigger (pathogen, food sensitivity, medication) PLUS suppressing the autoimmune response

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **GI-MAP + Zonulin** — Confirms barrier opening and identifies the specific organisms driving it
- **Serum Lipopolysaccharide-Binding Protein (LBP)** — Confirms endotoxemia; elevated LBP means the person's immune system is actively clearing bacterial endotoxins from the bloodstream
- **Tissue-Specific Antibodies** — Clarifies whether autoimmune activation is limited to the gut or has spread systemically (thyroid, joints, nervous system, etc.)
- **Organic Acids Test** — May show elevated LPS metabolites (lipoteichoic acid markers); correlates with Array 2 findings

**Key pattern:** Elevated IgA/IgG to LPS without elevated IgA to occludin/zonulin = endotoxemia without active autoimmune attack on the barrier; barrier repair + pathogen clearance is the primary intervention. Elevated IgA to occludin/zonulin = active autoimmune attack on the barrier; immune suppression + barrier repair are both needed. Elevated IgA to LPS + elevated tissue-specific antibodies = systemic autoimmune activation; this is a complex, multi-system condition requiring comprehensive immune regulation.

### Intervention Levers

If IgA to LPS is elevated, the barrier-opening trigger must be removed (pathogen clearance, food sensitivity elimination) + barrier repair (L-glutamine, zinc carnosine, butyrate). If IgA to occludin or zonulin is elevated, immune suppression is required: elimination diet (removing all potential dietary triggers), potentially low-dose naltrexone (0.5–4.5 mg at bedtime, which promotes regulatory T cell expansion), and epithelial repair nutrients. This is a 6–12 month process. Array 2 should be repeated at 6 and 12 weeks to monitor antibody decline; if antibodies are not declining, the triggering antigen (pathogen or food) has not been fully removed.
