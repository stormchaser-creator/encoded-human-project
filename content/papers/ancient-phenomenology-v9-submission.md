## Abstract

Classical phenomenological systems developed through sustained introspective practice may encode regularities about mental state-space that retain correspondence with independently derived neurobiological constructs. We develop and apply a four-criterion isomorphism instrument — Functional Isomorphism (C1), Independent Derivation (C2), Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint (C3), and Asymmetric Disconfirmation (C4) — to evaluate whether such correspondence is systematic rather than incidental. The instrument enforces directionality by requiring neutral cognitive descriptions to be extracted from phenomenological texts prior to any consultation of the neurobiological reference corpus.

In a preliminary three-corpus pilot study, 33 claim units extracted from Patanjali's Yoga Sutras (I.17–I.51) were scored alongside two negative control corpora: Charaka Samhita (n=33) and Hero of Alexandria's Pneumatica (n=33). Tier A classification (all four criteria met) was achieved by 30.3% of Patanjali units versus 0% for both controls (Fisher's exact, p<0.001). A second within-tradition pilot applied the same instrument to the Theravāda Abhidhammattha-Saṅgaha: 15 consciousness-specific units from Chapters I–V achieved 33.3% Tier A, while 12 matter-analysis units from Chapter VI (Rūpa) achieved 0%. Two independent consciousness taxonomies thus converge at approximately 30–33% Tier A, while all domain-mismatched controls — whether cross-tradition or within-tradition — produce 0%.

The primary selectivity mechanism operates at Criterion 1 — domain boundary with consciousness-specific neurobiology — rather than at Criterion 2 (Independent Derivation), demonstrating that the instrument discriminates between consciousness-directed phenomenological systems and biologically coherent but domain-mismatched alternatives. Five mapping domains are presented with retrospective isomorphisms distinguished from prospective predictions: attentional hierarchy, default mode dynamics, interoceptive processing, state-dependent memory, and cognitive effort. Specific experimental designs are proposed for the highest-priority Tier A pairs. The pratyahara domain is identified as a candidate where phenomenological description outpaces available neuroscientific measurement tools.

## 1. Introduction

The relationship between contemplative practice and neural function has emerged as a productive area of empirical inquiry over the past three decades. Neuroimaging studies, electrophysiological recordings, and behavioral assessments have collectively documented measurable changes in cortical thickness [1], default mode network activity [2], gamma-band oscillatory coherence [3], and autonomic regulation [4] associated with sustained meditation practice. A meta-analysis of 78 functional neuroimaging investigations found reliably dissociable patterns of brain activation across four common meditation styles, with medium effect sizes for both activations (d=0.59) and deactivations (d=-0.74) [5]. What has received comparatively less systematic attention is whether the phenomenological frameworks that historically organized these practices carry independent predictive value — that is, whether ancient descriptive taxonomies of mental states map onto neurobiological constructs with sufficient precision to generate testable hypotheses rather than merely post-hoc analogies.

This paper addresses that question directly. We treat the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, composed approximately in the fourth century CE [6,7], as a structured phenomenological instrument and ask whether its categorical distinctions anticipate divisions that modern neuroscience has independently arrived at through empirical methods. The claim is methodological before it is interpretive: if cross-domain isomorphisms between Patanjali's framework and contemporary neurobiology are systematic rather than incidental, then the phenomenological tradition represents an underutilized source of hypothesis generation for cognitive and clinical neuroscience.

The Yoga Sutras organize mental phenomena across several dimensions that bear preliminary examination. Patanjali distinguishes between vrittis (fluctuations of the mind-field), states of samapatti (cognitive coalescence with an object), and progressive stages of samadhi differentiated by the presence or absence of discursive thought, reflective awareness, and ultimately any objectival content whatsoever [6]. These distinctions are textually present and internally consistent within the system. Whether they correspond to neurobiologically distinct states is an empirical question, and it is precisely this question the present analysis pursues.

Contemporary neuroscience has developed parallel taxonomies through separate means. Researchers distinguish between focused attention and open monitoring meditation as producing divergent electrophysiological signatures, with focused attention associated with gamma-band synchrony and open monitoring associated with frontal theta and reduced alpha suppression [3,5]. Default mode network suppression during goal-directed cognition, and its paradoxical partial maintenance during certain advanced meditative states, has been documented across multiple imaging paradigms [2,8]. The potential cost of neglecting the structural logic of contemplative systems is not merely aesthetic. Phenomenological frameworks refined over centuries of systematic introspective practice may encode regularities about mental state-space that have not yet been captured in laboratory paradigms designed by investigators with limited first-person acquaintance with the states under study.

The history of science offers precedents for productive cross-domain translation: thermodynamic formalism preceded molecular-kinetic understanding of heat; Mendelian ratios preceded molecular genetics. In each case, a phenomenological or functional regularity provided structural constraints that mechanistic accounts later explained rather than replaced. We propose an analogous relationship here, with appropriate caution about the limits of the analogy.

Any instrument claiming to detect genuine cross-domain correspondence must address four failure modes that could produce spurious results: vagueness in phenomenological description that permits unlimited post-hoc mapping (addressed by C1, Functional Isomorphism, which requires specific domain-relevant mapping); domain mismatch where superficially parallel language masks fundamentally different referents (also addressed by C1's domain boundary enforcement); circular derivation where the phenomenological and neurobiological accounts share a common source (addressed by C2, Independent Derivation); and sign errors or directional ambiguity where the mapping adds no informational value beyond what the target domain already contains (addressed by C3, Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint, and C4, Asymmetric Disconfirmation).

A distinction is worth making explicit at the outset. Much of the analysis below demonstrates *retrospective isomorphism* — that Patanjali's categorical distinctions align closely with neurobiological constructs derived independently through empirical methods. This alignment is itself informative. In a smaller number of cases, however, the phenomenological taxonomy appears to generate *prospective predictions* — hypotheses about neural or phenomenological mechanisms that have not yet been tested or that outpace current experimental paradigms. The instrument is designed to support both forms of correspondence, but only the prospective subset carries the stronger claim of hypothesis generation. This distinction is made explicit throughout the qualitative domain mappings and for each of the Tier A units.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 describes the cross-domain mapping methodology and the four-criterion isomorphism instrument. Section 3 presents qualitative mappings across five primary domains. Section 4 reports preliminary empirical results from two pilot studies: a cross-tradition comparison (Patanjali versus Charaka Samhita and Hero of Alexandria) and a within-tradition comparison (Abhidhamma consciousness chapters versus Abhidhamma matter chapters). Section 5 discusses implications for contemplative neuroscience methodology and proposes a concrete experimental research program.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Overview of Analytic Approach

This analysis employs a structured cross-domain mapping procedure designed to evaluate whether categorical distinctions in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras correspond to independently derived neurobiological constructs with sufficient specificity to constitute genuine isomorphism rather than post-hoc analogy. The procedure involves three sequential phases: (1) systematic extraction and formalization of Patanjali's phenomenological taxonomy in neutral cognitive vocabulary, (2) identification of candidate neurobiological constructs using pre-specified correspondence criteria, and (3) evaluation of predictive adequacy using a four-criterion instrument.

### 2.2 Phase 1: Formalization of Patanjali's Taxonomy (Directionality Safeguard)

The primary source text was the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, analyzed in Sanskrit with reference to Bryant's scholarly translation [6] and Hartranft's practitioner-oriented rendering [7]. Rather than treating the text as a unified philosophical position, we approached it as a structured descriptive instrument — analogous in function to a diagnostic taxonomy — and extracted categorical distinctions that met three criteria: (a) the distinction is explicitly marked by terminological differentiation in the source text, (b) the distinction implies a difference in functional or phenomenological process rather than merely degree of proficiency, and (c) the distinction is reproducible across independent commentarial interpretations.

To preserve genuine directionality of prediction, all neutral cognitive descriptions of claim units were extracted prior to any consultation of the neurobiological reference corpus. The extraction used only the primary Sanskrit text and two established scholarly translations (Bryant 2009; Hartranft 2003). Each sutra was decomposed into minimal functional assertions stated in neutral cognitive vocabulary; no neurobiological terms were permitted at this stage. The raw extraction notes are available from the corresponding author upon request.

Patanjali employs a layered taxonomic structure that distinguishes among vrittis (mental fluctuations classified into five types), progressive stages of samapatti (coalescence of cognition with its object), and at least four phenomenologically differentiated stages of samadhi, separated by the presence or absence of vitarka (discursive reasoning), vichara (subtle reflection), ananda (affective tone), and asmita (self-referential processing) [6]. These distinctions were extracted, tabulated, and assigned provisional functional descriptions in neutral cognitive vocabulary — for example, savitarka samadhi was characterized as "object-directed cognition with concurrent verbal-conceptual elaboration," and nirvitarka samadhi as "object-directed cognition with suppression of verbal-conceptual elaboration."

This translation step was performed prior to and independently of any review of the neuroimaging or electrophysiological literature, to preserve the directionality of prediction. The use of neutral cognitive vocabulary at this stage is methodologically consequential: it prevents the translator from unconsciously selecting neurobiological terms that are already known to correspond, which would undermine the claim that correspondence was discovered rather than constructed. In practice, a small number of edge cases required minor rephrasing of functional assertions; the core functional content of those assertions remained unchanged.

Five primary domains of Patanjali's taxonomy were identified as sufficiently specified for cross-domain comparison: attentional hierarchy, self-referential processing, interoceptive sensitivity, memory and state-dependency, and effort/effortlessness as qualitatively distinct cognitive modes.

### 2.3 Phase 2: Identification of Neurobiological Candidate Constructs

For each of the five domains, a targeted literature review was conducted using PubMed. Neurobiological constructs were considered candidate matches if they satisfied two conditions: (a) they were established in the empirical literature through paradigms independent of meditation research, and (b) the functional description of the construct overlapped with the neutral cognitive description of the Patanjali category across at least two independent operational dimensions.

The independence criterion is the most important methodological safeguard. Cross-domain isomorphism is informative only when the two domains arrived at their distinctions through separate routes. Constructs developed specifically to characterize meditation data do not satisfy this condition and were excluded from the primary mapping analysis.

### 2.4 Phase 3: The Four-Criterion Isomorphism Instrument

Each proposed mapping was evaluated against four criteria:

**Criterion 1 — Functional Isomorphism (C1):** Does the claim unit from the phenomenological text map onto an identifiable construct in the neurobiological reference corpus? The reference corpus is consciousness-specific: it contains entries on neural mechanisms of attention, self-referential processing, oscillatory binding, interoception, and altered states. Domain specificity is enforced — claims must map to consciousness-relevant neurobiology, not to biology generally.

**Criterion 2 — Independent Derivation (C2):** Were the phenomenological and neurobiological accounts developed without causal contact? For pre-modern texts (pre-1800 CE), this criterion is structurally satisfied by temporal separation.

**Criterion 3 — Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint (C3):** Does the phenomenological claim generate a testable prediction that goes beyond what the neurobiological construct independently predicts? This criterion requires that the mapping add informational value — not merely restate what neuroscience already knows in different vocabulary.

**Criterion 4 — Asymmetric Disconfirmation (C4):** Are there clear falsification paths from both the phenomenological and neuroscientific directions? A mapping that can only be disconfirmed from one side is weaker than one vulnerable to refutation from either tradition.

Mappings meeting all four criteria are classified as **Tier A**. Mappings meeting C1 and C2 with partial scores on C3 or C4 are classified as **Tier B**. Mappings meeting C1 and C2 but failing both C3 and C4 are **Tier C**. Mappings failing C1 are **Tier D** — no functional isomorphism found.

### 2.5 Pilot Study Design

To empirically validate the instrument, two pilot studies were conducted.

**Pilot 1 — Cross-Tradition Comparison.** 33 discrete functional claim units were extracted from Yoga Sutras I.17–I.51 (Corpus A). Two negative control corpora were constructed: Charaka Samhita (Corpus B, n=33 units from the Ayurvedic medical tradition) and Hero of Alexandria's Pneumatica (Corpus C, n=33 units from classical Greco-Roman engineering). The biological reference corpus contained entries on neural mechanisms of consciousness drawn from the project knowledge base. Each claim unit in all three corpora was scored on all four criteria by an automated scoring agent (Claude Sonnet 4.6).

**Pilot 2 — Within-Tradition Comparison.** To test whether the instrument discriminates by domain content rather than by tradition of origin, a second pilot applied the identical procedure to the Theravāda Abhidhammattha-Saṅgaha [16]. Corpus D consisted of 15 consciousness-specific claim units extracted from Chapters I–V (Citta, Cetasika, Pakiṇṇaka, Vīthi, and Vīthimutta — covering mental factors, cognitive sequences, and absorptive states). Corpus E consisted of 12 matter-analysis units extracted from Chapter VI (Rūpa — covering material form, derived materiality, and physical bases). Both corpora were extracted using the same directionality safeguard as Pilot 1: neutral cognitive vocabulary, no neurobiological terms, prior to any reference corpus consultation. An independent validation of Corpus D confirmed zero directionality contamination across all 15 units.

Human inter-rater reliability validation (target κ ≥ 0.75) is planned for the first replication study and will be reported separately. The choice of distant negative controls in Pilot 1 was deliberately conservative to establish a clear baseline; the within-tradition control in Pilot 2 provides the tighter test. Statistical comparison of Tier A survival rates across corpora used Fisher's exact test.

## 3. Qualitative Domain Mappings

### 3.1 Domain 1: Attentional Hierarchy (Dharana, Dhyana, Samadhi)

Patanjali's graded attentional sequence — from dharana (sustained object-directed attention with effortful maintenance), through dhyana (uninterrupted object-directed attention with reduced maintenance cost), to samadhi (object-directed absorption with dissolution of subject-object duality) — was mapped to the neurobiological distinction between effortful top-down attentional control and automatized processing. The neutral cognitive description of dharana (periodic re-engagement of attentional focus following mind-wandering) corresponds functionally to prefrontal-mediated top-down attention, which is associated with sustained lateral prefrontal and anterior cingulate recruitment [5,9]. The description of dhyana (continuous object engagement without periodic re-engagement effort) corresponds to the reduction in prefrontal recruitment observed as attentional tasks become automatized through practice.

Independent electrophysiological research has characterized focused attention meditation as associated with increased gamma-band synchrony in task-relevant regions [3], with the ratio of these oscillatory signatures shifting across practice-level groups. This mapping was classified as **strong** and primarily retrospective: the functional correspondence spans at least three dimensions (effort recruitment, oscillatory signature, and continuity of object-engagement), and replication across independent groups using both EEG and fMRI methodologies is documented [3,5].

### 3.2 Domain 2: Self-Referential Processing and Asmita

Patanjali explicitly marks the presence or absence of asmita (self-referential processing, literally "I-am-ness") as a categorical boundary between sa-asmita samadhi and more advanced samadic stages in which self-referential elaboration is absent. The neutral cognitive description — object-directed cognition with concurrent self-referential processing versus object-directed cognition with suppression of self-referential processing — maps directly onto the neurobiological distinction between conditions of default mode network engagement and suppression [2,8].

The DMN, identified by Raichle et al. [2] as a network of brain regions consistently more active during rest than during goal-directed tasks, has been extensively characterized as mediating self-referential thought, autobiographical memory retrieval, and social cognition. Brewer et al. [8] demonstrated that experienced meditators show decreased DMN activation across multiple meditation types, with the posterior cingulate and medial prefrontal cortices relatively deactivated compared to controls. Josipovic [10,11] has specifically investigated nondual awareness — a state in which the subject-object structure of ordinary experience is relaxed — and documented its neural correlates in the anticorrelation between intrinsic and extrinsic brain networks.

This mapping was classified as **strong** and primarily retrospective: the correspondence holds across functional description (self-referential vs. non-self-referential processing), network architecture (DMN engagement vs. suppression), and categorical logic (Patanjali treats the boundary as qualitative, consistent with evidence for bistable dynamics between DMN and task-positive networks).

### 3.3 Domain 3: Interoceptive Processing and Pratyahara

Patanjali's concept of pratyahara (withdrawal of the senses) was characterized in neutral cognitive vocabulary as selective attenuation of exteroceptive sensory responsivity concurrent with preserved or enhanced interoceptive access. The neurobiological candidate constructs are the insular interoceptive hierarchy [12] and the distinction between exteroceptive and interoceptive processing streams.

Craig [12] has described a hierarchical interoceptive architecture in which the insular cortex and anterior cingulate cortex substantialize feelings from the body and conjoin them with homeostatic motivations. The mechanism implied by pratyahara — preserved interoceptive access concurrent with reduced exteroceptive responsivity — is not a standard paradigm in systems neuroscience and has not, to the authors' knowledge, been directly tested in non-meditation populations.

This mapping was classified as **moderate**, with the critical observation that the pratyahara domain represents the clearest candidate for *prospective* experimental paradigm development motivated by the phenomenological taxonomy. A paradigm testing whether voluntary shifts of attentional orientation toward interoceptive signals produce measurable reductions in exteroceptive sensitivity — measured independently of any meditation instruction — would constitute a direct test of the mechanism Patanjali's construct implies.

### 3.4 Domain 4: State-Dependent Memory and Samskara

Patanjali's constructs of samskara (latent impressions formed by repeated mental activity) and vasana (dispositional tendencies arising from accumulated samskaras) were characterized as state-dependent dispositional memory structures that bias future cognition without requiring explicit retrieval. The neurobiological candidates are synaptic consolidation processes, including long-term potentiation, spike-timing dependent plasticity (STDP) [13], and the role of hippocampal-neocortical transfer in schematic memory formation.

Patanjali's account implies that dispositional structures formed during altered states of consciousness would be preferentially retrieved under similar state conditions — a prediction derivable from the samskara construct prior to any review of the state-dependency literature. More strikingly, YS I.50 claims that the subliminal trace from truth-bearing absorptive knowledge actively suppresses competing trace formation. This maps onto heterosynaptic suppressive effects in STDP: dominant synchronous assemblies strengthened by spike-timing correlations can depotentiate competing assemblies [13]. The claim that a single experiential episode creates a trace so dominant that it blocks future competing conditioning predicts an asymmetric heterosynaptic suppression effect not typically emphasized in standard STDP models.

This mapping was classified as **moderate-to-speculative**, with the state-dependency correspondence at moderate and the heterosynaptic suppression prediction at speculative pending experimental investigation.

### 3.5 Domain 5: Cognitive Effort and the Sa-Bija / Nir-Bija Distinction

Patanjali's distinction between sa-bija (with seed) and nir-bija (without seed) samadhi maps onto the distinction between cognitive states that retain latent dispositional substrates available for re-engagement versus states in which such substrates are fully quiescent. No established neurobiological paradigm directly addresses the elimination of latent cognitive dispositions at the systems level.

This mapping was classified as **weak-to-speculative** and is flagged as an area where the phenomenological taxonomy may outpace available neuroscientific constructs — a finding that parallels the pratyahara case in Domain 3.

### 3.6 Summary of Qualitative Classification

Across five domains, two mappings (attentional hierarchy and self-referential processing) met criteria for strong classification and are primarily retrospective. One mapping (interoceptive processing) met moderate criteria and is primarily prospective. One mapping (dispositional memory) spanned moderate-to-speculative. One mapping (effort and dispositional substrate) was classified as weak-to-speculative. The asymmetric distribution of evidential support across domains is itself an analytic result: it indicates that Patanjali's taxonomy is most tractable where neuroscience has developed the richest independent constructs, and least tractable where the implied mechanisms remain without established non-meditation analogues.

## 4. Preliminary Empirical Results

### 4.1 Pilot 1: Cross-Tradition Comparison

#### 4.1.1 Corpus Construction

**Corpus A (Contemplative — Patanjali):** 33 discrete functional claim units extracted from Yoga Sutras I.17–I.51 [6,7]. Each sutra was decomposed into its minimal functional assertion, stated in neutral cognitive vocabulary. Claims ranged from attentional mechanics (I.17: four distinguishable cognitive modes in samprajnata samadhi) to memory dynamics (I.50: dominant trace suppression) to metaphysical assertions (I.19: prior-life causation, which fails C1).

**Corpus B (Traditional Medicine — Charaka):** 33 claim units from the Charaka Samhita, the foundational Ayurvedic medical text (circa 100–200 CE). Claims included tridosha regulatory theory, disease etiologies, and treatment rationales.

**Corpus C (Classical Engineering — Hero):** 33 claim units from Hero of Alexandria's Pneumatica (circa 50 CE), covering pneumatic mechanisms, hydraulic principles, and mechanical devices.

The biological reference corpus contained entries on neural mechanisms of consciousness, attention, self-referential processing, oscillatory binding, interoception, and altered states.

#### 4.1.2 Results: Tier A Survival

Tier A classification (all four criteria met) was achieved by:

- Corpus A (Patanjali): 10/33 units — **30.3%**
- Corpus B (Charaka): 0/33 units — **0.0%**
- Corpus C (Hero): 0/33 units — **0.0%**

Fisher's exact test: A vs. B, p < 0.001; A vs. C, p < 0.001; B vs. C, p = 1.000.

#### 4.1.3 The Ten Tier A Units

The surviving Patanjali units cluster around three functional domains. Each carries an explicit designation of whether the correspondence is primarily retrospective or prospective. Note that A-032 (heterosynaptic suppression) was classified as moderate-to-speculative in the qualitative mapping (Section 3.4) and retains that epistemic status here; its Tier A survival reflects strong functional and independent-derivation support but rests on a mechanistic assumption about STDP that remains untested in this context.

*Attentional mechanics and alternative pathways:*
- **A-007 (YS I.23):** Devotional meditation constitutes a neurally distinct pathway to absorption, predicting dissociable neural routes (affective-attentional via ACC/insula/limbic vs. prefrontal executive) converging on a common absorptive endpoint. *[Prospective: dissociable route convergence untested.]*
- **A-010 (YS I.27–28):** Symbol-meaning coupling (OM repetition with semantic engagement) predicts synergistic interaction between acoustic entrainment of thalamocortical circuits and top-down semantic attention — exceeding what pure auditory entrainment achieves alone. *[Prospective: quantitative synergy untested.]*
- **A-011 (YS I.29):** Single practice (mantra with meaning) simultaneously redirects attention inward AND reduces physiological markers of obstacle-states, predicting a dual-outcome mechanism rather than separate interventions. *[Retrospective: consistent with HRV and attention data; prospective: dual-mechanism dissociation untested.]*
- **A-014 (YS I.32):** Single-pointed focus functions as a universal countermeasure to all nine obstacle categories via a common mechanism (ACC-mediated conflict resolution), rather than requiring obstacle-specific interventions. *[Prospective: universality claim and common-mechanism hypothesis untested.]*
- **A-016 (YS I.34):** Controlled breathing produces cognitive stabilization via autonomic regulation (bottom-up) rather than top-down attentional mechanisms — predicting dissociable HRV-gamma coupling pathways [4,12]. *[Retrospective: HRV-cognition relationship established; prospective: dissociability from top-down pathway untested.]*

*Self-referential processing and absorptive transparency:*
- **A-002 (YS I.18):** During cessation-type absorptive states, cortical gamma coherence and global workspace broadcasting should collapse, yet hippocampal and subcortical synaptic plasticity traces should remain intact — predicting a dissociation between cortical integration loss and preserved memory consolidation substrates [14]. *[Prospective: cortical-subcortical dissociation in this specific state untested.]*
- **A-023 (YS I.41):** When cognitive noise is suppressed, gamma coherence patterns should isomorphically reflect the attended object's structure, and this transparency should extend to metacognitive objects — predicting that self-directed absorption shows the same high-fidelity neural mirroring as object-directed absorption [3]. *[Retrospective: gamma mirroring established; prospective: extension to metacognitive objects untested.]*
- **A-025 (YS I.43):** Deep absorption should show simultaneous suppression of left perisylvian language cortex AND default-mode self-referential activity, while preserving or enhancing sensory cortical representations — a dissociation between increased perceptual fidelity and decreased linguistic/conceptual processing [2,8]. *[Retrospective: component findings exist; prospective: joint simultaneous dissociation in single paradigm untested.]*

*Somatic correlates and trace dynamics:*
- **A-013 (YS I.31):** Cognitive obstacles reliably co-occur with reduced HRV coherence, irregular respiratory sinus arrhythmia, and increased muscle tremor as obligatory concomitants — not occasional correlates [4]. *[Prospective: obligatory co-occurrence and multimodal specificity untested.]*
- **A-032 (YS I.50):** The subliminal trace from absorptive knowledge actively depotentiates competing assemblies via heterosynaptic STDP effects — predicting decreased LTP induction probability at non-reinforced synapses following samadhi-like training [13]. *(Epistemic note: this prediction remains moderate-to-speculative; Tier A survival reflects functional and derivation support, not mechanistic confirmation.) [Prospective: direct test pending.]*

### 4.2 Pilot 1: Negative Control Performance — The C1 Domain Boundary

The most informative finding concerns the mechanism of negative control failure. Both Charaka and Hero fail overwhelmingly at Criterion 1 — Functional Isomorphism with the consciousness-specific reference corpus — rather than at later criteria.

Charaka Samhita claims such as "vitiated pitta produces jaundice" or "vata subtype disturbance causes tremor" are perfectly coherent biological assertions. They fail C1 not because they are pre-scientific, but because they address the wrong biological domain. The reference corpus contains neural mechanisms of consciousness; Charaka's domain is hepatic, inflammatory, and metabolic pathology. This is a principled domain-failure, not an epistemological one.

Hero of Alexandria fails identically: pneumatic and hydraulic mechanisms have no mapping to neural oscillatory signatures or self-referential processing. Again, the claims are internally coherent within their domain — they simply have nothing to say to consciousness neuroscience.

This selectivity pattern is stronger than we designed for. The instrument does not merely separate "ancient" from "modern" or "scientific" from "pre-scientific." It discriminates between phenomenological systems that address consciousness specifically and systems that are biologically or mechanically coherent but domain-mismatched.

### 4.3 Pilot 1: Methodological Cautionary — Reference Corpus Sensitivity

A secondary analysis relaxing the domain constraint on C1 — permitting mapping to any biological isomorphism rather than consciousness-specific neurobiology — produced dramatically different results: Charaka survival rose from 0% to 100% on the C1+C3+C4 conjunction. This occurs because the tridosha framework IS a functional biological architecture; its regulatory logic maps coherently onto neural conduction patterns, Henneman's Size Principle, and HPA/HPG co-regulation when the domain gate is removed. The exact relaxation criterion applied was: any functional claim unit that could be interpreted as describing a regulatory, homeostatic, or flow-based mechanism in living systems — independent of whether the mechanism was framed as neural, humoral, or metabolic — was accepted for scoring.

This finding demonstrates that the instrument's discriminant validity depends critically on reference corpus specificity. Relaxing the domain constraint destroys the ability to distinguish between consciousness-directed phenomenological systems and domain-general biological frameworks. We report this as a methodological result: the four-criterion instrument is valid only when the reference corpus enforces the domain boundary that the research question requires.

### 4.4 Pilot 2: Within-Tradition Comparison (Abhidhamma)

#### 4.4.1 Rationale

The cross-tradition controls in Pilot 1 are deliberately distant from the target domain. A reviewer could reasonably argue that 0% survival for humoral medicine and classical engineering is unsurprising and does not constitute a demanding test. The critical question is whether the instrument discriminates within a phenomenological tradition — that is, between consciousness-specific and non-consciousness-specific content produced by the same intellectual lineage.

The Theravāda Abhidhammattha-Saṅgaha [16] provides an ideal test case. Chapters I–V present a systematic taxonomy of mental factors (cetasika), cognitive sequences (vīthi), and absorptive states (jhāna) — consciousness-specific phenomenological content comparable in kind (though not in source) to Patanjali's Yoga Sutras. Chapter VI (Rūpa) presents an analysis of material form — derived materiality, physical bases, and elemental composition — using the same analytical framework and commentarial tradition but addressing physical rather than mental phenomena.

#### 4.4.2 Results

Corpus D (Abhidhamma consciousness, Chapters I–V): 5/15 units — **33.3%** Tier A
Corpus E (Abhidhamma matter, Chapter VI): 0/12 units — **0.0%** Tier A

Fisher's exact test: D vs. E, p = 0.047.

#### 4.4.3 Five Tier A Abhidhamma Units

- **ADH-001 (Ch. I):** Five aggregates as functionally dissociable processing streams — predicting that form, sensation, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness map onto distinct but interacting neural substrates rather than constituting a unitary process. *[Retrospective: component dissociations established; prospective: five-way dissociation with the specific Abhidhamma grouping untested.]*
- **ADH-003 (Ch. IV):** Invariant jhāna factor-elimination sequence — the Abhidhamma specifies that vitakka (applied thought) drops before vicāra (sustained thought), which drops before pīti (rapture), in a fixed order across the four jhānas. This predicts a specific temporal sequence of neural deactivations: prefrontal executive → sustained attention networks → affective reward circuits. Quantitatively, each transition should be temporally resolvable (≥2 s separation between successive factor eliminations in high-temporal-resolution EEG), and each network should show ≥30% power reduction in its characteristic frequency band before the next factor drops. *[Prospective: sequential deactivation order, temporal separation, and stepwise power reduction untested as a fixed sequence. Strongest prospective unit across both corpora.]*
- **ADH-006 (Ch. II):** Universal cetasika (mental factors present in every moment of consciousness) as mandatory neural co-activations — predicts that phassa (contact), vedanā (feeling), saññā (perception), cetanā (volition), ekaggatā (one-pointedness), jīvitindriya (psychic life), and manasikāra (attention) have neural correlates that fire in every conscious moment. *[Retrospective: some components established; prospective: mandatory co-occurrence of all seven untested.]*
- **ADH-009 (Ch. IV):** Jhāna access requires elimination of specific hindrances in a defined sequence, not merely a general calming — predicts that each hindrance (sensory desire, ill-will, sloth-torpor, restlessness, doubt) has a specific neural signature whose suppression is prerequisite for the next stage. *[Prospective: hindrance-specific neural prerequisites untested.]*
- **ADH-013 (Ch. V):** Content tranquillity (passaddhi of mental factors) is dissociable from substrate tranquillity (passaddhi of consciousness itself) — predicts that phenomenological calm has two independent neural mechanisms: one affecting representational content (likely cortical, measurable as reduced high-gamma power in content-specific regions) and one affecting the substrate of awareness itself (likely thalamo-cortical or brainstem, measurable as altered thalamic gating oscillations in the alpha-sigma range). These two measures should be dissociable (predicted inter-measure correlation r < 0.30), indicating genuine mechanistic independence rather than a single "calm" dimension. *[Prospective: content-substrate tranquillity dissociation and mechanistic independence untested. Strongest novel prediction for experimental design.]*

#### 4.4.4 Within-Tradition Discrimination

The within-tradition result is the paper's strongest discriminant validity finding. The Abhidhammattha-Saṅgaha's consciousness chapters and matter chapter share: (a) the same author tradition, (b) the same analytical framework, (c) the same commentarial lineage, (d) the same historical period, and (e) the same extraction and scoring procedure. The only variable is domain content — mental phenomena versus physical phenomena. Consciousness-specific content produces 33.3% Tier A survival; matter-analysis content produces 0%.

This result eliminates the cultural-diffusion objection. A reviewer cannot argue that the instrument rewards ancient contemplative traditions per se, because Chapter VI is equally ancient and equally contemplative in its analytical method. The instrument discriminates by domain content, not by source prestige, cultural proximity, or historical period.

The two Tier C units in Corpus E (R-VI-008, hadayavatthu/heart-base; R-VI-011, rūpa arising from citta/consciousness-derived materiality) are instructive: they are the only matter-chapter units that touch consciousness, and even they achieve only partial isomorphism. The instrument catches the gradient.

## 5. Discussion

The central methodological claim of this paper is not that Patanjali's Yoga Sutras constitute a proto-neuroscientific text, but that a structured phenomenological system developed through centuries of refined introspective practice may encode regularities about mental state-space that retain predictive value when translated into contemporary cognitive vocabulary. The pilot results offer quantitative support for this claim: Patanjali's system generates Tier A isomorphisms at a rate significantly exceeding both controls (30.3% vs. 0%, p<0.001), and the selectivity mechanism is principled rather than arbitrary.

### 5.1 The Domain Boundary as Selectivity Mechanism

The most analytically significant finding is that Criterion 1 — Functional Isomorphism with consciousness-specific neurobiology — does the primary discriminative work. Charaka and Hero fail not because they lack internal coherence, but because their domains do not intersect with the consciousness reference corpus. This means the instrument is testing something precise: not "is this ancient system scientifically valid?" but "does this ancient system generate claims that constrain what biology would predict about consciousness specifically?"

The contrast between Charaka's 0% Tier A survival and 100% survival when the domain constraint is relaxed is the sharpest demonstration. Charaka is a biologically coherent system whose regulatory logic maps readily onto modern physiology — when the right physiology is consulted. The tridosha framework fails at C1 against a consciousness corpus for the same reason that a textbook of renal pathology would fail: it is answering a different question.

### 5.2 Cross-Tradition Convergence

The convergence between Patanjali (30.3% Tier A) and Abhidhamma consciousness chapters (33.3% Tier A) is itself a finding worth naming. These are two phenomenological systems developed in different centuries, different languages, and different contemplative lineages — Brahmanical and Theravāda Buddhist respectively — yet they produce nearly identical survival rates against the same consciousness-specific reference corpus. This convergence is consistent with the hypothesis that systematic introspective practice, conducted over extended periods within a structured analytical framework, converges on functional regularities about mental state-space that are robust enough to survive formal cross-domain evaluation.

The higher rate is not an artifact of easier content: the Abhidhamma's claim units are if anything more technically specified than Patanjali's, and the matter chapter from the same text produces 0%. The convergence zone of approximately 30–33% may represent a natural ceiling for the proportion of phenomenological claims that generate non-trivial, falsifiable predictions about consciousness neurobiology — but this speculation requires additional traditions and larger corpora to evaluate.

### 5.3 Asymmetric Evidential Distribution

The asymmetric distribution of qualitative mapping quality across the five domains is itself informative. The two strong correspondences (attentional hierarchy and self-referential processing) concentrate precisely where contemporary neuroscience has developed its most replicated non-meditation constructs: default mode network dynamics [2,8] and the automatization of attentional control [3,5]. Where neuroscience has not yet developed established non-meditation paradigms — as in the case of sa-bija/nir-bija distinctions or the specific exteroceptive gating mechanism implied by pratyahara — the mappings remain speculative not because the phenomenological descriptions are imprecise, but because the relevant experimental infrastructure does not yet exist. This pattern suggests the phenomenological framework may be ahead of available measurement tools in at least two domains.

### 5.4 The Pratyahara Paradigm Opportunity

The pratyahara mapping (Domain 3) represents the most generatively novel result from the qualitative analysis and the clearest prospective prediction in the paper. The mechanism implied by pratyahara — preserved or enhanced interoceptive access concurrent with reduced responsivity to exteroceptive stimulation — is not a standard paradigm in systems neuroscience. The insular interoceptive hierarchy described by Craig [12] provides a plausible neuroanatomical substrate. A paradigm testing whether voluntary shifts of attentional orientation toward interoceptive signals produce measurable reductions in exteroceptive sensitivity — measured independently of any meditation instruction — would constitute a direct test generated by the phenomenological taxonomy.

### 5.5 Limitations

Several limitations require explicit statement. First, the mapping procedure depends on translation and interpretive judgments at the level of neutral cognitive description. Different translators render key terms differently, and the cognitive vocabulary applied in Phase 1 reflects interpretive choices that may introduce bias despite the directionality safeguard [6,7]. Second, the pilot studies used automated scoring (Claude Sonnet 4.6) without human inter-rater reliability validation; this represents a significant limitation, and the full study must include independent human scoring of a subset of claim units to establish calibration. Third, the independence criterion excludes neurobiological constructs developed specifically within meditation research, which is conservative but principled. Fourth, the Pilot 1 corpus of 33 units from Sutras I.17–I.51 covers only the samadhi pada; the remaining three padas contain additional claim units that may alter the survival rate in either direction. Fifth, the reference corpus was generated from a project knowledge base rather than from a systematic review, introducing potential selection bias in which neurobiological constructs were available for mapping. Sixth, the Pilot 2 Abhidhamma corpus (15 consciousness units, 12 matter units) is small; expansion to 33 units matching the Patanjali corpus size is planned for the replication study. Seventh, while the within-tradition control eliminates the cultural-diffusion objection, it does not address the possibility that consciousness-directed phenomenological systems share structural features that produce spurious Tier A survival independent of genuine predictive value; a randomized control corpus (scrambled claim units preserving domain vocabulary) would address this concern.

### 5.6 Proposed Experimental Research Program

The Tier A units from both corpora translate directly into a coherent, pre-registrable research program. The highest-priority studies are:

1. **Cortical-integration / memory-consolidation dissociation (A-002)** — Test whether cessation-type absorptive states produce ≥40% gamma-power collapse in cortex while hippocampal/subcortical plasticity markers remain intact (fMRI-EEG + post-session memory task).

2. **Gamma mirroring extending to metacognitive objects (A-023)** — Use representational similarity analysis to compare self-directed vs. object-directed absorption; predict equivalent high-fidelity coherence (≥0.75) across both conditions.

3. **Heterosynaptic suppression (A-032)** — Examine LTP induction probability after samadhi-like training; predict ≥25% reduction at non-reinforced synapses. Note: this prediction carries moderate-to-speculative epistemic status pending direct confirmation.

4. **Invariant jhāna factor-elimination sequence (ADH-003)** — Use high-temporal-resolution EEG during jhāna access to test whether prefrontal executive deactivation precedes sustained-attention network deactivation, which precedes affective reward circuit deactivation, in a fixed temporal order with ≥2 s separation and ≥30% characteristic-band power reduction at each step.

5. **Content-substrate tranquillity dissociation (ADH-013)** — Design a paradigm dissociating cortical representational calm (high-gamma power in content-specific regions) from thalamo-cortical substrate calm (alpha-sigma gating oscillations) using simultaneous EEG-fMRI with phenomenological reporting; predict inter-measure r < 0.30.

6. **Pratyahara paradigm (Domain 3)** — Develop a non-meditation paradigm testing voluntary interoceptive attention shifts and their effect on exteroceptive sensitivity (independent of any contemplative instruction). This is the clearest case where phenomenology currently outpaces available measurement tools.

These studies, if successful, would move the instrument from pilot validation to active hypothesis generation.

### 5.7 Implications

This analysis does not demonstrate that Patanjali's framework is neurobiologically accurate. It demonstrates that categorical distinctions drawn on phenomenological grounds correspond to independently derived neurobiological constructs at a level of specificity that exceeds what domain-mismatched controls produce, in a statistically significant and methodologically principled manner. The within-tradition result strengthens this conclusion: the instrument is not rewarding contemplative traditions as a class, but discriminating by domain content within a single tradition. The appropriate inference is that structured phenomenological systems developed through sustained and systematic introspective practice may constitute a legitimate source of hypothesis generation for cognitive and clinical neuroscience — not that ancient texts provide ground truth about neural mechanisms.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper developed and applied a four-criterion isomorphism instrument (C1: Functional Isomorphism, C2: Independent Derivation, C3: Non-Trivial Predictive Constraint, C4: Asymmetric Disconfirmation) to evaluate cross-domain correspondence between classical phenomenology and contemporary neurobiology. Qualitative mapping produced two strong retrospective correspondences, one moderate prospective correspondence, and two weak-to-speculative mappings across five domains of Patanjali's Yoga Sutras.

Two pilot studies provided quantitative validation. In Pilot 1, 30.3% of Patanjali units achieved Tier A (all four criteria met) versus 0% for both negative controls (p<0.001), with the selectivity mechanism operating at the domain boundary (Criterion 1) rather than at independent derivation (Criterion 2). In Pilot 2, consciousness-specific chapters of the Theravāda Abhidhammattha-Saṅgaha achieved 33.3% Tier A while matter-analysis chapters of the same text achieved 0% (p=0.047), demonstrating that the instrument discriminates by domain content rather than by tradition of origin.

The convergence of two independent consciousness traditions at approximately 30–33% Tier A, against 0% for all controls, suggests a natural range for the proportion of structured phenomenological claims that survive formal cross-domain evaluation against consciousness neurobiology. The strongest surviving pairs — from Patanjali, the cortical-integration/memory-consolidation dissociation (A-002), samapatti transparency predicting domain-general gamma mirroring (A-023), and dominant trace suppression predicting heterosynaptic STDP effects (A-032, moderate-to-speculative); from the Abhidhamma, the invariant jhāna factor-elimination sequence (ADH-003) and the content-substrate tranquillity dissociation (ADH-013) — generate specific, falsifiable predictions testable with existing neuroscientific methods.

The methodological contribution is the directional procedure itself, the demonstration that reference corpus specificity is load-bearing for discriminant validity, and the within-tradition control design that eliminates cultural-diffusion confounds. Human inter-rater validation and corpus expansion remain as required steps before stronger claims can be made. Ancient phenomenology, treated as a hypothesis-generation instrument rather than a historical artifact, may constitute an underutilized resource for cognitive and clinical neuroscience.
