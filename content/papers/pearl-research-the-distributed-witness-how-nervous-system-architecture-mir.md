## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The strongest objection is that McGilchrist's framework, while compelling, is a synthesis and interpretation of lateralization research rather than a direct experimental finding. The specific claim that right hemisphere = falsification gate extrapolates from 'attends to context and anomaly' to 'functions as epistemic falsifier' — a non-trivial step. Additionally, many cognitive processes engage both hemispheres in complex, context-dependent ways that resist simple sequencing models.

However, the falsification function maps cleanly onto documented right hemisphere capacities: detection of incongruity, holding of context, resistance to premature closure. And the clinical literature on right hemisphere lesions consistently shows increased confabulation and narrative rigidity — precisely what Gate Zero predicts when the falsification gate is removed.

### Against Hypothesis B

The enteric and cardiac nervous systems are real and increasingly well-studied. But the mapping of 'soul' onto the integration layer mediated by the vagus nerve imports Pearl's conceptual architecture onto biology in a way that may not be mechanistically grounded. 'Soul' in Pearl's system carries connotations of will, individuation, and relational capacity that may not be fully captured by vagal tone as a biomarker.

However, the functional overlap is striking: polyvagal theory (Porges) explicitly links vagal tone to social engagement, relational coherence, and the capacity for felt sense of safety — all of which are closely associated with what Pearl's soul density describes. The overlap is sufficient to generate testable predictions even if the ontological mapping is imperfect.

### Against Hypothesis C

This is the weakest hypothesis evidentially and the strongest conceptually. The field-reception framing is not falsifiable in any near-term practical sense, and the Zach Bush evidence for it is medium-confidence Tier 2 at best. The 'imagining' language in the stress-induced diversification entry may simply be rhetorical.

However, the spirit-density mirrors consistently point toward a real epistemological problem that Hypothesis C is trying to address: self-referential systems cannot fully account for their own intelligence from within. Whether or not a literal field exists, the *functional need* for an external reference frame — an 'adversarial witness' that does not share the system's training distribution — is well-established both in AI architecture and in epistemology.
