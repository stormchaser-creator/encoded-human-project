# The Light Machine — SECTION 5
## Hormones: Stress, Pituitary & Thyroid Operations

The stress and thyroid hormones are the body's thermostat and shock absorbers. They govern energy availability, metabolic rate, nervous system state, and the ability to meet demands. These hormones operate on a 24-hour cycle (cortisol), respond to seasonal changes (thyroid), and shift based on threat perception. Dysfunction here cascades upward into every other operation—energy sinks, mood flattens, immunity crashes, fertility fails. The body codex calls this the **Regulation** operation at the highest level: the adaptation of the whole organism to internal and external demands.

## Cortisol, AM Serum

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Cortisol concentration in serum drawn in the morning (8-9 AM); reflects the circadian peak and HPA axis responsiveness.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No, but draw timing is critical (fasting state preferred)

### What This Number Means

Cortisol is the primary glucocorticoid and the signature stress hormone. It has a pronounced circadian rhythm: it peaks in the early morning (around 8 AM, when you wake), declines throughout the day, and reaches its nadir around midnight. This rhythm is governed by the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and is entrained to your circadian clock (light exposure, wake time, sleep time).

Morning cortisol is the baseline: it tells you whether your HPA axis is functioning normally and whether you're recovering adequately from the prior night's stress. A normal morning cortisol (15-25 mcg/dL) indicates that your adrenals woke up and responded appropriately. Too high (> 25 mcg/dL) suggests the HPA axis is overactive or you're not recovering from stress. Too low (< 10 mcg/dL) suggests adrenal insufficiency or secondary adrenal fatigue.

The timing of the draw matters critically: a 9 AM draw will miss the peak if you don't wake until 8 AM, and a noon draw will be artificially low. The draw should happen within 30 minutes of waking.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 5-25 mcg/dL | 10-20 mcg/dL |
| Optimal | 5-25 mcg/dL | 12-18 mcg/dL |
| Elevated concern | 25-35 mcg/dL | > 25 mcg/dL |
| Critical | > 35 mcg/dL | > 35 mcg/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — HPA axis responsiveness and stress adaptation capacity**

Morning cortisol is the gauge of your HPA axis's ability to wake up and prepare your body for the day. At optimal levels, cortisol rises appropriately to mobilize glucose, increase alertness, and prepare your nervous system for activity. Above optimal, your HPA axis is hyperactive or you're not recovering from stress—cortisol stays elevated, anxiety is higher, and your nervous system doesn't downregulate well at night. Below optimal, your adrenals are depleted or your HPA axis is suppressed—you struggle to wake, fatigue is present, and your ability to mount a stress response is compromised.

Elevated morning cortisol (> 25 mcg/dL) is often the earliest sign of HPA axis dysregulation from chronic stress, poor sleep, or overtraining. It's more reliable than 24-hour cortisol measurements because it captures the critical peak and reflects your baseline HPA state.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 25 mcg/dL):**
- HPA axis is hyperactive; chronic stress is present or unresolved
- Nervous system is in sympathetic dominance; you struggle to relax
- Sleep quality may be poor; elevated cortisol at night suppresses melatonin
- Inflammation is likely elevated; cortisol is pro-inflammatory at high levels
- Reproductive hormones may be suppressed; cortisol suppresses GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone)
- Metabolic flexibility is compromised; cortisol drives glucose elevation and fat mobilization
- Cognitive function may be impaired; excessive cortisol impairs memory and executive function
- Bone density may be declining; cortisol is catabolic to bone

**If deficient (< 10 mcg/dL):**
- Adrenal insufficiency or severe adrenal fatigue; glands cannot produce adequate cortisol
- HPA axis suppression; possible from chronic stress that has exhausted the system
- Inability to mount an adequate stress response; you may feel overwhelmed by minor stressors
- Profound fatigue; cortisol is essential for waking up and mobilizing energy
- Possible hypotension (low blood pressure); cortisol is essential for blood pressure maintenance
- Possible autoimmune disease activity (though some autoimmune conditions suppress cortisol)
- Recovery from illness or training is very slow; cortisol is needed for recovery mobilization

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Diurnal Cortisol (4-point saliva)** — Morning serum cortisol is one point; the full rhythm reveals the whole picture.
- **ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)** — If cortisol is low with high ACTH, primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease). If low with low ACTH, secondary (pituitary/hypothalamic).
- **Sleep quality and timing** — Elevated morning cortisol correlates with poor sleep and late-night stress responses.
- **Stress history and perceived stress** — Psychological stressors should correlate with cortisol elevation.
**Key pattern:** Elevated morning cortisol + elevated evening cortisol (seen on diurnal testing) = HPA axis hyperactivity from chronic stress. Normal morning cortisol but elevated evening = circadian rhythm disruption (often from late-night screen time, irregular sleep).

### Intervention Levers
Morning cortisol responds powerfully to: stress reduction (meditation, breathwork, community), consistent sleep-wake times (entrains circadian rhythm), light exposure at waking (bright light suppresses melatonin, speeds cortisol response), evening wind-down (no screens 1-2 hours before bed, dim lighting), magnesium supplementation (supports HPA downregulation), and reduced overtraining. Results typically appear within 2-4 weeks with consistent intervention.

## Diurnal Cortisol (Saliva, 4-Point)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Cortisol concentration in saliva at four time points (typically 8 AM, 12 PM, 4 PM, 8 PM); reveals the complete circadian rhythm.
**Sample Type:** Saliva
**Fasting Required:** No (home collection)

### What This Number Means

The 4-point diurnal cortisol is the full picture of your HPA axis's circadian function. Unlike a single morning serum cortisol, it captures the entire rhythm: the morning peak, the midday decline, the afternoon trough, and the evening return to baseline (before sleep).

A healthy cortisol curve is high in the morning (12-18 mcg/dL at 8 AM), declining steadily through the day to reach a nadir at night (< 2 mcg/dL at 8-9 PM). This rhythm is driven by your circadian clock and your sleep-wake schedule. If the rhythm is disrupted—elevated evening cortisol, flattened curve, or inverted rhythm—it reveals HPA axis dysregulation.

Saliva cortisol is actually superior to serum cortisol for the diurnal rhythm because it measures free (bioavailable) cortisol, whereas serum cortisol includes both free and protein-bound forms. Saliva samples can be collected at home, avoiding the stress of blood draws, which can artificially elevate cortisol.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (4-Point Average)
| Time | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| 8 AM | 3-8 mcg/dL | 5-8 mcg/dL |
| 12 PM | 2-6 mcg/dL | 3-5 mcg/dL |
| 4 PM | 1-4 mcg/dL | 1-3 mcg/dL |
| 8 PM | 0.5-3 mcg/dL | 0.5-1.5 mcg/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Circadian HPA axis rhythm and adaptive capacity across the 24-hour cycle**

The 4-point diurnal curve is the full story of your HPA axis's adaptive capacity across the entire day. A healthy curve shows strong morning readiness, steady decline through the day, and deep rest at night. This allows you to mobilize energy when needed, sustain activity through the day, and fully downregulate at night for deep sleep.

When the curve is disrupted—elevated evening cortisol (called a "flattened" curve), an inverted rhythm, or an erratic pattern—it reveals that your HPA axis is dysregulated and your nervous system is not cycling properly. This cascades into poor sleep (elevated evening cortisol suppresses melatonin), daytime fatigue (morning cortisol is inadequate), and inflammation (dysregulated cortisol is pro-inflammatory).

### What Deviation Signals
**If morning elevated (> 8 mcg/dL), rest normal:**
- HPA axis is hyperactive in the morning; you may wake anxious or with racing thoughts
- Possible chronic stress or unprocessed trauma
- Sleep quality may be poor; elevated morning cortisol suggests you didn't fully downregulate at night
- Inflammatory state is likely present

**If evening elevated (> 1.5 mcg/dL), morning normal:**
- Circadian rhythm is disrupted; HPA axis is not downregulating at night
- Sleep quality is poor; elevated evening cortisol suppresses melatonin production
- Likely causes: late-night screen time, irregular sleep schedule, evening stress or rumination, shift work
- Daytime wakefulness and energy may be adequate, but nighttime recovery is poor

**If flattened curve (all four points relatively high, no decline):**
- HPA axis is chronically hyperactive; stress is sustained throughout the day
- Adrenal glands are in a state of constant mobilization
- Sleep quality is very poor
- Inflammation is significantly elevated
- Burnout is likely; nervous system is exhausted

**If inverted curve (low in morning, high in evening):**
- HPA axis has flipped; this is a sign of advanced HPA axis dysfunction
- Profound morning fatigue is present; you struggle to wake
- Energy improves through the day but collapses at night (insomnia despite nighttime exhaustion)
- Adrenal insufficiency or chronic suppression may be present
- Recovery capacity is severely compromised

**If low across all points (< 2 mcg/dL at 8 AM, < 1 mcg/dL afternoon/evening):**
- Adrenal insufficiency; cortisol production is inadequate
- Fatigue is profound; cortisol is essential for energy mobilization
- Possible Addison's disease (primary adrenal insufficiency)
- Possible pituitary dysfunction (secondary adrenal insufficiency)
- Requires medical evaluation and possible hormone replacement

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Sleep quality and timing** — Evening cortisol elevation directly causes poor sleep.
- **AM cortisol (serum)** — Should correlate with the 8 AM saliva point (roughly).
- **ACTH and DHEA-S** — If cortisol is low, ACTH helps determine whether it's primary (high ACTH) or secondary (low ACTH) insufficiency.
- **Sleep and wake times** — Circadian misalignment (variable sleep, shift work) explains curve flattening and inversion.
**Key pattern:** Elevated evening cortisol + poor sleep + daytime fatigue + visible stress = HPA axis dysregulation from circadian misalignment or chronic stress. This is one of the most common patterns and one of the most responsive to intervention.

### Intervention Levers
The diurnal cortisol curve responds dramatically to: strict sleep-wake timing (same sleep and wake time every day, even weekends), evening light cessation (no screens 2+ hours before bed, dim lighting in evening), morning light exposure (bright light within 30 minutes of waking), afternoon movement (walk, garden, light exercise), evening wind-down routine (meditation, journaling, warm bath), and stress-processing practices (breathwork, somatic experiencing, therapy). Magnesium glycinate in the evening (300-500 mg) and rhodiola or ashwagandha in the morning (adaptogenic herbs) support curve normalization. Results typically appear within 2-6 weeks.

## DUTCH Complete

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** A comprehensive hormone metabolite panel from urine over 24 hours; includes sex hormone metabolites, cortisol/cortisone diurnal pattern, melatonin, and organic acids.
**Sample Type:** Urine (24-hour collection, typically 4 aliquots)
**Fasting Required:** No (home collection)

### What This Number Means

DUTCH (Dried Urine Tests for Comprehensive Hormones) is a functional medicine favorite because it captures hormone metabolites (the breakdown products of hormones) over 24 hours, revealing both production and metabolism. Unlike serum cortisol (a single snapshot), the DUTCH gives you the full diurnal cortisol/cortisone pattern plus the metabolites that tell you *how* your body is metabolizing hormones.

The panel includes: cortisol and cortisone at four time points (showing metabolism rate), sex hormone metabolites (revealing how the body is clearing estrogen, testosterone), pregnenolone and DHEA-S, melatonin (sleep quality marker), and organic acids (revealing mitochondrial and metabolic dysfunction).

The cortisol:cortisone ratio is particularly revealing. Cortisol is the active form; cortisone is the inert form. A high cortisol:cortisone ratio means you're producing cortisol and not metabolizing it efficiently—stress hormones are accumulating. A low ratio means you're metabolizing cortisol quickly, which can indicate either good clearance or insufficient cortisol production.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Key Markers)
| Marker | Functional Optimal | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| AM Cortisol | 3-8 mcg/dL | Morning cortisol peak |
| PM Cortisol | 0.5-2 mcg/dL | Evening cortisol trough |
| Cortisol:Cortisone | 0.5-1.5 | Cortisol metabolism efficiency |
| Melatonin | 4-30 ng/dL | Sleep quality and circadian function |
| Estrone | 2-8 mcg/24h | Estrogen production and metabolism |
| Estradiol (men) | < 3 mcg/24h | Appropriate estrogen level |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Complete 24-hour HPA axis function, hormone metabolism, and circadian rhythm integrity**

The DUTCH is the complete functional hormone assessment. It tells you not just what you're producing but how you're metabolizing and clearing hormones. A person can have "normal" cortisol on a serum test but show poor cortisol:cortisone ratio on DUTCH, revealing that cortisol is accumulating and not being efficiently metabolized.

The organic acids section (which includes markers like 8-OHdG for oxidative stress, vanillylmandelic acid for sympathetic nervous system activity, and others) gives you insight into whether elevated cortisol is translating into actual cellular stress or whether it's being managed. Similarly, the melatonin measurement directly reveals sleep quality—you can have low-normal evening cortisol and still have poor sleep if melatonin isn't rising.

### What Deviation Signals
**If high cortisol, high cortisone (elevated ratio > 1.5):**
- Cortisol is being produced but not efficiently metabolized to cortisone
- Possible 11-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (11β-HSD) dysfunction
- Stress hormones are accumulating and causing inflammation
- Liver may be stressed; cortisol metabolism happens in the liver

**If low cortisol throughout the day:**
- Adrenal insufficiency; production is inadequate
- Possible HPA axis suppression from chronic stress or burnout
- Fatigue and poor stress resilience are present

**If elevated evening cortisol with low melatonin:**
- Circadian rhythm is disrupted
- Sleep quality is poor; elevated cortisol suppresses melatonin
- HPA axis is not downregulating at night

**If elevated organic acid markers (8-OHdG, etc.):**
- Oxidative stress is elevated; cortisol is translated into actual cellular damage
- Mitochondrial stress is present
- Antioxidant support is needed

**If sex hormone metabolites are elevated/imbalanced:**
- Estrogen metabolism is impaired; estrogen is not being efficiently cleared
- Risk of estrogen dominance (in women) or excess estradiol (in men)
- Liver support is needed

**If pregnenolone or DHEA-S low:**
- Adrenal steroidogenic capacity is limited
- Anabolic hormone production is suppressed
- Chronic stress has depleted the steroid pathway

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Serum AM cortisol** — Should correlate with 8 AM DUTCH cortisol (roughly; different matrices).
- **Liver function tests** — If cortisol metabolism is poor, liver should be evaluated.
- **Sleep quality and melatonin** — DUTCH melatonin should correlate with sleep complaints.
- **Sex hormone serum panel** — DUTCH metabolites should explain serum hormone levels.
**Key pattern:** Elevated cortisol + elevated cortisone + elevated organic acids + low melatonin = chronic stress with poor metabolism and circadian disruption. This pattern responds beautifully to comprehensive intervention.

### Intervention Levers
DUTCH abnormalities respond to: the same interventions as diurnal cortisol (sleep timing, light exposure, stress reduction) plus liver support (milk thistle, NAC, cruciferous vegetables), antioxidant support (CoQ10, vitamins C and E), and hormone metabolism support (calcium d-glucarate for estrogen, DIM). Results typically show within 4-8 weeks. DUTCH is particularly useful for tracking progress because it's sensitive to lifestyle changes and shows them quickly.

## DUTCH Plus

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** DUTCH Complete plus the cortisol awakening response (CAR), which measures the cortisol surge in the first 30 minutes after waking.
**Sample Type:** Urine (same 24-hour collection as DUTCH Complete, plus saliva for CAR)
**Fasting Required:** No (home collection)

### What This Number Means

The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is the magnitude of cortisol rise in the first 30 minutes after waking. In a healthy system, cortisol surges within 30 minutes of waking—this is the HPA axis saying "wake up, it's time." A robust CAR (rise of 3-5 mcg/dL in 30 minutes) indicates that your HPA axis is responsive and that waking is expected and prepared for. A blunted CAR (rise of < 1 mcg/dL) indicates either adrenal insufficiency or HPA axis suppression.

Paradoxically, an *excessive* CAR (rise of > 5-6 mcg/dL) can indicate anticipatory stress—you're anxious about the day ahead. This is different from a robust normal response: it's an overreaction.

The CAR is particularly useful for distinguishing between different types of HPA axis dysfunction. A person with low morning cortisol but a normal CAR might have adequate HPA axis responsiveness despite low baseline levels. A person with normal morning cortisol but blunted CAR might have poor stress reactivity despite adequate baseline.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Functional Optimal | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Waking cortisol (8 AM saliva) | 3-5 mcg/dL | Baseline upon waking |
| +30 min cortisol | 5-8 mcg/dL | 30-minute post-waking level |
| CAR (delta rise) | 3-5 mcg/dL | Appropriate awakening response |
| Excessive CAR | > 6 mcg/dL | Anticipatory anxiety |
| Blunted CAR | < 1 mcg/dL | Adrenal insufficiency or HPA suppression |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — HPA axis reactivity and anticipatory stress response**

The CAR is the moment-to-moment responsiveness of your HPA axis. It's one of the most sensitive biomarkers of stress and HPA axis function. A robust CAR indicates your HPA axis is waking up appropriately. A blunted CAR indicates either that you need help waking up (adrenal insufficiency) or that your HPA axis is suppressed from burnout.

An excessive CAR (without elevated morning cortisol) indicates anticipatory anxiety: your body is bracing for a stressful day even though there's no actual threat. This is different from a person with elevated morning cortisol from chronic stress. The treatment is different: the blunted CAR person needs adrenal support; the excessive CAR person needs anxiolytic support and stress processing.

### What Deviation Signals
**If normal CAR (3-5 mcg/dL rise):**
- HPA axis is responsive and healthy
- Waking response is appropriate; you wake up naturally
- Stress resilience is good

**If blunted CAR (< 1 mcg/dL rise):**
- HPA axis is suppressed or adrenals are fatigued
- You likely struggle to wake up; grogginess is present
- Stress resilience is poor; you're easily overwhelmed
- Possible burnout or advanced HPA axis dysfunction

**If excessive CAR (> 6 mcg/dL rise):**
- Anticipatory anxiety is present; you wake up stressed about the day
- HPA axis is overreactive but not chronically elevated
- Sleep quality may be good (cortisol is low at bedtime) but waking is anxious
- Psychological stress and anticipatory worry are present

**If waking cortisol low + blunted CAR:**
- Severe adrenal insufficiency; profound fatigue
- Possible Addison's disease or advanced burnout
- Requires medical evaluation and possible hormone replacement

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Full DUTCH Complete results** — CAR is just one component; full pattern matters.
- **Sleep quality** — A blunted CAR often correlates with poor sleep quality (can't wake up because sleep was poor).
- **Anxiety symptoms** — An excessive CAR correlates with anticipatory anxiety and worry.
- **Cognitive function** — A blunted CAR correlates with brain fog and poor morning cognition.
**Key pattern:** Elevated morning cortisol + elevated CAR = chronic stress with hypervigilance. Low morning cortisol + blunted CAR = burnout with HPA suppression. Low morning cortisol + normal CAR = primary adrenal insufficiency.

### Intervention Levers
CAR abnormalities respond to: the same DUTCH interventions plus additional stress-processing work for excessive CAR (therapy, anxiolytic herbs like ashwagandha, breathwork), and additional HPA axis support for blunted CAR (adaptogenic herbs like rhodiola, morning light exposure to acutely increase alertness, caffeine timed appropriately). The CAR is one of the fastest-responding markers—you can see shifts in 1-2 weeks with intervention.

## Comprehensive Female Panel (ZRT)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation, Synthesis
**What It Measures:** A multi-modal panel combining serum hormones (estradiol, progesterone, testosterone, FSH, LH) and dried urine metabolites (estrogen metabolites, cortisol) in a single panel.
**Sample Type:** Blood (serum) + urine (dried urine metabolites)
**Fasting Required:** Preferred; draw should be in luteal phase (day 21 of 28-day cycle) for optimal interpretation

### What This Number Means

The ZRT Comprehensive Female Panel is designed to capture the complexity of female hormone metabolism in one test. It combines serum measurements (the circulating hormones available to tissues) with urine metabolite measurements (the breakdown products that reveal how hormones are being cleared).

The panel is particularly valuable because it shows not just what's being produced but how the body is metabolizing estrogen. The estrogen metabolites reveal whether estrogen is being shunted toward protective pathways (2-hydroxyestrone, which is protective against breast cancer) or problematic pathways (16-alpha-hydroxyestrone, which is more estrogenic and potentially cancer-promoting).

Drawing in the luteal phase (when progesterone is high) is critical for interpretation, as progesterone levels vary dramatically across the cycle. Follicular-phase testing would miss luteal dysfunction.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Luteal Phase)
| Marker | Functional Optimal | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Estradiol | 150-250 pg/mL | Appropriate luteal estradiol |
| Progesterone | 15-25 ng/mL | Robust ovulation and corpus luteum |
| 2:16-OHE1 ratio | > 2:1 | Protective estrogen metabolism |
| Cortisol (AM) | 5-8 mcg/dL | Normal HPA axis |
| Cortisone | 3-6 mcg/dL | Appropriate cortisol metabolism |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation & Synthesis — Female reproductive and stress hormone integration**

The ZRT Female Panel is the complete read of female reproductive capacity and stress resilience. A woman with optimal results has: robust ovulation (high luteal progesterone), appropriate estrogen levels (not excess, not deficient), protective estrogen metabolism (favoring 2-OHE1), and a healthy HPA axis (cortisol and cortisone in balance).

When results diverge—normal estradiol but very low progesterone, or normal hormones but poor estrogen metabolism—it reveals where the system is failing. A woman with low luteal progesterone but normal estradiol has a corpus luteum problem (luteal phase defect). A woman with normal hormones but elevated 16-alpha-OHE1 has a metabolism problem and needs estrogen metabolism support.

### What Deviation Signals
**If low progesterone (< 10 ng/mL luteal):**
- Anovulation or luteal phase defect; ovulation didn't occur or corpus luteum is failing
- Luteal phase is likely short (< 12 days)
- Mood, sleep, and metabolic stability are compromised in second half of cycle
- Fertility is compromised; inadequate progesterone prevents implantation

**If elevated estradiol (> 250 pg/mL) + low 2:16-OHE1 ratio:**
- Estrogen dominance; estrogen is elevated relative to progesterone
- Estrogen metabolism is unfavorable; more estrogenic (16-alpha) metabolites are being produced
- Risk of breast cancer and estrogen-dependent conditions is increased
- Liver support and estrogen metabolism support are needed

**If elevated cortisol + low progesterone:**
- Stress is suppressing ovulation and corpus luteum function
- Stress-driven reproductive dysfunction is present
- HPA-HPG axis interaction is the primary problem

**If low cortisol + low progesterone:**
- Both axes are suppressed; adrenal fatigue and reproductive failure coexist
- Severe metabolic stress or burnout is present

**If good 2:16-OHE1 ratio with high hormones:**
- Estrogen metabolism is favorable; despite high estrogen, it's being cleared into protective pathways
- This is protective, though excess absolute estrogen still warrants attention

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Cycle tracking** — Progesterone should be drawn at peak luteal (day 21); earlier or later gives falsely low results.
- **Symptoms** — Luteal-phase symptoms (breast tenderness, mood changes, water retention) should correlate with high estradiol/low 2-OHE1.
- **Menstrual cycle length** — Short cycles (< 25 days) or short luteal phases (< 12 days) correlate with low luteal progesterone.
- **Fertility history** — Low progesterone is a known cause of infertility.
**Key pattern:** Normal follicular hormones but low luteal progesterone = isolated luteal dysfunction (common and treatable). Low hormones across the board = anovulation (needs investigation for PCOS, thyroid dysfunction, etc.).

### Intervention Levers
The ZRT Female Panel responds to: confirmed ovulation (necessary for progesterone production), stress reduction (supports HPG axis), adequate caloric intake (undereating suppresses ovulation), body fat sufficiency (< 18% body fat suppresses ovulation), vitamin E and selenium for luteal support, vitex (chasteberry) for luteal enhancement in some cases, and liver support (milk thistle, NAC, cruciferous vegetables) for estrogen metabolism. If estrogen metabolism is poor (high 16-OHE1), DIM or calcium d-glucarate supplementation helps. Results typically take 1-2 full cycles to show, as hormone production patterns need to re-establish.

## Comprehensive Male Panel (ZRT)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation, Synthesis
**What It Measures:** A multi-modal panel combining serum hormones (testosterone total/free, SHBG, estradiol, DHT, FSH, LH) and dried urine metabolites (cortisol, metabolite ratios).
**Sample Type:** Blood (serum) + urine (dried urine metabolites)
**Fasting Required:** Preferred; morning draw essential (testosterone peaks at dawn)

### What This Number Means

The ZRT Comprehensive Male Panel is designed to capture the complete male reproductive and stress hormone profile. Unlike a simple testosterone test, it includes the full axis: testosterone (total and free), SHBG (which determines bioavailability), estradiol (which can be excessive in men), DHT (the concentrated form), FSH and LH (which reveal whether low testosterone is central or peripheral), and stress hormone metabolites.

The panel is particularly valuable for men with testosterone concerns because it answers: Is the testosterone low because of inadequate production (low LH = central) or testicular failure (high LH = peripheral)? Is bioavailable testosterone normal despite low total testosterone (high SHBG = true deficiency)? Is estradiol elevated relative to testosterone (metabolic dysfunction)?

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| Marker | Functional Optimal | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Total Testosterone | 600-800 ng/dL | Healthy anabolic state |
| Free Testosterone | 12-18 pg/mL | Bioavailable testosterone |
| SHBG | 25-45 nmol/L | Appropriate hormone availability |
| Estradiol | 18-24 pg/mL | Balanced androgen:estrogen |
| DHT | 40-70 ng/dL | Appropriate androgen potency |
| LH | 2-9 mIU/mL | Normal gonadotropin signaling |
| FSH | 1-8 mIU/mL | Normal spermatogenesis |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation & Synthesis — Male reproductive capacity and stress-reproductive axis integration**

The ZRT Male Panel is the complete read of male reproductive health and stress resilience. A man with optimal results has: robust testosterone production (600+ ng/dL), good bioavailability (low SHBG, high free testosterone), balanced estradiol (not excess, not deficient), appropriate DHT, normal gonadotropins (LH and FSH), and healthy cortisol metabolism.

When results diverge—normal total testosterone but low free testosterone due to high SHBG, or normal testosterone but excessive estradiol—it reveals where the system is failing. A man with normal total testosterone but high SHBG and low free testosterone is functionally deficient. A man with normal testosterone but DHT > 100 ng/dL has excessive 5-alpha reductase activity and is at higher risk for hair loss and prostate issues.

### What Deviation Signals
**If low testosterone (< 400 ng/dL total) + high LH:**
- Primary hypogonadism; testes are failing
- Testicular pathology: viral infection, injury, genetic, toxin exposure
- Possible Klinefelter syndrome or other genetic condition
- Fertility is likely compromised

**If low testosterone (< 400 ng/dL total) + low LH:**
- Secondary hypogonadism; pituitary or hypothalamus is not signaling
- Possible pituitary tumor or hypothalamic dysfunction
- Possible from chronic stress, overtraining, or opioid use
- Requires pituitary evaluation

**If normal/high testosterone + high estradiol (> 30 pg/mL):**
- Excess body fat driving aromatase activity
- Poor liver function (impaired estrogen clearance)
- Possible hormone supplementation (exogenous testosterone converting to excess estradiol)
- Risk of sexual dysfunction and gynecomastia

**If normal testosterone + high SHBG + low free testosterone:**
- Apparent testosterone deficiency despite "normal" total
- Insulin resistance (low insulin) is elevating SHBG, but in dysfunction context
- Thyroid dysfunction may be present (hypothyroidism raises SHBG)
- True functional testosterone deficiency despite "normal" labs

**If high DHT (> 100 ng/dL) + family history of hair loss:**
- 5-alpha reductase hyperactivity
- Hair loss is likely accelerating
- Benign prostate hyperplasia (BPH) risk is elevated

**If low DHT (< 30 ng/dL):**
- 5-alpha reductase inhibitor use (finasteride, dutasteride)
- Possible sexual dysfunction; DHT is essential for libido
- Prostate health may be improved; DHT drives prostate growth

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Body composition** — Excess body fat explains elevated estradiol and low SHBG.
- **Sexual function** — Should correlate with free testosterone and DHT (low = dysfunction, high = robust).
- **Prostate symptoms** — High DHT + age > 50 + prostate symptoms = BPH.
- **Fertility** — FSH should be normal; if low FSH with low testosterone, secondary hypogonadism.
**Key pattern:** Normal total testosterone + high SHBG + low free testosterone = functional deficiency despite "normal" labs. The ZRT panel catches this.

### Intervention Levers
Male Panel abnormalities respond to: resistance training (increases testosterone and LH), adequate sleep (GH and testosterone are produced during sleep), reduced body fat (eliminates excess aromatase activity), omega-3 supplementation (supports androgen production), zinc and vitamin D sufficiency, reduction of endocrine disruptors (pesticides, BPA, phthalates), and stress management (chronic stress suppresses LH). If SHBG is elevated, insulin sensitivity improvement (fasting, resistance training) lowers SHBG. Results typically show within 8-12 weeks.

## FSH (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** FSH concentration in serum; a pituitary hormone that stimulates follicle development (women) and spermatogenesis (men).
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

FSH is a gonadotropin produced by the pituitary gland. In women, it drives follicle development and estradiol production in the follicular phase. In men, it drives spermatogenesis (sperm production). FSH rises when the pituitary senses that gonadal hormones are low (negative feedback), and falls when gonadal hormones (estrogen in women, testosterone in men) are high.

FSH is the pituitary's way of saying "make more sex hormones." High FSH means the pituitary is sending a loud signal, which can indicate either that the gonads are failing (and can't respond) or that gonadal hormone levels are low (and the pituitary is trying to stimulate production). Low FSH means the pituitary is satisfied with current hormone levels or is suppressed (from high estradiol, high cortisol, or central hypogonadism).

In women, FSH is highest in the follicular phase and drops dramatically in the luteal phase. In men, FSH should be steady year-round (no monthly cycle).

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women, Follicular Phase)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 2 mIU/mL | > 3 mIU/mL |
| Optimal | 2-8 mIU/mL | 4-8 mIU/mL |
| Elevated concern | 8-15 mIU/mL | > 12 mIU/mL |
| Critical | > 15 mIU/mL | > 20 mIU/mL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 1-5 mIU/mL | 1.5-5 mIU/mL |
| Optimal | 1-8 mIU/mL | 2-6 mIU/mL |
| Elevated concern | 8-12 mIU/mL | > 8 mIU/mL |
| Critical | > 12 mIU/mL | > 15 mIU/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Pituitary-gonadal axis signaling and gonadal function status**

FSH is the pituitary's messenger to the gonads. When FSH is normal, it means the pituitary is appropriately signaling gonad function and the gonads are responding adequately. When FSH is elevated, the pituitary is sending a stronger signal, which can indicate that the gonads are not responding (ovarian insufficiency in women, spermatogenic failure in men) or that sex hormone levels are suppressed (from stress, underweight, overtraining, or malnutrition).

Low FSH in the context of low sex hormones indicates that the pituitary itself is suppressed (secondary hypogonadism). In women, this is often from stress, undereating, or overtraining. In men, it's often from central hypogonadism (pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction).

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 12 mIU/mL women, > 8 mIU/mL men):**
- Gonadal insufficiency; gonads are not responding adequately to FSH
- In women, possible diminished ovarian reserve (approaching menopause) or ovarian failure
- In men, possible testicular failure or spermatogenic dysfunction
- Fertility is compromised; it's difficult for the gonads to respond at higher levels
- Pituitary is working hard to stimulate inadequate gonad function

**If low (< 2 mIU/mL women, < 1.5 mIU/mL men):**
- Pituitary suppression or central hypogonadism; pituitary is not signaling adequately
- In women, possible from stress, undereating, overtraining, or anovulation
- In men, possible from chronic stress, opioid use, or pituitary pathology
- Gonadal hormone production is suppressed because the pituitary signal is weak
- Fertility is compromised; without FSH, follicles (women) or sperm (men) can't develop

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **LH** — FSH and LH should be in rough balance; both should rise and fall together.
- **Sex hormone levels (estradiol/testosterone)** — High FSH + low sex hormones = gonadal insufficiency. Low FSH + low sex hormones = central suppression.
- **Cycle history (women)** — Rising FSH suggests diminishing ovarian reserve.
- **Sperm analysis (men)** — FSH correlates with sperm count; high FSH + low sperm = spermatogenic failure.
**Key pattern:** Elevated FSH in a woman < 40 years old is unusual and warrants investigation for autoimmune ovarian disease or genetic factors. Elevated FSH in a woman > 45 is more likely from perimenopause/menopause.

### Intervention Levers
FSH can be supported indirectly: in women by ensuring adequate energy intake (especially in athletes), stress reduction, and body fat sufficiency (< 18% body fat suppresses FSH). In men, by ensuring adequate sleep, stress reduction, and removing gonadotoxins. FSH itself cannot be supplemented orally (it's a peptide hormone), but gonadotropin injections (hCG, FSH itself) can be used in fertility contexts. Prevention of suppression (maintaining energy, managing stress) is more important than trying to raise FSH after it's been suppressed.

## LH (Luteinizing Hormone)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** LH concentration in serum; a pituitary hormone that triggers ovulation (women) and stimulates testosterone production (men).
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

LH is the second gonadotropin, and it has a more acute, episodic role than FSH. In women, LH surges mid-cycle to trigger ovulation and sustain the corpus luteum. In men, LH stimulates testosterone production in the Leydig cells of the testes.

LH is particularly important in women because the LH surge is the trigger for ovulation. Without an LH surge, ovulation doesn't occur—you get a follicle that grows but never releases (anovulation). The LH surge can be detected with ovulation predictor kits (which detect the surge 24-36 hours before ovulation occurs). Absence of an LH surge indicates anovulation and warrants investigation.

In men, LH tells you whether the pituitary is signaling testosterone production. Low LH with low testosterone indicates that the signal is weak (secondary hypogonadism). High LH with low testosterone indicates that the signal is strong but the testes aren't responding (primary hypogonadism).

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women, Follicular Phase)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 1 mIU/mL | > 1.5 mIU/mL |
| Optimal | 1-12 mIU/mL | 2-8 mIU/mL |
| Elevated concern | 12-20 mIU/mL | > 15 mIU/mL |
| Critical (LH surge) | 25-100+ mIU/mL | 30-50+ mIU/mL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 1-5 mIU/mL | 1.5-5 mIU/mL |
| Optimal | 1-10 mIU/mL | 2-7 mIU/mL |
| Elevated concern | 10-15 mIU/mL | > 12 mIU/mL |
| Critical | > 15 mIU/mL | > 20 mIU/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Gonadotropin axis acuity and sex hormone production signaling**

LH is the acute messenger from the pituitary to the gonads. In women, a robust LH surge is the gate to ovulation. Without it, ovulation is impossible. In men, LH is the signal for testosterone production, and its level correlates with testosterone-producing capacity.

Low LH in the context of low sex hormones indicates central suppression: the pituitary is not sending a strong signal. This is often from stress, undereating, or overtraining in women, or from chronic stress, opioid use, or pituitary pathology in men.

High LH with normal or low sex hormones indicates that the signal is strong but the gonads aren't responding—gonadal insufficiency. In women, this is unusual and warrants investigation. In men, it's a sign of primary testicular failure.

### What Deviation Signals
**If no LH surge detected (women, trying to conceive):**
- Anovulation is present; ovulation is not occurring
- Ovulation predictor kits are negative throughout the cycle
- Causes: PCOS, stress, undereating, overtraining, thyroid dysfunction
- Fertility is compromised without ovulation

**If elevated LH in follicular phase (> 15 mIU/mL women):**
- Possible PCOS; elevated LH:FSH ratio (LH > 2x FSH) is characteristic of PCOS
- Gonadal insufficiency or ovarian resistance to FSH
- Metabolic dysfunction is likely

**If low LH (< 1 mIU/mL women, < 1.5 mIU/mL men):**
- Pituitary suppression; central hypogonadism
- Stress, undereating, or overtraining is likely
- Sex hormone production is suppressed because the pituitary signal is weak
- Fertility is compromised; eggs (women) or sperm (men) can't develop

**If high LH with low testosterone (men):**
- Primary testicular failure; testes are not responding to LH
- Possible genetic (Klinefelter), infectious, or toxic cause
- Requires urological evaluation

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **FSH** — LH and FSH should be in rough balance; both should change together.
- **Sex hormones** — High LH + low testosterone (men) = primary failure. Low LH + low testosterone (men) = secondary failure.
- **LH:FSH ratio** — In women, > 2:1 or 3:1 suggests PCOS.
- **Cycle tracking** — LH surge predicts ovulation within 24-36 hours.
**Key pattern:** In women trying to conceive, the LH surge is the most important event in the cycle. If no surge is detected, ovulation is not occurring and conception is impossible without intervention.

### Intervention Levers
LH can be supported indirectly through: energy sufficiency (especially in athletes), stress reduction, body fat sufficiency (< 18% suppresses LH), and sleep (LH is produced during sleep). In women, confirming an LH surge with ovulation predictor kits and timing intercourse around the surge optimizes fertility. In men, LH-driven testosterone production can be optimized through resistance training, sleep, and stress management.

## Prolactin

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Prolactin concentration in serum; a pituitary hormone that promotes lactation and has immune-modulating effects.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** Preferred (draw 1-3 hours after waking; prolactin has mild circadian variation)

### What This Number Means

Prolactin is produced by lactotroph cells in the anterior pituitary. Its primary role is to promote lactation (breast milk production). But prolactin also has roles in immune function, mood regulation, and sexual function. Elevated prolactin can suppress LH and FSH, which can cause reproductive dysfunction: amenorrhea or anovulation in women, erectile dysfunction and low libido in men.

Prolactin is unique among pituitary hormones because it's primarily under *inhibitory* control (dopamine suppresses it), not stimulatory. This means that pituitary damage (which can disrupt dopamine delivery to the pituitary) often causes prolactin elevation. Similarly, certain medications (dopamine antagonists like antipsychotics, some antidepressants) can elevate prolactin by blocking dopamine's inhibitory effect.

Mildly elevated prolactin (20-30 ng/mL) is often seen in women with PCOS and correlates with anovulation. Very high prolactin (> 100 ng/mL) warrants brain imaging to rule out a prolactinoma (prolactin-secreting pituitary tumor).

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Women)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | < 20 ng/mL | < 15 ng/mL |
| Optimal | < 25 ng/mL | 5-12 ng/mL |
| Elevated concern | 25-50 ng/mL | > 15 ng/mL |
| Critical | > 50 ng/mL | > 50 ng/mL |

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges (Men)
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | < 15 ng/mL | < 12 ng/mL |
| Optimal | < 20 ng/mL | 3-9 ng/mL |
| Elevated concern | 20-30 ng/mL | > 12 ng/mL |
| Critical | > 30 ng/mL | > 30 ng/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Pituitary function and reproductive axis suppression via prolactin**

Prolactin is the brake on reproduction. When prolactin is high, it suppresses LH and FSH, which suppresses sex hormone production and ovulation/spermatogenesis. This makes physiological sense in lactating women (you don't want to get pregnant while nursing), but when prolactin is elevated without lactation, it's problematic.

Elevated prolactin in women manifests as amenorrhea or anovulation, irregular cycles, sexual dysfunction, and mood changes (prolactin influences dopamine and affects mood). In men, it manifests as erectile dysfunction, low libido, and gynecomastia (elevated prolactin promotes breast tissue growth).

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 20 ng/mL women, > 15 ng/mL men):**
- Reproductive dysfunction is likely; prolactin is suppressing LH/FSH
- In women, amenorrhea or anovulation is present
- In men, sexual dysfunction and erectile dysfunction are present
- Possible medication effect (antipsychotics, some antidepressants)
- Possible pituitary tumor (prolactinoma) if very elevated (> 100 ng/mL); requires MRI
- Possible hypothyroidism; TRH stimulates prolactin production
- Possible stress-driven elevation; stress suppresses dopamine

**If low (< 3 ng/mL):**
- Rare; indicates either severe pituitary insufficiency or dopamine excess
- Usually not clinically significant
- May indicate hypothalamic-pituitary disconnection

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **TSH and Free T4** — Hypothyroidism elevates prolactin; TRH stimulates both TSH and prolactin.
- **LH and FSH** — If prolactin is elevated, LH and FSH should be suppressed.
- **Sex hormones** — If prolactin is elevated, sex hormones should be low (due to LH/FSH suppression).
- **Medications** — Antipsychotics, some antidepressants, metoclopramide elevate prolactin.
**Key pattern:** Elevated prolactin + normal TSH + amenorrhea = possible prolactinoma (requires MRI). Elevated prolactin + elevated TSH = hypothyroidism driving prolactin elevation.

### Intervention Levers
Elevated prolactin responds to: dopamine support (dopamine-precursor supplements like L-tyrosine; avoiding dopamine antagonists), thyroid optimization (treat hypothyroidism if present), stress reduction (stress suppresses dopamine), and medication review (switch dopamine-antagonizing medications if possible). Vitex (chasteberry) may help mild elevation by supporting dopamine. Elevated prolactin from medication often resolves when the medication is stopped (do not stop without medical guidance). Very elevated prolactin (> 100) may require dopamine agonist medication (bromocriptine, cabergoline).

## TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** TSH concentration in serum; a pituitary hormone that stimulates thyroid hormone production.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No, but timing matters; TSH has diurnal variation (higher in evening, lower in early morning)

### What This Number Means

TSH is the pituitary's signal to the thyroid. When thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) are low, TSH rises to stimulate the thyroid to produce more. When thyroid hormones are high, TSH falls (negative feedback). TSH is thus the inverse of thyroid hormone levels: low TSH suggests high thyroid hormones (or suppression), high TSH suggests low thyroid hormones.

TSH is the standard screening test for thyroid dysfunction, but it's incomplete: a person can have "normal" TSH (by convention, 0.4-4.0 mIU/L) but still have low thyroid hormones (subclinical hypothyroidism) or high thyroid hormones (subclinical hyperthyroidism). This is why TSH alone is not sufficient—you need free T4 and free T3 to complete the picture.

TSH has a diurnal rhythm: it's highest in the evening and lowest in early morning. For standardized testing, draw early morning (before 9 AM) so results are comparable across time. An afternoon TSH can be falsely high compared to morning.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 0.4-2.0 mIU/L | 0.8-1.5 mIU/L |
| Optimal | 2.0-4.0 mIU/L | 1.0-2.5 mIU/L |
| Elevated concern | 4.0-6.0 mIU/L | > 2.5 mIU/L |
| Critical | > 6.0 mIU/L | > 4.0 mIU/L |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Pituitary-thyroid axis signaling and thyroid hormone production**

TSH is the pituitary's way of saying "make more thyroid hormones." When TSH is elevated, the pituitary is sending a strong signal, which usually means thyroid hormone levels are low (and the pituitary is trying to stimulate production). When TSH is low, the pituitary is satisfied with current thyroid hormone levels (or is suppressed).

The key insight is that TSH alone doesn't tell you thyroid hormone levels. A person with TSH 3.0 could have normal T4 and T3 (healthy thyroid), or could have low T4 and high TSH (subclinical hypothyroidism). You need free T4 and free T3 to understand the true status.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 2.5 mIU/L):**
- Pituitary is sending a strong signal; thyroid hormones are likely low
- Hypothyroidism is present (subclinical if TSH elevated but T4 normal; clinical if TSH + T4 both low)
- Metabolic rate is likely slowed; fatigue, cold intolerance, slow recovery
- Possible primary hypothyroidism (thyroid gland failure) if TSH is very high (> 10)
- Possible iodine deficiency, autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's), or thyroid damage
- Requires free T4 testing to distinguish subclinical from clinical

**If low (< 0.8 mIU/L):**
- Pituitary is signaling less; thyroid hormones are likely high
- Hyperthyroidism is possible (especially if TSH suppressed and T4/T3 elevated)
- Possible from Graves' disease, thyroiditis, thyroid nodule, or thyroid cancer
- Possible from central hyperthyroidism (pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction) if TSH low with low T4/T3
- Metabolic rate is elevated; anxiety, heat intolerance, rapid recovery from training
- May be iatrogenic from over-replacement in hypothyroid patients

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Free T4** — Critical for interpretation; TSH + low T4 = primary hypothyroidism. TSH normal + low T4 = central hypothyroidism (rare).
- **Free T3** — TSH + low T3 = classic hypothyroidism. TSH + high T3 = possible T4→T3 conversion problem.
- **Thyroid antibodies (TPO, thyroglobulin)** — If elevated, autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's or Graves') is likely.
- **Symptoms** — Hypothyroid symptoms (fatigue, cold intolerance, dry skin, constipation) should correlate with elevated TSH + low T4.
**Key pattern:** TSH 2.5-4.0 with normal T4 = subclinical hypothyroidism (often treated in functional medicine even if conventional medicine considers it "normal").

### Intervention Levers
Elevated TSH responds to: thyroid hormone replacement if primary hypothyroidism is confirmed (T4 replacement like levothyroxine, or combination T4+T3 like desiccated thyroid), selenium and iodine sufficiency (if deficient), iron sufficiency (required for thyroid peroxidase function), zinc (required for thyroid hormone activation), and stress reduction (chronic stress suppresses TSH and T4). Autoimmune thyroiditis may respond to: gluten elimination (if celiac or gluten-sensitive), gut healing (L-glutamine, collagen, zinc carnosine), and stress management. Results take 6-8 weeks post-intervention to show meaningful changes.

## Free T4 (Thyroxine)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Free T4 concentration in serum; the unbound, bioavailable form of the primary thyroid hormone.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

T4 (thyroxine) is the primary hormone produced by the thyroid gland. It's produced in much larger amounts than T3 and serves as a reservoir: the body converts T4 to T3 (the active form) as needed. Free T4 is the unbound fraction—the only form that can activate T3 receptors on cells.

Most T4 is bound to thyroid-binding globulin (TBG) or albumin and is functionally unavailable. Only free T4 (typically 1-5% of total T4) actually works. This is why free T4 is a much better marker of thyroid status than total T4.

Free T4 has a long half-life (~7 days), so it doesn't vary much day-to-day, making it a reliable marker of sustained thyroid function. TSH, by contrast, can fluctuate more. A person can have "normal" TSH but low free T4 (central hypothyroidism) or "elevated" TSH with normal free T4 (subclinical hypothyroidism). Free T4 clarifies the situation.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 0.7 ng/dL | > 0.8 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 0.7-1.8 ng/dL | 1.0-1.5 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 1.8-2.5 ng/dL | > 1.8 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 2.5 ng/dL | > 2.5 ng/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Thyroid hormone availability and metabolic rate signaling**

Free T4 is the baseline thyroid hormone available to tissues. When free T4 is optimal, tissues receive a steady signal to maintain metabolic rate, energy production, and thermal regulation. When free T4 is low, metabolic rate slows, energy production declines, and tissues become cold. When free T4 is high, metabolic rate accelerates, anxiety rises, and tissues overheat.

The key distinction from TSH is that free T4 tells you the actual hormone level, not the pituitary's attempt to regulate it. A person with TSH 0.5 and free T4 1.8 has high normal thyroid hormones. A person with TSH 2.5 and free T4 0.8 has low thyroid hormones (subclinical hypothyroidism). The TSH and free T4 together reveal the true story.

### What Deviation Signals
**If low (< 0.8 ng/dL):**
- Hypothyroidism is present; thyroid hormones are inadequate
- Metabolic rate is slowed; fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, slow recovery
- May be primary (TSH elevated) or central (TSH normal/low); requires TSH context
- Possible autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's), iodine deficiency, or thyroid medication underdose
- Conversion from T4 to T3 may be impaired (see free T3)
- Tissue T3 availability is low; all T3-dependent functions are slowed

**If elevated (> 1.8 ng/dL):**
- Hyperthyroidism is present; thyroid hormones are excessive
- Metabolic rate is elevated; anxiety, heat intolerance, rapid weight loss, fast recovery
- May be from Graves' disease, thyroiditis, thyroid cancer, or thyroid hormone overdose
- Possible increased risk of atrial fibrillation and bone loss with chronic elevation
- Tissue T3 availability is elevated; all T3-dependent functions are accelerated

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **TSH** — Free T4 + TSH together reveal the mechanism. Low T4 + high TSH = primary hypo. Low T4 + low TSH = central hypo.
- **Free T3** — If T4 is normal but T3 is low, conversion is impaired. If both T4 and T3 are low, primary hypothyroidism is confirmed.
- **Reverse T3** — If T4 is normal but reverse T3 is high, T4 is being shunted toward reverse T3 (inactive) instead of T3 (active); conversion is impaired.
- **Thyroid antibodies** — If free T4 is low with elevated TPO antibodies, autoimmune thyroiditis is confirmed.
**Key pattern:** Normal TSH + low free T4 = secondary hypothyroidism (pituitary/hypothalamic). Elevated TSH + low free T4 = primary hypothyroidism (thyroid gland failure).

### Intervention Levers
Low free T4 responds to: thyroid hormone replacement (levothyroxine 25-100+ mcg daily, adjusted based on TSH and symptoms), adequate iodine intake (150 mcg daily; more if hypothyroid), selenium sufficiency (required for thyroid peroxidase), iron sufficiency (required for thyroid function), and gut health (many thyroid medications require good absorption). If autoimmune, add: gluten elimination, gut healing, stress reduction. Free T4 takes 4-6 weeks to fully respond to medication changes. High free T4 requires investigation (Graves' disease workup) and possible endocrinology referral.

## Free T3 (Triiodothyronine)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Free T3 concentration in serum; the active form of thyroid hormone, produced from T4 conversion.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

T3 is the metabolically active form of thyroid hormone. It's produced primarily by peripheral conversion of T4 in the liver, kidneys, and other tissues. Free T3 (unbound T3) is the form that activates T3 receptors on cells and drives metabolic rate, heat production, and energy availability.

T3 is 3-4 times more potent than T4, so even though it's present in much smaller amounts, it drives most of the metabolic effects. A person can have "normal" T4 but low T3 if the conversion is impaired. This happens with: caloric restriction (undereating suppresses conversion), selenium deficiency (required for conversion), zinc deficiency, chronic stress (high cortisol impairs conversion), and high reverse T3 (which competes with T3 for receptor binding).

Free T3 is more variable than free T4 (it fluctuates more with diet, stress, and conversion efficiency), so a single measurement is less reliable than TSH or free T4. But it's critical for understanding whether thyroid problems are from hormone production or from conversion problems.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | > 2.3 pg/mL | > 2.8 pg/mL |
| Optimal | 2.3-4.2 pg/mL | 3.2-4.2 pg/mL |
| Elevated concern | 4.2-5.5 pg/mL | > 4.5 pg/mL |
| Critical | > 5.5 pg/mL | > 5.5 pg/mL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — Active thyroid hormone availability and metabolic rate acceleration**

Free T3 is the accelerator of metabolism. When free T3 is optimal, every cell with T3 receptors (which is most of them) receives a signal to speed up: ATP production increases, heat generation increases, and cellular work accelerates. When free T3 is low, despite adequate T4, the problem is conversion, not production.

This distinction is critical because it changes treatment: if you have low T3 from poor conversion, supplementing more T4 (levothyroxine) won't help—you need to fix the conversion problem or add supplemental T3 directly. If you have low T3 from low T4 production, supplementing T4 is appropriate.

### What Deviation Signals
**If low (< 2.8 pg/mL) with normal free T4:**
- T4→T3 conversion is impaired
- Metabolic rate is slowed despite adequate T4; fatigue and cold intolerance persist
- Possible causes: selenium deficiency, zinc deficiency, caloric restriction, chronic stress, high cortisol, high reverse T3
- Possible copper excess (which inhibits T4→T3 conversion)
- Tissue metabolic rate is low; T3 receptors are not receiving adequate signal

**If elevated (> 4.5 pg/mL):**
- Hyperthyroidism is present; T3 is excessive
- Metabolic rate is very high; anxiety, heat intolerance, rapid weight loss
- Increased cardiovascular stress; elevated T3 increases heart rate
- Possible Graves' disease or thyroiditis
- Possible thyroid hormone overdose
- Bone loss risk is elevated; excess T3 is catabolic to bone

**If low free T4 + low free T3:**
- Primary hypothyroidism is confirmed; both hormones are inadequate
- Thyroid gland is not producing enough hormone
- Both T4 replacement and conversion support are needed

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Free T4** — If T4 is normal but T3 is low, conversion is the problem. If both are low, production is the problem.
- **Reverse T3** — High reverse T3 competes with T3 for receptors; if high, conversion is being shunted toward reverse T3.
- **Selenium** — Required for T4→T3 conversion; deficiency impairs conversion.
- **Cortisol** — High cortisol impairs conversion and increases reverse T3 production.
**Key pattern:** Low free T3 + normal free T4 + fatigue + cold intolerance = conversion problem (not production problem). Treat with selenium, reduce stress, and consider adding T3 supplementation.

### Intervention Levers
Low free T3 responds to: selenium sufficiency (200 mcg daily; critical for conversion), zinc sufficiency, caloric sufficiency (undereating suppresses conversion), stress reduction (cortisol impairs conversion), and reducing reverse T3 production (see next section). If conversion remains poor despite these interventions, consider: desiccated thyroid hormone (which contains both T4 and T3), or adding cytomel (synthetic T3) directly. Results take 6-8 weeks to show with interventions.

## Reverse T3

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Reverse T3 concentration in serum; an inactive metabolite of T4 that competes with T3 for receptor binding.
**Sample Type:** Blood
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Reverse T3 (rT3) is an isomer of T3 that's structurally similar but metabolically inactive. It's produced when T4 is converted down an alternate pathway (instead of toward active T3). This can happen as a protective response under stress: when cortisol is elevated or caloric intake is low, the body shunts T4 toward rT3 (which is inert) instead of T3 (which would accelerate metabolism).

This is actually adaptive in survival situations: if food is scarce, you want to slow your metabolism to conserve energy, so producing rT3 instead of T3 makes sense. But in modern chronic stress, this adaptation becomes maladaptive: rT3 stays elevated, it competes with T3 for receptor binding, and metabolic rate stays suppressed despite adequate T4.

Reverse T3 is a marker of "tissue resistance to thyroid hormone": even if T4 and T3 are normal, if rT3 is high, tissues aren't receiving the thyroid signal because rT3 is blocking the receptors.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges
| | Conventional Range | Functional Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Low concern | 9.2-24 ng/dL | 9-17 ng/dL |
| Optimal | 9.2-24 ng/dL | 11-15 ng/dL |
| Elevated concern | 24-35 ng/dL | > 18 ng/dL |
| Critical | > 35 ng/dL | > 28 ng/dL |

### Body Codx Operation Reading
**Regulation — T4 metabolic fate and tissue thyroid hormone receptor availability**

Reverse T3 is the brake on metabolism. When rT3 is high, T4 is being shunted away from active T3 production and toward an inert form. This is adaptive in acute stress or starvation, but becomes pathological in chronic stress or restrictive eating.

High rT3 explains a paradoxical pattern: normal TSH, normal free T4, normal free T3—but profound fatigue, cold intolerance, and slow metabolism anyway. The problem isn't hormone production; it's that the hormones are being blocked from working by the high rT3 occupying receptors.

### What Deviation Signals
**If elevated (> 18 ng/dL):**
- T4 conversion is being shunted toward reverse T3 instead of active T3
- Tissues are resistant to thyroid hormone signaling despite normal levels
- Likely causes: chronic stress, high cortisol, caloric restriction, excessive exercise without recovery, selenium deficiency, high iron
- Metabolism is suppressed; fatigue and cold intolerance are present despite normal free T4 and T3
- Weight loss is difficult; metabolic rate is artificially lowered
- Requires lifestyle change more than medication change

**If low (< 10 ng/dL):**
- Reverse T3 is minimal; T4 is being converted preferentially toward active T3
- Metabolic rate is not suppressed by rT3 blockade
- Rare to be problematic; often indicates good thyroid conversion

### Pattern Recognition
*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Free T3:Reverse T3 ratio** — Optimal ratio is > 0.2 (T3 > 2x rT3). If ratio < 0.2, rT3 dominance is present.
- **Cortisol** — High cortisol drives rT3 production; if both elevated, stress management is critical.
- **Caloric intake and exercise** — Undereating and overtraining both increase rT3; both must be addressed.
- **Selenium** — Required for T4→T3 conversion; deficiency shifts pathway toward rT3.
**Key pattern:** Normal TSH + normal free T4 + normal free T3 + elevated rT3 + fatigue = tissue thyroid resistance from stress/undereating, not thyroid disease.

### Intervention Levers
High reverse T3 responds to: stress reduction (critical; cortisol is the primary driver), adequate caloric intake (undereating is a common cause), reduced exercise intensity (overtraining increases rT3), selenium sufficiency, and iron normalization (excess iron shifts toward rT3). Crucially, supplementing T4 or T3 won't help if rT3 is blocking the receptors—you must address the underlying stress/energy deficit. Results take 4-8 weeks, but require lifestyle change more than supplementation.
