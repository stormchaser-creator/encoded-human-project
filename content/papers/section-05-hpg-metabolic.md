### Preamble: The Axis as Architectural Spine

The hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis is not merely an endocrine relay. It is a primary organizing architecture of the human body—a verticalized signal cascade that integrates reproductive readiness with metabolic state, stress load, circadian timing, and energetic sufficiency. To study it clinically is to enter one of the most densely encoded systems in human physiology: a system where the body's decision to reproduce is inseparable from its assessment of whether survival conditions are adequate to sustain new life. That assessment is metabolic. It is nutritional. It is inflammatory. It is circadian. And it is continuously updated.

This section develops a comprehensive framework for understanding the HPG axis not in isolation but as the metabolic cluster it has always been—a regulatory ensemble in which gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) pulse dynamics, insulin sensitivity, adipokine signaling, thyroid-adrenal cross-talk, and mitochondrial energetics participate as co-equal voices. The clinical reading of this axis requires density at the level of body, soul, and spirit: density of mechanism, density of relational pattern, density of meaning-making.

Seven structural elements organize the material that follows: Pulsation, Shadow, Pathway, Encoding Question, Facilitation Hook, and two additional registers—Clinical Translation and Architectural Synthesis—that bring the encoded framework into applied territory.

### I. PULSATION: The Pulse as the Axis

#### The GnRH Oscillator

At the apex of the HPG axis sits the GnRH neuron network in the mediobasal hypothalamus. These neurons do not secrete tonically. They pulse. GnRH is released in discrete, rhythmic bursts into the hypothalamic-pituitary portal circulation, with interpulse intervals that range from approximately 60 to 120 minutes depending on the phase of the menstrual cycle and the sex of the individual . The importance of this pulsatility cannot be overstated: continuous GnRH exposure causes paradoxical downregulation of pituitary GnRH receptors, a pharmacological principle now used therapeutically in the form of GnRH agonists to suppress reproductive hormones in conditions ranging from endometriosis to prostate cancer. The pulse is not decoration. The pulse is the signal.

FACT: GnRH pulsatility is required for normal LH and FSH secretion. Pituitary gonadotroph cells require pulsatile rather than continuous GnRH stimulation to maintain receptor sensitivity and appropriate gonadotropin output. This was demonstrated conclusively in the classic experiments of Knobil and colleagues using rhesus monkeys with lesioned arcuate nuclei, in whom physiological pulsatile GnRH replacement restored normal reproductive function while continuous infusion failed to do so .

INTERPRETATION: The pulse is the body's primary encoding mechanism for reproductive readiness. Frequency, amplitude, and regularity of GnRH pulses encode not just "activate reproduction" but carry nuanced metabolic information downward into the gonadotroph. Slow pulses (low frequency) favor FSH release; faster pulses favor LH. This differential decoding at the gonadotroph level means that the upstream metabolic and neural inputs that regulate pulse frequency are simultaneously shaping the ratio of LH to FSH, and through that ratio, shaping follicular development, ovarian steroid output, and testicular function.

#### Kisspeptin-Neurokinin B-Dynorphin: The KNDy Ensemble

The GnRH pulse generator is not autonomous. It is regulated by a tripartite neural ensemble: neurons co-expressing kisspeptin, neurokinin B (NKB), and dynorphin, known collectively as KNDy neurons, located primarily in the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus . This ensemble functions as an internal oscillator:

- NKB drives synchronized kisspeptin release, which stimulates GnRH neurons via Kiss1R receptors
- Dynorphin provides autoinhibitory feedback, terminating each pulse
- The interplay between excitation (NKB → kisspeptin → GnRH) and inhibition (dynorphin) generates the oscillatory rhythm

FACT: Kisspeptin is a potent direct stimulator of GnRH release. Loss-of-function mutations in KISS1 or KISS1R in humans result in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism and absent puberty, confirming that kisspeptin signaling is not modulatory but obligatory for reproductive axis activation .

HYPOTHESIS: The KNDy oscillator is the primary node at which metabolic signals are translated into reproductive pulse dynamics. Because KNDy neurons express receptors for leptin, insulin, ghrelin, and glucocorticoids, they sit at a metabolic intersection. Changes in body weight, fasting state, insulin resistance, or stress load alter KNDy firing patterns before they alter GnRH output. The metabolic translation happens at this node.

INTERPRETATION: This architectural arrangement means that reproductive suppression in conditions of caloric deficit, excessive exercise, or chronic psychological stress is not a failure of the reproductive axis but an intelligent metabolic gate. The KNDy system reads the energetic and stress environment and adjusts pulse dynamics accordingly. The body does not reproduce when it cannot afford to.

#### Metabolic Inputs to Pulse Dynamics

The GnRH pulse generator receives metabolic information through multiple channels:

**Leptin.** Produced by adipocytes in proportion to fat mass, leptin signals nutritional sufficiency to the hypothalamus. Leptin receptors are expressed on KNDy neurons and on GnRH neurons themselves. FACT: Leptin-deficient mice (ob/ob) are infertile, and leptin replacement restores fertility in a dose-dependent fashion . In humans, severe leptin deficiency causes hypogonadotropic hypogonadism that responds to recombinant leptin treatment . The threshold model: a minimum leptin signal is required to sustain GnRH pulsatility. Below that threshold, the oscillator decelerates.

**Insulin.** Insulin resistance and hyperinsulinemia alter GnRH pulsatility in complex ways that depend on whether the hypothalamic insulin signaling is itself intact. HYPOTHESIS: In early insulin resistance states, compensatory hyperinsulinemia may paradoxically increase GnRH pulse frequency and LH amplitude, contributing to the elevated LH-to-FSH ratios observed in polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). In more advanced insulin resistance with hypothalamic insulin signaling dysfunction, the opposite pattern may emerge. The insulin signal to the hypothalamus is therefore not linearly proportional to circulating insulin levels.

**Ghrelin.** The hunger hormone ghrelin, secreted by gastric cells during fasting, inhibits GnRH pulsatility. INTERPRETATION: This inhibitory link between hunger signaling and reproductive pulse suppression is mechanistically coherent with the evolutionary logic of the system: high ghrelin signals food scarcity, and food scarcity is suboptimal reproductive timing. The ghrelin-GnRH inhibitory axis is one biological substrate of the clinical observation that acute caloric restriction rapidly disrupts menstrual cyclicity before body composition changes are sufficient to explain the effect through leptin alone.

**Glucocorticoids.** Cortisol and its regulatory hormone CRH exert inhibitory effects on GnRH pulsatility at multiple levels. FACT: CRH directly inhibits GnRH neurons. Glucocorticoids suppress kisspeptin expression in the arcuate nucleus and reduce pituitary responsiveness to GnRH [1]. This cortisol-HPG suppression is the mechanistic basis of the well-documented reproductive disruption in chronic stress states, including hypothalamic amenorrhea in athletes and in women with high psychological stress loads.

### II. SHADOW: What the Axis Suppresses

#### Functional Hypothalamic Suppression as a Clinical Archetype

The shadow dimension of the HPG axis is its suppression—not the pathological failure of the axis but its functional, intelligent withdrawal in response to environmental signals that it reads as incompatible with reproductive success. Functional hypothalamic amenorrhea (FHA) is the clinical archetype of this shadow.

FACT: Functional hypothalamic amenorrhea is defined by absence of menstruation for three or more months in the absence of organic pelvic pathology, associated with a low-frequency GnRH pulse pattern, low-normal or low LH, low FSH, low estradiol, and typically elevated cortisol [2]. It accounts for approximately 20–35% of cases of secondary amenorrhea in reproductive-age women [3].

INTERPRETATION: FHA is not a diagnosis of deficit. It is a diagnosis of suppression. The axis is intact. The gonads are intact. The pituitary is intact. What has changed is the upstream assessment of safety. The KNDy oscillator has downregulated. The body has decided, in its metabolic and stress-computational wisdom, that this is not the time.

The three canonical triggers of FHA—caloric restriction/low energy availability, excessive exercise, and psychological stress—are all metabolically interpretable:

1. **Low energy availability** reduces leptin below a reproductive threshold, reduces insulin, elevates ghrelin, and signals to KNDy neurons that energetic reserves are insufficient.

2. **Excessive exercise** creates a relative energy deficit (even without intentional caloric restriction) and elevates cortisol, both of which suppress KNDy firing. The mechanism of exercise-induced FHA is primarily the energy availability deficit rather than exercise per se—an important distinction because moderate exercise with adequate fueling does not suppress the axis.

3. **Psychological stress** activates the HPA axis, elevates CRH and cortisol, and through both direct and kisspeptin-mediated pathways suppresses GnRH pulsatility. HYPOTHESIS: The chronic stress model of HPG suppression suggests that unresolved high-load psychological environments may maintain the axis in a suppressed state regardless of nutritional adequacy—a clinical pattern seen in women who restore weight but do not recover menstrual function without psychological therapeutic intervention.

#### The Metabolic Shadow in Male HPG Function

The shadow dimension of the male HPG axis is less frequently discussed but mechanistically parallel. Low testosterone in men (hypogonadism of varying degrees) is now recognized to have a significant functional, metabolically-driven component beyond classical testicular failure.

FACT: Obesity is associated with lower total and free testosterone in men, and this relationship is bidirectional: low testosterone promotes fat accumulation, particularly visceral, and visceral fat promotes testosterone suppression through multiple mechanisms including aromatase activity, inflammatory cytokines, and leptin resistance .

**Aromatase and the adipose-testosterone loop.** Adipose tissue is a major site of aromatase expression, the enzyme that converts androgens to estrogens. In obese men, elevated adipose aromatase activity converts testosterone to estradiol at increased rates, lowering testosterone levels. Elevated estradiol feeds back negatively on the hypothalamus and pituitary to suppress GnRH and LH. The result is a suppressed axis with low testosterone and paradoxically elevated estradiol—an estrogenic male endocrine environment driven metabolically by adipose mass. INTERPRETATION: This pattern—elevated BMI, low testosterone, elevated estradiol, suppressed LH—represents a metabolically-driven HPG shadow that is often clinically misread as primary hypogonadism requiring testosterone replacement. Addressing the metabolic substrate (excess adipose) can restore the axis without exogenous hormone administration.

**Inflammatory cytokines and HPG suppression.** Adipose-derived pro-inflammatory cytokines including TNF-alpha, IL-6, and IL-1beta directly suppress testicular Leydig cell testosterone production and central GnRH pulsatility . This inflammatory channel means that any condition increasing systemic inflammation—including metabolic syndrome, sleep apnea, gut dysbiosis, and chronic infection—carries HPG-suppressive potential in men.

HYPOTHESIS: The male HPG shadow is an under-recognized contributor to the cluster of symptoms labeled "andropause" or "late-onset hypogonadism." A significant proportion of men presenting with fatigue, reduced libido, mood dysregulation, and declining body composition in middle age may have metabolically driven functional HPG suppression rather than primary testicular aging, with correspondingly different treatment implications.

### III. PATHWAY: Metabolic Cluster Architecture

#### The HPG Axis is a Metabolic Cluster, Not a Solitary Axis

The term "HPG axis" is architecturally incomplete. It names three nodes (hypothalamus, pituitary, gonads) and implies a linear hierarchy. The clinical and physiological reality is a cluster: a constellation of endocrine systems that share regulatory inputs, share output effects, and share vulnerability patterns. The metabolic cluster around the HPG axis includes at minimum:

- **Thyroid axis** (HPT)
- **Adrenal-cortisol axis** (HPA)
- **Insulin-glucose system**
- **Adipokine system** (leptin, adiponectin)
- **Growth hormone-IGF-1 axis**
- **Mitochondrial energetics**
- **Circadian timing system**

The clinical necessity of this cluster framework is that isolated HPG intervention—treating the axis without attending to the metabolic cluster—produces incomplete and often temporary results.

#### Thyroid-HPG Integration

The thyroid axis participates in HPG function at multiple levels and in both directions.

FACT: Thyroid hormone receptors are expressed in the hypothalamus, pituitary, and gonads. Thyroid hormone influences GnRH secretion, pituitary sensitivity to GnRH, and direct ovarian steroidogenesis . FACT: Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism disrupt menstrual cyclicity, though through distinct mechanisms.

In hypothyroidism: TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone), which is elevated in hypothyroidism due to reduced negative feedback, directly stimulates pituitary prolactin secretion. Hyperprolactinemia, even at mild degrees, suppresses GnRH pulsatility through dopaminergic and direct mechanisms. INTERPRETATION: Many cases of "unexplained" subfertility and menstrual irregularity that respond to thyroid hormone optimization are mediated through this TRH-prolactin-GnRH pathway rather than through direct thyroid hormone effects on the gonads.

HYPOTHESIS: The optimal thyroid function for HPG support may not coincide with TSH values at the population midpoint of the reference range. Emerging clinical data suggest that TSH values in the lower-normal range (approximately 1–2 mIU/L) may be associated with better reproductive outcomes than values in the upper-normal range (3–4.5 mIU/L), though this remains an area of active investigation and debate [4].

FACT: Thyroid antibodies (anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin) are associated with increased risk of early pregnancy loss and reduced IVF success rates even when thyroid function tests are normal, suggesting that the thyroid-HPG interaction is partially immune-mediated and not fully captured by TSH measurement alone .

#### Adrenal-HPG Integration

The HPA axis and HPG axis share hypothalamic real estate, regulatory inputs, and output effects in a pattern that makes their distinction partly conceptual.

CRH neurons in the paraventricular nucleus project to GnRH neurons and suppress them directly. Cortisol at the gonadotroph level reduces LH pulse amplitude. At the ovarian level, cortisol receptors on granulosa cells reduce steroidogenic capacity under stress conditions. At the testicular level, glucocorticoid receptors on Leydig cells mediate cortisol-induced testosterone suppression. INTERPRETATION: The adrenal-HPG interaction is fundamentally hierarchical in conditions of physiological stress: the stress response takes priority, and the reproductive axis is suppressed as a resource allocation decision. Cortisol is the biological instrument of that decision.

HYPOTHESIS: Chronic sub-clinical HPA activation—the kind associated with persistent psychological stress, sleep disruption, dysglycemia, or inflammatory load without acute physiological crisis—may maintain tonic partial HPG suppression below the threshold of clinical amenorrhea in women and below the threshold of classifiable hypogonadism in men, yet contributing to symptom clusters including reduced libido, mood instability, fatigue, and difficulty maintaining lean mass.

The DHEA bridge. The adrenal cortex co-produces DHEA and DHEA-sulfate (DHEA-S) alongside cortisol. DHEA serves as a precursor for peripheral androgen and estrogen synthesis and is the primary source of androgens in women. In the context of an underactivated or aging adrenal cortex, DHEA-S levels decline, reducing the peripheral androgenic substrate available for tissues including the brain, bone, skin, and clitoris/vulva in women and contributing to the estrogen pool in postmenopausal women through peripheral aromatization. FACT: Adrenal DHEA production peaks in the mid-twenties and declines approximately 10% per decade thereafter, a process called adrenopause . This is independent of the gonadal reproductive decline and represents a separate component of the metabolic cluster aging.

#### Insulin System Integration

The insulin-HPG intersection is one of the most clinically consequential metabolic cluster relationships, and its most visible expression is PCOS.

FACT: Polycystic ovary syndrome, affecting approximately 8–13% of women of reproductive age by current estimates [5], is characterized by hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, and polycystic ovarian morphology, and is strongly associated with insulin resistance in approximately 65–80% of affected women regardless of BMI [6]. This insulin resistance appears to be partially intrinsic to the syndrome—a primary metabolic feature rather than a consequence of weight gain alone.

The central mechanism linking insulin resistance to PCOS-pattern HPG dysregulation involves at least three parallel pathways:

1. **Elevated insulin at the theca cell level** stimulates androgen production disproportionately, because theca cells retain insulin sensitivity at the steroidogenic level even as systemic insulin resistance develops. This selective insulin sensitivity means that hyperinsulinemia drives excess ovarian androgen production.

2. **Insulin reduces hepatic SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin) production**, increasing the free fraction of both androgens and estrogens. FACT: SHBG is inversely regulated by insulin; higher insulin states reduce SHBG, increasing bioavailable sex hormones. FACT: Low SHBG is one of the most reliable peripheral markers of hyperinsulinemia in clinical practice .

3. **Central effects of insulin resistance on LH pulsatility.** HYPOTHESIS: Hypothalamic insulin resistance may alter KNDy neuron function in ways that favor increased LH pulse frequency, contributing to the elevated LH-to-FSH ratio characteristic of PCOS and promoting theca cell stimulation over follicular maturation.

INTERPRETATION: PCOS is architecturally a metabolic cluster disorder with HPG expression. Its reproductive features (anovulation, fertility challenges) are downstream of metabolic dysregulation (insulin resistance, androgen excess), and its treatment requires metabolic addressing first. The clinical mistake is to treat the HPG features (stimulating ovulation without correcting insulin resistance) without correcting the metabolic substrate.

#### Adipokine System Integration

Adipose tissue is not an inert energy store. It is an endocrine organ that communicates actively with the HPG axis through a portfolio of adipokines. The primary signaling molecules include leptin, adiponectin, resistin, and visfatin, with leptin and adiponectin having the most established HPG interactions.

**Leptin** has already been introduced as a permissive signal for GnRH pulsatility. The full leptin-HPG story is more complex. FACT: Leptin receptors are expressed not only in the hypothalamus but in the pituitary and in ovarian follicular cells, suggesting direct gonadal effects of leptin independent of central signaling . HYPOTHESIS: The dose-response relationship between leptin and reproductive function follows an inverted U-curve: too little leptin (low body fat states) suppresses the axis through hypothalamic under-stimulation, while leptin resistance (high body fat states with paradoxically reduced central leptin signaling despite high circulating leptin) may also impair HPG function through a different mechanism—not signal absence but signal blindness.

**Adiponectin** is the anti-inflammatory adipokine produced inversely to fat mass. It enhances insulin sensitivity, reduces inflammatory cytokines, and may have direct effects on GnRH and pituitary function. HYPOTHESIS: Low adiponectin in obese states may contribute to HPG dysregulation through both insulin resistance-mediated mechanisms and through withdrawal of direct adiponectin-mediated cytoprotection at the hypothalamic and pituitary level.

#### Growth Hormone–IGF-1 Integration

The GH-IGF-1 axis participates in the metabolic cluster supporting HPG function primarily through its anabolic and insulin-sensitizing effects at the ovarian and testicular levels.

FACT: IGF-1 receptors are present in ovarian granulosa and theca cells, and IGF-1 synergizes with gonadotropins to promote follicular development and steroidogenesis . FACT: In states of undernutrition, both GH secretion and IGF-1 levels are reduced (despite the paradox that GH itself may rise—this reflects GH resistance rather than GH deficiency) . The consequence is that gonadal response to gonadotropins is blunted in undernourished states not only because GnRH pulses are suppressed but because the ovarian machinery is itself less responsive without adequate IGF-1 support.

INTERPRETATION: Optimizing the GH-IGF-1 axis—primarily through adequate protein intake, resistance exercise, sufficient sleep (since the majority of GH is secreted during slow-wave sleep), and correction of insulin resistance—supports HPG function from below the gonadal level. This is a metabolic cluster intervention that does not directly target the HPG axis but improves HPG efficacy.

#### Mitochondrial Energetics

Perhaps the most underrecognized participant in the HPG metabolic cluster is mitochondrial function. The HPG axis is an energetically expensive system. GnRH neuron firing, pituitary gonadotropin synthesis and secretion, folliculogenesis, steroidogenesis (cholesterol to pregnenolone conversion), corpus luteum maintenance, and early pregnancy implantation support are all mitochondrial-energy-dependent processes.

HYPOTHESIS: Mitochondrial dysfunction—whether from nutrient deficiencies (CoQ10, carnitine, B vitamins, magnesium), oxidative stress, toxin exposure, or aging-related mitochondrial decline—may represent an underappreciated etiology of HPG dysfunction that does not fit neatly into the classical categories of hypothalamic, pituitary, or gonadal failure. It is a systems-level energy insufficiency affecting the entire cluster.

FACT: Mitochondrial density is highest in steroidogenic cells (ovarian granulosa cells, Leydig cells, adrenal cortex cells) among all non-cardiac tissue types [7]. This reflects the energetic cost of steroid synthesis: converting cholesterol to active steroid hormones via the cholesterol side-chain cleavage enzyme (CYP11A1) in the inner mitochondrial membrane is the rate-limiting, energy-dependent step in steroidogenesis. INTERPRETATION: Any condition reducing mitochondrial efficiency disproportionately impacts steroidogenesis, making mitochondrial health a foundational clinical target for HPG support.

HYPOTHESIS: Aging-related mitochondrial decline in oocytes—the progressive reduction in mitochondrial copy number and function in oocytes over the reproductive lifespan—is a central mechanism of declining ovarian reserve and egg quality with age. This has clinical implications for strategies aimed at supporting oocyte mitochondrial function (including CoQ10 supplementation, PQQ, and mitochondria-targeted antioxidants), though the clinical evidence base for these interventions remains developing .

#### Circadian Timing System Integration

The HPG axis is temporally organized. GnRH pulsatility has circadian modulation; LH surges in women are typically timed to early morning; follicular development follows a multi-day temporal program; melatonin participates directly in gonadal function modulation.

FACT: Melatonin receptors are expressed in the human ovary and testis, and melatonin has antioxidant effects within follicular fluid . FACT: Circadian disruption—as experienced by shift workers, individuals with chronic sleep restriction, or those with severely irregular sleep-wake schedules—is associated with menstrual irregularity, reduced fertility, and elevated miscarriage risk in some studies .

INTERPRETATION: The circadian dimension of HPG function means that sleep hygiene, light exposure patterns, and mealtiming are not peripheral wellness considerations but primary HPG regulatory inputs. Light exposure in the early morning anchors the master clock, which in turn anchors circadian hormone profiles. Irregular light exposure, late-night bright light (particularly blue-spectrum), and inconsistent sleep timing are architectural disruptions to the temporal organization of the HPG axis.

HYPOTHESIS: The wide variation in response to hormonal optimization therapies may partly reflect differences in circadian entrainment. Patients with well-entrained circadian rhythms may show more robust HPG responses to therapeutic interventions than those with significant circadian disruption, because the temporal organization of receptor sensitivity, downstream hormone synthesis, and clearance all depend on circadian architecture.

### IV. ENCODING QUESTION: What Is the Axis Asking?

The encoding question is the question embedded in the biology itself—the question that the system is perpetually asking, to which its regulatory outputs are the answers.

The HPG axis is asking, continuously and simultaneously:

**1. Is there sufficient energy?**
Leptin, insulin, ghrelin, IGF-1, glucose availability, and mitochondrial ATP generation all feed into this single question. The GnRH oscillator is a metabolic energetic sensor first, a reproductive activator second.

**2. Is there sufficient safety?**
Cortisol, CRH, inflammatory cytokines, and sympathetic nervous system tone all report on threat level. Safety here is not merely psychological but biological: infection, injury, toxin exposure, and psychosocial threat are all read by the same stress-sensing circuitry. The HPG axis is suppressed by the same CRH that is elevated in any of these conditions.

**3. Is there temporal coherence?**
Melatonin, cortisol circadian rhythm, and clock gene expression in hypothalamic neurons report on whether the organism is temporally organized and entrained. Temporal incoherence is metabolically costly and sends ambiguous timing signals to the reproductive pulse generator.

**4. Is there relational context?**
This encoding question extends into the psychoneuroendocrine dimension: social isolation, chronic loneliness, and relationship insecurity are associated with HPA activation patterns that extend into HPG regulation. HYPOTHESIS: The neuroendocrine embedding of relational context in HPG function is an evolutionary adaptation: reproductive investment is optimally timed when social support structures are present to aid offspring survival. The neurochemistry of social bonding (oxytocin, vasopressin) has direct interfaces with the HPG axis, particularly in the regulation of affiliative behavior and pair bonding, which are oxytocin-mediated and downstream of reproductive hormones.

The clinical reading of an HPG axis dysregulation pattern should always include these four encoding questions. What is this body's answer to: Is there energy? Is there safety? Is there temporal coherence? Is there relational context? The answers to these questions, read across the metabolic cluster, predict where the axis is suppressed, amplified, or dysregulated.

### V. FACILITATION HOOK: Clinical Entry Points

The facilitation hook is where the theoretical architecture meets clinical action—the specific, leverage-rich entry points that allow a practitioner to shift the metabolic cluster in ways that restore HPG function from within its own logic.

#### Hook 1: Energy Availability Before Hormones

The most foundational facilitation is correction of low energy availability before any hormonal intervention. In athletes, in women with disordered eating patterns, in individuals with high metabolic demands and insufficient intake, and in anyone presenting with FHA or with low testosterone in a low-fat-mass context, increasing caloric and nutritional density is the primary HPG therapeutic intervention.

INTERPRETATION: The clinical logic is: the axis is suppressed because the body reads insufficient energy. Providing a hormonal signal (exogenous GnRH analogs, FSH, LH, or sex steroids) into a system that has suppressed itself for metabolic reasons does not resolve the underlying encoding. It overrides the message. In some contexts that override is necessary and appropriate. In many contexts it bypasses the most accessible and durable treatment pathway.

Specific nutritional targets for HPG support include: adequate total caloric intake (no agreed universal threshold, but energy availability above 45 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day has been proposed as above the suppression threshold in female athletes) [1], adequate dietary fat (since sex steroids are cholesterol-derived, very low fat diets reduce steroidogenic substrate), adequate protein (for GH-IGF-1 support and general anabolic signaling), and micronutrient sufficiency, particularly zinc (for testosterone synthesis and aromatase regulation), vitamin D (for sex hormone synthesis and receptor function), magnesium (for mitochondrial function and insulin sensitivity), and B vitamins (for steroidogenic enzyme cofactor support).

#### Hook 2: Insulin Sensitivity as HPG Architecture

In the subset of patients where insulin resistance is driving HPG dysregulation (primarily PCOS-pattern in women, metabolic hypogonadism in men), insulin sensitization is the primary HPG architectural repair.

Clinical strategies for insulin sensitization with HPG-specific relevance include:

- **Time-restricted eating and carbohydrate distribution**: Concentrating carbohydrate intake toward post-exercise and morning windows, reducing evening carbohydrate load, and incorporating periods of overnight fasting improve insulin sensitivity through circadian-metabolic mechanisms.

- **Resistance training**: Skeletal muscle is the primary site of non-insulin-mediated glucose uptake and of insulin-dependent glucose uptake. Increasing muscle mass and muscle glycogen storage capacity through resistance training is one of the most potent interventions for improving whole-body insulin sensitivity. FACT: Meta-analyses confirm that resistance training improves insulin sensitivity in women with PCOS and is associated with improvement in menstrual regularity and androgen levels [1].

- **Metformin and inositols**: Metformin's primary mechanism of action (AMPK activation and hepatic glucose production reduction) has direct HPG-relevant effects including SHBG elevation, LH normalization, and androgen reduction in PCOS. Myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol, acting as insulin second messengers, show promising effects on ovarian insulin sensitivity and have a favorable evidence base in PCOS management .

#### Hook 3: Cortisol Load Reduction

Given the mechanistic clarity of the HPA-HPG interaction, reducing cortisol load is a high-leverage HPG facilitation. Cortisol load reduction is not equivalent to stress reduction (the latter is desirable but often incompletely achievable). It includes:

- **Sleep optimization**: Growth hormone, cortisol rhythm, and HPG axis activity all depend on sleep architecture. A minimum of 7–9 hours of quality sleep, with stable sleep-wake timing, restores the normal cortisol diurnal rhythm (high morning, low evening) that is permissive for HPG activity. Chronic sleep deprivation elevates evening cortisol, disrupts LH pulsatility, and reduces testosterone .

- **Blood glucose stabilization**: Hypoglycemic episodes are physiological stressors that activate the HPA axis via counter-regulatory hormone release. Stabilizing blood glucose through adequate and well-timed carbohydrate intake, sufficient protein, and avoidance of prolonged fasting in individuals whose adrenal reserve is insufficient for extended fasting reduces cortisol spikes and the cumulative cortisol load on the HPG axis.

- **Anti-inflammatory nutrition**: Since inflammatory cytokines activate HPA axis and suppress HPG axis, a dietary pattern that reduces systemic inflammation (omega-3 fatty acids, polyphenol-rich plant foods, minimizing ultra-processed food and refined carbohydrate load) is an HPG-protective intervention.

#### Hook 4: Thyroid Optimization as HPG Support

Given the TRH-prolactin-GnRH pathway and the direct thyroid hormone effects at gonadal level, thyroid optimization is an HPG facilitation in subclinical thyroid dysfunction and in the context of autoimmune thyroid disease.

HYPOTHESIS: Targeting TSH to the 1–2 mIU/L range in patients with reproductive goals and autoimmune thyroid disease, even in the absence of overt hypothyroidism, may improve HPG function through prolactin normalization. The threshold for thyroid optimization should be functionally informed rather than solely population-range-based.
