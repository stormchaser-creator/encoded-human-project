**Robert Becker, *The Body Electric* (1985)**: Becker documented the existence of a DC perineural current system — a network of analog direct-current signals running along nerve sheaths, distinct from digital action potential transmission. He showed that these currents correlate with regenerative capacity across species: salamanders with high regenerative capacity show strong DC injury currents; frogs with low regenerative capacity show weak ones. He demonstrated that externally applied DC fields could partially induce limb regeneration in non-regenerating species. This established that the body runs two electrical systems — a fast digital (synaptic) and a slow analog (DC field) — and that the analog system may be more fundamental to morphogenesis.

**Michael Levin (2012, *Development*)**: Levin and colleagues demonstrated that bioelectric gradients — established by ion channels, gap junctions, and electrogenic pumps — encode positional information that is instructive for organogenesis. Specifically, disrupting bioelectric gradients with pharmacological ion channel blockers produces predictable morphogenetic errors (e.g., randomized left-right organ placement, ectopic eye formation, cancer-like growth) that can be rescued by restoring correct voltage patterns — independently of genetic sequence. This established bioelectricity as an epigenetic layer upstream of transcription.

**Min Zhao et al. (2006, *Nature*; 2012, *Journal of Physiology*)**: Zhao's group provided the most rigorous experimental evidence that physiological DC electric fields (50-150 mV/mm — well within the range found in wounds) direct keratinocyte migration with high directional fidelity. Cells migrate cathodally (toward the negative pole) in wound healing contexts. This grounded McCaig's theoretical work in precise quantitative measurements.

**Colin McCaig DC wound field reviews**: McCaig synthesized evidence that wound-induced electric fields (generated by disruption of epithelial tight junction potentials) are among the earliest and most directionally consistent signals in tissue repair — preceding growth factor gradients, cytokine signals, and matrix remodeling. His reviews argued that DC wound fields are not epiphenomena but causal drivers of repair, and that disruption of these fields (e.g., in diabetic wounds) contributes directly to healing failure.
