## 2. Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: The Information-Theoretic Transmission Failure

**Conservative (Tier 1)**

Publication bias represents a systematic information-theoretic failure in scientific knowledge systems. The scientific record preferentially amplifies low-entropy signals (clean, significant, replicable results) while suppressing high-entropy signals (null findings, ambiguous results, negative replications). This creates a cumulative distortion — the 'winner's curse' — that compounds through citation networks. AI systems trained on published literature inherit this distorted prior, making AI disclosure standards a downstream problem whose root lies in upstream publication norms.

The analytical lenses most relevant here are **information theory** (signal-to-noise ratio, compression artifacts in citation networks), **network theory** (citation hubs, propagation of credibility through hub nodes regardless of underlying evidence quality), and **control theory** (feedback loops in the publication reward system that reinforce the distortion rather than correcting it).

Evidence from the corpus: The Sildenafil entry demonstrates that even well-documented scientific discoveries contain opacity about their own provenance. The NMN entry demonstrates how credentialed sources substitute for direct evidence. Both model the structural dynamic whereby confidence ratings detach from evidentiary chains.

What would falsify this: If AI systems trained exclusively on pre-registered, fully disclosed research datasets (e.g., from registered reports in high-transparency journals) showed no systematic bias amplification compared to models trained on standard publication corpora, the upstream-downstream transmission hypothesis would be substantially weakened.

### Hypothesis B: The Credibility-Performance Attractor

**Integrative (Tier 2)**

The research transparency crisis and the AI disclosure problem are not distinct problems but expressions of the same attractor state: systems optimized for credibility-signaling rather than truth-tracking. When self-regulatory mechanisms in any complex adaptive system — biological, social, or institutional — are calibrated toward external validation signals rather than internal coherence signals, they produce the same failure mode: confident presentation of incomplete or distorted information.

This hypothesis draws on multiple convergent threads in the evidence:

The **somatosensory self-regulation** entry (Siegel) describes a process in which direct, unmediated somatic input bypasses higher cortical processing to access autonomic ground truth. The analogy is precise: pre-registration and raw data sharing bypass the interpretive layers (framing, spin, cherry-picking) to access methodological ground truth. When these direct-access channels are absent, the organism (or institution) regulates against a processed representation of reality rather than reality itself.

The **age-30 hormonal dysregulation** entry and its soul-density mirror describe 'the withdrawal of automatic self-correction' — the gradual loss of precision in homeostatic mechanisms under accumulated adaptive load. Scientific fields show the same trajectory: early in a field's development, informal self-correction is rapid (replication attempts are common, methodological debates are vigorous). As fields mature and institutionalize, prestige gradients form, replication attempts are discouraged, and the automatic self-correction mechanisms lose precision.

Gabor Maté's **trauma as root of chronic illness** hypothesis, applied fractally to institutions, suggests that the chronic dysfunction in scientific publishing may trace to a foundational wound in how scientific identity was constructed — around certainty, prestige, and the suppression of negative results. The 'illness' (replication crisis, p-hacking, selective reporting) is the body's way of saying what could not be said within the original framework.

Stan Tatkin's **pro-social reward loop** suggests that transparency behaviors, like pro-social behaviors in relationships, are governed by social reinforcement dynamics. If the social reward for honest uncertainty disclosure were as strong as the social reward for confident positive findings, the attractor would shift. Current incentive structures in academia and AI development do not provide this reward.

Analytical lenses: **chaos attractors** (credibility-performance as strange attractor pulling all behaviors toward it), **control theory** (misaligned setpoints), **complexity emergence** (how institutional norms crystallize from individual incentive responses), **fractals** (same pattern at individual researcher, lab, journal, and field scales).

What would falsify this: If transparency interventions focused on social incentive restructuring (e.g., creating strong social rewards for honest uncertainty disclosure, treating null results as high-status contributions) consistently failed to improve disclosure outcomes, the attractor model would need revision.

### Hypothesis C: The Epistemological Phase Transition

**Radical (Tier 3)**

AI disclosure requirements are a phase transition marker in the epistemology of knowledge production itself. The emergence of AI as a knowledge-producing (or knowledge-transmitting) agent forces a symmetry-breaking moment: the tacit assumptions embedded in human research transparency norms — assumptions about authorship, intentionality, uncertainty, and evidentiary chains — become visible for the first time because they cannot be applied to a system that has no author in the traditional sense, no intentionality that can be queried, and no evidentiary chain that can be reconstructed post-hoc.

The spirit-density fractal mirror of the Sildenafil entry states this with precision: *'Consciousness does not move toward its own revelation through direct intention — the ego cannot aim at its own dissolution.'* An AI system cannot fully disclose what it doesn't know it doesn't know. But neither can the human researcher. The demand for AI transparency is therefore not primarily about AI — it is consciousness encountering its own opacity through a new mirror that lacks the social camouflage that allows human opacity to remain invisible.

The McGilchrist entry — that the scientific understanding of life's emergence from non-life necessitates that consciousness is emergent — has direct implications here. If consciousness is emergent, then epistemic transparency is also emergent: not a property of the base system but of sufficient complexity and reflexive capacity. We are currently at the threshold below which AI systems have insufficient reflexive capacity to be transparent about their own processes — but approaching the threshold above which something like genuine epistemic reflexivity could emerge.

The MTHFR entry models the appropriate epistemic response under conditions of irreducible opacity: use calibrated partial signals (family history as proxy) with explicit acknowledgment of the inference step and its limitations. This is not a failure mode — it is the correct response when direct measurement is unavailable. The hypothesis suggests that 'total transparency' is not the right target for either research or AI disclosure; 'calibrated partial disclosure with explicit inference mapping' is.

Analytical lenses: **phase transitions** (symmetry-breaking in epistemological norms), **topology/morphogenesis** (new institutional shapes forming around the transparency problem), **complexity emergence** (epistemic reflexivity as emergent property), **information theory** (uncertainty quantification as primary data).

What would falsify this: If comprehensive AI disclosure frameworks were developed and successfully implemented — producing AI systems that could reliably surface their own training biases, uncertainty ranges, and reasoning gaps with measurable accuracy — this would suggest the problem is technical rather than epistemologically fundamental, weakening the phase-transition framing.

## 3. Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The information-theoretic framing assumes that null and ambiguous findings are equally valuable to positive findings as a matter of epistemic principle. But there are legitimate scientific reasons to prioritize replicable, high-effect findings — not all entropy suppression is distortion. Furthermore, the claim that AI inherits publication bias assumes training data is primarily composed of published literature, which is decreasingly true for large language models trained on web-scale corpora including preprints, gray literature, forum discussions, and informal scientific communication. The upstream-downstream transmission model may be too simple for the actual complexity of AI training data ecosystems.

### For Hypothesis A

The citation network amplification problem is well-documented independently of this evidence corpus. Effect sizes inflate systematically through meta-analyses of already-biased primary literature (the 'decline effect'). The structural problem visible in Pearl's own knowledge base — where 'high confidence' ratings attach to entries with weak evidentiary chains — is a precise microcosm of the macroscopic publication bias problem. Information theory gives us precise, actionable tools to address this: effect size registries, confidence interval reporting standards, pre-registration of primary outcomes.

### Against Hypothesis B

The credibility-vs-truth attractor model risks being unfalsifiable — any failure of transparency can be attributed post-hoc to credibility-seeking behavior. The extension of Maté's trauma model to institutional behavior is metaphorical: scientific institutions don't have nervous systems, and the mechanisms that produce trauma responses in individuals may not have institutional analogs. The pro-social reward loop may not scale from dyadic relationships to the complex multi-stakeholder environments of academic publishing or AI governance.

### For Hypothesis B

The convergence of multiple independent frameworks — Maté's trauma model, Tatkin's relational reward loops, Siegel's somatosensory regulation, and the age-related dysregulation data — all pointing toward the same dynamic is evidentiarily meaningful even if none of the frameworks was developed with institutions in mind. The strongest support comes from the observation that transparency reform movements have consistently succeeded when they created new social rewards (e.g., the Registered Reports format at top journals created prestige for honest uncertainty) and failed when they relied purely on rule-based compliance. This is precisely what Tatkin's pro-social reward loop predicts.

### Against Hypothesis C

This hypothesis risks philosophical inflation of a practical regulatory problem. Policymakers and AI developers need actionable disclosure standards, not epistemological phase transition theory. The claim that 'total transparency is not achievable' could be weaponized to justify any level of opacity. The spirit-density fractal mirrors, while poetically resonant, are not scientifically operative — they cannot be tested.

### For Hypothesis C

Existing AI disclosure frameworks are failing precisely because they are asking the wrong questions. The EU AI Act, NIST AI Risk Management Framework, and most proposed disclosure standards focus on output explanation (what did the model say? how confident was it?) when the real opacity lies at the training layer: what data, with what biases, weighted by what loss function, producing what emergent capabilities that were not anticipated at design time? None of these are visible at the output layer. The phase-transition framing correctly identifies that we need new epistemological infrastructure — not just new rules applied to old infrastructure.

## 4. Synthesis

The three hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. They describe the same problem at different scales and levels of abstraction.

Hypothesis A is correct at the technical level: publication bias is an information-theoretic failure with measurable, correctable parameters.

Hypothesis B is correct at the institutional level: the technical failures persist because they are stabilized by an attractor in the social incentive landscape, and technical fixes without social incentive restructuring will be insufficient.

Hypothesis C is correct at the epistemological level: the AI disclosure moment is forcing a reckoning with the tacit assumptions embedded in all human knowledge-production systems, and the deepest solution requires new epistemological frameworks, not just new rules.

The evolved synthesis proposes a **three-layer disclosure framework**:

**Layer 1 — Provenance Transparency**: Where did the signal come from? What data, what sources, what selection process? For research: pre-registration, raw data archiving, methods transparency. For AI: training data documentation, data provenance standards, model cards.

**Layer 2 — Uncertainty Calibration**: What is the confidence distribution, not just the point estimate? For research: effect size reporting with confidence intervals, power analysis, replication status. For AI: uncertainty quantification, out-of-distribution detection, calibration curves.

**Layer 3 — Inference Transparency**: What assumptions connect evidence to claim? What inferential steps were taken? What is the logical chain? For research: explicit causal model statement, confound acknowledgment, limitations sections that are substantive rather than performative. For AI: chain-of-thought logging, reasoning trace disclosure, explicit acknowledgment of training-data-based inference vs. novel reasoning.

This framework is grounded in the recognition that **total transparency is not achievable** — the MTHFR entry correctly models that calibrated partial disclosure with explicit inference mapping is the correct epistemic response under conditions of irreducible opacity. The target is not omniscience but **calibrated honesty about the limits of what is known**.

## 5. Implications

### For Research Publication Standards

The three-layer framework suggests that current reform efforts (pre-registration, open data) address Layer 1 well but Layer 2 and Layer 3 poorly. Uncertainty calibration is still treated as optional in most journals. Inference transparency — the explicit mapping of assumptions — is almost entirely absent from standard publication formats. Registered Reports represent the most complete implementation of all three layers and should be treated as the gold standard.

### For AI Disclosure Standards

Current AI disclosure debates are almost entirely focused on output explanation (Layer 3 at the output layer) while ignoring Layer 1 (training data provenance) and Layer 2 (uncertainty calibration across the full output distribution). The analogy to clinical trial pre-registration suggests that **AI development pre-registration** — documenting intended training objectives, evaluation criteria, and capability bounds before training begins — could be a tractable and epistemologically meaningful intervention.

### For the Soul/Spirit Dimensions of Transparency

The fractal mirrors suggest that transparency is not only an institutional problem but a psychological and spiritual one. The same dynamics that make individual humans resist honest acknowledgment of uncertainty (the threat to credibility, the social cost of saying 'I don't know') operate at institutional and AI-system levels. Transparency reform movements that ignore the psychological and relational dimensions of disclosure will continue to produce compliance theater rather than genuine epistemic integrity.

The Tatkin pro-social reward loop suggests the most tractable entry point: create genuine social rewards for honest uncertainty disclosure. Make saying 'I don't know, and here is what I know about what I don't know' the highest-status epistemic act in a field, and the attractor will shift.
