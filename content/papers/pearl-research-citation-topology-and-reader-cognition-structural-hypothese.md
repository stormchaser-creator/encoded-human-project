## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: Cognitive Load Differential (Conservative, Tier 1-adjacent)

**Claim**: Author-date citation systems provide marginally superior inline information density for field-expert readers by preserving author-name cues that activate prior knowledge associations, while Vancouver's numeric system reduces cognitive load for novice readers by minimizing text interruption. These effects are likely small and expertise-dependent.

**Theoretical grounding**: 
- Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988) distinguishes intrinsic load (content complexity), extraneous load (format-imposed demands), and germane load (schema formation). Vancouver numbers impose extraneous load on readers who must map numbers to authors mentally; author-date imposes less extraneous load for those with author-knowledge but may distract novices with unfamiliar names.
- Working memory capacity (~4 chunks; Cowan 2001) limits how many citation-number-to-content mappings can be held simultaneously. Author-date partially offloads this by encoding author identity directly.
- Eye-tracking research on academic reading suggests readers skip most inline citations on first pass, which would reduce both the advantage of author-date and the disadvantage of Vancouver for typical reading behavior.

**Analytical lenses**: Information theory (signal-to-noise ratio of inline citation), signal processing (what information is filtered at the inline citation stage), control theory (does the reader's comprehension monitoring system behave differently under each format?).

**Falsifiable by**: Controlled experiment presenting matched scientific passages to readers at varying expertise levels, measuring comprehension accuracy, reading time, eye fixation patterns on citations, and reference look-up frequency. Large N required given expected small effect sizes.

**Confidence: Low** — plausible from theory but no empirical support in evidence base.

### Hypothesis B: Citation Network Topology Divergence (Integrative, Tier 2)

**Claim**: Vancouver's sequential numbering constraint produces measurably different citation degree distributions in published papers compared to author-date systems, with Vancouver papers showing higher citation concentration (fewer unique sources cited more times) due to reader-side information compression effects, even if author-side software eliminates sequential friction.

**Structural argument**:

Consider what information is available to a reader encountering citations in each format:

*Vancouver*: "This effect has been well documented [1,4,7,12]." — Four numbers. Reader receives: (a) quantity of citations, (b) their sequential positions in the document's reference universe. Zero semantic content about who conducted the cited work.

*Author-date*: "This effect has been well documented (Smith 2019; Jones & Kumar 2020; Chen et al. 2021; Williams 2019)." — Reader receives: (a) quantity, (b) approximate temporal distribution, (c) author names enabling social network activation, (d) implicit signal about whether this is recent or established consensus.

From an information-theoretic standpoint, author-date carries significantly higher bits-per-citation in the inline text. This may have several downstream effects:

1. **Author visibility differential**: Authors whose names appear inline (author-date) receive reputation signals proportional to citation frequency visible to readers. In Vancouver, this signal is suppressed. This could produce different patterns of author recognition and subsequent citation behavior.

2. **Citation cluster behavior**: In author-date papers, readers can see when the same first author appears repeatedly across a paper, potentially creating social salience. In Vancouver, this repetition is invisible unless reference lists are consulted. This might lead Vancouver authors to cite more diversely (to avoid the apparent monotony of citing [1] repeatedly) while author-date authors may more freely re-cite prominent figures.

3. **Temporal distribution of citations**: Author-date reveals publication years inline, which may influence how readers and authors weight recent vs. foundational work. Vancouver provides no such inline temporal cue.

**Network topology predictions**:
- Vancouver papers should show *higher citation concentration* (Gini coefficient of citations received within a paper will be higher — fewer sources cited more times) if authors avoid the visual awkwardness of high-frequency single numbers.
- Alternatively, if Vancouver authors are freed from author-name salience, they may cite more liberally across less-known sources, producing *lower* citation concentration.
- The directional prediction depends on which pressure dominates: aesthetics of number repetition (concentration) vs. liberation from name-brand pressure (dispersion).

**Analytical lenses**: Network theory (degree distribution, clustering coefficients), information theory (bits per citation, entropy of citation distribution), topology (does the citation graph have different connectivity structure?), entropy (is citation distribution more or less uniform across formats?).

**Falsifiable by**: Bibliometric analysis of matched corpora from Vancouver-dominant journals (high-impact medical journals: NEJM, Lancet, JAMA) vs. author-date dominant journals (ecology, psychology, social science journals of equivalent impact) controlling for field, paper length, year, and citation count. Metrics: citation frequency distribution Gini coefficient, unique reference count normalized by paper length, author citation concentration.

**Confidence: Low** — structurally coherent but no empirical support; directional prediction uncertain.

### Hypothesis C: Epistemic Community Formation Effects (Radical, Tier 3)

**Claim**: Citation format conventions, through their daily practice by every researcher in a field, subtly shape the cognitive architecture of scientific argumentation and social network formation within academic communities — Vancouver's name-suppressing convention reinforcing claim-centric epistemology in medicine, while author-date systems reinforce person-centric epistemology in social sciences, and these differences partly explain (rather than merely correlate with) documented differences in citation inequality (Matthew effect strength) across fields.

**Argument structure**:

This is the most speculative hypothesis and requires the most careful framing.

The core claim is that format conventions are not merely presentational choices but cognitive technologies that are practiced thousands of times per researcher per year, potentially shaping how scientific arguments are mentally structured.

In author-date fields, every act of writing a citation is also an act of invoking an author's name — which activates social network representations, authority hierarchies, and reputation schemas. Researchers in these fields are continuously reminded that knowledge claims are attached to persons.

In Vancouver fields, every act of writing a citation produces a number — neutral, sequential, person-free. This may reinforce a different epistemological habit: the claim stands independent of who made it; the source is retrievable but not salient.

If this account is correct:
- Medical fields should show lower author-name recognition among their own researchers (harder to name authors of landmark papers) compared to social science fields controlling for citation volume
- Author-date fields should show stronger Matthew effects (rich-get-richer citation dynamics) because name-recognition directly feeds back into citation decisions
- Switching a journal from author-date to Vancouver should, over time, measurably reduce citation inequality in that journal's references

**Important caveat**: Medical fields are known to have HIGH citation inequality and strong Matthew effects, which appears to contradict this hypothesis. The response would be: medical citation inequality is driven by clinical authority, journal prestige, and treatment guideline citation patterns — factors that swamp any format effect. The format effect, if real, would be detectable only in within-field variation or in cross-field comparisons controlling for these confounders.

**Structural analogy from retrieved evidence**: The soul-density mirror on Saturn transit relationships notes that "commitments made during periods of contraction and pressure become load-bearing structures in the self." While this is not scientific evidence, it points to a structural pattern worth noting: conventions adopted under institutional pressure (journal mandates, style guide requirements) may become architecturally determinative of cognitive practice in ways that transcend their surface function. This is an analogy, not evidence, and should be weighted accordingly.

**Analytical lenses**: Chaos attractors (citation format as attractor state in academic practice), coupled oscillators (synchronization of citation behavior within and across fields), complexity emergence (does community epistemology emerge from individual citation format practices?), fractals (does the claim-vs-person distinction replicate at multiple scales within the two traditions?).

**Falsifiable by**: Author name recognition tests administered to matched cohorts of medical vs. social science researchers; longitudinal citation inequality analysis for journals with documented format switches; analysis of citation behavior in interdisciplinary fields that mix format conventions.

**Confidence: Low** — speculative, confounded by many alternative explanations, no direct evidence.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

**Strongest objection**: The premise that readers process inline citations as information may be fundamentally wrong. Eye-tracking studies of academic reading consistently show that readers skip most inline citations on first pass, treating them as noise rather than signal. If this is the dominant reading behavior, the cognitive load differential between formats is effectively zero during normal reading — only becoming relevant when a reader specifically chooses to look up a source. The effect, if it exists, may be too small and too contingent on deliberate reference-checking behavior to matter practically.

**Strongest support**: The objection addresses typical reading behavior, not all reading behavior. In fields where author identity is highly relevant to credibility assessment (controversial findings, contested methodologies), readers may attend to author-date citations specifically because they want to know *who* is claiming something. In these cases, author-date provides genuinely useful inline information. The hypothesis may be correct for a subset of high-stakes reading contexts even if it fails for typical skimming behavior.

### Against Hypothesis B

**Strongest objection**: Modern reference management software (Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote) automatically renumbers Vancouver citations in real-time as documents are edited, completely eliminating any sequential friction that might have existed when researchers manually numbered references. The 'constraint' is a software feature that creates no cognitive burden for contemporary authors. If the author-side mechanism is absent, the hypothesized topology difference loses its primary driver.

**Strongest support**: The author-side mechanism is only one of two proposed drivers. The reader-side information compression effect persists regardless of how authors manage their references. If readers in Vancouver fields receive less inline information about citation sources, they may develop different patterns of source exploration, potentially creating feedback loops that influence subsequent citation behavior. The topology effect, if real, operates through reader-side mechanisms that software cannot eliminate.

### Against Hypothesis C

**Strongest objection**: The hypothesis has a fatal confound: field selection. Medical fields use Vancouver AND have clinical authority hierarchies, regulatory citation requirements (citing FDA guidelines, clinical practice guidelines), and massive citation asymmetries driven by landmark trials. Social sciences use author-date AND have person-centric theoretical traditions where the author's framework is the unit of citation. The citation format and the field's epistemological culture co-evolved and are deeply entangled — it is essentially impossible to disentangle format effects from the cultural contexts in which formats were chosen and institutionalized.

**Strongest support**: Natural experiments may exist. Several journals have switched citation formats over their history. If pre- and post-switch citation inequality metrics shift in predicted directions while controlling for field trends, the confound would be partially addressed. The hypothesis is not unfalsifiable — it just requires careful experimental design.

## Synthesis

Across all three hypotheses, several structural observations emerge:

**The information asymmetry is real and directional**: Author-date systems unambiguously carry more bits of information per inline citation than Vancouver numeric systems. This is not a hypothesis — it is a mathematical fact derivable from the formats themselves. The hypotheses concern the *consequences* of this information asymmetry, which remain empirically undetermined.

**The most tractable research question is bibliometric, not experimental**: Controlled reader experiments are expensive, subject to ecological validity concerns (lab reading ≠ real reading), and likely to produce small, noisy effects. Bibliometric analysis of existing citation network data is cheaper, more scalable, and potentially more ecologically valid. The topology hypothesis (B) is therefore the highest-priority research direction.

**The most important missing piece is a literature search, not new data**: Before generating any new hypotheses, a systematic search of library science, cognitive psychology, and bibliometrics databases should be conducted. ## Implications

If Hypothesis B is correct — if citation format conventions produce measurably different network topologies — the implications extend beyond academic style guides:

1. **Open science and discovery**: If Vancouver suppresses author-name information and thereby reduces name-brand citation bias, it may inadvertently produce more equitable citation distributions. Conversely, if it reduces information available for source evaluation, it may make it harder to detect conflicts of interest or track research lineages.

2. **AI and citation recommendation systems**: Systems trained on Vancouver-format corpora would learn different association patterns than those trained on author-date corpora — specifically, they would have less inline signal linking claims to authors, potentially producing different generalization behavior.

3. **Citation metric reform**: If format conventions systematically influence citation inequality, then cross-field comparisons of h-indices and citation counts are confounded not only by field size and publication norms, but also by citation format conventions — a factor rarely considered in metric normalization.
