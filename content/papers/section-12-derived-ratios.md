## HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Calculated index of insulin resistance from fasting glucose and fasting insulin: [Fasting glucose (mg/dL) × Fasting insulin (μIU/mL)] / 405. Values >2.0 indicate insulin resistance.
**Sample Type:** Derived from fasting blood tests (glucose and insulin)
**Fasting Required:** Yes (12 hours)

### What This Number Means

Insulin resistance is the condition in which cells fail to respond efficiently to circulating insulin, forcing the pancreas to produce ever-higher insulin levels to maintain blood glucose control. HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance) was developed as a non-invasive estimate of this condition using only fasting glucose and fasting insulin. The formula assumes a proportional relationship between fasting insulin and insulin resistance, with the constant "405" derived from the kinetics of hepatic insulin clearance.

HOMA-IR operates on a simple principle: if fasting glucose is normal but fasting insulin is high, the pancreas is working overtime to maintain glucose control — the definition of insulin resistance. The model has been validated against the insulin clamp (the gold-standard direct measure of insulin sensitivity), with correlation coefficients of 0.7–0.9 across diverse populations. It is not perfect — it assumes linear insulin-glucose relationships and ignores individual variation in hepatic insulin clearance — but it provides a reasonable estimate of whole-body insulin sensitivity from two simple blood values.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| HOMA-IR | Interpretation | Clinical Significance |
|---------|---|---|
| <1.0 | Excellent insulin sensitivity | Optimal metabolic health; no insulin resistance |
| 1.0–1.5 | Normal insulin sensitivity | Population-average; no intervention needed |
| 1.5–2.5 | Mild insulin resistance | Early metabolic dysfunction; lifestyle intervention warranted |
| 2.5–3.5 | Moderate insulin resistance | Clear metabolic syndrome; carbohydrate/exercise intervention necessary |
| >3.5 | Severe insulin resistance | High risk for type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease; may warrant pharmacological intervention (metformin) |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Regulation — Glucose Homeostasis Efficiency**

HOMA-IR directly reflects the Regulation operation's capacity to maintain glucose homeostasis without escalating insulin demand. A low HOMA-IR indicates elegant glucose regulation: glucose remains stable with minimal circulating insulin. A high HOMA-IR indicates dysregulation: the body is producing excessive insulin to achieve the same glucose control, burning through pancreatic beta-cell function in the process.

### What Deviation Signals

**If HOMA-IR >2.5:**
- Clear insulin resistance; metabolic syndrome is likely
- Even without elevated fasting glucose, the high circulating insulin drives inflammation, weight gain, and fatty liver disease
- Lifestyle intervention (reduced refined carbohydrate, regular exercise, adequate sleep) is the first-line treatment
- If HOMA-IR remains elevated after 3 months of lifestyle change, consider metformin (250–500 mg twice daily) which improves insulin sensitivity

**If HOMA-IR trending upward over serial measurements:**
- Indicates progressive beta-cell dysfunction and loss of insulin sensitivity
- More urgent intervention needed; do not wait for elevated glucose
- Investigate underlying causes: sedentary lifestyle, excess weight, high-refined-carbohydrate diet, sleep deprivation, chronic stress

**If fasting glucose normal but HOMA-IR elevated:**
- Pancreatic compensation is still working; glucose is controlled
- But the system is being pushed hard; eventually glucose will rise as beta cells fail
- This is the window for prevention: intervening now prevents progression to diabetes

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Fasting glucose** — If glucose is normal but HOMA-IR is high, insulin resistance is present but compensated; if glucose is elevated, beta-cell failure is beginning
- **HbA1c** — Reflects average glucose over 3 months; HOMA-IR reflects current insulin sensitivity
- **Triglycerides and VLDL** — Insulin resistance drives triglyceride synthesis in the liver; high triglycerides suggest insulin resistance even if HOMA-IR is "normal" by cutoff
- **Weight and waist circumference** — Insulin resistance correlates with central obesity; weight loss often improves HOMA-IR
- **Adiponectin** — Adiponectin secretion decreases with insulin resistance; low adiponectin predicts HOMA-IR elevation
- **ALT and liver ultrasound** — Insulin resistance drives fatty liver disease; elevated ALT or fatty liver on imaging suggests HOMA-IR dysfunction

**Key pattern:** HOMA-IR is the earliest marker of metabolic dysfunction. It often elevates before glucose rises, before weight increases substantially, and before cardiovascular disease becomes apparent. Serial HOMA-IR measurements over years track the trajectory of metabolic health.

### Intervention Levers

Insulin resistance is powerfully reversible through: (1) carbohydrate quality reduction (eliminate refined carbohydrates; prioritize complex carbohydrates and fiber), (2) physical activity (150+ minutes/week of moderate activity or 75+ minutes/week of vigorous activity), (3) weight loss if overweight, (4) sleep quality optimization (7–9 hours, consistent sleep schedule), (5) stress management. Metformin (500–2,000 mg/day) improves HOMA-IR by ~25–30% if lifestyle changes are insufficient.

## TG/HDL Ratio (Triglyceride-to-HDL-Cholesterol Ratio)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation / Conduction
**What It Measures:** Fasting triglycerides divided by HDL cholesterol. Values >2.0 indicate metabolic dysfunction; values >3.0 indicate high metabolic risk.
**Sample Type:** Derived from fasting lipid panel (triglycerides and HDL-C)
**Fasting Required:** Yes (12 hours)

### What This Number Means

The TG/HDL ratio is a lipid-based proxy for insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome. Unlike LDL cholesterol (which is atherogenic but somewhat independent of insulin resistance), the TG/HDL ratio captures the lipid distortion that insulin resistance produces: elevated triglycerides (from hepatic VLDL synthesis driven by excessive hepatic glucose uptake and de novo lipogenesis) and low HDL cholesterol (from impaired ApoA-I-mediated reverse cholesterol transport). In a person with normal insulin sensitivity, triglycerides are low and HDL is high, producing a ratio <1.0. In insulin resistance, the ratio widens.

The TG/HDL ratio is a composite marker: it does not require calculating HOMA-IR (which requires fasting insulin testing, which many clinicians don't order); it only requires standard lipid values. Epidemiological studies have shown that TG/HDL ratio predicts cardiovascular events better than LDL cholesterol in some populations, particularly those with metabolic syndrome.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| TG/HDL | Interpretation | Metabolic Meaning |
|--------|---|---|
| <1.0 | Excellent | Insulin sensitive, healthy lipid metabolism |
| 1.0–2.0 | Normal | Population-average; no intervention needed |
| 2.0–3.0 | Elevated | Likely insulin resistance; lifestyle intervention warranted |
| 3.0–5.0 | High | Clear insulin resistance/metabolic dysfunction; intervention necessary |
| >5.0 | Very high | Severe metabolic dysfunction; urgent intervention needed |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Regulation & Conduction — Glucose-Lipid Metabolism Coherence**

TG/HDL ratio reflects the coupling between glucose regulation (Regulation) and lipid transport (Conduction). When these systems are synchronized — glucose is well-controlled, insulin is low, the liver is not overloaded with glucose — lipids reflect this: triglycerides are low (minimal VLDL production) and HDL is high (efficient reverse cholesterol transport). When systems decouple — glucose regulation fails, insulin rises, the liver is overwhelmed with glucose — the lipid pattern distorts: triglycerides rise and HDL falls.

### What Deviation Signals

**If TG/HDL >3.0:**
- Clear evidence of metabolic dysfunction/insulin resistance
- Risk for cardiovascular disease, even if LDL cholesterol is not elevated
- Lifestyle intervention is urgent: carbohydrate reduction, exercise, weight management, sleep optimization
- Consider calculating HOMA-IR to confirm insulin resistance

**If TG/HDL >5.0 + fasting triglycerides >200 mg/dL:**
- Very high triglyceridemia; risk of acute pancreatitis if triglycerides exceed 1,000–1,500 mg/dL
- Requires aggressive intervention: carbohydrate restriction, potentially pharmacological (fibrates, high-dose omega-3, or PCSK9 inhibitors if LDL also elevated)
- Investigate secondary causes: uncontrolled diabetes, hypothyroidism, severe liver disease, familial hypertriglyceridemia

**If TG/HDL ratio normal but HDL is very low (<30 mg/dL) with normal triglycerides:**
- Unusual pattern; suggests possible genetic low-HDL syndrome (LCAT deficiency, Tangier disease)
- Requires specialist evaluation; these conditions carry cardiovascular and systemic risk despite normal TG/HDL ratio

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **HOMA-IR** — TG/HDL ratio is an indirect marker of insulin resistance; HOMA-IR is direct; discordance suggests lipid distortion from other causes (genetic, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome)
- **Fasting glucose and HbA1c** — TG/HDL rises early in glucose dysregulation; fasting glucose remains normal longer, making TG/HDL an earlier warning signal
- **LDL cholesterol and particle size** — In insulin resistance, LDL particles are typically small and dense (sdLDL), even if total LDL is not markedly elevated; if TG/HDL is elevated, assume sdLDL present
- **Adiponectin** — Low adiponectin predicts elevated TG/HDL; together they indicate insulin resistance + adipose tissue dysfunction
- **Lipoprotein(a)** — Independent cardiovascular risk factor; if elevated, adds to the TG/HDL risk assessment

**Key pattern:** TG/HDL ratio is one of the earliest and most accessible markers of insulin resistance. It rises before glucose, before BMI increases substantially, and before cardiovascular disease becomes apparent. Serial TG/HDL monitoring tracks metabolic health trajectory.

### Intervention Levers

Same as HOMA-IR: carbohydrate quality, physical activity, weight loss, sleep, stress. The specific leverage for TG/HDL is dietary: refined carbohydrates raise triglycerides most directly; omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil 2–4 g/day) lower triglycerides by ~20–30%; alcohol (particularly beer) raises triglycerides. Effective intervention typically reduces TG/HDL by 30–50%.

## Total Cholesterol/HDL Ratio

**Body Codx Operation:** Conduction
**What It Measures:** Total cholesterol divided by HDL-cholesterol. Values <5.0 indicate good cardiovascular protection; values >5.0 indicate elevated risk.
**Sample Type:** Derived from lipid panel
**Fasting Required:** Ideally yes, though total cholesterol is not fasting-dependent

### What This Number Means

The Total Cholesterol/HDL ratio is a simple, age-neutral cardiovascular risk indicator. Unlike LDL cholesterol (whose cardiovascular risk is acknowledged to increase with age), the TC/HDL ratio is predictive across ages: a ratio of 4.0 at age 30 indicates the same cardiovascular risk as a ratio of 4.0 at age 70. The ratio captures both atherogenic burden (total cholesterol) and cardioprotective capacity (HDL), with the assumption that a higher HDL proportionally reduces cardiovascular risk regardless of age.

Epidemiological studies have shown that TC/HDL ratio predicts cardiovascular events in some populations better than LDL cholesterol or traditional risk factor modification.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| TC/HDL | Cardiovascular Risk | Intervention |
|--------|---|---|
| <3.5 | Excellent | No intervention needed |
| 3.5–5.0 | Desirable | Maintain lifestyle; recheck every 2–3 years |
| 5.0–6.0 | Borderline high | Consider lifestyle intervention |
| 6.0–8.0 | High | Lifestyle intervention + consider statin if other risk factors |
| >8.0 | Very high | Intensive intervention; statin likely warranted |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Conduction — Cardiovascular Transport Efficiency**

TC/HDL ratio reflects the Conduction operation's capacity to transport lipids safely through the circulation. High HDL is cardioprotective: it removes cholesterol from vessels and periphery, transports it to the liver for excretion, and produces anti-inflammatory and anti-oxidant substances directly. High total cholesterol with high HDL (producing a low TC/HDL ratio) is not as dangerous as high total cholesterol with low HDL (producing a high TC/HDL ratio), because the protective HDL function partially offsets the atherogenic burden.

### What Deviation Signals

**If TC/HDL >6.0:**
- Elevated cardiovascular risk, even if LDL cholesterol is not dramatically elevated
- Warrants lifestyle optimization and, if other risk factors are present (age >45, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, family history), statin therapy
- Investigate causes of low HDL: sedentary lifestyle, excess refined carbohydrate, poor sleep, insulin resistance, smoking

**If TC/HDL >8.0:**
- Very elevated cardiovascular risk; statin therapy is indicated
- Investigate secondary causes: familial hypercholesterolemia, uncontrolled diabetes, hypothyroidism, liver disease

**If total cholesterol is very high (>300 mg/dL) but TC/HDL is <5.0 (meaning HDL is also very high):**
- Pattern seen in familial hypoalphalipoproteinemia (genetic high HDL)
- Cardiovascular risk may be lower than total cholesterol suggests
- Still warrants evaluation for other risk factors and family history of early cardiovascular disease

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **LDL cholesterol** — TC/HDL captures both LDL and non-HDL cholesterol; if TC/HDL is high but LDL is normal, the problem is likely low HDL rather than elevated atherogenic burden
- **Triglycerides** — High triglycerides and low HDL often coexist; together they indicate insulin resistance
- **Age and gender** — TC/HDL is age-neutral, but other risk factors (smoking, hypertension, family history) modify interpretation
- **Lipoprotein(a)** — Independent risk factor that modifies TC/HDL interpretation
- **Apolipoprotein B** — Direct measure of atherogenic particle number; more precise than LDL, and useful if TC/HDL is discordant with perceived atherogenic burden

**Key pattern:** TC/HDL ratio is simplest cardiovascular risk marker requiring only two values. It is particularly useful in populations where LDL cholesterol is normal but cardiovascular risk is still elevated due to low HDL (characteristic of insulin resistance).

### Intervention Levers

Raise HDL through: aerobic exercise (highest-impact intervention), alcohol in moderation (1–2 drinks/day for men, 1 drink/day for women), weight loss if overweight, niacin (1–3 g/day) or fibrates if needed. Lower total cholesterol through: reduced saturated fat and trans fat, plant stanols/sterols, oats and soluble fiber, and statin therapy if lifestyle insufficient.

## LDL/HDL Ratio

**Body Codx Operation:** Conduction
**What It Measures:** LDL cholesterol divided by HDL cholesterol. Values <2.5 indicate low cardiovascular risk; values >3.5 indicate elevated risk.
**Sample Type:** Derived from lipid panel
**Fasting Required:** Yes (12 hours) for accurate LDL calculation

### What This Number Means

The LDL/HDL ratio isolates the atherogenic-to-protective cholesterol ratio, excluding VLDL and other lipid particles. It is the most specific lipid-based cardiovascular risk indicator, capturing the balance between the atherosclerosis-promoting particle (LDL) and the atherosclerosis-inhibiting particle (HDL). A ratio of 2.5 indicates that protective HDL is roughly equal in concentration to atherogenic LDL; a ratio of 5.0 indicates that atherogenic LDL is 5 times more concentrated than protective HDL.

The LDL/HDL ratio is more informative than LDL alone because it acknowledges that cardiovascular risk depends not on LDL in absolute terms but on the balance between atherogenic and anti-atherogenic forces. A person with LDL of 150 and HDL of 40 (ratio 3.75) faces higher cardiovascular risk than a person with LDL of 100 and HDL of 25 (ratio 4.0) despite the lower absolute LDL.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| LDL/HDL | Cardiovascular Risk | Interpretation |
|---------|---|---|
| <1.5 | Excellent | Exceptionally low cardiovascular risk |
| 1.5–2.5 | Desirable | Low cardiovascular risk; standard prevention measures |
| 2.5–3.5 | Borderline | Moderate cardiovascular risk; lifestyle optimization warranted |
| 3.5–5.0 | Elevated | High cardiovascular risk; intervention necessary |
| >5.0 | Very high | Severe atherosclerotic burden; intensive intervention urgently needed |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Conduction — Atherogenic-to-Protective Balance**

The LDL/HDL ratio is the most direct lipid-based measure of Conduction operation balance: the atherogenic particle (LDL) driving atherosclerotic plaque formation vs. the anti-atherogenic particle (HDL) removing cholesterol from plaques and reducing inflammation. A favorable ratio indicates that protective forces outnumber atherogenic forces.

### What Deviation Signals

**If LDL/HDL >4.0:**
- Clear atherogenic burden; cardiovascular disease risk is elevated
- Warrants both lifestyle and pharmacological intervention
- Consider LDL particle size: if LDL is not dramatically elevated but ratio is high, the problem may be small, dense LDL particles (more atherogenic than large, buoyant particles)

**If LDL very high (>200 mg/dL) and HDL very low (<30 mg/dL), producing ratio >7.0:**
- Severe atherogenic burden; likely familial hypercholesterolemia or severe metabolic syndrome
- Requires intensive statin therapy, potentially with additional agents (ezetimibe, bempedoic acid, PCSK9 inhibitors, inclisiran)
- Cardiovascular imaging (carotid ultrasound, coronary calcium score) warranted even if no symptoms

**If LDL/HDL ratio normal but LDL particle number is high (detectable by apoB or advanced lipid testing):**
- Particle-level atherogenic burden may be higher than LDL/HDL ratio suggests
- Consider advanced lipid testing to resolve discordance

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Particle size and particle number** — Small, dense LDL particles are more atherogenic; LDL/HDL ratio does not capture size, only amount
- **Apolipoprotein B** — Direct measure of atherogenic particle number; ApoB/ApoA1 ratio is another way to express LDL/HDL relationship
- **Advanced lipid panel** (Lp(a), oxidized LDL, lipoprotein size pattern) — Refines cardiovascular risk assessment beyond LDL/HDL
- **Inflammatory markers** (hs-CRP, IL-6) — Inflammation amplifies atherosclerotic risk; high inflammation + high LDL/HDL = very high risk
- **Carotid ultrasound or coronary calcium score** — Direct imaging of atherosclerotic burden confirms LDL/HDL prediction

**Key pattern:** LDL/HDL ratio is the most clinically actionable lipid-based cardiovascular risk marker. It directly tells you whether the balance of forces favors atherosclerosis prevention or progression.

### Intervention Levers

Lower LDL through statin therapy (high-intensity statins reduce LDL by 40–55%; target LDL <100 mg/dL, or <70 mg/dL if prior cardiovascular disease). Raise HDL through exercise, weight loss, and reduction of refined carbohydrates. Combine: a person with LDL/HDL >4.0 typically requires both statin therapy to lower the numerator AND lifestyle changes to raise the denominator.

## ApoB/ApoA1 Ratio

**Body Codx Operation:** Conduction
**What It Measures:** Apolipoprotein B (the protein in all atherogenic particles: LDL, VLDL, Lp(a)) divided by Apolipoprotein A-I (the primary protein in cardioprotective HDL). Values <0.8 indicate low risk; >1.0 indicate elevated risk.
**Sample Type:** Blood (standard lipid panel does not include apolipoproteins; must be ordered separately)
**Fasting Required:** No (apolipoproteins are fasting-independent)

### What This Number Means

While lipid ratios like LDL/HDL use cholesterol mass as a proxy for atherogenic and protective burden, the ApoB/ApoA1 ratio uses the actual lipoprotein proteins. ApoB is present on every atherogenic particle (LDL, VLDL, Lp(a)); ApoA1 is the major protein of anti-atherogenic HDL. The ApoB/ApoA1 ratio thus directly counts: how many atherogenic particles vs. how many anti-atherogenic particles?

The advantage of ApoB/ApoA1 is that it is not distorted by cholesterol mass variations. Someone with very high triglycerides (producing many VLDL particles but not reflected as "LDL cholesterol" because VLDL is not LDL) will have high ApoB. Someone with very high triglycerides but one large LDL particle will have high ApoB but deceptively low LDL cholesterol. Thus, ApoB/ApoA1 ratio is more accurate than LDL/HDL ratio in lipid-disordered states.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| ApoB/ApoA1 | Cardiovascular Risk | Clinical Meaning |
|------------|---|---|
| <0.5 | Exceptional | Exceptionally low atherogenic burden |
| 0.5–0.7 | Low | Good cardiovascular protection |
| 0.7–0.8 | Desirable | Standard risk; prevention measures sufficient |
| 0.8–1.0 | Borderline elevated | Moderate cardiovascular risk; lifestyle optimization |
| 1.0–1.3 | Elevated | High cardiovascular risk; intervention necessary |
| >1.3 | Very high | Severe atherogenic burden; intensive intervention |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Conduction — Particle-Level Atherogenic-Protective Balance**

ApoB/ApoA1 ratio operates at the particle level of the Conduction operation: each atherogenic particle carries ApoB; each protective particle carries ApoA1. A ratio of 0.8 indicates 8 atherogenic particles for every 10 protective particles — favorable odds. A ratio of 1.2 indicates 12 atherogenic for every 10 protective — unfavorable odds favoring atherosclerotic progression.

### What Deviation Signals

**If ApoB/ApoA1 >1.0:**
- Atherogenic particle burden exceeds protective particle burden
- Cardiovascular disease risk is elevated
- Warrants intensive lipid management (statin therapy, potentially additional agents)
- May indicate hidden dyslipidemia: high triglycerides, small dense LDL, or high Lp(a) masked by "normal" LDL cholesterol

**If ApoB very high (>150 mg/dL) despite normal LDL cholesterol:**
- Indicates high VLDL or small LDL particle burden
- Likely insulin resistance or genetic lipid disorder
- Requires intervention despite "normal" LDL
- Consider triglyceride reduction, carbohydrate limitation, omega-3 supplementation

**If ApoA1 very low (<100 mg/dL):**
- Indicates insufficient protective HDL particle production
- Associated with metabolic syndrome, sedentary lifestyle, or genetic low-HDL disorder
- Warrants intensive lifestyle intervention and possibly niacin or fibrates

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **LDL/HDL ratio** — Discordance between LDL/HDL and ApoB/ApoA1 suggests lipid particle distortion (many small LDL particles despite "normal" LDL cholesterol, for example)
- **Triglycerides** — If triglycerides are elevated and LDL cholesterol is normal, but ApoB is high, indicates many VLDL particles; ApoB reveals the true burden
- **Advanced lipid panel** (LDL particle size, LDL particle number, Lp(a)) — Together with ApoB/ApoA1, provides comprehensive particle-level assessment
- **Insulin and HOMA-IR** — High ApoB with high VLDL particles typically indicates insulin resistance
- **Inflammatory markers** (hs-CRP) — High inflammation + high ApoB/ApoA1 = multiplicative cardiovascular risk

**Key pattern:** ApoB/ApoA1 ratio is the most particle-accurate cardiovascular risk marker. It is particularly important in metabolic dysfunction (insulin resistance, high triglycerides) where lipid mass ratios may underestimate true atherogenic burden.

### Intervention Levers

Same as LDL/HDL but with additional focus on triglyceride reduction and VLDL particle reduction (via carbohydrate quality, omega-3 supplementation, fibrates if needed). ApoB can be reduced by statin therapy (which lowers LDL particles) and by triglyceride reduction (which lowers VLDL particles). ApoA1 can be raised through exercise and HDL-raising interventions.

## NLR (Neutrophil-to-Lymphocyte Ratio)

**Body Codx Operation:** Defense
**What It Measures:** Absolute neutrophil count divided by absolute lymphocyte count. Values >3.0 indicate systemic inflammation; values >5.0 indicate significant inflammatory burden.
**Sample Type:** Derived from complete blood count with differential (CBC-D)
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) is an emerging inflammatory biomarker capturing the balance between innate inflammatory response (neutrophils represent the first responders of innate immunity) and adaptive immune sophistication (lymphocytes represent acquired, antigen-specific immunity). In health, innate and adaptive immune arms are balanced; in chronic inflammation or immunosuppression, this balance shifts.

High NLR (>3) indicates that the innate inflammatory system is activated or the adaptive immune system is suppressed. This pattern is seen in chronic inflammatory diseases (rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease), cardiovascular disease (NLR predicts cardiac events and mortality), cancer (NLR predicts poor prognosis), and chronic infections. Low NLR (<1.5) indicates robust adaptive immunity and minimal innate inflammation, seen in young, healthy individuals.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| NLR | Interpretation | Clinical Significance |
|-----|---|---|
| <1.5 | Excellent | Young, healthy immune function; minimal systemic inflammation |
| 1.5–2.5 | Normal | Population-typical; adequate immune balance |
| 2.5–3.0 | Borderline elevated | Early immune dysregulation; monitor and address |
| 3.0–5.0 | Elevated | Significant systemic inflammation; intervention warranted |
| >5.0 | Very elevated | Severe inflammatory burden; urgent investigation and intervention |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Defense — Innate-Adaptive Immune Balance**

NLR reflects the Defense operation's balance between inflammatory (neutrophil) and immune-learning (lymphocyte) capacity. High NLR means the system is in "fight mode" — ramped innate immunity, suppressed adaptive responses — characteristic of acute infection, chronic inflammatory disease, or severe stress. Low NLR means the system has adequate adaptive capacity — the immune system has learned and refined its responses without needing excessive innate inflammation.

### What Deviation Signals

**If NLR >3.0:**
- Elevated systemic inflammation or immune dysregulation
- Investigate source: chronic infection, autoimmune disease, metabolic dysfunction, psychological stress, malignancy
- Implement anti-inflammatory interventions: omega-3 supplementation, regular exercise, sleep optimization, stress reduction

**If NLR >5.0:**
- Severe inflammatory burden
- May indicate acute infection, acute disease exacerbation, or serious underlying condition
- Requires clinical investigation; do not delay in attributing to "just stress"

**If NLR trending upward over time:**
- Indicates progressive immune dysregulation
- May precede symptomatic disease manifestation
- Early intervention may prevent progression

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Absolute neutrophil and lymphocyte counts** — Discordance (one elevated, one normal) vs. both elevated or both suppressed suggests different pathophysiology
- **Other inflammatory markers** (hs-CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha, ESR) — NLR correlates with other inflammation markers; discordance may suggest NLR is less sensitive in that person
- **Complete blood count** — Anemia, thrombocytopenia, or other CBC abnormalities coexisting with high NLR suggest more serious immune dysfunction
- **Symptoms and clinical presentation** — High NLR without symptoms may still warrant investigation; high NLR with symptoms confirms inflammatory process

**Key pattern:** NLR is one of the simplest and most accessible inflammatory markers. It requires only a CBC-differential, which is part of routine blood work. Monitoring NLR over time tracks immune health trajectory.

### Intervention Levers

Reduce NLR through: omega-3 supplementation (2–3 g/day EPA+DHA), regular aerobic exercise (150+ minutes/week), sleep optimization (7–9 hours, consistent schedule), stress reduction, weight loss if overweight, and anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean diet shows consistent NLR reduction). If NLR remains elevated despite lifestyle intervention, investigate underlying causes before assuming it's simply an inflammatory state.

## PLR (Platelet-to-Lymphocyte Ratio)

**Body Codx Operation:** Defense / Conduction
**What It Measures:** Absolute platelet count divided by absolute lymphocyte count. Values >130 indicate elevated inflammatory burden and/or thrombotic risk.
**Sample Type:** Derived from complete blood count with differential
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The platelet-to-lymphocyte ratio (PLR) combines hemostatic activation (elevated platelets, indicating coagulation system activation) with immune suppression (low lymphocytes, indicating suppressed adaptive immunity). High PLR indicates a dual threat: heightened thrombotic tendency plus impaired immune regulation — a pattern seen in cardiovascular disease, cancer, severe infection, and chronic inflammation.

PLR is less widely studied than NLR but increasingly recognized as an independent cardiovascular risk marker. High PLR predicts cardiovascular events and mortality independent of traditional risk factors.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| PLR | Interpretation | Clinical Significance |
|-----|---|---|
| <100 | Excellent | Normal hemostatic-immune balance; minimal thrombotic risk |
| 100–130 | Normal | Population-typical |
| 130–150 | Borderline elevated | Mild hemostatic activation or immune suppression |
| 150–200 | Elevated | Significant inflammatory burden and/or thrombotic risk |
| >200 | Very elevated | Severe hemostatic-immune dysregulation |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Defense & Conduction — Thrombotic-Immune Balance**

PLR reflects two operatic functions: Defense (lymphocyte-mediated immunity) and Conduction (platelet-mediated hemostasis and inflammation). High PLR indicates:

- **Platelet activation:** Either from direct thrombotic stimulus (atherosclerotic plaque, endothelial injury) or from inflammatory signaling (cytokines, chemokines activating platelets)
- **Lymphocyte suppression:** Either from immune exhaustion (chronic inflammation, cancer, severe stress) or from direct suppression (immunosuppressive therapy, HIV)

The combination indicates that the system is simultaneously: (1) prone to clotting and inflammation, and (2) unable to mount effective immune response. This is a dangerous combination, characteristic of advanced atherosclerotic disease, malignancy, and severe sepsis.

### What Deviation Signals

**If PLR >150:**
- Significant thrombotic and inflammatory burden
- If accompanied by cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, dyslipidemia, smoking), cardiovascular disease risk is high
- Consider antiplatelet therapy (aspirin) if not contraindicated; intensive cardiovascular risk factor modification
- Investigate source of lymphocyte suppression: chronic infection, immune activation, stress

**If PLR >200:**
- Severe hemostatic-immune dysregulation
- May indicate acute thrombotic event, acute infection, malignancy, or severe systemic disease
- Requires urgent clinical evaluation

**If PLR normal but platelet count is very high (>500k):**
- May indicate primary myeloproliferative disorder (essential thrombocythemia, CML)
- Requires hematology evaluation
- Thrombotic risk exists even if lymphocytes are normal

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **NLR** — Both NLR and PLR elevated indicates severe systemic inflammation; one normal and one elevated suggests different pathophysiology
- **D-dimer** — If PLR is elevated and D-dimer is elevated, thrombotic risk is very high; consider imaging (VTE screening, coronary imaging)
- **Cardiovascular imaging** — High PLR predicts atherosclerotic burden; imaging can confirm presence and extent
- **Malignancy screening** — High PLR is associated with cancer; if other causes ruled out, appropriate cancer screening warranted
- **Infection markers** (procalcitonin, CRP) — High PLR with elevated procalcitonin suggests sepsis or severe infection

**Key pattern:** PLR is most useful as a thrombotic-inflammatory composite marker in people with known cardiovascular disease or cancer, where both inflammation and thrombotic risk are heightened.

### Intervention Levers

Anti-inflammatory interventions (same as NLR) reduce platelet activation and support immune recovery. Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin 81 mg/day) reduces thrombotic risk if indicated by cardiovascular disease. Omega-3 supplementation reduces platelet reactivity and inflammation simultaneously. Investigate and treat underlying causes of lymphocyte suppression.

## Albumin/Globulin (A/G) Ratio

**Body Codx Operation:** Elimination / Defense
**What It Measures:** Serum albumin divided by globulin (calculated as total protein minus albumin). Values 1.0–2.5 indicate normal; values <1.0 indicate globulin excess or albumin deficiency.
**Sample Type:** Derived from basic metabolic panel with total protein and albumin
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Albumin is the primary protein synthesized by the liver, serving as the major plasma carrier for lipids, hormones, bilirubin, and drugs. Globulins are a diverse class of proteins including immunoglobulins (antibodies produced by B lymphocytes), acute-phase proteins (produced during inflammation), and other plasma proteins. The A/G ratio reflects the balance between hepatic protein synthesis capacity (albumin) and immune/inflammatory protein production (globulins).

A low A/G ratio (high globulins relative to albumin) indicates either decreased albumin production (liver dysfunction, malnutrition) or elevated globulin production (immune activation, inflammation, autoimmune disease, malignancy).

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| A/G Ratio | Interpretation | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|---|---|
| >2.0 | Normal/high | Albumin-dominant; healthy liver and immune system |
| 1.5–2.0 | Normal | Typical healthy adult |
| 1.0–1.5 | Low-normal | Mild globulin elevation or mild albumin deficiency; monitor |
| <1.0 | Abnormal | Significant globulin excess or albumin deficiency; investigate |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Elimination & Defense — Hepatic Synthetic Function and Immune Activation**

A/G ratio reflects both Elimination (liver's capacity to synthesize proteins for detoxification and metabolism) and Defense (immune system's production of immunoglobulins and inflammatory proteins).

- **High A/G (>2.0):** Albumin dominates; liver is producing protein robustly; immune system is not activated
- **Normal A/G (1.5–2.0):** Balanced hepatic and immune function
- **Low A/G (<1.0):** Either liver is failing (reduced albumin synthesis) or immune system is highly activated (excessive globulin production)

### What Deviation Signals

**If A/G <1.0:**
- Investigate cause: liver disease, immune activation, or malnutrition
- If accompanied by low albumin (<3.5 g/dL), suggests liver dysfunction or malnutrition
- If albumin is normal but A/G is low, suggests high globulins from immune activation (autoimmune disease, multiple myeloma, chronic infection, malignancy)

**If A/G <1.0 + elevated total protein (>8.0 g/dL):**
- Pattern suggestive of monoclonal gammopathy (multiple myeloma, Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia) or other malignancy
- Requires specialist evaluation: serum electrophoresis, immunofixation, free light chain assay

**If A/G <1.0 + low albumin + low total protein:**
- Suggests malnutrition, liver disease, or kidney disease (protein loss in urine)
- Requires nutritional assessment and possible hepatic/renal evaluation

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Albumin and total protein levels separately** — Low A/G from low albumin vs. high globulins requires different interpretation
- **Liver function tests** (ALT, AST, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin) — If A/G is low and liver enzymes are elevated, liver disease is likely
- **Prealbumin** — Better marker of recent hepatic synthetic function; lower half-life (2–3 days) vs. albumin (20 days)
- **Immunoglobulin levels and serum electrophoresis** — If globulins are elevated, immunofixation identifies monoclonal vs. polyclonal patterns
- **Autoimmune markers** (ANA, complement, ESR) — High globulins with autoimmune markers suggest autoimmune disease

**Key pattern:** A/G ratio is an overlooked composite marker. Low A/G raises the question: is this liver dysfunction or immune activation? The answer guides downstream investigation.

### Intervention Levers

If A/G is low from low albumin (liver dysfunction or malnutrition): protein intake optimization (80–100 g/day), liver support (reduce alcohol, optimize hepatitis status), and treatment of underlying cause. If A/G is low from high globulins (immune activation): identify and treat underlying autoimmune or inflammatory disease; anti-inflammatory interventions may help.

## BUN/Creatinine Ratio

**Body Codx Operation:** Elimination
**What It Measures:** Blood urea nitrogen divided by creatinine. Ratios 10–20 indicate normal; ratios >20 indicate dehydration or prerenal azotemia.
**Sample Type:** Derived from basic metabolic panel
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

BUN (blood urea nitrogen) and creatinine are both markers of kidney function, but they are filtered at different rates and reabsorbed at different sites. Creatinine is freely filtered by the glomerulus and minimally reabsorbed; BUN is filtered but significantly reabsorbed in the proximal tubule. Thus, the BUN/Creatinine ratio reflects the balance between glomerular filtration and tubular reabsorption.

A high BUN/Creatinine ratio (>20) indicates either: (1) prerenal azotemia (reduced glomerular filtration due to dehydration or reduced renal blood flow), where BUN rises disproportionately to creatinine, or (2) increased urea production from catabolic state (high protein intake, muscle breakdown, sepsis). A low BUN/Creatinine ratio (<10) indicates either reduced urea production (liver disease, malnutrition) or primary renal dysfunction where both BUN and creatinine rise equally.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| BUN/Cr | Interpretation | Pathophysiology |
|--------|---|---|
| <10 | Low | Liver disease, low protein intake, or intrinsic renal dysfunction |
| 10–20 | Normal | Healthy kidneys; normal hydration status; normal protein metabolism |
| 20–30 | Elevated | Dehydration, prerenal azotemia, or high protein catabolism |
| >30 | Severely elevated | Severe dehydration, shock, or acute kidney injury (prerenal) |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Elimination — Renal Filtration and Tubular Function**

BUN/Creatinine ratio reflects the Elimination operation's capacity to filter and reabsorb nitrogenous wastes. A normal ratio indicates:

- **Adequate glomerular filtration:** Creatinine is efficiently filtered
- **Appropriate tubular reabsorption:** BUN is reabsorbed at physiologically normal rate
- **Adequate hydration:** The system has sufficient perfusion pressure to maintain filtration

A high ratio indicates that the system is prioritizing water and urea reabsorption over creatinine reabsorption — characteristic of volume depletion or reduced renal perfusion.

### What Deviation Signals

**If BUN/Cr >20 + low urine sodium + orthostatic hypotension:**
- Classic prerenal azotemia from dehydration
- Warrants fluid repletion; assess hydration status and fluid intake

**If BUN/Cr >20 + high protein intake or catabolic state (sepsis, malignancy, muscle wasting):**
- High urea production driving elevated BUN
- Creatinine is normal or near-normal
- Warrants investigation of catabolism source

**If BUN/Cr <10 + high creatinine + elevated liver enzymes:**
- Possible liver disease (reduced urea synthesis + reduced protein catabolism)
- Requires hepatic evaluation

**If BUN and creatinine both elevated equally (BUN/Cr ≈ 10–15) with low urine sodium:**
- Pattern suggestive of intrinsic renal disease (glomerulonephritis, diabetic nephropathy, acute kidney injury)
- Warrants renal imaging, urine sediment analysis, and possible biopsy

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Urine sodium and osmolality** — If BUN/Cr is high and urine sodium is low (<10 mEq/L), prerenal azotemia is likely; if urine sodium is high, intrinsic renal disease is likely
- **Hydration markers** (blood pressure, orthostatic vital signs, urine output, body weight) — Confirm dehydration
- **Catabolic markers** (CRP, IL-6, albumin) — If BUN/Cr is high due to catabolism, inflammatory and nutritional markers should be abnormal
- **Urine creatinine and 24-hour urine collection** — Quantify actual renal function

**Key pattern:** BUN/Creatinine ratio is a simple way to discriminate prerenal (high ratio) from intrinsic renal (normal/low ratio) dysfunction. Combined with urine sodium, it has high discriminatory power.

### Intervention Levers

If BUN/Cr is high from dehydration: fluid repletion (oral if able, IV if necessary) and addressing underlying cause (diarrhea, vomiting, excessive sweating, inadequate intake). If high from catabolism: treat underlying process (sepsis, malignancy), ensure adequate protein and calorie intake, and optimize nutritional status. If low: investigate liver disease and treat appropriately.

## FIB-4 Index (Liver Fibrosis-4)

**Body Codx Operation:** Elimination
**What It Measures:** Calculated fibrosis risk score for nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): [Age (years) × AST (U/L)] / [Platelet count (10^9/L) × √ALT (U/L)]. Values <1.30 indicate low fibrosis risk; >2.67 indicate high risk.
**Sample Type:** Derived from liver enzymes and platelet count
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

The FIB-4 index is a non-invasive surrogate for liver fibrosis, developed to identify patients with NAFLD who have advanced fibrosis and would benefit from specialist evaluation. The formula combines: (1) age (fibrosis risk increases with age), (2) AST (aspartate aminotransferase, a hepatic enzyme that rises with inflammation and fibrosis), (3) ALT (alanine aminotransferase, also a hepatic enzyme), and (4) platelet count (which falls as the liver becomes cirrhotic and spleen enlargement develops).

FIB-4 was developed to screen the enormous population of NAFLD patients (present in ~25% of adults) to identify who needs liver imaging or biopsy vs. who can be monitored with simple lab tests. It has been validated against transient elastography (FibroScan) and liver biopsy.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| FIB-4 | Liver Fibrosis Risk | Interpretation |
|-------|---|---|
| <1.30 | Low | <5% probability of advanced fibrosis (F3–F4); routine surveillance sufficient |
| 1.30–2.67 | Indeterminate | 5–25% probability of advanced fibrosis; consider imaging (FibroScan/transient elastography) |
| >2.67 | High | >25% probability of advanced fibrosis; specialist evaluation and imaging warranted |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Elimination — Hepatic Structural Integrity and Synthetic Function**

FIB-4 index reflects the Elimination operation's structural and functional integrity. Progressive fibrosis indicates that the liver is being damaged faster than it can repair itself — a consequence of chronic hepatic inflammation (from alcohol, NAFLD, viral hepatitis, autoimmune disease, etc.).

### What Deviation Signals

**If FIB-4 >2.67:**
- High probability of advanced liver fibrosis (F3–F4, approaching or at cirrhosis)
- Warrants urgent specialist hepatology evaluation
- Needs transient elastography (FibroScan) or liver imaging (ultrasound, CT, MRI)
- May warrant antiviral therapy (if hepatitis B or C), steatosis treatment, or management of complications (varices, ascites)

**If FIB-4 1.30–2.67:**
- Indeterminate risk; borderline between low and high
- Consider transient elastography to clarify
- If FibroScan shows low fibrosis, can reassure and monitor; if shows advanced fibrosis, escalate care

**If FIB-4 <1.30 + elevated AST/ALT (>40 U/L):**
- Low fibrosis risk but active hepatic inflammation
- Warrants investigation of cause: NAFLD with active inflammation, viral hepatitis, autoimmune hepatitis, alcohol
- Treat underlying cause; repeat FIB-4 in 1–2 years to monitor progression

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **AST/ALT ratio** — If AST/ALT >1, suggests advanced fibrosis or cirrhosis (damaged hepatocytes preferentially lose ALT); if ALT >AST, suggests earlier fibrosis or inflammation without cirrhosis
- **Platelet count** — Directly used in FIB-4; low platelets worsen score; very low platelets (50–100k) may indicate cirrhosis with portal hypertension
- **Bilirubin and albumin** — If elevated bilirubin or low albumin, indicates synthetic dysfunction; suggests advanced cirrhosis
- **Transient elastography (FibroScan)** — Non-invasive imaging that directly measures liver stiffness; considered superior to FIB-4 but more expensive
- **Ultrasound** — Assess liver echogenicity (steatosis), surface nodularity (cirrhosis), splenomegaly (portal hypertension)
- **Viral hepatitis testing** — If FIB-4 is high, test for hepatitis B and C

**Key pattern:** FIB-4 is the most accessible and least expensive screening test for advanced fibrosis. It is particularly useful in screening large populations of NAFLD patients to identify who needs further evaluation vs. who can be monitored.

### Intervention Levers

If FIB-4 is elevated: identify and treat underlying cause (weight loss and exercise for NAFLD, antiviral therapy for hepatitis B/C, alcohol cessation, immune suppression for autoimmune hepatitis). Reduce inflammation through antioxidants, omega-3 supplementation, and treatment of metabolic syndrome. Avoid hepatotoxic drugs and substances. Monitor with serial FIB-4 and consider FibroScan if indeterminate.

## eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

**Body Codx Operation:** Elimination
**What It Measures:** Calculated kidney filtering capacity using creatinine, age, sex, and race (CKD-EPI equation or KDIGO 2021 equation without race). Normal >90 mL/min/1.73m²; <15 indicates kidney failure.
**Sample Type:** Derived from serum creatinine
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

eGFR is the gold standard estimate of kidney function, calculated from serum creatinine using equations that account for age, sex, and body surface area. The newer KDIGO 2021 CKD-EPI equation has replaced the older race-based equation, as it was recognized that the race adjustment was anthropologically unfounded and clinically harmful.

eGFR estimates the volume of blood filtered by the glomeruli per minute. A normal eGFR of 90+ indicates intact kidney function; eGFR <60 indicates at least moderate renal impairment; <15 indicates kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplant.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| eGFR (mL/min/1.73m²) | Stage | Kidney Function | |
|----------------------|-------|---|---|
| >90 | G1 | Normal or high | |
| 60–89 | G2 | Mildly decreased | Monitor; usually no intervention needed unless albuminuria present |
| 45–59 | G3a | Mildly to moderately decreased | Investigate cause; monitor closely; medication adjustments |
| 30–44 | G3b | Moderately to severely decreased | Intensive management; nephrology referral warranted |
| 15–29 | G4 | Severely decreased | Nephrology referral; prepare for renal replacement therapy |
| <15 | G5 | Kidney failure | Dialysis or transplant necessary |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Elimination — Glomerular Filtering Capacity**

eGFR is the primary measure of the Elimination operation's core function: filtering waste products from blood. As eGFR declines, the kidney's capacity to clear wastes, regulate fluid and electrolytes, and produce hormones (erythropoietin, calcitriol) deteriorates.

### What Deviation Signals

**If eGFR 60–90 (Stage G2) + albuminuria or proteinuria:**
- Early chronic kidney disease despite near-normal eGFR
- Warrants investigation of cause: diabetes, hypertension, autoimmune disease, genetic disease (PKD, Alport syndrome)
- Intensive management of cause (glucose control, blood pressure, immunosuppression) can slow progression

**If eGFR 30–60 (Stage G3) without albuminuria:**
- Kidney function is clearly impaired
- Investigate cause if not known
- Monitor closely; medication doses may need adjustment (avoid NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors may be protective)
- Assess for complications: anemia, bone disease, hypertension

**If eGFR <30 (Stage G4–G5):**
- Severe kidney disease; nephrology referral urgent
- Begin preparation for renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplant)
- Monitor for complications: fluid overload, electrolyte abnormalities, anemia, bone disease
- Address cardiovascular disease risk (high in kidney disease)

**If eGFR declining rapidly (>3–4 mL/min/1.73m² per year):**
- Indicates rapidly progressive kidney disease
- May indicate immune-mediated glomerulonephritis, malignancy-related kidney disease, or medication toxicity
- Urgent evaluation and treatment necessary

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Creatinine and BUN** — eGFR is estimated from creatinine; seeing the raw values informs interpretation
- **Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR)** — Albuminuria presence changes CKD stage interpretation; even with eGFR >60, albuminuria indicates kidney disease
- **Cystatin C** — Alternative filtration marker that may be more accurate than creatinine in some populations (elderly, low muscle mass, obesity)
- **Blood pressure and cardiovascular risk factors** — Hypertension is both cause and consequence of kidney disease; tight BP control slows progression
- **Glucose and HbA1c** — Diabetes is the leading cause of CKD worldwide; glucose control is paramount

**Key pattern:** eGFR is the single most important marker of kidney function. Serial eGFR tracking over years indicates progression rate and urgency of intervention.

### Intervention Levers

eGFR decline is slowed by: tight blood pressure control (goal <120 systolic), glucose control if diabetic (goal HbA1c <7% in most diabetics), and ACE inhibitor or ARB therapy which reduces glomerular hyperfiltration. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, cisplatin). If eGFR falls below 30, nephrology referral for advanced CKD management and renal replacement therapy planning is essential.

## Free Testosterone Percentage

**Body Codx Operation:** Synthesis
**What It Measures:** Free testosterone (unbound, bioavailable testosterone) as a percentage of total testosterone. Normal 2–3% of total testosterone is free; values <1% indicate excess binding protein or true testosterone deficiency.
**Sample Type:** Calculated from total testosterone and SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin), or directly measured via equilibrium dialysis
**Fasting Required:** Yes (9 AM collection preferred for morning peak)

### What This Number Means

Testosterone in serum exists in three forms: (1) free testosterone (unbound, ~2–3% of total), (2) albumin-bound testosterone (~20–30%), and (3) SHBG-bound testosterone (~60–70%). Only free and weakly albumin-bound testosterone can diffuse into tissues and produce effects. SHBG strongly binds testosterone, reducing bioavailability. Thus, a person with high total testosterone but very high SHBG (binding most of it) may have low free testosterone and symptoms of deficiency.

Free testosterone percentage can be calculated from total testosterone and SHBG, or directly measured via ultrafiltration or equilibrium dialysis. The calculated form is cheaper and widely available; direct measurement is more accurate but expensive.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Free Testosterone % | Interpretation | Clinical Significance |
|-----|---|---|
| >2.5% | High | Normal to elevated bioavailable testosterone; assess symptoms |
| 2.0–2.5% | Normal | Adequate bioavailable testosterone |
| 1.5–2.0% | Low-normal | Borderline low; assess symptoms and consider total testosterone context |
| 1.0–1.5% | Low | Reduced bioavailable testosterone; likely symptomatic |
| <1.0% | Very low | Significantly reduced bioavailable testosterone; warrants investigation |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Synthesis — Androgen Bioavailability**

Free testosterone percentage directly reflects the Synthesis operation's capacity to produce anabolic effects. High free testosterone % indicates robust androgen signaling; low free testosterone % indicates suppressed androgen availability, even if total testosterone is not markedly reduced.

### What Deviation Signals

**If free testosterone % <1.5% + total testosterone normal (>300 ng/dL):**
- Pattern suggests high SHBG binding excess testosterone
- May be caused by: high estrogen (obesity, alcohol, liver disease), high thyroid hormone, medications (hepatic inducers, finasteride), or genetic high-SHBG states
- Assess symptoms: fatigue, low libido, erectile dysfunction, loss of muscle mass despite normal total testosterone
- If symptomatic, may warrant testosterone replacement or investigation of SHBG elevation cause

**If free testosterone % <1.0%:**
- Significantly reduced bioavailable testosterone
- If accompanied by low total testosterone (<300 ng/dL), indicates primary or secondary hypogonadism
- Requires endocrinology evaluation: hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis assessment with LH and FSH

**If free testosterone % elevated (>3%) + elevated total testosterone:**
- May indicate high androgen production or low SHBG binding
- In women: may indicate PCOS or androgen-secreting tumor; requires evaluation
- In men: rarely pathological unless total testosterone is extremely elevated

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Total testosterone** — Interpretation of free testosterone % depends on total testosterone level; discordance (normal total but low free %) suggests SHBG issue
- **SHBG level** — Directly measured SHBG explains why free testosterone % is elevated or reduced
- **Estradiol** — High estrogen drives SHBG elevation (reducing free testosterone %), common in obesity and liver disease
- **LH and FSH** — If free testosterone and total testosterone are low, determine whether HPG axis dysfunction is primary (testicular) or secondary (pituitary)
- **Symptoms** — Fatigue, decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, loss of muscle mass, mood changes all correlate with free testosterone more than total testosterone

**Key pattern:** Free testosterone % is more clinically relevant than total testosterone alone. Many men with low symptoms have normal total testosterone but low free testosterone due to high SHBG; they benefit from either SHBG reduction (exercise, weight loss, treating liver disease) or testosterone replacement.

### Intervention Levers

If free testosterone is low: (1) weight loss and exercise (both reduce SHBG and improve testosterone status), (2) treatment of underlying conditions (liver disease, hypothyroidism, metabolic syndrome), (3) testosterone replacement if HPG axis dysfunction is primary and symptoms are significant. Monitor testosterone replacement to maintain physiologic range (450–900 ng/dL total, 10–25 pg/mL free), and monitor for adverse effects (erythrocytosis, prostate growth, cardiovascular effects).

## T3/T4 Ratio (Thyroid Hormone Conversion Efficiency)

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Free T3 (triiodothyronine, the active form) divided by free T4 (thyroxine, the prohormone). Ratios 2.0–4.0 indicate normal; ratios <2.0 indicate conversion dysfunction.
**Sample Type:** Derived from free T3 and free T4 blood tests
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

Thyroid hormones exist in a metabolic pathway: thyroid gland produces primarily T4 (thyroxine); peripheral tissues (liver, kidney, intestine, muscle) convert T4 to T3 (the more potent form) via deiodinase enzymes. This conversion is not 100% efficient — some T4 is converted to reverse T3 (an inactive form that blocks T3 action). The T3/T4 ratio reflects conversion efficiency. A high ratio indicates efficient conversion; a low ratio indicates impaired conversion.

Impaired T3/T4 conversion is seen in: aging, selenium or iron deficiency (needed for deiodinase function), high cortisol or chronic stress (which shifts metabolism toward reverse T3), liver disease, kidney disease, and certain medications (beta-blockers, amiodarone).

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| Free T3/Free T4 Ratio | Interpretation | Metabolic Meaning |
|-------|---|---|
| >3.5 | High | Efficient conversion; robust T3 availability; possibly mild hyperthyroidism |
| 2.5–3.5 | Normal | Good T3 conversion efficiency |
| 2.0–2.5 | Low-normal | Borderline conversion efficiency; assess symptoms |
| <2.0 | Low | Impaired conversion; reduced T3 availability; assess for causes |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Regulation — Thyroid Hormone Conversion Efficiency**

T3/T4 ratio directly reflects the Regulation operation's capacity to convert the stored hormone (T4) to the active hormone (T3). Even if the thyroid gland is producing adequate T4 (and TSH is normal), if conversion is impaired, tissues experience functional hypothyroidism despite normal lab values.

### What Deviation Signals

**If T3/T4 <2.0 + elevated reverse T3:**
- Classic pattern of impaired conversion with reverse T3 shunt
- Causes: chronic stress/high cortisol, selenium deficiency, iron deficiency, aging, liver disease
- May produce hypothyroid symptoms (fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, constipation, dry skin) despite normal or low-normal TSH
- Warrants investigation of cause and treatment

**If T3/T4 <2.0 + low free T3 + normal/low TSH:**
- Indicates conversion dysfunction, not primary hypothyroidism
- Thyroid gland function is intact; problem is peripheral metabolism
- Dietary supplementation with T3-containing thyroid extract (desiccated thyroid) or direct T3 replacement (liothyronine) may help; synthetic T4-only (levothyroxine) may not

**If T3/T4 <2.0 + normal T3 and T4 but high reverse T3:**
- Indicates metabolic shunt toward inactive metabolite despite adequate conversion capacity
- Suggests chronic stress, caloric restriction, or illness
- Address stress, improve nutrition, optimize sleep

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Reverse T3** — Should be measured alongside T3/T4 ratio; elevated reverse T3 with low T3/T4 suggests preferential shunt toward inactive metabolite
- **TSH** — If TSH is normal despite low T3, indicates peripheral conversion problem rather than primary thyroid dysfunction
- **Selenium and iron levels** — Both required for deiodinase function; deficiency impairs conversion
- **Cortisol** — High cortisol shifts metabolism toward reverse T3; assess 24-hour cortisol or midnight salivary cortisol
- **Symptoms** — Hypothyroid symptoms with normal TSH and normal T4 suggest impaired conversion; T3/T4 ratio can confirm

**Key pattern:** T3/T4 ratio reveals conversion dysfunction masked by normal TSH. Many patients on levothyroxine alone feel persistently hypothyroid despite normal labs; T3/T4 ratio <2.0 may explain why.

### Intervention Levers

Improve conversion through: selenium supplementation (200 mcg/day), iron status optimization (ferritin 40–80 ng/mL), stress reduction and sleep optimization (reduce cortisol), liver support (reduce alcohol, optimize viral hepatitis status), and dietary improvement. If conversion remains impaired, consider T3-containing medications (desiccated thyroid, levothyroxine + liothyronine combination, or liothyronine alone) rather than T4-only therapy.

## Reverse T3/Free T3 Ratio

**Body Codx Operation:** Regulation
**What It Measures:** Reverse T3 (inactive metabolite) divided by free T3 (active form). Ratios >0.2 indicate excessive reverse T3 production.
**Sample Type:** Derived from reverse T3 and free T3 blood tests
**Fasting Required:** No

### What This Number Means

When T4 is metabolized, it can be converted either to T3 (active, thermogenic) or to reverse T3 (rT3, inactive, metabolically suppressive). Under stress, caloric restriction, illness, or aging, the body preferentially shunts T4 to rT3 instead of T3. This is a metabolic adaptation (reduces energy expenditure, preserves resources) but it produces functionally hypothyroid state.

The rT3/T3 ratio captures this shunt: a high ratio indicates that most T4 is being converted to inactive metabolite, not active hormone. This produces hypothyroid symptoms despite normal TSH and T4.

### Optimal vs. Conventional Reference Ranges

| rT3/Free T3 Ratio | Interpretation | Metabolic State |
|--------|---|---|
| <0.15 | Low | Efficient conversion toward T3; euthyroid |
| 0.15–0.20 | Normal | Balanced conversion; healthy |
| 0.20–0.30 | Elevated | Increased reverse T3 shunt; mild hypothyroid state |
| >0.30 | Very elevated | Severe reverse T3 shunt; significant hypothyroid state |

### Body Codx Operation Reading

**Regulation — Metabolic Suppression Response**

rT3/T3 ratio reflects the Regulation operation's response to metabolic stress. High rT3/T3 indicates the system is in conservation mode: it has detected insufficient resources (or perceived threat) and is downregulating metabolism to preserve energy. This is adaptive in acute crisis but becomes pathological if sustained.

### What Deviation Signals

**If rT3/T3 >0.20:**
- Elevated metabolic suppression; person is in "conservation mode"
- Causes: chronic stress, caloric restriction (dieting), illness, sleep deprivation, high training volume
- Produces: fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain (despite caloric restriction), constipation, dry skin, depression
- First intervention: remove the stressor or improve the stressor response

**If rT3/T3 >0.30 + high cortisol + low sleep quality:**
- Pattern of severe metabolic suppression from chronic stress
- Requires comprehensive stress management: sleep optimization, exercise (moderate, not excessive), meditation/mindfulness, nutritional support
- May warrant counseling or psychiatric support if depression/anxiety are significant

**If rT3/T3 >0.20 + caloric restriction (dieting):**
- Metabolic adaptation to underfeeding
- First step: increase caloric intake; excessive dieting drives reverse T3 shunt
- If dieting is necessary, ensure adequate protein and micronutrients to minimize metabolic suppression

### Pattern Recognition

*This test becomes more meaningful when read alongside:*
- **Free T3 and reverse T3 separately** — Discordance (normal T3 but high rT3 vs. low T3 and high rT3) indicates severity of shunt
- **TSH and free T4** — If these are normal but rT3/T3 is elevated, indicates peripheral conversion problem, not primary thyroid dysfunction
- **Cortisol (24-hour urine, midnight salivary, or diurnal curve)** — High cortisol drives rT3 shunt; identify and treat stress source
- **Symptoms and lifestyle** — Active stress, excessive training, caloric restriction, poor sleep should be addressed before blaming thyroid
- **Energy expenditure assessment** — May warrant metabolic testing (indirect calorimetry) if resting metabolic rate is unexpectedly low

**Key pattern:** rT3/T3 ratio is the most specific marker of metabolic suppression. It indicates whether the thyroid problem is glandular dysfunction or metabolic stress response.

### Intervention Levers

Primary: remove or address the stressor (stress management, adequate sleep 7–9 hours, moderate exercise, adequate caloric intake). Secondary: selenium supplementation (200 mcg/day), iron optimization, and stress-reducing adaptogenic herbs (rhodiola, ashwagandha). Tertiary: if ratio remains elevated despite stress reduction, consider T3-containing medications. Do not initiate thyroid hormone replacement until stressors are addressed; replacing hormone in someone in "survival mode" may not resolve symptoms if metabolism is still suppressed.
