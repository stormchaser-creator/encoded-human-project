## The Guild: Who They Are

The HDAC Inhibitor Guild is a functional class of organisms, not a taxonomic one. What unites them is a shared metabolic output: **butyrate**, a four-carbon short-chain fatty acid produced by anaerobic fermentation of dietary fiber.

**Primary members:**

- _Faecalibacterium prausnitzii_ — the most abundant single organism in a healthy human colon; lowest abundance in IBD, depression, metabolic disease
- _Roseburia intestinalis_ — major butyrate producer via butyryl-CoA:acetate CoA-transferase pathway
- _Eubacterium rectale_ — obligate anaerobe, strict butyrate producer
- _Butyrivibrio fibrisolvens_ — fiber-fermenting specialist
- _Clostridiales_ (order-level) — the broader clade containing most guild members; Ruminococcaceae and Lachnospiraceae are the primary families

**Presence in KB:** Confirmed across multiple Tier 1 entries. PMID 29438462 (butyrate as HDAC inhibitor, dual G-protein coupled receptor signaling) confirmed with source authority 0.85.

## The Mechanism: How They Run the Program

### Step 1 — Substrate arrives

Dietary fiber (especially resistant starch, arabinoxylan, inulin, pectin) reaches the distal colon undigested. The guild ferments it via anaerobic pathways.

### Step 2 — Butyrate is produced

Fermentation yields butyrate (and acetate, propionate). Butyrate is the primary colonocyte energy source — colonocytes derive ~70% of their ATP from butyrate oxidation.

### Step 3 — HDAC inhibition begins

Butyrate crosses the colonocyte membrane and enters the nucleus. There it **inhibits histone deacetylases (HDACs)** — the enzymes that remove acetyl groups from histone tails. When HDACs are inhibited, histones remain acetylated. Acetylated histones keep chromatin open. Open chromatin = genes get read.

**This is not a side effect. This is the primary mechanism.** Butyrate was the first identified HDAC inhibitor (Canani et al., 2011; Davie 2003). Every HDAC inhibitor in oncology is essentially a synthetic version of what _Faecalibacterium prausnitzii_ has been producing in human colons for hundreds of thousands of years.

### Step 4 — Gene expression changes

The genes upregulated by butyrate-mediated HDAC inhibition include:

- Tight junction proteins (claudin, occludin) → barrier integrity
- Anti-inflammatory cytokine profiles (IL-10 upregulation, IL-6 suppression)
- Neuroprotective genes in CNS (confirmed in spinal cord injury models, source_authority 0.85)
- Regulatory T-cell (Treg) induction genes — confirmed Tier 1 (Atarashi et al., 2011)
- Neurotransmitter synthesis genes — serotonin, GABA precursor pathways

### Step 5 — The program propagates systemically

Butyrate doesn't stay in the colon. It enters portal circulation, reaches the liver, crosses the blood-brain barrier in measurable quantities. Systemic HDAC inhibition from gut-derived butyrate has downstream effects on:

- Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis set point
- Neuroinflammatory tone
- Microglial activation thresholds
- Mood and stress reactivity

**The guild is not just maintaining gut health. It is running a continuous epigenetic program that shapes how the entire organism responds to stress, inflammation, and threat.**

**Gap fills (crawled 2026-03-26):**

### Gap 1: Specific Histone Marks

Butyrate exhibits mark-selective histone acetylation, increasing H3K9ac and H3K27ac at distinct genomic loci. At promoters of stress-response and immune genes, H3K9ac deposition frequently precedes transcriptional silencing through concurrent recruitment of Mi-2/NuRD repressor complexes — histone acetylation alone does not guarantee gene activation. Context-dependent chromatin architecture (enhancer state, insulator placement, transcription factor availability) determines functional outcome. Butyrate also drives H3 acetylation at the Foxp3 promoter and conserved non-coding sequence regions during Treg differentiation.

- Chang et al., PNAS 2014 (PMID 24390544) — H3K9ac at NOS2, IL-6, IL-12b promoters in macrophages
- Furusawa et al., Nature 2013 (PMID 24226770) — H3 acetylation at Foxp3 regulatory regions
- Butyrate drives H3K9 acetylation via PPARγ/PGC1α in ovarian granulosa cells (PMID 33433947)

### Gap 2: Stress-Regulatory Gene Classes

Butyrate orchestrates a coordinated shift in stress-response gene expression: HPA axis normalization (CRHR2↑, corticosterone↓), immune tolerance consolidation (Foxp3↑, IL-10↑, Helios↑), and selective proinflammatory suppression (NOS2↓, IL-6↓, IL-12↓) while preserving baseline macrophage antimicrobial capacity (TNF-α and MCP-1 unchanged). This selective rewiring operates through histone acetylation at stress-regulatory promoters paired with recruitment of Mi-2/NuRD repressor complexes at secondary response loci, creating a coherent epigenetic architecture that subordinates acute stress reactivity to chronic immune homeostasis. Butyrate also inhibits type 2 innate lymphoid cell-dependent airway hyperreactivity by suppressing GATA3.

- Wang et al., mSystems 2023 (PMID 37358267) — CRHR2 normalization via HDAC inhibition at promoter
- Furusawa et al., Nature 2013 (PMID 24226770) — Foxp3+ Treg induction
- Chang et al., PNAS 2014 (PMID 24390544) — selective NOS2/IL-6/IL-12 suppression
- Butyrate inhibits ILC2 via GATA3 downregulation (PMID 29522844)

### Gap 3: Ancestral Reference Ranges

Hadza hunter-gatherer microbiota shows marked _Faecalibacterium_ depletion relative to modern industrialized populations. The ancestral pattern is a Prevotella co-abundance group (CAG) dominance, with butyrate synthesis achieved through metabolically diverse producers (_Butyricicoccus_, _Eubacterium_, _Sporobacter_, _Oscillibacter_) rather than _Faecalibacterium_ monoculture. _Faecalibacterium_ bloom may be a post-agricultural, processed-food phenomenon rather than an evolutionary baseline. This reframes the clinical marker: microbiota diversity and metabolic redundancy — not _Faecalibacterium_ abundance per se — may be the operative health indicator. Hadza microbiota exhibits seasonal oscillation (Bacteroidetes cycling between wet/dry seasons) with stable Firmicutes.

- Schnorr et al., Nature Communications 2014 (PMID 24736369) — first comprehensive Hadza microbiome study
- Smits et al., Science 2017 (PMID 28839072) — seasonal cycling in Hadza microbiota, n=350 over 12 months
- Cervantes-Luevano et al., mBio 2018 (PMID 30118385) — environment-diet linkage

**Important reframe:** The M-01 paper's clinical translation (depleted _F. prausnitzii_ = missing ancestral encoding) must be qualified. The ancestral encoding was carried by a _diverse guild_, not a single species. _F. prausnitzii_ dominance in Western guts may itself be a compensatory bloom in an otherwise depleted ecosystem. The clinical marker should be guild diversity and total butyrate production capacity, not single-species abundance.

### Gap 4: Transgenerational Guild Depletion

Confirmed: low-fiber (low microbiota-accessible carbohydrate/MAC) diet produces cumulative, partially irreversible diversity loss across generations. Within a single generation, depletion is largely reversible upon dietary restoration. However, by generation 4, approximately 75% of bacterial species present in the original cohort become extinct and remain absent even after dietary reintroduction. Recovery requires active fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) — high-fiber diet alone restores only ~33% of lost taxa; 67% remain permanently extinct. This is a ratchet dynamic: each generation's depletion compounds because rare taxa face extinction risk from genetic drift in bottlenecked founder populations and transfer inefficiently to offspring.

- Sonnenburg et al., Nature 2016 (PMID 26762459) — "Diet-induced extinctions in the gut microbiota compound over generations." Multi-generational humanized mouse model; demonstrates irreversible 4-generation extinction, FMT requirement for restoration.

**Translation:** Modern microbiota depletion constitutes a semi-permanent transgenerational modification of the ecosystem. Dietary reversal alone is necessary but insufficient. Restoration requires active microbial inoculation — writing the missing genomes back into the intestinal niche.
