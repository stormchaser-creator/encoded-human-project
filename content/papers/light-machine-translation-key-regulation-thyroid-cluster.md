## MARKER ONE: TSH — Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone

### What It Measures

TSH is produced by the anterior pituitary gland in response to signals from the hypothalamus via thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH). Its primary function is to stimulate the thyroid gland to produce and release thyroid hormones — predominantly T4, with a smaller amount of T3. TSH operates within a classic negative feedback loop: when circulating thyroid hormone levels fall, TSH rises to stimulate more production; when thyroid hormone levels are sufficient or elevated, TSH falls to reduce stimulation .

Standard laboratory reference ranges for TSH typically span 0.4–4.0 mIU/L, though functional medicine practitioners often prefer a narrower optimal window of approximately 1.0–2.5 mIU/L for symptomatic assessment [1]. INTERPRETATION: The "normal" range for TSH is itself a contested terrain. Large population studies establish these ranges from averaged values across individuals who may include subclinically hypothyroid participants, potentially skewing the "normal" band upward .

TSH measures, at the biological level, the brain's demand on the thyroid. A high TSH means the pituitary is calling loudly — the thyroid is not keeping up. A low TSH means the pituitary has stepped back — either because there is adequate hormone circulating, or because pituitary function itself is suppressed (as in secondary or tertiary hypothyroidism, which is a distinct and often underrecognized pattern).

### Clinical Reading

**Elevated TSH (above functional optimal):** FACT: Elevated TSH is the primary screening indicator for primary hypothyroidism — the thyroid is being asked to produce more but is underperforming. Clinically this presents as fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, cognitive slowing ("brain fog"), constipation, hair thinning, dry skin, slowed reflexes, and depression. FACT: These symptoms correlate with reduced metabolic rate consequent to insufficient thyroid hormone action at the cellular level.

**Suppressed TSH (below range or very low):** FACT: Suppressed TSH indicates either exogenous thyroid hormone excess (overmedication), endogenous hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease, toxic nodular goiter, thyroiditis with release phase), or — critically — pituitary suppression from chronic stress-driven hypercortisolemia, which can downregulate TRH release from the hypothalamus [2].

INTERPRETATION: In the LMT framework, TSH is specifically read as the signal of **demand intensity** — the degree to which the system is being pressed to generate metabolic momentum. A high TSH in this reading is the body amplifying its call for more energy, more tempo, more metabolic drive. A suppressed TSH without genuine hyperthyroidism is read as a silencing of the demand signal — the pituitary has been worn down or suppressed from above.

### Pulsation

TSH does not hold a flat value across time. FACT: TSH displays circadian variation, with levels peaking in the late evening/early night hours and reaching their nadir in the afternoon [3]. This means a single-point measurement captures only one position in an ongoing oscillation. The pulsation pattern of TSH is also responsive to acute physiological stress — fasting, illness, extreme caloric restriction can all alter TSH levels independently of true thyroid function .

In LMT reading, the pulsation of TSH is relevant because it points to the organism's **rhythmic calling capacity**. ## MARKER TWO: Free T4 — The Reserve

### What It Measures

Free T4 (thyroxine) is the primary secretory product of the thyroid gland. FACT: Approximately 80% of thyroid hormone production is T4, with the remaining 20% being direct T3 secretion [5]. T4 is largely a prohormone — it has limited direct biological activity. Its primary function is to circulate as a reservoir that peripheral tissues can convert into the active form, T3, according to local need.

FACT: "Free" T4 refers to the unbound fraction — the portion not attached to transport proteins (principally thyroxine-binding globulin, TBG, transthyretin, and albumin). Only free T4 is biologically available for conversion and cellular uptake. Bound T4 is functionally inactive at the moment of measurement [2]. Standard reference ranges for Free T4 typically span 0.8–1.8 ng/dL, though optimal functional ranges are often placed at the higher end — roughly 1.1–1.7 ng/dL .

### Clinical Reading

**Low Free T4:** FACT: Reduced Free T4 indicates reduced thyroid hormone output — either because the thyroid gland itself is underproducing (primary hypothyroidism), or because pituitary drive is insufficient (secondary hypothyroidism, rarer). Low Free T4 with high TSH confirms primary hypothyroidism. Low Free T4 with low or normal TSH suggests secondary or tertiary hypothyroidism — a pattern that is frequently missed because TSH screening alone does not catch it.

**High Free T4:** FACT: Elevated Free T4, particularly with suppressed TSH, indicates hyperthyroidism. In Graves' disease, TSH receptor antibodies directly stimulate the thyroid, bypassing normal pituitary control and producing excess hormone [6].

**Normal Free T4 with symptoms:** INTERPRETATION: This is the clinically challenging territory. Normal Free T4 with persistent hypothyroid symptoms often indicates a conversion problem — T4 is being produced but not adequately converted to active T3. This is where Reverse T3 and Free T3 become essential companion markers.

A healthy Free T4 level means the reserve is full; a depleted Free T4 means the reserve has been drawn down or is not being replenished.

There is a quality of T4 at this level that is related to quiet confidence: the knowing that resources exist even when they are not currently deployed. They may experience not just fatigue but a more fundamental sense of being tapped out — no buffer, no depth, no safety margin.

FACT: T4 has a relatively long half-life of approximately 7 days, making it a measure of thyroid economy over time rather than acute regulation [7]. This long half-life means Free T4 changes slowly in response to thyroid status — it reflects weeks of thyroid output, not days. This gives T4 a pulsation quality that is **slow, tidal, foundational** rather than rapid and responsive. Changes in Free T4 signal sustained trends in the thyroid's productive capacity.

1. **TBG (thyroxine-binding globulin) pathway**: FACT: Estrogen increases TBG production, which can reduce Free T4 availability even when total T4 appears adequate. This is clinically relevant in women using oral contraceptives or estrogen therapy [8].

## MARKER THREE: Free T3 — The Active Signal

### What It Measures

Free T3 (triiodothyronine) is the biologically active form of thyroid hormone. FACT: T3 is approximately three to five times more potent than T4 in terms of receptor binding and cellular metabolic effect [10]. The majority of circulating T3 — approximately 80% — is derived from peripheral conversion of T4, with only about 20% coming from direct thyroid secretion .

FACT: T3 exerts its effects primarily by binding to thyroid hormone receptors in the cell nucleus, where it directly influences gene transcription. This nuclear action affects virtually every cell in the body, regulating basal metabolic rate, thermogenesis, cardiac output, protein synthesis, neurological function, and the metabolism of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins. T3 is, in the most direct sense, the metabolic signal made operational .

Standard reference ranges for Free T3 span approximately 2.0–4.4 pg/mL, with functional practitioners typically preferring levels in the upper third of this range — roughly 3.2–4.4 pg/mL — for optimal metabolic expression [11].

### Clinical Reading

**Low Free T3 (with normal TSH and T4):** This is the pattern of **conversion hypothyroidism** — the thyroid is producing T4 adequately and the pituitary is not raising an alarm, but the conversion to active T3 is insufficient. INTERPRETATION: This pattern is frequently missed in standard TSH-only or TSH + T4 screening because both those markers may appear normal. The person continues to experience hypothyroid symptoms — fatigue, brain fog, depression, weight resistance, cold intolerance — while being told their thyroid is "normal."

FACT: Common causes of impaired T4-to-T3 conversion include chronic stress and elevated cortisol, selenium deficiency, iron deficiency, low-calorie dieting, inflammatory conditions, and significant physiological illness [12].

**Optimal Free T3:** Optimal Free T3 in the upper functional range correlates with robust metabolic tempo, clear cognition, stable mood, and efficient weight regulation.

### Body/Soul/Spirit Density Readings

**Body density:** Free T3 at the body level is the **active metabolic signal** — the thyroid system's operational output, the hormone that is actually doing the work of metabolic regulation at the cellular level. If TSH is the brain's demand and Free T4 is the reserve, Free T3 is the execution. Body-level reading: is the metabolic signal reaching the cells? Is the work being done?

There is a quality of suppressed expression here — potential that stalls in the conversion zone.

INTERPRETATION: T3 at the soul level is also connected to **voice** — particularly in relation to the thyroid gland's anatomical position at the anterior throat, adjacent to the larynx and vocal structures. FACT: T3 has a much shorter half-life than T4 — approximately 1 day — making it a highly responsive, rapidly oscillating signal compared to T4's slow tide [7]. This short half-life means Free T3 levels respond quickly to changes in conversion efficiency, deiodinase activity, and cellular demand. The pulsation of T3 is therefore **rapid, responsive, and immediate** — more like a heartbeat than a tide.

This rapid pulsation is diagnostically significant: T3 levels can fall quickly in response to acute stressors (illness, fasting, psychological shock) and recover quickly when conditions normalize. Chronic depression of Free T3 — maintained across weeks and months — indicates a sustained conversion block, not an acute stress response.

2. **Cortisol-T3 suppression**: FACT: Elevated cortisol shifts deiodinase activity from T4-to-T3 conversion toward T4-to-rT3 conversion — the body preferentially making the inactive form during stress . 4. **Inflammatory cytokine pathway**: FACT: Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha) have been shown to suppress deiodinase activity and reduce T4-to-T3 conversion, creating what is sometimes called "low T3 syndrome" in chronic inflammatory states .

## MARKER FOUR: Reverse T3 — The Brake

### What It Measures

Reverse T3 (rT3) is an isomer of T3 — it contains the same molecular components but in a different structural configuration that renders it biologically inactive. FACT: rT3 is produced from T4 by the D3 deiodinase enzyme, which removes a different iodine atom than D1/D2 do, producing the mirror-image molecule . rT3 does not activate thyroid receptors; instead, it may actually block T3 receptor binding, competing with active T3 for receptor sites .

FACT: Under normal physiological conditions, the body produces relatively small amounts of rT3. Under conditions of significant physiological stress — illness, fasting, caloric restriction, chronic psychological stress, trauma, heavy metals toxicity — the balance shifts dramatically toward rT3 production .

The rT3:T3 ratio is clinically more useful than rT3 alone. FACT: A ratio above approximately 20 (when rT3 is in pg/mL and Free T3 is in pg/mL, or using the alternative calculation of rT3 ÷ Free T3 × 100) may indicate that rT3 is competitively blocking T3 action even when Free T3 appears numerically adequate .

### Clinical Reading

**Elevated rT3:** INTERPRETATION: Elevated rT3 is the body's deliberate downregulation of metabolic rate — a purposive braking of the metabolic engine. This is not a malfunction; it is an adaptive response. The body increases rT3 production to slow metabolism during states of perceived threat, resource scarcity, severe illness, or chronic overload. The problem occurs when this adaptive braking becomes chronic — when the "emergency slow-down" becomes a permanent operating mode.

FACT: Prolonged elevation of rT3 can create a clinical picture indistinguishable from hypothyroidism even when TSH is normal and Free T4 is adequate. Patients may be told repeatedly that their thyroid is "normal" while experiencing all the hallmarks of hypothyroidism — fatigue, cognitive impairment, weight resistance, mood dysregulation.

**Body density:** rT3 at the body level is the **physiological brake** — the body's explicit mechanism for reducing metabolic speed. The body reading asks: Is this brake being applied appropriately (acute, time-limited, protective) or has the brake been stuck in the applied position (chronic, maladaptive, depleting)?

Elevated rT3 at the soul level reads as the pattern of a person who has learned to keep themselves below full operational speed as a protective strategy. Slowing down, staying small, not drawing too much energy or attention — these are the psychological-behavioral expressions of chronically elevated rT3.

INTERPRETATION: The soul reading of rT3 is also connected to the experience of **not being safe to go fast** — where historical contexts have made full expression dangerous, exhausting, or punished. The rT3 signal says: *slow down, go quiet, reduce the metabolic signature.* At the soul level, this often translates as habitual self-dimming.

### Pulsation

rT3 pulsation follows a stress-responsive pattern — it rises in response to physiological and psychological stress cues and should fall when those cues are removed and safety is restored. FACT: The half-life of rT3 is approximately 0.2 days (roughly 4–5 hours), making it one of the most rapidly clearing thyroid metabolites . This means that if the underlying stressor is removed, rT3 levels can normalize relatively quickly. Persistent rT3 elevation therefore indicates either a persistent, unresolved stressor, or a dysregulated stress-response system that continues to produce rT3 signals even in the absence of active threat.

2. **D3 deiodinase regulation**: D3 activity is upregulated by inflammatory cytokines, caloric restriction, and physiological stress. Understanding what is activating D3 is the clinical key to addressing elevated rT3 .

## MARKER FIVE: TPO Antibodies and Thyroglobulin Antibodies — The Autoimmune Signal

### What They Measure

Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) antibodies and thyroglobulin (TG) antibodies are immunological markers indicating that the body's immune system has generated antibodies against components of the thyroid gland itself. FACT: TPO antibodies target the thyroid peroxidase enzyme, essential for hormone synthesis. TG antibodies target thyroglobulin, the protein scaffold on which thyroid hormones are assembled within the follicular colloid [15].

FACT: The presence of elevated thyroid antibodies is the diagnostic hallmark of Hashimoto's thyroiditis — the most common cause of hypothyroidism in iodine-sufficient populations — and Graves' disease, where TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) drive hyperthyroidism [16]. FACT: Elevated TPO antibodies are present in approximately 95% of Hashimoto's cases and approximately 75% of Graves' cases [17].

FACT: A person can have elevated thyroid antibodies with completely normal TSH and Free T4/T3 for years or decades before any measurable hormonal disruption occurs. This "euthyroid Hashimoto's" represents the early autoimmune phase — immune attack ongoing but compensated .

### Clinical Reading

**Elevated TPO and/or TG antibodies:** The presence of elevated thyroid antibodies indicates ongoing immune-mediated inflammation of the thyroid gland. INTERPRETATION: The degree of antibody elevation does not reliably predict the speed of hormonal decline or symptom severity — some individuals with very high antibodies have minimal symptoms; others with modestly elevated antibodies have significant functional impairment. The antibody level measures immunological activity, not necessarily current functional damage.
