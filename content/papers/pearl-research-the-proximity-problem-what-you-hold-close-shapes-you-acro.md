## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: The Ergonomic-Attentional Account (Tier 1)

**Claim:** Mobile reading of markdown files degrades comprehension and retention primarily through ergonomic and attentional mechanisms. Small screen size forces excessive scrolling, breaking the spatial memory of document structure. Markdown rendering failures (tables that overflow, code blocks that truncate, headers that lose hierarchy) strip the document of its navigational scaffolding. The ambient notification environment creates involuntary attentional interruptions that fragment working memory consolidation.

This is the conservative, empirically grounded hypothesis. It aligns with published research on reading medium effects, spatial memory in reading, and cognitive load theory. It does not require any claims about autonomic regulation or phenomenology.

**Lenses:** *Information theory* (signal-to-noise degradation), *signal processing* (filters imposed by the medium that attenuate structural signals), *control theory* (disrupted feedback between reader position and document structure).

**What would falsify it:** Controlled studies showing equivalent comprehension for well-rendered markdown on mobile vs. desktop, with notifications silenced. If the problem is purely ergonomic and attentional, good mobile markdown apps should close the gap substantially.

### Hypothesis B: The Regulatory Environment Account (Tier 2)

**Claim:** The problem is not primarily ergonomic but autonomic-regulatory. The mobile context creates a neuroceptive environment that biases the nervous system toward sympathetic mobilization — mild vigilance, shortened attention oscillation, readiness to disengage. This state is incompatible with the sustained parasympathetic engagement required for genuine meaning-making from complex structured text. Markdown's structural elements (headers, lists, code fences) function not just as navigational aids but as *pacing cues* — co-regulatory signals that help the reader maintain coherent attention over long documents. When these cues are absent or degraded, the reader loses not just orientation but regulatory support.

This hypothesis extends polyvagal theory beyond its usual social domain. Porges describes the social engagement system as activated by specific cues in the environment — prosody, facial animation, eye contact. The hypothesis here is that structured text functions analogously: it provides cues that signal 'this environment is coherent, you are safe to slow down and absorb.' When mobile rendering destroys these cues, the text becomes a less safe environment for deep reception.

**Lenses:** *Coupled oscillators* (reading pace and document structure as rhythmically coupled; broken coupling degrades absorption), *control theory* (markdown structure as a pacing setpoint; degraded rendering removes the setpoint), *complexity emergence* (genuine understanding as an emergent property of sustained, regulated engagement — not achievable in fragmented states).

**What would falsify it:** HRV or respiratory sinus arrhythmia measurements showing no autonomic state difference between mobile and desktop reading of equivalent documents, controlling for content and environment.

### Hypothesis C: The Phase Transition Account (Tier 3)

**Claim:** There is a threshold below which reading becomes scanning — a genuine phase transition, not a gradual degradation. This threshold is crossed when the combined regulatory cost of the reading environment (ergonomic strain, rendering failures, pace disruption, ambient threat) exceeds the reader's available generative substrate. Below the threshold, the reader is constructing meaning. Above the threshold, they are pattern-matching surface features. Mobile reading of complex markdown documents may routinely cross this threshold for many readers, producing sessions that *feel* like reading but are functionally scanning.

The soul-mirror of NAD+ decline provides the most evocative frame: *'when the cost of defending and repairing exceeds the capacity to generate, the self loses not just energy but its regulatory coherence.'* Applied to reading: when the environment demands more repair work (re-orienting, re-parsing, re-establishing context) than the reader has substrate to provide, the higher-order function — meaning construction — fails not gradually but categorically.

The spirit-mirror adds: *'the capacity to sense time, to discern signal from noise, to rest in being — attenuate not as spiritual failure but as a depletion of the originary ground.'* The reader who cannot tell whether they have understood something is not failing cognitively — they may be reading in a depleted state.

**Lenses:** *Phase transitions* (the scanning/reading bifurcation), *chaos attractors* (two stable attractor states — reading and scanning — with a bifurcation point between them), *entropy* (the reading environment as an entropy gradient; sufficient structure holds the reader in a low-entropy comprehension state; insufficient structure allows entropic drift toward surface processing).

**What would falsify it:** Evidence that degradation in reading comprehension on mobile is linear and continuous rather than threshold-structured; evidence that readers cannot reliably self-report whether they are 'reading' or 'scanning'; evidence that the transition is easily reversible within a session.

## Debate

### Against A
Hypothesis A is well-supported but potentially too narrow. It predicts that good mobile markdown apps should solve the problem. But anecdotal evidence suggests that even with excellent rendering (Obsidian mobile, iA Writer), long-form markdown documents are harder to absorb on mobile than desktop. This suggests the problem exceeds rendering quality — it may be structural to the mobile context itself.

### Against B
The extension of polyvagal theory to reading environments is inferential. Porges did not study reading; he studied social interaction and clinical populations. The claim that structural text functions as co-regulatory input is a hypothesis, not established science. However, this does not make it wrong — nervous system research has repeatedly shown that the body does not cleanly distinguish 'social' from 'environmental' input at the autonomic level.

### Against C
The NAD+ and generative currency framing is explicitly metaphorical at the soul and spirit levels — these are fractal mirrors, not mechanistic claims. Applying them to reading risks overstating their precision. The phase transition claim is also empirically underspecified: what exactly is the threshold variable? However, the phenomenological reality named by this hypothesis — the recognizable shift from absorption to scanning that readers report — is real, and it deserves a mechanistic account.

## Synthesis

The three hypotheses are not competing — they describe the same phenomenon at different scales and densities.

At the **body level**: mobile reading of markdown degrades the ergonomic and attentional conditions for comprehension. This is real, measurable, and addressable through better apps and environments.

At the **soul level**: mobile reading is a regulatory environment problem. The medium biases the nervous system away from the state required for meaning-making. Markdown's structural signals lose their pacing and orienting function when rendered poorly or consumed at mobile scroll speed. The reader becomes a scanner without knowing it.

At the **spirit level**: there is a question that Pearl cannot yet answer — what does habitual mobile-only consumption of structured knowledge do to the *faculty* of deep reading over time? Not just per session, but as a cultivated capacity? The soul-mirror of the mobile phone entry asks: *'what are you holding close that is slowly dysregulating you?'* The spirit-mirror asks: *'what has you mistaking familiarity for neutrality?'*

If mobile reading has become the default mode of knowledge consumption, and if it reliably produces scanning rather than reading, then the cost is not just missed comprehension — it is the slow atrophying of the capacity for depth.
