## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: Information-Completeness Protocol Differences

The conservative scientific hypothesis frames the narrative vs. PRISMA distinction as primarily an **information protocol** problem. PRISMA-compliant reviews mandate: (1) pre-registered PICO question, (2) exhaustive multi-database search, (3) documented PRISMA flow diagram showing included/excluded studies, (4) risk-of-bias assessment using validated tools (Cochrane RoB, Newcastle-Ottawa), (5) GRADE evidence quality assignment. Narrative reviews mandate none of these — relying instead on expert judgment at each step.

The information-theoretic prediction: because narrative reviews have higher degrees of freedom in study selection, they have higher signal-to-noise ratio **variance** — capable of producing both more insightful and more biased conclusions than systematic reviews, depending on reviewer quality. Systematic reviews sacrifice ceiling performance for floor protection.

**Analytical lenses engaged**: Information theory (signal-to-noise), network theory (hub vs. distributed topology of evidence selection), control theory (PRISMA as closed-loop feedback vs. narrative as open-loop).

**Falsified by**: Studies showing equivalent conclusion accuracy between formats across diverse clinical domains.

### Hypothesis B: Domain-Dependent Phase Transition

The integrative hypothesis argues that the appropriate methodology is determined by where the evidence field sits relative to its **bifurcation point** — the threshold between stable-consensus and contested-contested dynamics.

In high-consensus, mechanistically well-characterized domains (glucocorticoid neurophysiology, sulforaphane biochemistry), the evidence attractor is stable. Expert narrative synthesis can reliably navigate to the basin without systematic search scaffolding because the attractor is large, well-defined, and not contested. Sapolsky's narrative synthesis on glucocorticoids is Tier 1 established precisely because he is expert in a stable attractor domain.

In contested, multi-mechanism, commercially-influenced domains (supplement effects on inflammation, dietary protein optimization, cancer biomarkers), the evidence landscape is near a bifurcation point — small differences in study selection produce dramatically different conclusions. Here, PRISMA's structural constraints prevent the system from collapsing into reviewer-specific attractor basins.

The quercetin/RA narrative review exemplifies the mismatch: a contested, commercially-relevant domain being addressed with narrative methodology, yielding 'moderate' evidence strength that cannot be externally validated.

**Analytical lenses engaged**: Chaos attractors, phase transitions, complexity emergence.

**Falsified by**: PRISMA reviews systematically outperforming expert narratives even in high-consensus domains.

The radical hypothesis, drawing primarily from the fractal mirror entries, proposes that the narrative review / PRISMA distinction is a surface manifestation of a **scale-invariant truncation pattern** that appears across biological (cfDNA fragmentation), psychological (defensive relational truncation), and epistemological (incomplete search arc) scales.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The information-completeness framing assumes that exhaustive search improves signal-to-noise. But in nutrition science — Pearl's primary domain — the published literature is notoriously heterogeneous: wildly varying populations, doses, durations, outcome measures, and funding sources. Aggregating this noise systematically may produce **false precision** — a forest plot with a pooled effect size that has no biological meaning because the pooled studies are too dissimilar. Expert reviewers, applying domain knowledge to weight and contextualize, may actually produce better-calibrated conclusions in high-noise literatures.

The strongest support: PRISMA's pre-registration (PROSPERO) and funnel plot analysis directly address publication bias in pharmacological literatures with commercial funding — where the stakes of false positives are highest and expert memory is most prone to motivated omission.

### Against Hypothesis B

The domain-dependency argument risks becoming unfalsifiable in practice. Who decides when a domain has reached 'stable attractor' status sufficient to warrant narrative review? The very researchers most likely to choose narrative methodology (senior experts with strong prior beliefs) are the least well-positioned to judge their domain's bifurcation status objectively. The criterion for when PRISMA is unnecessary may itself require a systematic review to establish.

The strongest support: The Journal of Clinical Epidemiology's 'systematic review waste' literature documents enormous resource expenditure on systematic reviews confirming established consensus — suggesting that Hypothesis B's domain-sensitivity concern is recognized even within the PRISMA-advocating community.

### Against Hypothesis C

This hypothesis commits the cognitive error of **analogical essentialism** — treating structural similarity as evidence of identity. The fact that cancer cell cfDNA fragmentation, psychological defensiveness, and literature search incompleteness all involve 'cutting short' establishes a shared metaphorical frame, not a shared mechanism. The fractal mirror entries are explicitly labeled as consciousness-level reflections, not empirical claims about isomorphic structure. Varela's autopoiesis is a rigorous theory, but applying it to review methodology requires formal argument, not poetic resonance.

The strongest support: Resource-constrained truncation IS a genuine cross-scale phenomenon in complex systems theory. Sapolsky's 'emergency energy budget' framing has direct mechanistic grounding at the neuronal level, and extending it to cognitive/epistemological function under resource pressure is not obviously wrong — it is precisely how cognitive load and decision fatigue research frames research quality degradation under time pressure.

## Synthesis

The three hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. The most defensible integrated position is:

1. **Hypothesis A is mechanistically correct** in specifying what differentiates the methodologies — information protocol completeness. This is established empirical fact about how each method works.

2. **Hypothesis B is strategically correct** in specifying when each methodology is appropriate — domain complexity and evidence stability determine the cost-benefit of PRISMA rigor. This is the clinically actionable insight.

3. **Hypothesis C is heuristically suggestive** but epistemically premature — the truncation-as-dysregulation fractal requires formal structural demonstration, not poetic isomorphism. It may generate productive research questions without being adoptable as a working hypothesis yet.
