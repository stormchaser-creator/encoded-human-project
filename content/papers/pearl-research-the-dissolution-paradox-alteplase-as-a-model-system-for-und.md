## Evidence Review

### Biological Layer: Mechanism

Alteplase (Activase, Cathflo Activase; RxCUI 1804799) is a serine protease that acts as a fibrin-specific activator of plasminogen. Its mechanism involves two sequential steps: (1) binding to fibrin at the clot site via its fibrin-binding domain, which dramatically increases local concentration of the enzyme at the target; (2) catalytic conversion of plasminogen to plasmin, which then degrades the fibrin matrix through direct proteolytic cleavage.

The critical pharmacological innovation of tPA over earlier thrombolytics (e.g., streptokinase) is precisely this fibrin-specificity. Streptokinase activates plasminogen throughout the circulation, creating a systemic lytic state with attendant hemorrhagic risk. Alteplase preferentially activates plasminogen at the fibrin surface, concentrating its effect at the site of occlusion. This is not merely a chemical distinction — it is an information-processing distinction: the fibrin-binding domain encodes an address, and therapeutic action is gated by correct address recognition.

The pathways affected span three coupled systems: the fibrinolytic pathway (plasminogen → plasmin → fibrin degradation), the coagulation cascade (indirectly disrupted as fibrin is degraded), and the plasminogen activator inhibitor system (PAI-1 and PAI-2 are the endogenous brakes on this activation). This three-system involvement means alteplase is not acting on a single target but is modulating a regulatory network.

The frequency notation ('not specified') reflects genuine clinical uncertainty: ICH risk in ischemic stroke is estimated at 4-6% with alteplase in major trials (NINDS, ECASS), but this aggregate figure conceals enormous patient-level variation based on blood pressure, prior anticoagulation, clot age, and time from symptom onset. ## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: The Gain-Modulation Model (Tier 1 — Published Science)

**Claim**: Alteplase's therapeutic window is a control-theory problem. The fibrinolytic system operates as a coupled feedback oscillator between pro-coagulant and anti-coagulant forces. Alteplase shifts the gain of this oscillation, and hemorrhagic risk reflects insufficient damping in patients whose coagulation/fibrinolysis system was already near its stability boundary.

**Analytical Lenses**: Control theory, coupled oscillators, chaos attractors.

The coagulation cascade is one of biology's canonical positive-feedback amplification systems: thrombin amplifies its own generation through multiple feedback loops (factors V, VIII, XI activation), while simultaneously activating anti-coagulant pathways (protein C, thrombomodulin). The fibrinolytic system sits downstream as a counter-regulatory oscillator. When alteplase is introduced, it effectively increases the gain of the fibrinolytic arm.

In a patient with well-damped, robustly regulated coagulation, this gain increase produces therapeutic fibrinolysis at the target site without runaway systemic effects. In a patient with impaired regulatory capacity (recent surgery, active bleeding, severe hypertension, prior cerebrovascular disease), the same gain increase pushes the oscillator past its stability boundary, producing ICH.

**Falsifiable by**: If ICH risk were uncorrelated with pre-treatment coagulation parameters, or if the dose-response relationship for hemorrhage were flat, this model would fail.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The control-theory framing is seductive but may overestimate our ability to measure the relevant parameters. Despite decades of clinical research, the NIHSS score, blood pressure thresholds, and time-from-onset criteria used in stroke thrombolysis protocols are blunt proxies for the underlying regulatory state of the coagulation oscillator. The persistence of ICH risk even in carefully selected patients suggests either that the control-relevant variables are unmeasured, or that the system is chaotic (sensitive to initial conditions in ways that make prediction fundamentally limited). If chaotic, control theory is structurally correct but practically useless at individual patient prediction.

**Counter**: Even in a chaotic system, identifying the stability boundary and designing interventions that avoid pushing the system past it is a genuine contribution. The value of the model is in population-level risk architecture even if individual prediction is limited.

### Against Hypothesis B

The 'information signal' framing risks being poetic rather than mechanistic. Alteplase is an enzyme with catalytic rate constants, not merely a molecular address label. The fibrin-binding domain enhances local concentration (a kinetic effect) and may allosterically enhance catalytic activity — but the work is still done by chemistry, not information. Stretching this to psychological/consciousness scales may introduce category errors.

**Counter**: The pharmacological superiority of fibrin-specific tPA over streptokinase is entirely attributable to address-specificity, not to greater catalytic power. In that sense, the information component carries demonstrable clinical weight. The cross-scale analogy is not claiming identical mechanisms but identical structural features of the operation.

### Against Hypothesis C

Phase transition language is increasingly applied in biology and psychology without mathematical rigor. Invoking phase transitions without specifying the order parameter, conjugate field, and transition universality class is metaphor, not mechanism. The cross-scale generalization — from molecular fibrinolysis to consciousness dissolution — may be an impressive-sounding narrative that cannot generate testable predictions.

**Counter**: The specific prediction that agents with shorter half-lives should show lower hemorrhagic rates (if the reverse-transition propagation model is correct) is testable and has preliminary support in comparisons between alteplase and urokinase. The framework is generating falsifiable predictions even at the speculative tier.

## Implications

### Clinical Implications

If ICH risk is fundamentally a boundary-condition problem (insufficient structural integrity at potential bleeding sites to contain the reverse phase transition), then pre-treatment assessment should focus on vascular boundary integrity — not merely coagulation parameters. Cerebral microangiopathy, amyloid angiopathy, and prior microhemorrhages are already recognized contraindications, but this framework provides a unifying principle: they are markers of degraded boundary conditions at potential hemorrhage sites.

Catheter clearance (mechanical endpoint, short required duration) may tolerate or benefit from even shorter-acting agents than acute stroke (which requires sustained patency over hours).
