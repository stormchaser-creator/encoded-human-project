## Hypothesis Generation

### Hypothesis A: The Chokepoint Capture Mechanism

**Claim:** The pre-registration movement succeeded not primarily through scientific argument but through the installation of an institutional chokepoint — mandatory registration as a condition of journal publication (ICMJE 2004) — and AI disclosure frameworks are structurally positioned to replicate this mechanism through mandatory disclosure as a condition of deployment or procurement.

The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors' 2004 policy requiring trial registration as a condition of publication was decisive not because it covered all clinical research but because it covered enough high-prestige research to make the norm propagate. Journals are chokepoint nodes in the legitimacy network of clinical science; controlling them controlled the credibility economy downstream.

The structural question for AI is: what are the equivalent chokepoint nodes? The answer is more complex because AI deployment has a more distributed legitimacy topology than medical publishing. However, candidate chokepoints include: major cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP) for enterprise AI deployment; government procurement standards (EU AI Act, US federal acquisition regulations) for high-stakes applications; liability insurance underwriters for AI in regulated sectors (medicine, law, finance); and professional licensing bodies for practitioners who deploy AI in regulated contexts.

**Analytical lenses:** network_theory (hub node identification), control_theory (installing a setpoint where none existed), phase_transitions (norm propagation dynamics).

### Hypothesis B: The Psycho-Cultural Precondition

**Claim:** Institutional chokepoint capture is the proximate mechanism but not the sufficient condition for the phase transition. A prior psycho-cultural shift — a critical minority of practitioners experiencing credibility-performance culture as corrupting rather than normal — is necessary to provide the coalition that pushes for and accepts mandatory reform.

In clinical trials, this minority existed by the late 1990s: researchers who had directly experienced trial suppression, journal editors who had personally encountered p-hacking, and methodologists who had documented publication bias quantitatively. The Vioxx withdrawal in 2004 (which revealed that Merck had data showing cardiovascular risk before approval) made the cost of the status quo legible to the public and to regulators, providing the political window for ICMJE to act.

In AI, the equivalent minority is currently forming: the interpretability research community, alignment-focused practitioners, and AI auditing professionals constitute a growing group who experience opacity as corrupting. The psycho-cultural shift is underway but has not yet reached critical mass, and the equivalent Vioxx moment — a failure that is both legible to non-specialists and attributable specifically to opacity rather than to AI capability limits — has not yet occurred.

**Analytical lenses:** fractals (same transition at individual/institutional/field levels), chaos_attractors (bifurcation point approaching), complexity_emergence (coalition dynamics).

### Hypothesis C: Thermodynamic Inevitability

**Claim:** Both pre-registration and AI disclosure represent manifestations of a thermodynamic pressure: as systems increase in complexity and claim-generation capacity, the information environment entropy exceeds the discrimination capacity of existing epistemic immune systems, forcing either the development of second-order epistemology (provenance + uncertainty architecture) or the recurrence of credibility collapse.

This hypothesis is more speculative but is consistent with the evidence. The intelligence threshold entry's model of discontinuous jumps in environmental exploitation capacity, combined with the immunosenescence language of the Echinacea spirit mirror ('a dimming of the witness function: the field loses its ability to recognize what is not-self'), suggests a general pattern: when the claim-generation complexity of a field exceeds its epistemic discrimination capacity, one of two trajectories follows — institutional reform that installs second-order tools, or credibility cascade failure that discredits the field.

## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The chokepoint capture mechanism assumes structural equivalence between ICMJE journals and AI deployment platforms. This equivalence does not hold in several important respects. ICMJE journals had a near-monopoly on scientific legitimacy in medicine; there was essentially no credible path to impact for a clinical trial result that had not been published in an ICMJE member journal. AI deployment has no equivalent monopoly: open-source models can be self-hosted, fine-tuned locally, and deployed without passing through any regulated chokepoint. The mechanism that worked for a concentrated, prestige-driven legitimacy economy may not transfer to a distributed, capability-driven one.

However, the chokepoint argument is strengthened by noting that the relevant legitimacy economy for high-stakes AI deployment is not the same as for consumer AI tools. Medical AI, financial AI, and legal AI all operate in regulated environments where liability, professional licensing, and government procurement create concentration equivalent to ICMJE's role. The EU AI Act's risk-tiering approach — applying strict transparency requirements only to 'high-risk' applications — is structurally isomorphic to ICMJE's journal-tier approach.

### Against Hypothesis B

The psycho-cultural framing risks being unfalsifiable and may misidentify the rate-limiting step. The practical history of pre-registration suggests that a small number of institutional decisions by a small number of journal editors was more decisive than any broad cultural shift. Attributing the transition to soul-density values changes may be analytically satisfying but directs reform energy toward consciousness-raising when the leverage point is institutional policy.

The counterargument is that the editors who made those institutional decisions were themselves members of the psycho-cultural minority who had already made the values-level shift. The question is not whether individual transformation is sufficient (it is not) but whether it is necessary (it may be) as a precondition for the coalition that makes institutional reform politically viable.

### Against Hypothesis C

The thermodynamic inevitability argument proves too much. Financial derivatives, legal code complexity, and tax system complexity all exceeded first-order comprehension capacity without triggering pre-registration-equivalent reforms. Instead, they produced opacity as a stable equilibrium — a complexity that serves incumbent interests precisely because it is opaque. There is no thermodynamic law that forces second-order epistemic reform; the alternative stable state is managed ignorance.

The defense is that the 'managed ignorance' stable states in finance and law have also produced periodic credibility cascade failures (2008 financial crisis, legal scandals) — which is precisely the alternative trajectory the hypothesis predicts. The claim is not that reform is thermodynamically forced but that the choice space is between second-order epistemology and recurrent collapse, not between opacity and transparency as permanent equilibria.

## Synthesis

The strongest evolved position integrates all three hypotheses into a temporal model with three layers:

**Layer 1 (Prerequisite): Psycho-cultural minority formation.** A critical minority of practitioners must come to experience the dominant epistemic culture as corrupting rather than normal. This is the soul-density layer. In clinical trials, this took approximately 15-20 years (from early publication bias documentation in the 1980s to the ICMJE decision in 2004). In AI, this process appears to be accelerating — the interpretability and alignment communities have grown rapidly since 2015-2020, and public discourse around AI hallucination, bias, and opacity has created a larger-than-usual non-specialist audience for these concerns.

**Layer 2 (Catalyst): Legible failure event.** A failure that is (a) attributable specifically to opacity/lack of disclosure rather than to capability limits, (b) legible to non-specialist audiences, and (c) consequential enough to create political will for institutional change. In clinical trials, this was the Vioxx withdrawal. The AI equivalent has not yet occurred, but the structural candidates are: a high-profile medical misdiagnosis attributable to undisclosed training data bias; a financial fraud facilitated by opaque AI decision-making; or a legal proceeding where AI-generated evidence is shown to be systematically unreliable in ways that a model card would have disclosed.

**Layer 3 (Institutionalization): Chokepoint capture.** Following the legible failure event, institutional actors at chokepoint nodes (regulators, procurement officers, liability insurers, professional licensing bodies) install mandatory disclosure requirements. The norm propagates from chokepoint to periphery. This is the body-density layer — the structural/institutional operationalization of what was previously a values-level demand.

The pre-registration movement demonstrates that this three-layer sequence is achievable even against strong incumbent resistance. The pharmaceutical industry's opposition to trial registration was substantial and well-resourced; it was overcome not by defeating the incumbents but by making the status quo politically untenable through the combination of documented harm (Vioxx) and available reform mechanism (ICMJE policy).

For AI disclosure frameworks, the strategic implication is that reform energy should be allocated across all three layers simultaneously: (1) continuing to build and articulate the psycho-cultural minority position (soul layer), (2) monitoring for and preparing response frameworks around legible failure events (catalyst layer), and (3) mapping and preparing chokepoint capture strategies for the institutional layer, with particular attention to the distinctive topology of AI deployment compared to medical publishing.

## Implications

### For AI Governance Practice

The pre-registration analogy suggests that voluntary disclosure schemes will not produce durable norm change — they will be adopted by practitioners who have already made the psycho-cultural shift (who are least in need of incentive) and avoided by incumbents (who are most in need of it). Mandatory requirements at institutional chokepoints are necessary, but the chokepoints must be correctly identified for the specific deployment context.

The EU AI Act's risk-tiering approach represents the most structurally sophisticated current attempt to replicate the ICMJE mechanism. Its success will depend on whether 'high-risk AI systems' as defined capture enough of the legitimacy economy to drive norm propagation, or whether the definition is narrow enough that incumbents can route around it through classification arbitrage.

### For Epistemic Reform Theory

The evidence from both clinical trials and AI suggests that epistemic reform follows a predictable three-layer sequence (psycho-cultural precondition → legible failure catalyst → institutional chokepoint capture) rather than a gradual rational persuasion model. This has implications for where reform energy is best spent: documenting harms that meet the 'legibility' criteria (attributable to opacity, not just to bad outcomes) and mapping chokepoint topology in advance of the catalyst event, so that reform infrastructure is ready when political will becomes available.

### For the Relationship Between Soul and Institutional Layer

Perhaps the most underappreciated finding of the pre-registration story is that the soul-layer shift (a minority of practitioners experiencing publication bias as corrupting) was not sufficient to produce reform but appears to have been necessary. This suggests that institutional reform without psycho-cultural foundation is fragile — it will be gamed, undermined by compliance-theater, and reversed when political conditions change. Durable epistemic integrity requires that the disclosure practices be experienced by practitioners as expressive of their identity as scientists/engineers, not merely as regulatory burden.
