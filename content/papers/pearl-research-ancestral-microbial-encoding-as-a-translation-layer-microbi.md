## Debate

### Against Hypothesis A

The strongest objection is that 'ancestral encoding' risks over-reading evolutionary significance into ecological function. Evolution shapes all biological systems, so calling the microbiome an 'archive' implies a structured, retrievable record that may not exist. Functional redundancy in microbial ecosystems further complicates the information-loss narrative — many microbial functions are performed by multiple species, so diversity loss does not necessarily mean proportional information loss.

However, the keystone species concept provides a counter: some microbial species perform non-redundant functions whose loss cannot be compensated. The Tier 1 confidence of the Bush entry provides the strongest available epistemic anchor.

### Against Hypothesis B

The gut-brain axis literature is real but has been significantly overstated in popular science. Many correlational findings between microbiome composition and mental health have failed to replicate. Causality is contested — psychological stress alters microbiome composition as readily as the reverse. The 'translation key' metaphor imports a precision that the current data does not support.

Nevertheless, the structural parallel between the missing soul/spirit density entries and the user's query about 'pipeline status' gives Hypothesis B unusual internal consistency. The gap is real and architectural.

### Against Hypothesis C

Biophotonic communication within the gut microbiome as a functionally significant signal channel has not been demonstrated in mammalian systems at the required scale. Quantum coherence in biology is real but highly localized (protein complexes, individual molecules) — extrapolating to community-level coherent field effects requires several unverified assumptions. This hypothesis generates a compelling narrative but may be outpacing available evidence by several research generations.

The strongest support is Zach Bush's theoretical framework, which explicitly invokes these mechanisms — but Bush's most radical claims have not been subjected to the same peer review scrutiny as the core dysbiosis findings.
