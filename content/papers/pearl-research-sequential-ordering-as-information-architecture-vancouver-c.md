Vancouver sequential numbering is primarily a signal-compression technology. In biomedical literature, where a paper may contain 40–80 references, inline citation must not interrupt reading flow. A superscript number [1] occupies one character and one glance; an author-date citation (Smith et al., 2023) occupies 15+ characters and requires parsing. Sequential numbering by first appearance means that the number also encodes relative importance (earlier-appearing citations are typically foundational), but this is a secondary benefit of a primarily space-saving convention.

## Debate

### Challenging Hypothesis A

The compression argument is plausible but incomplete. Author-date systems are also efficient — they carry author identity information that helps expert readers recognize sources before looking them up, something sequential numbers cannot do. Experts in a field often recognize 'Smith 2019' immediately but must look up '[34]'. For expert readers, author-date may actually be MORE efficient per unit of information transferred. Vancouver format's efficiency advantage may be domain-specific: it favors novice readers and high-citation-density texts, but may disadvantage experts who use citation recognition as a parallel reading channel.

### Challenging Hypothesis B

The argumentative topology claim may be reverse-engineering meaning from a pragmatic convention. Authors do not necessarily place their most foundational citations first — introductions often cite background literature first, which may be less central to the paper's core argument than methods-section citations. The topology encoding, if real, is noisy and inconsistent across papers and authors.

However, the counter-argument is that first-appearance ordering has a systematic bias: introductory citations establish the conceptual field, which is a form of foundational grounding even if not the paper's core empirical contribution. The topology is real even if the encoding is approximate.

### Challenging Hypothesis C

The consciousness-architecture mapping is structurally elegant but may be coincidental. Many sequential systems share cascade properties because sequentiality itself imposes cascade-like structure — this doesn't mean Vancouver format was designed to mirror consciousness, only that both are sequential. The fractal mirrors in the evidence are Pearl's own synthesis entries, not independent Tier 1 sources, which limits their evidentiary weight.

The strongest support for this hypothesis is negative: no one seems to have asked WHY sequential citation conventions survive and spread, only HOW they should be implemented. This absence of 'why' investigation is itself a signal that the consciousness-architecture frame hasn't been brought to bear.
